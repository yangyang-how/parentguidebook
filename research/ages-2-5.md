# Ages 2-5: Comprehensive Research Notes

Research compiled for parentguidebook.org bilingual article.
Sources: AAP, CDC, WHO, NHS, Mayo Clinic, AAPD, ASHA, peer-reviewed journals (2022-2026).

---

## CHILD'S BODY (2-5yr)

### 1. Growth

**Expected Growth Rate**
- Children ages 2-5 typically grow **5-7 cm (2-3 inches) per year** in height
- Weight gain: approximately **2 kg (4-5 lbs) per year**
- Growth rate is slower and steadier than infancy (where length increases ~50% in year one)

**BMI Tracking**
- AAP recommends BMI screening for **all children aged 2 and older at least once per year** (Bright Futures Guidelines)
- BMI percentile classification for children:
  - Underweight: < 5th percentile
  - Healthy weight: 5th to < 85th percentile
  - Overweight: 85th to < 95th percentile
  - Obesity: >= 95th percentile
- CDC released Extended BMI-for-Age Growth Charts (2022) for children with very high BMIs (> 97th percentile)

**Growth Chart Interpretation**
- WHO growth standards used for children 0-24 months; CDC growth reference charts for ages 2-20 years
- At age 2, clinicians switch from WHO to CDC charts -- this transition can cause an apparent shift in z-scores even when growth is stable (AAP Pediatrics, 2025)
- Consistent tracking along a percentile curve matters more than any single measurement
- Crossing two major percentile lines warrants evaluation

**Short Stature Evaluation Criteria**
- Height below the 3rd percentile for age and sex
- Growth velocity < 5 cm/year after age 2
- Height significantly below mid-parental height target
- Crossing downward across two major percentile lines
- Referral to pediatric endocrinologist for bone age X-ray and further workup

> Sources: CDC Growth Charts (cdc.gov/growthcharts); AAP Pediatrics 2025 (publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/156/3/e2025070697); CDC BMI-for-Age (cdc.gov/growth-chart-training); HealthyChildren.org

### 2. Vision

**Amblyopia Screening**
- **US Preventive Services Task Force recommends vision screening for all children ages 3-5** to detect amblyopia or risk factors
- Amblyopia ("lazy eye") affects approximately **2-3% of children**
- Critical window: treatment is most effective when started **before age 7**; children identified before age 2 had outcomes approximately one line better on eye charts than those diagnosed at ages 2-4
- Screening methods: photoscreening devices, visual acuity charts (Lea symbols), cover/uncover test

**Myopia Prevention**
- Children who spend **1-2 hours daily outdoors** have significantly lower risk of developing myopia
- A landmark study showed 40 extra minutes of daily outdoor time reduced myopia risk
- Mechanism: bright natural light stimulates dopamine release in the retina, which inhibits axial elongation
- Near-work activities (screens, reading) increase myopia risk; balance with outdoor time is key
- Global myopia prevalence in children is rising dramatically -- projected to affect 50% of the world population by 2050

**First Comprehensive Eye Exam**
- AAO recommends eye screening at ages 1, 3, and 5 (or whenever concern arises)
- Between ages 3-5, vision and eye alignment should be checked
- First comprehensive eye exam by an ophthalmologist if any screening is abnormal

> Sources: AAO Clinical Statement on Vision Screening (aao.org); AAO Myopia Prevention (aao.org/eye-health/news); NEI Amblyopia (nei.nih.gov); CDC MMWR Vision Screening

### 3. Teeth

**Primary Dentition Timeline**
- Children typically have **20 primary teeth fully erupted by age 3** (range: 2.5-3.5 years)
- Central incisors: 6-12 months
- Lateral incisors: 9-16 months
- First molars: 13-19 months
- Canines: 16-23 months
- Second molars: 23-33 months
- Primary teeth begin falling out around age 6

**Dental Caries Prevalence**
- Dental caries is the **most common chronic disease of childhood** in the United States -- more common than asthma
- Prevalence of lifetime tooth decay in primary teeth: **23.3% in children aged 2-5**
- Untreated decay: **10.4% in ages 2-5**
- Early childhood caries is the single greatest risk factor for caries in permanent teeth

**First Dental Visit**
- AAPD recommends first visit **within 6 months of first tooth eruption or by age 1**
- Dental costs for children whose first visit is before age 1 are **40% lower** in the first five years
- Establish a "dental home" for ongoing preventive care

**Fluoride Recommendations**
- **Under 3 years:** Use a tiny smear (grain-of-rice size) of fluoride toothpaste, twice daily
- **Ages 3-6:** Increase to a pea-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste
- USPSTF recommends fluoride varnish applied by primary care clinicians starting at tooth eruption through age 5, at least every 6 months
- Higher-risk children: fluoride varnish every 3 months
- Community water fluoridation remains a cornerstone of caries prevention

**Brushing Supervision**
- Children should be supervised during brushing and taught to spit (not swallow) toothpaste
- Young children lack the manual dexterity for effective brushing; **parents should brush or assist until approximately age 6-8**

**Thumb-Sucking/Pacifier Impact on Bite**
- Pacifier use beyond 18 months increases risk of anterior open bite, posterior crossbite, and Class II malocclusion
- Daily pacifier use significantly increases chances of anterior open bite vs non-daily use
- Thumb sucking usually ceases spontaneously between ages 2-4
- If habit persists beyond age 4, management/intervention can be instituted
- Mild malocclusion from sucking habits can self-correct once the habit stops
- Pacifier use after 12 months increases risk of acute otitis media (ear infections)

> Sources: AAPD Policy on Early Childhood Caries; AAP Fluoride Clinical Report (Pediatrics 2020); AAPD Pacifier Policy (2024 revision); ADA fluoride toothpaste guidance; HealthyChildren.org

### 4. Motor Skills

**Gross Motor Milestones**
- **Age 2:** Runs with improved coordination, kicks a ball, walks up/down stairs holding rail, begins jumping with both feet
- **Age 3:** Runs well, climbs well, pedals tricycle, walks up stairs alternating feet, jumps with both feet
- **Age 4:** Hops on one foot, catches a bounced ball, jumps with a running start, pedals and steers tricycle well
- **Age 5:** Gallops leading with one foot, skips, balances on one foot for 10 seconds, throws ball overhand, rolls like a log
- Locomotor and object control scores increase progressively from ages 3-5

**Fine Motor Milestones**
- **Age 2:** Stacks 6+ blocks, turns pages of a book, scribbles spontaneously, turns doorknobs
- **Age 3:** Copies a circle, draws a person with 2-4 body parts, uses scissors (snips), threads large beads
- **Age 4:** Copies a cross and square, draws a person with more detail, cuts along a line with scissors, buttons and unbuttons
- **Age 5:** Copies a triangle, writes some letters, uses a fork and knife, dresses independently

**Drawing Progression**
- 2 years: random scribbles
- 3 years: circles, vertical/horizontal lines
- 4 years: crosses, squares, first recognizable human figures ("tadpole" people)
- 5 years: triangles, detailed human figures, beginning letter formation

**Handedness Establishment**
- Early hand preference observable at **6-7 months** during object manipulation
- Handedness continues to strengthen throughout preschool years
- Pre-school children (especially left-handed) have weaker hand preference than older children
- Full lateralization strengthens progressively through age 10
- **Red flag:** Established handedness before 18 months is considered aberrant, especially with increased tone (may indicate hemiplegia/cerebral palsy)

> Sources: CDC Developmental Milestones (cdc.gov/act-early); AAP Motor Delays (Pediatrics 2013); ASHA Communication Milestones; PMC research on laterality (2020)

### 5. Common Health Issues

**Frequent Colds**
- **6-8 colds per year is normal** for preschoolers; up to **12/year** may be normal for children in daycare
- Each cold can last 10-14 days; coughs may persist up to 6 weeks
- Most are caused by viruses (200+ types of rhinoviruses)
- No cause for concern unless: fever >5 days, breathing difficulty, ear pain, or symptoms worsen after initial improvement

**Adenoid and Tonsil Issues**
- Adenoids are largest at **ages 3-5** and naturally shrink after that
- Temporary enlargement from colds/infections is common
- Chronic enlargement can cause mouth breathing, snoring, sleep apnea, recurrent ear infections
- Indications for tonsillectomy: recurrent infections (7 episodes in 1 year, or 5/year for 2 years, or 3/year for 3 years), obstructive sleep apnea

**Functional Constipation**
- Global prevalence: **9.5-14.4%** of children, especially common during toilet training
- **95% of childhood constipation** has no underlying organic cause
- Treatment: behavioral management (regular toilet sits), osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol), adequate fiber and fluids
- AAP fiber guideline: age in years + 5 grams per day (e.g., 7g for a 2-year-old, 10g for a 5-year-old)
- ESPGHAN/NASPGHAN updated treatment protocol published in 2024

**Growing Pains**
- Prevalence: **24-37%** of children aged 4-6 (varies by definition)
- Typically bilateral, non-articular leg pain occurring in the evening or at night
- Pain in lower legs, occurring 1-3 times per week
- **No evidence** they are caused by actual growth -- peak incidence (~age 6) does not align with rapid growth phases
- Self-limiting; most resolve by adolescence
- Red flags: unilateral pain, joint swelling, limping, daytime pain, fever, weight loss
- Management: stretching (quads, hamstrings, calf muscles), massage, heat packs, acetaminophen

**Allergies and Asthma Emergence: The Atopic March**
- The "atopic march": eczema in infancy --> food allergy --> asthma --> allergic rhinitis
- 2024 prevalence in US children: seasonal allergies **20.6%**, eczema **12.7%**, food allergies **5.3%**
- Atopic dermatitis starts in early childhood: **65% affected by 18 months**
- About **30% of children with eczema** develop asthma
- Peak incidence: eczema at ~4 months, food allergy at ~13 months, asthma at ~13 months, allergic rhinitis at ~26 months
- Preschool prevalence: asthma **13.8%**, allergic rhinitis **40.7%**, eczema **20.8%**
- Wheezing before age 3 is common and often viral; persistent wheeze beyond age 3 with family history/eczema increases asthma likelihood

> Sources: HealthyChildren.org (colds); UpToDate (common cold in children); NHS (colds/coughs); PMC growing pains review (2024); AAP Pediatrics (allergy patterns 2023); NCHS Data Brief (2024 diagnosed allergies); ESPGHAN/NASPGHAN (2024 constipation protocol)

### 6. Sleep

**Total Sleep Needs (per 24 hours, including naps)**
- **Ages 1-2:** 11-14 hours (AASM recommendation, endorsed by AAP)
- **Ages 3-5:** 10-13 hours
- **Ages 6-12:** 9-12 hours
- These are total sleep (nighttime + naps)

**Nap Dropping**
- Most children transition from 2 naps to 1 nap around **12-18 months**
- Most children drop their last nap between **ages 3 and 5**
- ~50% of 4-year-olds still nap; by age 5, most do not
- Signs of readiness to drop nap: difficulty falling asleep at nap or bedtime, sleeping well at night without nap

**Nightmares vs Night Terrors**
- **Nightmares:** Occur during REM sleep (second half of night); child wakes up frightened, remembers the dream, can be comforted
- **Night terrors:** Occur during deep non-REM sleep (first third of night); child appears awake but is not, screams/thrashes, does NOT remember the episode, difficult to console during the event
- Night terrors peak in **toddlers and preschoolers** (ages 3-7)
- Night terrors affect approximately **3-6%** of children
- Parent should not try to wake child during night terror; ensure safety

**Bedtime Resistance**
- Common in preschoolers; related to developing autonomy and fears
- Consistent bedtime routine is the most evidence-based approach
- Bedtime routine associated with: earlier bedtimes, shorter sleep onset latency, reduced night wakings, increased sleep duration
- Dose-dependent: more consistent routine = better outcomes

**Melatonin Evidence**
- AAP urges parents to make decisions about melatonin **together with their pediatrician -- cautiously and carefully**
- Most children respond to low doses: **0.5-1 mg**, 30-90 minutes before bedtime
- Most children, including those with ADHD, don't need more than **3-6 mg**
- Melatonin is **unregulated** in the US; actual content may not match labels
- Limited evidence: 5 trials showed benefit in children with ASD, but **no studies in typically developing children**
- No long-term safety data in children
- Prescriptions and overdoses in children aged 0-6 have increased over the past 2 decades
- Should only be used **after healthy sleep habits are established and found insufficient**

> Sources: AASM/AAP sleep recommendations (Pediatrics 2016); Sleep Foundation; HealthyChildren.org (melatonin); PMC melatonin clinical review (2024); Mayo Clinic (preschool sleep)

## NUTRITION (2-5yr)

### 7. Picky Eating Peak

**Food Neophobia Research**
- Food neophobia (fear of new foods) is a **normal developmental phase** peaking between ages **2-6**
- Prevalence of picky eating: **14-50% of preschoolers** depending on definition
- Neophobia is evolutionarily adaptive -- protects newly mobile children from ingesting toxins
- Despite initially negative reactions, children **learn to accept new foods with time and repeated neutral exposures**

**How Many Exposures Needed**
- Research indicates **8-15 exposures** to a new food may be needed before acceptance (some sources cite up to **15-20**)
- Exposure must involve actual tasting, not just visual presentation
- Parents often give up after 3-5 tries
- Repeated exposure should be **pressure-free** -- simply offering alongside accepted foods

**Division of Responsibility (Ellyn Satter Model)**
- Parent decides: **what, when, where** the child eats
- Child decides: **whether and how much** to eat
- Supporting child self-regulation of hunger and intake is associated with eating competence and wider food acceptance
- Evidence supports that shared control in feeding produces better long-term outcomes than pressure-based approaches
- Restrictive snack-management practices can undermine the division of responsibility

**When Picky Eating Is Clinical: ARFID**
- ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder) is diagnosed when eating restrictions result in:
  - Significant weight loss or growth compromise
  - Nutritional deficiency
  - Reliance on nutritional supplements
  - Marked interference with psychosocial functioning
- Unlike typical picky eating, ARFID **does not improve with age** and tends to escalate
- Estimated prevalence: **0.3-5.9%** depending on population studied (5.9% in Swedish preschool screening study, 2025)
- Comorbidities: anxiety disorders (9-72%), ASD (8-55%), language delays (39% vs 13.5% in non-ARFID)
- Three presentations: sensory sensitivity, lack of interest in eating, fear of aversive consequences (choking, vomiting)
- Average delay between onset and diagnosis: **2 years**
- Red flags for referral: losing weight, nutritional deficiencies, eating fewer than 20 foods, severe anxiety around new foods

> Sources: PMC food neophobia review; Satter's Division of Responsibility research (PMC 2014); PMC ARFID review (2024); PubMed ARFID Swedish preschool study (2025); StatPearls ARFID

### 8. Nutritional Needs

**Iron**
- Required daily intake: **7 mg/day** (ages 1-3), **10 mg/day** (ages 4-8)
- Supports brain development, immune function, attention, and learning
- Common deficiency in toddlers, especially with excess milk intake
- Good sources: fortified cereals, lean meats, beans, spinach
- Vitamin C enhances iron absorption

**Calcium**
- **700 mg/day** for ages 1-3
- **1,000 mg/day** for ages 4-8
- Essential for bone development; dairy is primary source
- Non-dairy sources: fortified plant milks, tofu, broccoli, almonds

**Vitamin D**
- **600 IU/day** for all children after age 1 (IOM/AAP recommendation)
- Supplement with vitamin D drops if child drinks **less than 32 oz of milk per day**
- Important for calcium absorption and bone health
- Deficiency is common, especially in darker-skinned children, northern latitudes, and children with limited sun exposure

**Milk Consumption Limits**
- **Ages 2-3:** Up to **2 cups/day (16 oz)**
- **Ages 4-5:** Up to **2.5 cups/day (20 oz)**
- After age 2: transition to **nonfat (skim) or low-fat (1%) milk**
- Excess milk displaces iron-rich foods and can cause iron deficiency anemia

**Juice Guidelines (AAP)**
- **No juice before age 1**
- **Ages 1-3:** Maximum **4 oz/day**
- **Ages 4-6:** Maximum **4-6 oz/day**
- Juice should not be given in bottles or sippy cups for sipping throughout the day
- No juice at bedtime
- Whole fruit is always preferred over juice

**Snacking Patterns**
- Preschoolers typically need **3 meals + 2-3 snacks per day** due to small stomach size
- Scheduled snack times (rather than grazing) support appetite regulation
- Snacks should be nutritious, not calorie-dense/nutrient-poor

> Sources: AAP/IOM calcium and vitamin D guidelines; CDC iron and vitamin D guidance; HealthyChildren.org recommended drinks; AAP Juice Policy (Pediatrics 2017)

### 9. Obesity Prevention

**Prevalence Trends**
- US: Obesity prevalence in children aged 2-5: **12.7%** (2017-2020 data)
- Ages 6-11: 20.7%; ages 12-19: 22.2%
- Global: overweight/obesity in children 5-19 rose from **8% in 1990 to 20% in 2022** (WHO)
- 35 million children under 5 were overweight globally in 2024
- Disparities: highest in Hispanic children (26.2%), non-Hispanic Black (24.8%), lowest in non-Hispanic Asian (9.0%)
- Prevalence increases as family income decreases

**Early Risk Factors**
- Genetic predisposition accounts for **40-70%** of observed obesity
- Maternal obesity is one of the strongest predictors
- Rapid weight gain in first 2 years
- Excess screen time, inadequate sleep, sugar-sweetened beverages
- Food insecurity (paradoxically linked to obesity)
- Social determinants of health: neighborhood safety, food access, poverty

**Evidence-Based Family Approaches**
- AAP 2023 Clinical Practice Guideline:
  - Family-centered, non-stigmatizing approach
  - **Intensive health behavior and lifestyle treatment**: 26+ hours of face-to-face, family-based counseling over 3-12 months
  - Address nutrition, physical activity, sleep, screen use, and social-emotional wellness
  - Earlier intervention (preschool age) may be more effective than waiting for school age
- Prevention pillars: breastfeeding, responsive feeding, active play, limited screen time, adequate sleep, family meals
- Focus on behavior changes, not weight
- Avoid restrictive dieting in young children

> Sources: CDC Childhood Obesity Facts; WHO Obesity Fact Sheet; AAP Clinical Practice Guideline (Pediatrics 2023); HealthyChildren.org obesity

## MIND & DEVELOPMENT (2-5yr)

### 10. Language Explosion

**Vocabulary Growth**
- **18 months:** ~50 words; growth rate ~1-2 words/week
- **24 months (age 2):** ~200-300 words; growth accelerates to ~1-2 new words/day
- **Age 3:** ~1,000 words; uses 3-5 word sentences
- **Age 4:** ~1,500-2,000 words; uses complex sentences, tells stories
- **Age 5:** ~2,000-5,000+ words; uses adult-like grammar, defines simple words
- "Vocabulary explosion" occurs after reaching ~50-word threshold

**Grammar Development**
- **Age 2:** Two-word combinations ("more milk"), begins using pronouns (I, you, me, we), understands "mine"
- **Age 3:** 3-4 word sentences, uses plurals and past tense (often with errors: "goed," "mouses")
- **Age 4:** Complex sentences with conjunctions ("and," "because"), asks many "why" questions
- **Age 5:** Masters most basic grammar rules, uses 5-8 word sentences, understands passive voice
- Children master basic grammar rules **without formal instruction**, just by listening and practicing

**Bilingual Development Patterns**
- Bilingual children learn at comparable rates to monolingual children overall
- Vocabulary in each individual language may lag behind monolinguals, but **total vocabulary across both languages** is comparable
- "Conceptual vocabulary" (total unique concepts across both languages) is the best indicator of learning capacity
- Code-switching (mixing languages) is normal and shows linguistic sophistication, not confusion
- Overdiagnosis risk: bilingual children may score below average on monolingual-normed tests
- Underdiagnosis risk: clinicians may attribute true delays to bilingualism

**Stuttering: Normal Developmental vs Clinical**
- **Normal disfluency** (ages 18 months - 7 years):
  - Whole-word repetitions ("I-I-I want"), phrase repetitions, interjections ("um"), revisions
  - Child is unaware and unfrustrated
  - Comes and goes, especially when excited or tired
- **Clinical stuttering (red flags):**
  - Part-word repetitions ("w-w-want"), sound prolongations ("sssnake"), blocks (silent struggle)
  - Physical tension, secondary behaviors (eye blinking, head nodding)
  - Child shows awareness, frustration, avoidance of speaking
  - Family history of persistent stuttering
- **Up to 90% of children who stutter naturally recover** during childhood
- Recovery mostly occurs within **15 months** of onset
- Higher risk for persistence: boys, onset after age 3.5, family history, co-occurring language difficulties, stuttering >6-12 months
- **Early intervention is key:** Delaying therapy permits maladaptive behaviors and negative attitudes to develop

> Sources: HealthyChildren.org language development; CDC milestones; ASHA Communication Milestones and Fluency Disorders portal; PMC stuttering clinical review; StatPearls Stuttering

### 11. Cognitive Development

**Theory of Mind**
- Children master basic Theory of Mind by **ages 4-5** (understanding that others have different thoughts, beliefs, knowledge)
- Classic "false belief" test: between ages 3-6, children shift from answering based on reality to understanding another person's mistaken belief
- Before ToM develops: children are egocentric (assume everyone sees/knows what they do)
- Related to success on executive function tests and language/social experience

**Magical Thinking**
- Characteristic of Piaget's preoperational stage (ages 2-7)
- Children believe their thoughts can cause events ("I wished it and it happened")
- May think illness is caused by misbehavior
- Animism: believing objects have feelings ("the teddy is sad")
- This is normal and important for imagination and creativity -- not a sign of confusion

**Imagination vs Lying**
- Before age 4, children have difficulty distinguishing fantasy from reality
- "Lying" in young preschoolers is usually confabulation or wishful thinking
- Deliberate deception (true lying) requires ToM (understanding that others can hold false beliefs)
- By age 4-5, children can intentionally deceive, but this is actually a **cognitive milestone** reflecting ToM development

**Pre-Literacy Skills**
- **Age 3:** Recognizes some letters, enjoys being read to, pretend-reads familiar books
- **Age 4:** Counts to 4, identifies 4 colors, understands opposites, recites parts of the alphabet
- **Age 5:** Counts to 10, recites alphabet by rote, recognizes several letters, may write own name
- Print awareness, phonological awareness (rhyming, syllables), and narrative skills are key pre-literacy foundations
- **Shared reading** (dialogic reading) is the strongest predictor of early literacy

**Pre-Numeracy Skills**
- **Age 3:** Understands "more" vs "less," counts to 3, sorts by shape/color
- **Age 4:** Counts to 10, understands "same" vs "different," begins one-to-one correspondence
- **Age 5:** Counts to 20+, simple addition/subtraction with objects, recognizes numerals

> Sources: StatPearls Cognitive Development; PMC Theory of Mind research; PMC false belief studies; HealthyChildren.org developmental milestones

### 12. Emotional Regulation

**Tantrum Peak Age and Decline**
- Tantrums peak at approximately **age 3** and decline thereafter
- **83.7% of children** ages 3-5 had at least one tantrum in the past month
- Only **4.4% of children** had daily tantrums (which may warrant evaluation)
- Tantrums characterized by high anger (screaming, kicking, hitting) decrease with age as cognitive and regulatory skills improve

**Emotional Vocabulary Building**
- Children need to learn to **name emotions** before they can regulate them
- By age 2: identifies happy, sad, angry
- By age 4-5: identifies more nuanced emotions (frustrated, worried, disappointed, embarrassed)
- Parents labeling emotions in real time ("I can see you're frustrated") supports development
- Children's emotional understanding predicts social competence

**Self-Regulation Development Timeline**
- **Age 2:** Primarily relies on external regulation (parent soothes); beginning of internal strategies
- **Age 2-3:** Uses comfort-seeking strategies (runs to parent, clutches comfort object)
- **Age 3-4:** Shift from comfort-seeking to **planful strategies** (distraction, self-talk)
- **Age 4-5:** Growing ability to delay gratification, use verbal strategies ("I can wait")
- Development of self-regulation begins at the start of preschool years and improves through childhood
- Shift from interpsychic (other-regulated) to intrapsychic (self-regulated) emotion management

**Aggressive Behavior: When Normal, When Concerning**
- **Normal:** Physical aggression (hitting, biting, pushing) peaks around **age 2-3** and declines as language develops
- Biting is especially common in toddlers who lack words to express frustration
- **Red flags for concern (< 5% of children):**
  - Daily tantrums after age 3-4
  - Aggression that is getting worse, not better, over time
  - Self-harm during tantrums
  - Aggression toward self AND others during tantrums (strongest predictor of later ODD and depression)
  - Inability to be calmed
  - Tantrums lasting >25 minutes regularly
  - Property destruction

> Sources: PMC developmental pathways from tantrums (2024); PMC emotion regulation in toddlerhood; PMC aggression in early years; PMC self-regulation in preschoolers

### 13. Social Skills

**Cooperative Play Emergence**
- **Parten's stages of play:**
  - Solitary play: predominant before age 2
  - Parallel play (playing beside, not with): ages 2-3
  - Associative play (interacting during play): emerging ages 3-3.5
  - **Cooperative play** (shared goals, role assignment): emerging **ages 3.5-4.5**, increasing through age 5
- By age 3, children transition from side-by-side to interactive play

**Friendship Development**
- **Age 3:** Begins drifting toward preferred playmates; recognizes that peers have unique qualities
- **Age 4-5:** Real friendships form based on shared interests; can name "best friends"
- Preschool friendships are often based on proximity and shared activities rather than deep emotional bonds
- Quality of early friendships predicts later social competence

**Sharing and Turn-Taking: Realistic Expectations**
- **Age 2:** Sharing is extremely difficult; "mine" is a dominant concept; parallel play predominates
- **Age 3:** Beginning to share and take turns with support; can work out simple disputes
- **Age 4:** More consistent sharing; understands fairness but still struggles with it
- **Age 5:** Can share and take turns more reliably; understands rules of games
- Forced sharing can backfire; modeling and positive reinforcement are more effective

**Conflict Resolution**
- 4-year-olds fight less than 2-year-olds and engage in more cooperative social interactions
- By age 3-4, children can begin to verbalize problems and negotiate simple solutions with adult guidance
- Perspective-taking ability (linked to ToM at ~age 4) is foundational for resolving conflicts

> Sources: PMC Peer Play (StatPearls); HealthyChildren.org social development; PMC cooperative play research; PMC social play evaluation

### 14. Screen Time

**AAP Guidelines for Ages 2-5**
- **Ages 2-5:** Limit to **1 hour/day or less** of high-quality programming
- Co-view with children to promote learning and digital literacy
- Avoid screens during meals and for 1 hour before bedtime
- 2025 update: AAP's Digital Ecosystems policy moves toward a more nuanced "5 C's" framework (Child, Content, Context, Co-use, Continual monitoring) rather than strict time limits

**Quality Programming Research**
- Well-designed programs (e.g., Sesame Street, PBS KIDS) can improve cognitive, literacy, and social outcomes in children **ages 3-5**
- fMRI research: children whose brain responses during Sesame Street clips most resembled adult patterns performed better on math and verbal IQ tests
- Key quality indicators: interactive format, educational goals, age-appropriate pacing, diverse representation
- Children learn expressive language and vocabulary **best from live interactions**, but quality media provides supplementary benefit

**Background TV Effects**
- Background TV **reduces** play episode length and focused attention during play
- **Reduces quantity and quality** of parent-child interaction
- Delays language development even when child pays little overt attention
- Affects cognitive development and executive function in children under 5
- Recommendation: **turn off TV when no one is actively watching it**

> Sources: AAP Media and Young Minds (Pediatrics 2016); AAP Digital Ecosystems Policy (Pediatrics 2025); PMC Sesame Street neuroscience studies; PMC background TV effects; HealthyChildren.org media guidance

### 15. Preschool/Kindergarten Readiness

**What Research Says Matters Most**
- **Social-emotional skills** are increasingly recognized as critical for school readiness, alongside early academic skills
- Head Start REDI research: promoting social-emotional skills in preschool enhances academic AND behavioral functioning in kindergarten
- Self-regulation (ability to focus attention, follow directions, manage emotions) is one of the strongest predictors of school success
- Teachers ranked readiness as: physical nourishment/rest, verbal communication, enthusiasm/curiosity, following directions, sensitivity to others' feelings -- **not academic knowledge**
- Duncan et al. (2007) meta-analysis found early math, reading, and attention skills predicted later achievement, but more recent research documents the importance of socioemotional skills as well
- SEL (Social and Emotional Learning) interventions improve self-regulation, which mediates lasting cognitive improvements

**School Readiness Myths**
- Myth: Children need to know letters and numbers to be "ready"
- Reality: A child who can regulate emotions, follow 2-step directions, and interact positively with peers is often better prepared than one who can read but can't sit in circle time
- Myth: Earlier academic instruction is better
- Reality: Play-based preschool programs show equal or better long-term outcomes compared to academically focused programs

**Redshirting Evidence**
- "Academic redshirting" (delaying kindergarten entry by one year) shows **mixed to no benefit**
- Redshirting did NOT bring additional advantages for reading acquisition success
- Age of school entry was unrelated to social functioning
- Any initial age advantage tends to fade by 3rd grade
- May disadvantage children by delaying access to instruction and peer socialization
- Consider individual readiness, not just age

> Sources: PMC Head Start REDI (2013); PMC school readiness and self-regulation; PMC redshirting reading acquisition; PMC kindergarten age of entry study

## BEHAVIORAL

### 16. Toilet Training

**Readiness Signs**
- Bladder and bowel control signals appear between **18-24 months**
- Key readiness indicators:
  - Stays dry for at least 2 hours during the day
  - Shows interest in the toilet or wearing underwear
  - Can follow simple directions
  - Can pull pants up and down
  - Communicates need to go (verbally or behaviorally)
  - Uncomfortable in dirty diapers
  - Regular, predictable bowel movements

**Average Age**
- US average: training begins between **ages 2-3**
- Average age for daytime training: **28.5 months**
- Most children are bowel and bladder trained by **age 4**
- Wide cultural variation: some cultures begin at 12-18 months with parent-led approaches
- Girls typically train earlier than boys

**Evidence on Methods**
- **Child-oriented approach (Brazelton/AAP recommended):** Start at 18 months with readiness signs; unregimented; follow child's lead. AAP officially recommends this approach, though based on expert opinion rather than level-1 evidence.
- **Parent-oriented approach (Azrin & Foxx):** Structured 4-step method: increased fluids, scheduled toileting, positive reinforcement, overcorrection of accidents. More rapid results but higher stress.
- **No level-1 evidence** proves which method is definitively best
- Key principle: **never punish accidents**; praise successes

**Regression Triggers**
- New sibling, move, divorce, illness, starting preschool
- Regression is common and usually temporary
- Return to supportive, pressure-free approach

**Nighttime Dryness Timeline**
- **Separate developmental process** from daytime training
- Nighttime dryness requires brain-bladder maturity (ADH hormone production during sleep)
- **~80% of children** achieve nighttime dryness by age 3
- **~85% by age 5**, ~90% by age 7
- **Nocturnal enuresis prevalence:** ~15-20% of 5-year-olds, ~10% of 7-year-olds, 4% at age 8
- 2-3x more common in boys than girls
- Resolves spontaneously at **~15% per year** without treatment
- Not considered a clinical problem until **age 5** (DSM-5 criteria: involuntary urination during sleep >= 2x/week for >= 3 months in children > 5)
- Parents should NOT wake children to use the toilet, limit fluids after dinner, or punish bedwetting

> Sources: PMC toilet training review (2012); HealthyChildren.org; Mayo Clinic potty training; StatPearls Enuresis; PMC global nocturnal enuresis meta-analysis (2025); AAPD toilet training readiness scale (2024)

### 17. Discipline

**Time-Outs: Evidence**
- Can be effective when done correctly: brief (1 minute per year of age), in a boring but safe location, followed by reconnection
- Research limitations: preschoolers perceived time-outs as punitive; associated with negative emotions (fear, sadness)
- About **half of preschoolers could NOT correctly explain** why they were in time-out
- "Time-in" (staying with the child, co-regulating) is emerging as an evidence-based alternative for younger preschoolers

**Natural Consequences**
- Allowing safe natural consequences helps children learn cause-and-effect
- Appropriate when the consequence is immediate, related, and not dangerous
- Example: refusing coat = feeling cold (natural learning)
- Not appropriate when safety is at risk

**Positive Reinforcement Research**
- Positive discipline programs significantly decrease physical and emotional punishment and increase proactive parenting
- Specific, descriptive praise ("You put your shoes on all by yourself!") is more effective than general praise ("Good job!")
- 4:1 ratio of positive to corrective interactions recommended

**Spanking Evidence (Overwhelming Negative)**
- AAP 2018 policy statement: **"Effective Discipline to Raise Healthy Children"** -- explicitly recommends against corporal punishment
- Meta-analysis: 13 of 17 effect sizes linked spanking to **increased** risk of detrimental child outcomes
- Effect sizes did not substantially differ between spanking and physical abuse
- Spanking is associated with:
  - Increased aggression in preschool and school-aged children
  - Increased defiance (opposite of intended effect)
  - Increased risk of mental health disorders
  - Cognition problems
  - Makes future aggression **more, not less, likely**
- Spanking teaches children that physical force is an acceptable way to solve problems

**Authoritative vs Authoritarian Parenting Outcomes**
- **Authoritative** (warm + firm boundaries): children show least conduct problems, highest prosocial behavior, best adjustment
- **Authoritarian** (strict + low warmth): children show **most internalizing and externalizing problems**, less prosocial behavior
- **Permissive** (warm + no boundaries): children show difficulty with self-regulation and peer relationships
- Two authoritarian parents = poorest behavioral outcomes
- Two authoritative parents = best behavioral outcomes

> Sources: AAP Effective Discipline Policy (Pediatrics 2018); PMC spanking meta-analyses (2016, 2021); PMC parenting styles review; PMC positive discipline program; PMC time-out vs time-in research

### 18. Fears and Anxiety

**Common Age-Appropriate Fears**
- **Ages 1-4:** Tangible fears (dogs, loud noises) and vague fears (monsters, the dark, separation)
- **Ages 2-4:** Thunder/lightning, animals, costumed characters
- **Ages 4-6:** The dark, monsters, "bad guys," being alone, death of parents
- **School age shift:** Evaluation fears, social fears, school-related anxiety
- Separation anxiety is **normative** at 12-18 months and typically diminishes by age 3

**When Anxiety Is Clinical**
- Clinical anxiety disorders affect approximately **7-10%** of preschool-aged children
- Separation Anxiety Disorder (SAD) is the **most frequently diagnosed** childhood anxiety disorder (~50% of child anxiety referrals)
- Clinical vs normal: anxiety is excessive relative to development, persists beyond expected age, **interferes with daily functioning** (school refusal, sleep disruption, social withdrawal)
- Red flags: avoidance of age-appropriate activities, physical symptoms (stomachaches, headaches), regression, excessive reassurance-seeking

**Evidence-Based Approaches**
- **For normal fears:** Validation ("I understand that's scary"), gradual exposure, modeling brave behavior, NOT dismissing or forcing confrontation
- **For clinical anxiety:** Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is gold-standard treatment
- Parent-child interaction therapy adapted for anxiety shows promise in preschoolers
- Avoid accommodation (changing family routines to avoid child's anxiety triggers) -- this reinforces anxiety

**Selective Mutism**
- Anxiety disorder: persistent inability to speak in certain social situations (e.g., school) while speaking normally in others (e.g., home)
- Prevalence: **0.03-1.9%** depending on setting
- More common in: girls, multilingual households, immigrant families
- **Over 50%** have comorbid social anxiety disorder
- Average delay from onset to diagnosis: **2 years**
- Bilingualism does not cause SM but may be a contributory factor
- Treatment: CBT with exposure hierarchy, behavioral training (contingency management, shaping, gradual desensitization), parent training
- SSRIs may be used in severe cases alongside behavioral therapy
- Early intervention improves outcomes significantly

> Sources: PMC childhood anxiety disorders review; PMC selective mutism review (2010); PMC selective mutism 2024 update; HealthyChildren.org fears and anxieties; PMC separation anxiety disorder review; StatPearls separation anxiety

## THE PARENT

### 19. Parental Mental Health

**Burnout**
- Parental burnout: emotional exhaustion in parental role, emotional distancing from children, loss of fulfillment
- Prevalence: approximately **5% globally**, up to **9% in Western/individualistic countries** (42-country study)
- Higher in individualistic cultures where intensive parenting norms dominate
- Three dimensions: exhaustion, inefficacy, emotional distancing
- Risk factors: perfectionism, high expectations, lack of support, parenting alone, children with special needs

**Decision Fatigue**
- Parents make an estimated **35,000+ decisions per day** (general adult estimate); parenting adds substantial decision load
- Women disproportionately carry "invisible" household labor: scheduling, planning, remembering, coordinating, monitoring
- This "mental load" or "cognitive labor" is linked to reduced life satisfaction, partner dissatisfaction, and role overload
- Decision fatigue depletes self-regulation capacity and increases irritability

**Social Media and Comparison**
- Negative emotions from social media comparisons (envy, worry) correlate with **higher** parental burnout
- Positive emotions (optimism, pride) correlate with **lower** burnout, but negative emotions are more common and more strongly linked to burnout
- "Do it all discrepancy" -- gap between feeling one should do everything vs perception of capability -- strongly correlates with burnout
- Digital emotion contagion: parents' moods affected by others' emotions encountered online

**Maintaining Adult Identity**
- Loss of identity beyond "parent" is a significant contributor to burnout
- Protective factors: maintaining hobbies, social connections, professional identity, couple relationship
- Social support (online and offline) is associated with lower burnout

> Sources: PMC parental burnout 42-country study (2021); PMC social media and parental burnout (2022); PMC invisible household labor (2021); PMC parental burnout progressive review (2025)

### 20. Relationship Maintenance

**Research on Couples with Preschoolers**
- Birth of first child is associated with a **decline in relationship satisfaction** for most couples, especially women
- Meta-analyses consistently show moderate decline in marital satisfaction during early parenthood
- Satisfaction tends to stabilize or partially recover as children enter school age
- Couples who maintain friendship behaviors and date-night rituals report higher satisfaction

**Division of Cognitive Labor ("Mental Load")**
- After birth, women take on **2+ additional hours of work per day** vs ~40 additional minutes for men
- Most women report they **alone** organize family schedules and maintain household order
- The gender gap in work division is **not present before birth** but emerges after
- Perception of fairness matters more than strict equality -- but most mothers perceive the distribution as unfair
- Father involvement in childcare strengthens couple relationship satisfaction (pre-pandemic and pandemic research)
- Same-gender couples distribute cognitive labor more equitably on average

**Date Nights / Relationship Investment**
- Wives report highest satisfaction when division of labor is perceived as **fair to both partners**
- Couples intervention programs (e.g., "Love Together, Parent Together") show measurable improvements in relationship quality and coparenting
- Dedicated couple time (even brief, regular connection) helps buffer against relationship erosion
- Coparenting quality (working as a team) is distinct from relationship quality but strongly linked to it

> Sources: PMC transition to parenthood meta-analysis; PMC invisible household labor; PMC division of labor and conflict; PMC couples intervention secondary benefits (2024); PMC relationship satisfaction trajectories

### 21. Work-Life Balance

**Childcare Costs and Quality**
- US national average childcare cost: **~$11,582/year** (2023 data, 3.7% increase from 2022)
- Takes **10% of married couple's median income** or **32% of single parent's median income**
- Childcare supply remains flat while prices continue rising
- Quality childcare is associated with better language, cognitive, and social outcomes
- Publicly funded childcare significantly increases mothers' life satisfaction

**Part-Time vs Full-Time Impact Research**
- Full-time maternal employment in first 12 months associated with modestly lower cognitive scores at age 3 (for non-Hispanic white children); part-time employment was NOT associated with these effects
- Mothers working part-time had **higher home environment quality and maternal sensitivity** than those working full-time OR not working
- Part-time employment at 12 months associated with **fewer externalizing problems** at age 4.5
- Both full-time and part-time working mothers were more likely to place children in **high-quality childcare**
- Working mothers trade quantity of time for better quality time; maternal work reduces time in potentially detrimental activities

**Guilt Research**
- Maternal guilt is pervasive regardless of work status
- Working mothers feel guilty about insufficient time; stay-at-home mothers feel guilty about insufficient stimulation/income
- Nonstandard work schedules (evenings, nights, unpredictable hours) associated with poorer maternal well-being and less developmental activity time
- Key factor is **maternal well-being**, not work status per se -- a fulfilled, supported parent provides better caregiving regardless of employment arrangement

> Sources: Child Care Aware 2024 Price Report; PMC maternal employment and child development; PMC first-year employment study; PMC childcare and parental well-being

## THE FAMILY

### 22. Sibling Dynamics

**New Baby Arrival Timing**
- Children **over age 2** showed greater decrease in attachment security to mothers after new sibling arrival than children under 2
- Common reactions: aggression toward newborn, behavioral regression, attention seeking -- but also independence and maturity
- Research does NOT support a "crisis model" -- most firstborns adjust without lasting problems
- Factors influencing adjustment: child's age, gender, temperament, parental behavior, and preparation

**Sibling Rivalry Research**
- Normal and nearly universal; peaks in early childhood when both children are under 5
- Constructive conflict between siblings actually teaches negotiation, perspective-taking, and emotional regulation
- Parental differential treatment (real or perceived) is the strongest predictor of sibling conflict
- Parenting strategies: validate feelings, avoid comparison, ensure individual attention, don't always intervene

**Birth Order Effects: What Evidence Actually Shows**
- Firstborn advantage in early cognitive development is real but context-dependent
- Firstborns typically experience higher-quality home environment and more parental attention
- Birth order effects interact with family resources, sibling structure, and ecological context
- Later-borns may benefit from sibling interaction and teaching/modeling from older siblings
- Popular "birth order personality" stereotypes (e.g., firstborns are leaders, middle children are peacemakers) have **weak to no empirical support** in large-scale studies
- Effect sizes are very small when found

**Fostering Sibling Relationships**
- Avoid constant comparison or labeling ("the smart one," "the athletic one")
- Create opportunities for cooperative activities
- Protect individual space and possessions
- Allow age-appropriate conflict resolution before intervening
- One-on-one time with each child reduces rivalry

> Sources: PMC family transitions and sibling birth (2012); PMC sibling relationships review; PMC birth order and cognitive development; HealthyChildren.org sibling dynamics

### 23. Safety

**Drowning (Leading Cause of Death Ages 1-4)**
- Drowning is the **#1 cause of death** for children ages 1-4 in the US
- Over 4,500 people died from drowning annually (2020-2022), 500 more per year vs 2019
- Groups with greatest increase: children 1-4 and Black people of all ages
- Prevention layers:
  - **4-sided isolation fencing** around ALL pools (most effective single measure)
  - Constant, dedicated adult supervision (not distracted by phones)
  - Approved life jackets near open water
  - **Swim lessons**: AAP recommends starting at age 1 for many children
  - Learn CPR
- Critical: Swim lessons do **NOT "drown-proof" a child** -- they are one layer in multi-layered prevention
- Drowning can happen in as little as 1 inch of water in under 2 minutes
- Bathtub drowning risk: never leave child unattended, even briefly

**Poisoning Prevention**
- ~3 million poisoning exposures per year, many in children under 5
- Most dangerous: medicines, cleaning products, liquid nicotine, antifreeze, pesticides, lamp oil
- Prevention: safety latches on cabinets, medicines up high and locked, Poison Control number posted (1-800-222-1222)
- Keep products in original labeled containers

**Car Seat Guidelines (AAP)**
- **Rear-facing:** As long as possible (until child exceeds weight/height limit of their seat -- many seats allow rear-facing to 40+ lbs)
- **Forward-facing with harness:** From when they outgrow rear-facing through **at least age 4** (many harness seats accommodate up to 65 lbs)
- **Belt-positioning booster:** From when they outgrow forward-facing harness through **at least age 8** or until 4'9" tall (typically ages 8-12)
- **Seat belt alone:** When lap and shoulder belt fits properly (4'9" tall, usually ages 8-12)
- Every transition to a less protective seat increases risk -- keep children in each stage as long as possible

**Water Safety and Swim Lessons**
- AAP recommends swim lessons as one layer of protection starting at **age 1** for many children
- No evidence that infant swim programs (under age 1) reduce drowning risk
- Formal swim lessons reduce drowning risk in children ages 1-4
- "Touch supervision": adult within arm's reach for young children near any water
- Empty buckets, wading pools, bathtubs when not in use

**Playground Safety**
- Falls are the most common playground injury
- Appropriate surfacing (rubber, wood chips) under equipment reduces injury severity
- Age-appropriate equipment; supervision required
- Check for entrapment hazards, hot surfaces, broken equipment

> Sources: CDC Drowning Facts (2024); AAP Drowning Prevention; AAP Child Passenger Safety (Pediatrics 2018); HealthyChildren.org swim lessons and car seats; HealthyChildren.org poison prevention

### 24. Structure & Routines

**Research on Routine Benefits for Preschoolers**
- Higher frequency of family routines associated with **lower externalizing behaviors** in children
- Routines at 14 months linked to **higher social competence at 36 months**
- Predictable daily structure reduces anxiety and supports emotional regulation
- Routines serve as "scaffolding" for self-regulation development
- Children with consistent routines show better executive function (working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibility)
- Daily classroom routines in preschool support language development
- Routines are particularly beneficial as protective factors for children with neurodevelopmental delays

**Bedtime Routines Specifically**
- Consistent bedtime routine = earlier bedtimes, shorter sleep onset latency, fewer night wakings, more total sleep
- **Dose-dependent:** More consistent routine = better outcomes
- Benefits beyond sleep: language development, literacy, emotional regulation, parent-child attachment, dental health, school readiness
- Optimal routine: 3-4 calming activities (bath, brush teeth, read books, sing), lasting 20-45 minutes, same sequence nightly

**Transition Warnings**
- Advance notice of upcoming changes (e.g., "2-minute warning") reduces transition-related problem behavior
- Effective for all children but especially well-documented for children with ASD/ADHD
- Timer or visual countdown helps make abstract time concrete for preschoolers

**Visual Schedules**
- Picture-based daily schedules help children anticipate and participate in routines
- Reduce adult prompting needs; promote independence
- Benefits: on-task behavior, independent transitions, daily living skills, social initiations
- Useful for all preschoolers, not just those with developmental differences
- Even simple picture charts (wake up, eat breakfast, brush teeth, get dressed) support autonomy

> Sources: PMC routines and child behavior (2024); PMC bedtime routine benefits (2019); PMC visual schedules research; HealthyChildren.org family routines; PMC activity schedules (2024)

### 25. Technology in the Home

**Family Media Plans**
- AAP recommends creating a family media plan that sets boundaries fitting family routines and values
- AAP provides a free interactive tool at HealthyChildren.org
- Plan should address: when screens are allowed, which content, device-free zones (bedrooms, dinner table), and family screen-free times
- Plans should address **both children and parents**, as family media use is intertwined with childcare access, social support, and family stress
- 2025 AAP Digital Ecosystems policy emphasizes "5 C's": Child, Content, Context, Co-use, Continual monitoring

**Co-Viewing Research**
- Co-viewing (watching together) enhances learning from media by allowing parents to explain, ask questions, and connect content to real life
- Children learn best from media when an adult **actively mediates** (not just sits nearby)
- Quality screen media with educational goals can support early language and literacy for children ages 2-4
- **But**: preschoolers still learn expressive language and vocabulary best from live, direct interactions with caring adults
- When parents explain their own digital media use, children develop better understanding of appropriate media habits

**Digital Literacy Beginnings**
- Ages 2-5: focus on **media literacy** rather than technical skills
- Teach: screens show things that are both real and pretend; commercials want you to buy things
- Model healthy tech use (putting phone away during conversations)
- Name what you're doing on your device when children ask
- Help children understand that people online are real people with feelings

> Sources: AAP Family Media Plan (Pediatrics 2024); AAP Digital Ecosystems Policy (Pediatrics 2025); HealthyChildren.org digital media guidance; PMC screen time and preschool children (2023); PMC co-viewing research
