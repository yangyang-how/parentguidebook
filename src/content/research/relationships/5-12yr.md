## Research Brief

School age is when relationships expand and deepen — friendships become central, peer influence grows relative to parent influence, family communication patterns solidify, and divorce/co-parenting and blended family dynamics become significant for many families. This article covers deepening friendships, the shifting balance of peer vs parent influence, family communication as the relationship's operating system, and navigating divorce, co-parenting, and blended families.

- **Date:** 2023
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8851058/
- **Type:** systematic-review

**Key passages:**
> "Parent influence remains dominant for values, long-term goals, and major decisions; peer influence is dominant for daily behavior, social norms, and style."

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Friendships Deepen
- Key points: Intimacy-based, unique emotional functions, rejection risk, parent bond buffers peer influence
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Your child's best friend isn't just a playmate — they're becoming a confidant

### Section 2: Family Communication
- Key points: Four horsemen, active listening, family meetings, open communication as quality indicator
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: The family that talks together stays together. But how you talk matters more than how much.

### Section 3: Navigating Divorce
- Key points: Co-parenting quality is key, never put kids in the middle, both-parent relationships matter
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your child doesn't need you to stay together. They need you to co-parent well apart.

### Section 4: Blended Families
- Key points: 2-5 year timeline, gradual step-parent role, loyalty conflicts
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Blended family love takes years, not months. Be patient with yourself and the kids.

### Section 5: Siblings and Family Rituals
- Key points: Sibling warmth is protective, differential treatment harms, rituals build identity
- Supported by: Source 9, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: The weekly family game night isn't just fun — it's building the bonds that carry your children through life

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Divorce stigma in Chinese culture may prevent families from seeking healthy separations or co-parenting support
- Blended families are less common in Chinese culture but increasing — cultural scripts are limited
- Chinese communication patterns tend to be less explicit about emotions — open communication may need to be modeled differently
- Sibling dynamics in Chinese families who had a second child after the one-child policy are unique — no cultural template
- Peer influence on academic values is particularly strong in Chinese school culture
