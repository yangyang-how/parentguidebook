# Bones & Movement — Building a Strong Foundation for Life

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — understanding bone and movement development helps you support your child's growth at every stage.**

**The short version:** Your child's skeleton is a work in progress that won't fully mature until their mid-twenties. The recipe for strong bones is straightforward: vitamin D, calcium from food, and plenty of weight-bearing activity. Meanwhile, motor milestones — rolling, crawling, walking, running — are your best early window into whether development is on track. This article covers how bones grow, what fuels them, how much movement children need, and when something might warrant a conversation with your doctor.

---

## How bones grow — from cartilage to skeleton

Your baby's bones didn't start as bones. They started as softer tissues that gradually transformed — and that transformation is still happening right now, no matter how old your child is.

There are two ways the body builds bone. **Intramembranous ossification** converts soft connective tissue directly into bone. This is how the flat bones of the skull and the clavicle (collarbone) form. **Endochondral ossification** starts with a cartilage template that is gradually replaced by bone tissue — this is how the long bones of the arms and legs develop. [Source: src7]

This process begins between the sixth and seventh weeks of embryonic development and continues until about age twenty-five. [Source: src7] By the time a baby is born, most cartilage has already been replaced with bone, but secondary ossification centers continue developing after birth, forming the ends (epiphyses) of long bones. [Source: src8]

The growth plates — called **epiphyseal plates** — are bands of cartilage near the ends of long bones where new bone is actively being added. These plates are the reason children grow taller. They remain open throughout childhood and adolescence and close in the early twenties, which is when height growth stops. [Source: src7, src8]

This is why growth plate injuries in children are taken seriously: damage to an open growth plate can potentially affect future bone growth in ways that adult fractures simply don't.

## The three pillars of bone health

The American Academy of Pediatrics identifies three factors of key importance for achieving optimal bone health throughout childhood: **vitamin D, calcium, and weight-bearing activity**. [Source: src1]

### Vitamin D — the non-negotiable supplement

Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption and bone mineralization. Without enough of it, bones can become soft and weak — a condition called **rickets** in children.

The AAP recommends that **all infants and children, including adolescents, have a minimum daily intake of 400 IU of vitamin D beginning soon after birth**. [Source: src2]

This applies to breastfed babies in particular. Breast milk, for all its benefits, does not contain enough vitamin D. Infants who are breastfed — and children and adolescents who consume less than 1 liter of vitamin D-fortified milk per day — will likely need supplementation to reach that 400 IU daily minimum. [Source: src2]

A few groups may need extra attention when it comes to vitamin D status: children with darker skin need more sun exposure to produce equivalent amounts of vitamin D, and children who spend very little time outdoors or live in northern latitudes may also fall short. This isn't a reason to worry — it's a reason to talk to your pediatrician about whether supplementation makes sense for your child.

### Calcium — food first

Calcium is the primary mineral that makes bones hard and strong. But the AAP does not recommend routine calcium supplementation for healthy children and adolescents. Instead, the emphasis is on **increased dietary intake to meet daily requirements**. [Source: src1]

Good dietary sources of calcium include milk, yogurt, cheese, fortified cereals, leafy greens (like kale and broccoli), and calcium-set tofu. For children who can't tolerate dairy, fortified plant milks and other calcium-rich foods can fill the gap.

### Weight-bearing activity — the bone builder

Bones respond to mechanical stress by getting stronger. Every time your child runs, jumps, hops, or climbs, the impact sends signals to the bone cells to build more mineral. This is why physical activity isn't just good for muscles and hearts — it's one of the most important things your child can do for their skeleton.

Children and adolescents who are more physically active than their peers have greater bone mass, higher bone mineral content or density, and greater bone strength. [Source: src4] Even in early childhood, physical activity promotes bone health and encourages healthy growth and development of muscle. [Source: src9]

## Movement milestones — what to watch for

Motor milestones — the ages at which children typically learn to hold their head up, roll over, sit, crawl, walk, and run — are one of the most important tools for tracking healthy development.

The **CDC's "Learn the Signs. Act Early." framework** provides milestone checklists that help parents and pediatricians monitor development over time. These checklists were updated in 2022 and are designed so that most children (75% or more) would be expected to achieve each milestone by the listed age. [Source: src5]

That 75th-percentile threshold is important to understand: it means that if your child hasn't reached a particular milestone by the listed age, they're not necessarily behind — but it's worth discussing with your doctor. **Not reaching milestones, or reaching them much later than children the same age, can be the earliest indication of a developmental delay.** [Source: src5]

Developmental **surveillance** — meaning ongoing monitoring over time — is preferred over one-time screening, because development unfolds gradually and a single snapshot can miss things. [Source: src6] This is why pediatric well-child visits happen frequently in the early years: each visit is a chance to check that your child's motor development is progressing.

It's also worth noting that motor milestone delays don't point to a single cause. They can reflect musculoskeletal issues, neurological concerns, or simply the wide range of normal variation in how children develop. A delay is a signal to evaluate — not a diagnosis.

## How much activity do children need?

### Ages 5 through 17

Both the **World Health Organization** and the **CDC** recommend that children and adolescents ages 5 through 17 accumulate **at least 60 minutes of moderate- to vigorous-intensity physical activity daily**. [Source: src3, src11]

Within that daily hour, **vigorous-intensity activities that strengthen muscle and bone should be incorporated at least 3 times per week**. [Source: src3] Bone-strengthening activities are a distinct category from aerobic or muscle-strengthening — think jumping, hopping, skipping, running, and sports that involve impact with the ground.

### Early childhood (under 5)

Formal activity guidelines are less prescriptive for younger children, but the evidence is clear: physical activity in early childhood promotes bone health, encourages healthy muscle development, and improves both motor and cognitive development. [Source: src9] Meeting physical activity recommendations during childhood has significant benefits for bone development and long-term bone health. [Source: src12]

For babies and toddlers, "physical activity" means tummy time, reaching, crawling, cruising along furniture, and eventually walking and running. For preschoolers, it means active play — climbing, chasing, dancing, throwing, kicking.

The bottom line: **movement at every age matters for bones.** The earlier healthy activity habits start, the better the payoff.

## Building peak bone mass — the window that closes

Here's a fact that changes how you think about childhood nutrition and activity: **approximately 90% of peak bone mass is achieved by age 18**. [Source: src10]

Peak bone mass is the maximum amount of bone mineral a person will ever have. After it's reached (typically in the mid- to late twenties), bone density gradually declines for the rest of life. The higher the peak, the more bone a person has in reserve — and the better protected they are against osteoporosis and fractures decades later.

This means that childhood and adolescence are the critical window for bone mineral accrual. [Source: src10] What your child eats, how much they move, and whether they get enough vitamin D during these years has lifelong consequences for their skeleton.

Maximizing bone health in childhood protects against osteoporosis later in life. [Source: src4] The investments you make now — the milk at breakfast, the vitamin D supplement, the afternoons spent running around outside — are deposits in a bone bank that your child will draw from for the rest of their life.

Physical activity directly correlates with bone mineral density in children, and meeting activity guidelines during childhood correlates with better bone outcomes across the lifespan. [Source: src4, src12]

## When to talk to your doctor

Most bone and movement development happens smoothly without intervention. But there are times when a conversation with your pediatrician is the right call.

**🔴 See your doctor promptly:**
- **Missed motor milestones** — your child hasn't reached a milestone by the age listed on the CDC checklist, or you notice they are significantly behind peers [Source: src5]
- **Frequent fractures** — multiple broken bones from minor falls or low-impact injuries could suggest underlying bone fragility
- **Bone pain** — persistent pain in the limbs or joints that isn't explained by a specific injury
- **Growth concerns** — your child seems to have stopped growing, or is growing significantly slower than expected
- **Limping or refusal to bear weight** — especially if it appears suddenly or worsens over time

**🟡 Discuss at your next well-child visit:**
- Whether your child needs **vitamin D supplementation** — especially if they are breastfed, have darker skin, spend limited time outdoors, or consume little fortified milk [Source: src2]
- Whether your child is getting **enough dietary calcium** for their age [Source: src1]
- Any concerns about **physical activity levels** — if your child is significantly less active than peers
- Questions about **growth plate injuries** after a fracture near a joint
- General questions about your child's **motor development trajectory** [Source: src6]

> **A note for parents:** Bone and movement development is one of those areas where steady, everyday habits matter more than any single intervention. You don't need to be perfect. A child who gets reasonable nutrition, stays active, and sees their pediatrician for regular checkups is doing the most important things for their skeleton. If something seems off — a milestone that's not coming, a limp that won't go away, a pattern of easy fractures — bring it up. That's what your doctor is there for.
