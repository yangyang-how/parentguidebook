## Research Brief

The preschool years bring major transitions: starting preschool, new siblings, possible divorce or separation, moving homes, and the ongoing tension between wanting routine and needing flexibility. This article covers how preschoolers handle these changes, the developmental shift toward greater cognitive flexibility, the role of pretend play in building adaptability, and specific strategies for supporting children through common early childhood transitions.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Starting Preschool
- Key points: Multi-dimensional transition, 4-6 week adjustment, preparation strategies
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: If your child cries at preschool drop-off for weeks, they're not suffering — they're adjusting

### Section 2: New Siblings
- Key points: Temporary regression, preparation helps, maintained one-on-one time, 3-6 month adjustment
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Your firstborn's "bad behavior" after the new baby arrives is just their way of saying "I still need you"

### Section 3: Building Flexibility Through Play
- Key points: Cognitive flexibility at 3-5, games that build it, pretend play as flexibility training
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Simon Says and make-believe are adaptability training in disguise

### Section 4: When Big Changes Happen (Divorce, Moving)
- Key points: Self-blame risk in divorce, co-parenting quality, moving impacts, regression is normal
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 7, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: During big changes, consistency is your child's anchor. Keep what you can the same.

### Section 5: Practical Tools
- Key points: Visual schedules, time warnings, transition rituals, transitional objects
- Supported by: Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: A picture schedule on the wall is worth 50 verbal reminders

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, new sibling adjustment has become relevant again with relaxed birth policies — families may lack cultural scripts for multi-child dynamics
- Divorce stigma in Chinese culture may prevent children from getting the open communication they need
- Frequent moves between China and other countries are common in Chinese diaspora families — specific guidance is valuable
- Over-preparing anxious children can backfire — balance preparation with trust in their capacity
