## Key Data Points

### Breastfeeding
- AAP (2022): exclusive breastfeeding for ~6 months, continued for 2 years or beyond
- WHO: exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months, continued with complementary foods to 2 years or beyond
- Benefits: reduced otitis media, diarrhea, lower respiratory illness, SIDS, IBD, childhood leukemia, diabetes, obesity, asthma, atopic dermatitis
- Maternal benefits: reduced type 2 diabetes, hypertension, breast cancer, ovarian cancer
- 44% of infants globally are exclusively breastfed to 6 months (WHO target: 50% by 2025)

### Formula Feeding
- Three forms: ready-to-feed, liquid concentrate, powder
- Volume: ~2.5 oz per lb of body weight per day
- First weeks: 2-3 oz per feed → by 1 month: 3-4 oz per feed → by 6 months: 6-8 oz per feed
- Max recommended: 32 oz (1 liter) per day
- Safe prep: boil water, wait 5 minutes, use within 2 hours (or refrigerate and use within 24 hours)

### Introducing Solids
- Around 6 months (not before 4 months)
- Readiness signs: head control, sitting upright, interest in food, loss of tongue-thrust reflex
- BLW vs traditional: no increased choking risk with BLW; both approaches valid
- Gagging (loud, coughing, tongue thrusts forward) vs choking (silent, blue coloring)

### Allergen Introduction
- LEAP trial: 81% reduction in peanut allergy when introduced at 4-11 months
- Current guidelines: introduce peanut and egg around 6 months (not before 4 months)
- No pre-screening recommended before introduction
- Real-world data (2025): 27% decrease in peanut allergy diagnoses post-guidelines

### Food Allergies
- 5.3% of children have diagnosed food allergy (CDC 2024)
- 1 in 13 children affected
- Big 9: milk, eggs, peanuts, tree nuts, fish, shellfish, wheat, soy, sesame
- 40% of food-allergic children have experienced anaphylaxis
- Xolair (omalizumab) approved Feb 2024 for food allergy in ages 1+
- Epinephrine: each minute of delay reduces survival odds by 4%

### Toddler Nutrition (1-3 years)
- Cow's milk transition at 12 months; whole milk recommended
- Max 16 oz (2 cups) cow's milk per day
- ~10% of toddlers have iron deficiency; 50% of those develop anemia
- Excess milk = leading cause of toddler anemia
- Iron need: 7 mg/day (ages 1-3)
- 30-50% of parents report picky eating in toddlers

### Satter Division of Responsibility
- Parent decides: what, when, where
- Child decides: how much, whether
- Food neophobia is developmental — peaks around age 2-6
- Pressure tactics backfire; structured meals work

### Vitamins & Minerals
- Vitamin D: 400 IU/day from birth (breastfed infants); 600 IU/day ages 1-18
- Iron: 11 mg/day (7-12 months); 7 mg/day (1-3 years); breastfed infants supplement at 4 months
- Calcium: 200 mg/day (0-6 mo); 260 mg/day (7-12 mo); 700 mg/day (1-3 yr); 1000 mg/day (4-8 yr); 1300 mg/day (9-18 yr)

### School-Age (5-12 years)
- Breakfast → improved cognition, reduced absenteeism
- >80% of bone mineral content accrued by end of school age
- Peak bone mineral accrual: 9-14 years (girls), 1-2 years later (boys)
- 24.2% of middle/high schoolers report negative body image despite normal BMI

### Teen Nutrition (12-18 years)
- Caloric needs: girls 1,800-2,400 kcal/day; boys 2,000-3,200 kcal/day
- Calcium: 1,300 mg/day
- Iron: higher for menstruating girls
- Eating disorder warning signs: sudden weight loss, lethargy, vomiting after meals, appetite suppressant use

### ARFID
- Prevalence: 0.5-5% general population; ~6% preschoolers (2025 Swedish study)
- Differs from picky eating: leads to weight loss, nutritional deficiencies, psychosocial problems
- Comorbid with anxiety (9-72%) and ASD (8-55%)

### Juice Limits (AAP)
- No juice under 1 year
- Ages 1-3: max 4 oz/day
- Ages 4-6: max 4-6 oz/day
- Ages 7+: max 8 oz/day

### Vegetarian/Vegan in Children
- Can support healthy growth with proper planning
- Critical nutrients: B12, calcium, iodine, iron, zinc
- Only 4% of vegan children meet B12 recommendations without supplementation
- B12 supplementation essential for all vegan children
- Better cardiovascular markers, more fiber/vitamins on plant-based diets

### Tongue-Tie & Feeding Challenges
- Tongue-tie → poor latch → aerophagia → reflux
- 93% of infants showed immediate reflux improvement after frenotomy
- Signs for specialist referral: poor weight gain, painful feeding, clicking during feeds
