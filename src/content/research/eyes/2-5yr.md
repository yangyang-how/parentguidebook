## Research Brief

Ages 2-5 represent a critical window for detecting and treating vision problems — particularly amblyopia (lazy eye), which becomes progressively harder and eventually impossible to treat after age 7-9. The USPSTF recommends vision screening for all children aged 3-5 years, and the AAP recommends visual acuity screening starting at age 3. Strabismus (eye misalignment) is the most common cause of amblyopia in children under 3 and is treatable with glasses, patching, or surgery. Instrument-based screening can detect risk factors even in preverbal children. Refractive errors (especially hyperopia and astigmatism) are common in this age group. Eye safety becomes increasingly important as preschoolers explore their environment — toys with projectiles, household chemicals, and sharp objects are leading causes of pediatric eye injuries.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Amblyopia is called "lazy eye" but the eye itself is healthy — the problem is in the brain's processing. Parents may not notice because the child compensates with the stronger eye
- A child who passes a vision screening at the pediatrician may still have amblyopia — instrument-based screening catches risk factors that simple acuity charts miss
- The "critical period" is not a hard cutoff — some benefit from treatment exists after age 7, but outcomes deteriorate significantly. The message should be "earlier is much better" not "it's too late after 7"
- Strabismus in a preschooler is NEVER normal — unlike in the first few months of life, any persistent eye misalignment after 3-4 months requires evaluation
- Mild hyperopia (farsightedness) is normal in young children and often self-corrects — the article should not alarm parents about this
- Eye safety messaging should be practical (childproof cabinets, toy selection) not fear-inducing
- If a child resists having one eye covered (screams or tries to remove a patch during screening), that may indicate they rely heavily on that eye and the other eye has poor vision — this is itself a warning sign
