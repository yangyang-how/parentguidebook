**Quick Summary (60 seconds)**

**Urgency: Routine information — but know the red flags at every age.**

**The short version:** Your child's skin will go through an extraordinary number of changes from birth to adulthood. Most of what you see — the peeling, the spots, the mysterious rashes — is completely normal. The trick is knowing what's normal, what needs a little help at home, and what deserves a call to the doctor.

**What to expect at a glance:**
- **Newborn (0–3 months):** Peeling, milia, baby acne, cradle cap, diaper rash — almost all are harmless and temporary
- **Infant/toddler (3 months–3 years):** Eczema is the big one (affects up to 1 in 4 children), plus drool rash and viral rashes
- **Preschool/school-age (3–12 years):** Warts, ringworm, impetigo, head lice, and the start of sun protection habits
- **Teen (12–18 years):** Acne affects roughly 85% of adolescents — effective treatment exists at every severity level

**When to talk to your doctor:** A rash with fever that's spreading rapidly. Non-blanching spots (spots that don't fade when you press on them). Blistering that covers a large area. Any skin change that isn't improving with appropriate home care. A mole that's changing shape, color, or size.

## Skin by age

### Newborns (0–3 months): A whole new world for their skin

Your baby's skin spent nine months submerged in fluid. In the first days and weeks of life, it's adjusting to air, temperature changes, and everything else the outside world throws at it. Most of what you'll see is part of that adjustment — not a problem to solve.

**Vernix caseosa** is the white, waxy coating some babies are born with. It's not dirt — it's a natural protective layer made of water, lipids, and proteins that served as a moisturizer and antimicrobial barrier in the womb. Current evidence supports leaving vernix on the skin to absorb naturally rather than washing it off immediately. If possible, delay the first bath for at least 24 hours. [Source: src13]

**Skin peeling** in the first one to two weeks is normal, especially in babies born past their due date. Think of it as the outer layer adapting from a liquid environment to an air environment. No treatment is needed, though a gentle, fragrance-free moisturizer is fine if the dryness bothers you.

**Milia** — those tiny white or yellowish bumps on your baby's nose and cheeks — appear in roughly 40–50% of newborns. They're caused by trapped keratin (a skin protein) just beneath the surface. They resolve on their own within weeks to a few months. Don't squeeze them. [Source: src9]

**Erythema toxicum neonatorum** has a terrifying name for something completely harmless. Red blotchy patches with small yellow or white pustules, it appears in 40–70% of full-term newborns within the first two to five days. It looks alarming. It means nothing. It resolves on its own within a week or two and sometimes reappears briefly. No treatment needed. [Source: src9]

**Mongolian spots** (now called dermal melanocytosis) are flat, blue-gray patches usually found on the buttocks and lower back. They appear in more than 80% of Asian, Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous newborns and about 10% of white newborns. They're caused by melanocytes in the deeper layers of the skin and typically fade by age three to five, though some persist longer. They are completely benign — but it's worth making sure they're documented in your baby's medical record, because they can occasionally be mistaken for bruises. [Source: src5]

**Baby acne** affects about 20% of newborns, usually appearing at two to four weeks as small red bumps or pimples on the cheeks, nose, and forehead. It's driven by maternal hormones still circulating in your baby's system and typically clears by three to four months. Gentle cleansing with water is all that's needed. If it's unusually severe or persists beyond six weeks, mention it to your pediatrician — persistent acne in a young infant (called infantile acne) is a different condition that occasionally needs treatment. [Source: src5]

#### Cradle cap

Cradle cap (infantile seborrheic dermatitis) produces thick, yellowish, greasy-looking scales on the scalp. It affects up to 70% of infants in the first three months. It's caused by overactive oil glands combined with a yeast called Malassezia — not by poor hygiene, and it's not contagious. [Source: src6]

**What works:** Gently massage baby oil or mineral oil into the scales to soften them, then use a soft-bristled brush to loosen the flakes before shampooing. Regular shampooing helps. Most cases resolve by 12 months. If the scales are thick and stubborn, your pediatrician may suggest a medicated shampoo or a mild topical steroid.

#### Diaper rash

Diaper rash affects up to half of all infants, with a peak around 9–12 months. It's primarily caused by prolonged contact between skin and the moisture, enzymes, and bacteria in urine and stool. [Source: src17]

**Prevention (the best treatment):**
- Change diapers frequently — don't let a wet or soiled diaper sit
- Use fragrance-free, alcohol-free wipes, or just warm water and a soft cloth
- Pat dry gently — never rub
- Apply a barrier cream (zinc oxide or petroleum jelly) at every change
- Let the skin air-dry when you can — diaper-free time helps

**When the rash appears:** Apply a thick layer of zinc oxide paste with each change. If the rash is bright red with small "satellite" spots around the edges, it may have become a yeast (candida) infection — your pediatrician can prescribe an antifungal cream. Call your doctor if the rash doesn't improve within two to three days of home treatment, if it blisters or develops pus, or if your baby has a fever.

### Infants and toddlers (3 months–3 years): The eczema years

#### Eczema (atopic dermatitis)

Eczema is the most common chronic skin condition in children, affecting 15–25% of children worldwide. About 60% of cases begin in the first year of life, and 90% start before age five. If your child has eczema, you're dealing with something very common — and something that, while frustrating, is highly manageable. [Source: src2]

**What's happening in the skin:** Eczema involves a defective skin barrier — the outer layer of skin doesn't hold moisture or block irritants as well as it should. In 20–30% of children with eczema, this is partly due to mutations in a gene called filaggrin, which plays a key role in skin barrier function. The result is dry, irritated skin that's prone to inflammation and infection. [Source: src2]

**The foundation — daily moisturization:** This is not optional. Apply a thick, fragrance-free moisturizer (ointment or cream — not lotion, which contains more water and less oil) within three minutes of bathing, while the skin is still damp. This "soak and seal" technique traps moisture in the skin. Studies show that consistent daily moisturization alone can reduce eczema flares significantly. [Source: src2]

**Bathing:** Lukewarm water (not hot), once daily or every other day, with a gentle fragrance-free cleanser. Keep baths short — 5 to 10 minutes. Pat dry gently and moisturize immediately.

**When moisturizer isn't enough — topical steroids:** If your child's eczema is red, itchy, and inflamed, moisturizer alone won't cut it. Topical corticosteroids are the first-line treatment for eczema flares and have been used safely for decades. Use low-potency steroids (like hydrocortisone 1%) on the face and skin folds, and medium-potency steroids on the body, as prescribed by your pediatrician. [Source: src2]

A word about "steroid phobia": Many parents are understandably cautious about using steroids on their child's skin. The concern about skin thinning is valid at high doses used for long periods, but low-to-medium-potency topical steroids used appropriately for eczema flares are safe. Undertreating eczema — letting a child scratch and suffer — carries its own risks, including skin infections and sleep disruption. Talk to your pediatrician about the right strength and duration for your child. [Source: src2]

**Other prescription options:** For sensitive areas or maintenance therapy, your doctor may prescribe calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus or pimecrolimus), which are non-steroidal anti-inflammatory creams. Newer options include crisaborole (approved for ages 3 months and older) and topical ruxolitinib (approved for ages 12 and older). For moderate-to-severe eczema that doesn't respond to topical treatment, dupilumab (a biologic injection) is now approved for children as young as six months. [Source: src2]

**The food allergy connection:** About 30% of children with moderate-to-severe eczema also have food allergies. However — and this is important — removing foods from your child's diet without proper testing is not recommended. Unnecessary food restrictions can lead to nutritional problems and may actually increase the risk of developing food allergies. If you suspect a food trigger, work with your pediatrician or allergist to get appropriate testing rather than guessing. [Source: src2]

**Proactive management:** Once a flare is controlled, applying topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors to previously affected areas two times per week (even when the skin looks clear) can help prevent flares from coming back. This approach, called proactive therapy, is a key strategy from the AAP's 2025 clinical report. [Source: src2]

#### Drool rash

As teeth start coming in (typically around four to seven months), the constant drool can irritate the skin around the mouth, chin, and cheeks. Prevention is the best approach: apply a thin layer of petroleum jelly to the area before meals and naps to create a barrier. Pat (don't rub) drool away throughout the day. If the rash develops, a gentle moisturizer and barrier cream usually resolve it.

#### Viral rashes

**Roseola:** Your child has a high fever for three to five days — then the fever breaks and a pink, lacy rash appears on the trunk and spreads outward. By the time the rash shows up, the child is usually feeling better. Roseola is caused by human herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6), is most common between 6 and 24 months, and resolves on its own. The fever can be high (up to 104F/40C), which is understandably alarming. Treat the fever with acetaminophen or ibuprofen (ibuprofen only for children 6 months and older) and keep your child hydrated. [Source: src18]

**Hand-foot-and-mouth disease:** Painful sores inside the mouth plus a blistering rash on the palms, soles, and sometimes buttocks. Caused by coxsackievirus, it's most common in children under five and highly contagious. There's no specific treatment — manage pain with acetaminophen or ibuprofen, offer cold fluids and soft foods, and watch for dehydration (the mouth sores can make children reluctant to drink). It resolves in 7–10 days. [Source: src15]

### Preschool and school-age children (3–12 years): Infections, infestations, and the sun

This is the age of shared classrooms, playgrounds, and sleepovers — all excellent opportunities for skin infections to spread. Most are more annoying than dangerous.

#### Warts

Caused by human papillomavirus (HPV), warts are extremely common in school-age children, affecting 10–20% of them at any given time. Common warts appear on the hands as rough, raised bumps; plantar warts grow on the soles of the feet and can be painful with pressure. [Source: src8]

The reassuring truth: most warts resolve on their own within two years as the immune system clears the virus. If a wart is painful or bothersome, over-the-counter salicylic acid (applied daily, with patience — it takes weeks) is the most studied treatment. Cryotherapy (freezing) at the doctor's office is another option. Neither is dramatically faster than the other.

#### Molluscum contagiosum

Small, flesh-colored, dome-shaped bumps with a characteristic central dimple. Caused by a poxvirus and spread by direct skin-to-skin contact, molluscum is common in children ages one to ten. The bumps are painless and benign, but they can spread to new areas on the body and to other children. [Source: src8]

Treatment is often not necessary — the bumps resolve on their own, though this can take 6 to 12 months and occasionally up to four years. If treatment is desired (for example, if the bumps are spreading rapidly or are in a visible area), options include cryotherapy, curettage (scraping), or a topical blistering agent called cantharidin applied in the doctor's office.

#### Impetigo

The most common bacterial skin infection in young children, impetigo typically appears as red sores that quickly develop honey-colored crusts, usually around the nose and mouth. It's caused by Staphylococcus aureus or Group A Streptococcus and is highly contagious. [Source: src8]

Treatment depends on severity: a few small patches can be treated with a topical antibiotic (mupirocin); more widespread infection requires oral antibiotics. Keep the affected areas clean and covered, and keep your child home from school or daycare until they've been on antibiotics for at least 24 hours. Good handwashing prevents spread.

#### Ringworm

Despite the name, ringworm has nothing to do with worms — it's a fungal infection. On the body (tinea corporis), it appears as red, scaly, ring-shaped patches that grow outward with a clearing center. On the scalp (tinea capitis), it causes scaling, itching, and sometimes patchy hair loss. [Source: src8]

An important distinction: ringworm on the body can usually be treated with over-the-counter topical antifungal cream (clotrimazole or terbinafine) for two to four weeks. But ringworm on the scalp requires oral antifungal medication — topical creams don't penetrate deeply enough to reach the hair follicle. If you see patchy hair loss with scaling in your child, see your pediatrician.

#### Head lice

Head lice affect an estimated 6–12 million children per year in the United States, primarily ages 3–11. They are not a sign of poor hygiene and do not transmit disease. They spread primarily through direct head-to-head contact — not commonly through shared hats, brushes, or helmets (lice can't survive long off a human head). [Sources: src10, src11]

**Treatment:** Over-the-counter permethrin 1% or pyrethrin-based products are the first-line treatment. Apply according to the package directions and repeat in 7–10 days to kill any newly hatched nymphs. If over-the-counter products don't work after two properly applied treatments, see your pediatrician for prescription options (ivermectin, spinosad, or malathion).

**A note on school policies:** The AAP does not support "no-nit" policies that exclude children from school for nits (eggs) remaining after treatment. A child treated for lice should be allowed to return to school immediately. The social stigma of lice often causes more harm than the lice themselves. [Source: src10]

### Teens (12–18 years): Acne, identity, and skin

#### Acne

Acne affects roughly 85% of adolescents, making it nearly universal during the teen years. It occurs when oil glands in the skin produce excess sebum, which combines with dead skin cells to plug hair follicles. Bacteria (Cutibacterium acnes) colonize the plugged follicles, triggering inflammation. Hormonal changes during puberty drive the process. [Source: src3]

**Types of acne and what they look like:**
- **Comedonal:** Blackheads (open comedones) and whiteheads (closed comedones) — non-inflamed
- **Inflammatory:** Red, tender papules and pus-filled pustules
- **Nodulocystic:** Deep, painful nodules and cysts — the most severe form, with the highest risk of scarring

**Treatment by severity (based on AAD 2024 guidelines):** [Source: src3]

**Mild (mostly comedones, few papules):**
- Benzoyl peroxide (2.5–5% — higher concentrations aren't more effective, just more irritating)
- Topical retinoid (adapalene 0.1% is available over the counter)
- Use both together for best results

**Moderate (more papules and pustules):**
- Add a topical antibiotic (clindamycin) — always combined with benzoyl peroxide to prevent antibiotic resistance
- Consider oral antibiotics (doxycycline is preferred) for a limited course of no more than three months

**Severe or scarring:**
- Oral isotretinoin is strongly recommended by the AAD for severe acne, acne causing scarring, or acne not responding to standard treatments
- Isotretinoin achieves complete or near-complete clearance in the majority of patients
- Side effects include dry skin and lips (expected and manageable), and it must not be taken during pregnancy due to severe birth defect risk
- Routine lab monitoring has been simplified — the AAD's 2024 update notes that frequent blood tests are not necessary for healthy young patients [Source: src3]

**For hormonal acne in females:**
- Combined oral contraceptives and spironolactone are effective options

**Important:** Start treatment early. The goal isn't just clearing skin — it's preventing scars. Acne scarring is permanent, and every month of undertreated inflammatory acne increases the risk.

#### Skin picking and body image

Acne and body image are deeply connected during adolescence. Studies have shown that acne causes quality-of-life impairment comparable to chronic conditions like asthma and epilepsy. Some teens develop a habit of picking at their skin (excoriation), which can worsen scarring significantly and may signal underlying anxiety. [Source: src3]

If your teen is struggling with how their skin affects their confidence, take it seriously. Effective acne treatment — starting with a visit to the pediatrician or dermatologist — is one of the most concrete things you can do to help. If picking is persistent, cognitive-behavioral therapy (specifically habit reversal training) can be effective.

## Sun protection: Building habits for a lifetime

Sun protection is one of the most impactful things you can do for your child's long-term health. A significant portion of UV exposure accumulates during childhood and adolescence, and childhood sunburns — especially blistering sunburns — significantly increase the risk of skin cancer later in life. [Source: src4]

### By age

**Under 6 months:** Keep babies out of direct sunlight. Use shade (stroller canopies, umbrellas, trees) and lightweight clothing that covers arms and legs. If shade and clothing aren't enough to protect exposed skin, the AAP says it's acceptable to apply a small amount of mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide) to exposed areas like the face and backs of the hands. [Source: src4]

**6 months and older:** Apply broad-spectrum, water-resistant sunscreen with SPF 30 or higher to all exposed skin. Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) are recommended for young children because they sit on top of the skin and physically block UV rays rather than being absorbed. Reapply every two hours and immediately after swimming or sweating. [Source: src4]

### Understanding SPF

SPF measures protection against UVB rays (the primary cause of sunburn). SPF 15 blocks about 93% of UVB rays, SPF 30 blocks about 97%, and SPF 50 blocks about 98%. The jump from 30 to 50 is small — what matters far more than the SPF number is applying enough sunscreen and reapplying it consistently. Most people apply only 25–50% of the recommended amount, which dramatically reduces the effective protection. [Source: src14]

**UVA vs UVB:** UVB rays cause sunburn and directly damage DNA. UVA rays penetrate deeper into the skin, causing premature aging and contributing to skin cancer. "Broad spectrum" on the label means the product protects against both. Always choose broad spectrum. [Source: src14]

### Beyond sunscreen

Sunscreen is important but it's not the only tool — and for young children, it shouldn't be the first tool.

- **Clothing:** UPF-rated sun-protective clothing is the most reliable protection. A regular cotton T-shirt provides roughly UPF 5 — not much.
- **Hats:** Wide-brimmed hats that shade the face, ears, and neck
- **Sunglasses:** Look for 100% UV protection or UV400 on the label
- **Shade:** Seek shade during peak UV hours (10 AM to 4 PM)
- **Lead by example:** Children learn sun habits from watching their parents

### Vitamin D

Some parents worry that sun protection will cause vitamin D deficiency. The AAP recommends that infants receive 400 IU/day of supplemental vitamin D, and children over one year receive 600 IU/day — from diet and supplements, not from unprotected sun exposure. You can protect your child's skin without compromising their vitamin D status. [Source: src4]

### Treating sunburn

If sunburn happens (and sometimes it does, despite your best efforts):
- Cool compresses or a cool bath
- Aloe vera gel for comfort
- Acetaminophen or ibuprofen for pain (ibuprofen for children 6 months and older)
- Extra fluids — sunburned skin loses moisture faster
- Do NOT pop blisters — they protect the healing skin underneath
- For any sunburn in a baby under 12 months, or a blistering sunburn at any age, call your pediatrician

## Birthmarks: What to watch and what to leave alone

Most birthmarks are harmless. A few deserve attention.

### Infantile hemangiomas

The most common benign tumor of infancy, hemangiomas appear in up to 4–5% of babies — more commonly in girls (3:1 ratio), premature infants, and white infants. They typically appear in the first few weeks of life as a small red mark, grow rapidly over the first one to three months, then gradually shrink (involute) over years. Most begin to shrink after the first year and continue to improve gradually, with the majority substantially involuted by ages five to nine. Some leave residual skin changes such as faint blood vessels or textural differences. [Source: src1]

**Most hemangiomas need nothing but monitoring.** However, some need early treatment — ideally before one month of age for high-risk lesions:

- **Near the eye:** Can block vision and cause permanent visual impairment (amblyopia)
- **On the airway:** Hemangiomas in the throat area (subglottic) can obstruct breathing
- **Large facial hemangiomas:** May be associated with PHACE syndrome, which requires additional screening
- **Ulcerating hemangiomas:** Painful, prone to infection and scarring
- **Multiple hemangiomas (more than five):** May indicate internal involvement, particularly in the liver

The first-line treatment when needed is oral propranolol, which has transformed hemangioma management since its discovery in 2008. For small, superficial hemangiomas, topical timolol (a beta-blocker eye drop applied to the skin) can be effective. [Source: src1]

### Port wine stains

Flat, pink-to-purple patches present at birth that do not fade over time (unlike many other vascular birthmarks). They affect about 0.3% of newborns. A port wine stain on the face, particularly on the forehead and upper eyelid area (V1 distribution of the trigeminal nerve), should prompt screening for Sturge-Weber syndrome, which can involve glaucoma and seizures. Pulsed dye laser treatment can lighten port wine stains, and results are generally better when treatment begins early. [Source: src1]

### Cafe-au-lait spots

Light brown, flat patches that can appear anywhere on the body. One or two are common — found in 10–20% of the general population and are entirely harmless. [Source: src12]

**When to pay attention:** Six or more cafe-au-lait spots larger than 5 mm in a child before puberty (or larger than 15 mm after puberty) is one of the diagnostic criteria for neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1), a genetic condition that affects about 1 in 3,000 people. If your child has three or more cafe-au-lait spots, mention it to your pediatrician — it warrants a clinical evaluation, even though many children with a few spots will turn out to be perfectly healthy. [Source: src12]

## Allergic skin reactions

### Contact dermatitis

When the skin reacts to something it touches, the result is contact dermatitis — red, itchy, sometimes blistering skin at the site of contact. In children, common triggers include nickel (from jewelry, belt buckles, or jean snaps), poison ivy and poison oak, fragrances in soaps or lotions, and rubber or latex.

The key to treatment is identifying and avoiding the trigger. Topical corticosteroids and cool compresses provide relief. For recurrent rashes where the trigger isn't obvious, a dermatologist can perform patch testing to identify the specific allergen.

### Hives (urticaria)

Raised, itchy welts that can appear anywhere on the body, change shape, and move around over hours. Hives affect 15–25% of children at some point. In children, the most common cause of acute hives is a viral infection — not a food allergy, though food allergy is also possible. About 80% of acute hive episodes in children are either viral or have no identifiable cause. [Source: src7]

Treatment for hives is straightforward: a non-drowsy oral antihistamine (cetirizine, loratadine, or fexofenadine). If hives last longer than six weeks, they're considered chronic and may benefit from evaluation by an allergist.

### Angioedema

Angioedema is deeper swelling — usually affecting the lips, eyelids, hands, or feet. It often occurs alongside hives and is usually managed the same way (antihistamines). [Source: src7]

**When angioedema is an emergency:** If swelling involves the tongue, throat, or airway — causing difficulty breathing, swallowing, or speaking — this is anaphylaxis. Administer intramuscular epinephrine immediately (EpiPen Jr for children under 30 kg; EpiPen for 30 kg and over) and call 911. Do not wait to see if it gets better. Biphasic reactions (a second wave of symptoms) can occur hours later, so children must be observed in an emergency department for at least four hours after an anaphylactic reaction. [Source: src7]

## When to see a dermatologist

Most childhood skin conditions can be managed by your pediatrician. But some situations benefit from a dermatologist's expertise.

### Red flags — see a doctor promptly

- **Rapidly spreading rash with fever** — could indicate a serious infection (cellulitis, or rarely, conditions like Kawasaki disease or meningococcemia)
- **Petechiae or purpura** — small, non-blanching red or purple spots (they don't fade when you press a glass against them). These can indicate a serious infection or a blood disorder and should be evaluated urgently.
- **Widespread blistering** — especially if the child is unwell
- **Any rash that's getting worse despite appropriate treatment**

### When a referral helps

- Eczema that isn't responding to proper topical treatment after two to four weeks
- Rashes causing significant sleep disruption or quality-of-life impact
- Extensive or rapidly growing birthmarks
- Patchy hair loss (may be alopecia areata or tinea capitis)
- Skin infections not responding to first-line treatment
- Any skin finding that's causing you significant concern — parental instinct counts

### Moles: The ABCDE rule

Melanoma in children is rare, but it does occur. Teach your child (and yourself) to watch for changes in moles using the ABCDE framework: [Source: src19]

- **A — Asymmetry:** One half doesn't match the other
- **B — Border:** Irregular, ragged, or blurred edges
- **C — Color:** Multiple colors or uneven color distribution
- **D — Diameter:** Larger than 6 mm (about the size of a pencil eraser)
- **E — Evolving:** Any mole that's changing in size, shape, color, or behavior (itching, bleeding)

Any mole that meets one or more of these criteria deserves evaluation by a dermatologist.
