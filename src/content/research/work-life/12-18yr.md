## Research Brief

As children become teenagers, the work-life equation shifts again — teens need less supervision but more emotional availability, modeling career and work ethic becomes important, supporting teen employment, deciding when to reduce work to be present during critical years, and preparing for the "empty nest" transition. This article covers these evolving dynamics.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: They Need You Less — and More
- Key points: Less supervision, more availability, after-school accessibility, quality > hours
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Your teen doesn't need you to drive them everywhere. They need you to be home when they want to talk at 10 PM.

### Section 2: Modeling Work and Career
- Key points: Talk about your work, model ethic and purpose, modeling > directing
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your teen is watching how you handle your career. Make it worth watching.

### Section 3: Supporting Teen Employment
- Key points: ≤15-20 hours beneficial, >20 harmful, job quality matters, life skills
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: A part-time job teaches your teen more about real life than any classroom

### Section 4: Managing Your Own Stress
- Key points: Work-family conflict affects teens, they absorb your stress, stress management as modeling
- Supported by: Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Your teen can tell when you're stressed even when you think you're hiding it. They can't.

### Section 5: Preparing for What's Next
- Key points: Empty nest preparation, multi-faceted identity, anticipatory grief, opportunity ahead
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Start investing in your non-parent identity now. You'll need it when the nest empties.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese parents may resist teen employment, preferring academic focus — present evidence for moderate work benefits
- Career exploration in Chinese culture may be heavily parent-directed — present evidence for supporting exploration
- Empty nest is complicated in Chinese culture where children may live at home longer or parents may live with children
- Chinese parental work stress often centers on providing for educational costs (tutoring, college)
- Modeling work purpose is powerful in Chinese families that value hard work (吃苦精神)
