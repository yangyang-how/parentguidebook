## Research Brief

The preschool years are when sibling rivalry peaks, parent favoritism becomes a concern, first friendships form, and family routines become the glue that holds relationships together. This article covers sibling dynamics and rivalry management, the perception of favoritism (even when it doesn't exist), friendship formation in early childhood, and family rituals that strengthen bonds.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Sibling Rivalry — The Real Kind
- Key points: Peaks in preschool, mediation > punishment, some conflict is beneficial
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Your children fighting over toys is annoying AND developmental. Your job is to coach, not referee.

### Section 2: "You Love Them More!" — Favoritism
- Key points: Perception matters regardless of reality, explain differential treatment, consistent favoritism is harmful
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Even if you don't have a favorite, your child might think you do. Ask.

### Section 3: First Friendships
- Key points: Form at 3-4, activity-based, train social skills, having a friend is protective
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Arranging playdates isn't social engineering — it's developmental support

### Section 4: Strengthening Family Bonds
- Key points: Routines and rituals, family meals, 15 min one-on-one, child-led play, emotion coaching
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5, Source 6, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: 15 minutes of undivided attention per child per day costs nothing and changes everything

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese families, sibling dynamics have unique features due to previous one-child policy — many parents have no personal sibling experience
- Favoritism perception is acute in Chinese culture where academic achievement may lead to differential treatment of siblings
- Chinese family rituals (family meals, holiday gatherings, ancestor veneration) are powerful relationship-builders — leverage existing cultural practices
- Interparental conflict may be expressed more indirectly in Chinese families — children still sense it
