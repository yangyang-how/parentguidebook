## Research Brief

Adolescence brings the highest nutrient demands of any life stage outside pregnancy — rapid growth, puberty, and increasing autonomy over food choices collide. This article covers teen caloric and micronutrient needs (iron for menstruating teens, calcium for bone growth), eating disorders awareness and prevention, sports nutrition, energy drink risks, and supporting teens in developing healthy food autonomy without triggering disordered eating.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Fueling the Growth Spurt
- Key points: Highest caloric needs of any age, macronutrient balance, caloric needs by sex and activity
- Supported by: Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your teen is not "eating you out of house and home" — they're building an adult body

### Section 2: Iron, Calcium, and the Nutrients Teens Miss
- Key points: 1,300 mg calcium, iron needs especially for menstruating teens, vitamin D, most teens are deficient
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The nutrients your teen is most likely to miss are the ones they need most right now

### Section 3: Eating Disorders — What Every Parent Must Know
- Key points: Highest mortality of any mental illness, warning signs, atypical presentations, screening, boys too
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: You cannot tell by looking whether your teen has an eating disorder. Know the behavioral signs.

### Section 4: Sports Nutrition Without Disordered Eating
- Key points: Athlete nutritional needs, performance-focused language, iron for female athletes, no weight-based coaching
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: "Eat for what your body can do" is the message that protects both performance and mental health

### Section 5: Energy Drinks, Caffeine, and Sugar
- Key points: Energy drinks never appropriate, SSBs as top sugar source, caffeine limits, breakfast matters
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: Energy drinks are not sports drinks. One can send teens to the ER.

### Section 6: Supporting Food Autonomy
- Key points: Increasing independence, family-based approach, home food environment, no dieting
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Your job shifts from controlling what they eat to stocking the kitchen and modeling good habits

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Eating disorders are severely under-recognized in Asian populations — cultural stigma around mental health and different body ideals complicate detection
- In Chinese culture, commenting on body weight is common and socially acceptable (你胖了/你瘦了). The article must explicitly address why this is harmful during adolescence.
- Academic pressure in Chinese culture often leads to meal skipping, snacking while studying, and energy drink use — address this directly
- Sports nutrition culture can be a gateway to both healthy eating and disordered eating — the framing matters enormously
- Teen vegetarianism/veganism can be either a healthy choice or a cover for restriction — parents should support the choice while ensuring nutritional adequacy
