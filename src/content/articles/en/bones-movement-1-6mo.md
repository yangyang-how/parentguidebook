# Bones & Movement at 1–6 Months — Tummy Time, Head Control, and Growing Fast

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to Know — understanding normal growth and movement helps you support your baby's development.**

**The short version:** Between 1 and 6 months, your baby's skeleton is growing rapidly — they'll double their birth weight by around 5 months. Tummy time is the single most important exercise during this period: it builds the strength your baby needs for head control and rolling, and it helps prevent flat spots on the skull. Most babies gain steady head control by 2 to 4 months and begin rolling by 4 to 6 months, while newborn reflexes gradually fade as voluntary movement takes over.

**Key points:**
- Growth rate: about 1.5–2.5 cm per month in length, with birth weight doubling by roughly 5 months [Source: src2]
- Tummy time goal: 15–30 minutes total per day by 7 weeks of age [Source: src5]
- Head control develops around 2–4 months; rolling from tummy to back around 4–6 months [Source: src1]
- The Moro (startle) reflex fades by about 6 months as voluntary movement takes over [Source: src8]
- Continue 400 IU of vitamin D daily to support bone mineralization [Source: src10]

## Rapid growth — your baby's bones are building fast

In the first 6 months, your baby grows about 1.5 to 2.5 centimeters per month in length and gains roughly 140 to 200 grams (5 to 7 ounces) per week. By around 5 months, most babies have doubled their birth weight. [Source: src2]

All that growth reflects intense activity beneath the surface. Your baby's skeleton is still largely cartilage at birth, and during these months new bone is actively forming. Ossification centers — the spots where cartilage converts to hard bone — continue to appear; the capitate bone in the wrist, for example, begins ossifying at around 2 months. [Source: src9]

**The fontanelles:** Your baby's skull has soft spots where the bones haven't fused yet, which allowed the head to pass through the birth canal. The smaller posterior fontanelle (at the back of the head) typically closes by 6 to 8 weeks of age. The larger anterior fontanelle (on top) stays open much longer, usually closing between 12 and 18 months. [Source: src6]

## Tummy time — the most important exercise right now

Tummy time is supervised, awake time spent on the belly. It's the single best thing you can do to build your baby's head, neck, and shoulder strength — and it's a key strategy for preventing positional plagiocephaly (flat spots on the skull). [Sources: src3, src4]

### How much and how soon

The AAP recommends starting tummy time soon after hospital discharge and building up to at least 15 to 30 minutes total per day by 7 weeks of age. This doesn't need to be all at once — short sessions of a few minutes spread throughout the day work well, especially early on. [Source: src5]

### Why it matters for skull shape

Since the "Back to Sleep" campaign began in 1992, rates of positional plagiocephaly have risen from roughly 2–5% to an estimated 20–46% of infants. The campaign was a critical success — SIDS rates dropped significantly — but it also meant babies spent more time on their backs, putting sustained pressure on the soft skull. Tummy time is the primary prevention strategy. [Source: src4]

Plagiocephaly is a positioning issue, not a bone disease. Most cases improve on their own as babies gain the strength to change head position and spend more time upright. Severe cases may be evaluated for helmet therapy, but this is uncommon. [Source: src4]

### Tips when your baby fusses

Many babies protest tummy time initially. This is normal. A few strategies that help:

- **Start on your chest** — lying skin-to-skin or chest-to-chest counts as tummy time and is usually better tolerated
- **Get down on the floor with them** — eye contact and talking make the position more interesting
- **Place a rolled towel under the chest** — this gives a small boost that makes it easier to lift the head
- **Try it after a diaper change** — build tummy time into the routine rather than treating it as a separate event
- **Keep sessions short** — a few minutes at a time is fine. Gradually increase as your baby builds tolerance

Brief fussing during tummy time is expected. If your baby becomes truly distressed, take a break and try again later.

## Motor milestones to watch for

The transition from reflexive to voluntary movement is one of the most important developmental shifts in these months. [Source: src7]

### Head control (2–4 months)

Head control is the foundation for everything that follows. By around 2 months, most babies can briefly lift their head during tummy time. By 3 to 4 months, they can hold their head steady and upright when supported in a sitting position. [Source: src1]

**Pull-to-sit test:** When you gently pull your baby from lying to sitting by the hands, their head should come along with the body by about 4 months. Significant head lag after 4 months — where the head falls back as you pull — is worth mentioning to your pediatrician.

### Pushing up (3–5 months)

During tummy time, your baby will progress from lifting just the head to pushing up on their forearms and eventually to straight-arm push-ups. By 6 months, most babies can push up with straight arms when on their tummy. [Source: src1]

### Rolling (4–6 months)

Rolling usually happens tummy-to-back first, since the head is heavy and gravity helps. By 6 months, most babies can roll from tummy to back. Rolling back-to-tummy typically comes a bit later. [Source: src1]

Keep in mind that the CDC defines milestones as what 75% or more of children achieve by a given age. Some babies roll at 3 months; others not until after 6 months. A wide range is normal. [Source: src1]

### Early sitting (5–6 months)

Toward the end of this period, some babies begin leaning on their hands to support themselves while sitting (the "tripod sit"). This isn't independent sitting yet — they still need supervision and often topple — but it's a sign that core strength and balance are developing. [Source: src1]

## Primitive reflexes fading

Newborns arrive with a set of primitive reflexes — automatic motor responses controlled by the brainstem that help with survival in the early weeks. During the 1-to-6-month period, these reflexes gradually integrate (disappear) as the brain matures and voluntary movement takes over. [Source: src7]

**The Moro (startle) reflex** is one of the most noticeable. When startled, your baby throws their arms out and then draws them back in. This reflex begins fading around 12 weeks and should be gone by 6 months. [Source: src8]

The disappearance of primitive reflexes on schedule is actually a positive sign — it means the higher brain centers are developing normally and gaining control over movement. [Source: src7]

## Keeping bones healthy

### Vitamin D

All infants should receive 400 IU of vitamin D daily, whether breastfed, formula-fed, or a combination. Breast milk does not contain enough vitamin D on its own, and vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption and bone mineralization. Continue supplementation throughout this period and beyond. [Source: src10]

### Back to sleep, tummy to play

This phrase captures the balance: always place your baby on their back for sleep (the safest position), and use awake time for tummy time and floor play. Both practices work together — safe sleep protects against SIDS, and tummy time builds the strength and prevents the skull flattening that back-sleeping can cause. [Source: src5]

### Watch the "containers"

Car seats, bouncers, swings, and infant seats all have their place — but too much time in these devices (sometimes called "container baby syndrome") can limit your baby's opportunity to move freely and practice the motor skills they're developing. Try to balance convenience with plenty of floor time.

## When to talk to your doctor

**Mention at your next visit:**
- Your baby doesn't seem to be lifting their head during tummy time by 2 months
- You notice a flat spot on the skull that seems to be getting worse despite regular tummy time and position changes [Source: src4]
- Your baby consistently turns or tilts their head to one side only (possible torticollis)

**Call your doctor:**
- Your baby is not holding their head up at all by 4 months [Source: src1]
- Your baby is not rolling in any direction by 6 months [Source: src1]
- You notice asymmetric movement — one side of the body moves differently from the other [Source: src7]
- The Moro reflex or other primitive reflexes have not faded by 6 months [Source: src8]
- Significant head lag during pull-to-sit after 4 months
- Your baby seems stiff or floppy (unusually high or low muscle tone)
