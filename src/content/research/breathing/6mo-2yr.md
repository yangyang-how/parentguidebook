## Research Brief

Between 6 months and 2 years, respiratory illnesses are the leading cause of pediatric emergency visits. Croup (viral laryngotracheobronchitis) peaks in this age group, causing the characteristic barking cough and stridor. Bronchiolitis remains a risk, especially in the first RSV season. Foreign body aspiration becomes a major hazard as infants begin eating solids and exploring with their mouths. Parents need to distinguish stridor (upper airway) from wheezing (lower airway) and know when to seek emergency care.

- **Date:** 2024

- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**
> "Stridor is a high-pitched sound heard during inspiration, caused by upper airway narrowing. Wheezing is heard during expiration and indicates lower airway obstruction."

**Claims supported:**

- Wheezing = lower airway (expiratory) — think bronchiolitis, asthma

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Croup sounds terrifying (barking cough, stridor) but is usually mild — parents need reassurance alongside clear escalation criteria
- Night worsening of croup is very common — parents should know this so they are not blindsided at 2 AM
- Cool night air can temporarily improve croup symptoms (the "car ride to ER" effect)
- OTC cough suppressants should NEVER be used in children under 6 (AAP recommendation)
- Choking prevention is more important than choking response — food preparation guidance is key
- The difference between back blows (infants <1) and abdominal thrusts (children >1) is life-or-death knowledge
- Stridor at rest (not just when crying) is a red flag requiring immediate medical evaluation
- Epiglottitis is rare since Hib vaccination but should be suspected with drooling, high fever, and toxic appearance
