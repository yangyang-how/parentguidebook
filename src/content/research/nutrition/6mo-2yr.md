## Research Brief

The transition from exclusive milk feeding to solid foods is one of the most consequential nutritional windows in childhood. This article covers when and how to introduce solids, early allergen introduction (the evidence has shifted dramatically), iron-rich first foods, baby-led weaning vs traditional spoon-feeding, choking hazards, and the gradual transition away from breast/bottle. The 2024 AAP allergen guidelines represent a major shift from previous "delay" recommendations.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: When to Start Solids (and How to Know Your Baby Is Ready)
- Key points: Around 6 months, developmental readiness signs, not before 4 months
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: It's about what your baby can do, not what the calendar says

### Section 2: The Allergen Revolution — Introduce Early, Not Late
- Key points: 2024 AAP guidelines reverse old advice, peanut/egg/dairy early, LEAP study evidence
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: The science has flipped — early introduction protects against allergies, not the other way around

### Section 3: Iron — The Nutrient to Prioritize
- Key points: Most common deficiency, affects brain development, iron-rich first foods, vitamin C pairing
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: When choosing first foods, think iron first

- Key points: BLW evidence, choking data, combined approach is most common, high-risk foods to modify
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 5

- Key points: Cow's milk at 12 months, bottle weaning by 12-18 months, juice limits, cup transition
- Supported by: Source 8, Source 9, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: This transition is gradual — nobody flips a switch at 12 months

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, rice porridge (粥) is a traditional first food and is fine but low in iron — supplement with iron-rich additions
- Bone broth (骨头汤) is culturally valued in Chinese families but doesn't provide significant calcium despite popular belief
- The allergen introduction reversal is still not widely known — many grandparents and older pediatricians still advise delaying, creating family conflict
- BLW may be more acceptable in Western cultures; Chinese families often prefer spoon-feeding purees, especially congee — both approaches are valid
- Honey must be avoided before 12 months (botulism risk) — this is absolute, not cultural preference
