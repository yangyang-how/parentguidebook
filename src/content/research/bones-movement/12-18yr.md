## Research Brief

Adolescence (12-18) is the single most critical period for bone health: over 25% of adult bone is accumulated in just 2 years around peak pubertal growth, and approximately 90% of peak bone mass is achieved by age 18-20. Calcium requirements peak at 1,300 mg/day. Major concerns include sports injuries (ACL tears, stress fractures), scoliosis screening, the devastating bone effects of eating disorders (up to 90% of anorexia patients have low bone density), and the female athlete triad/RED-S. This is the last major opportunity to build the "bone bank" for life.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The Last Big Window — Building Bone for Life
- Key points: 90% of peak bone mass by 18-20, 25% of adult bone built in 2 pubertal years, boys and girls have different timelines, this is the "bone bank" deposit period
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 4, Source 5

### Section 2: Calcium and Vitamin D — Teenagers Need the Most
- Key points: 1,300 mg calcium/day (most teens fall short), 600 IU vitamin D, <15% of teen girls meet calcium RDA, vitamin D deficiency in 17-47% of teens
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 3, Source 5, Source 6

### Section 3: Sports and the Adolescent Skeleton
- Key points: ACL injuries (female 2-9x higher risk), stress fractures, Osgood-Schlatter, growth plate injuries, prevention through multi-sport and neuromuscular training
- Supported by: Source 10

### Section 4: The Female Athlete Triad and RED-S
- Key points: Disordered eating + menstrual issues + low bone density, under-fueling risks, teen athletes at higher risk than adult athletes, coaches and parents should watch for warning signs
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 10

### Section 5: Eating Disorders and Bone Health
- Key points: 90% of anorexia patients have low bone density, bone loss happens within months, younger onset = worse outcomes, 3x fracture risk, recovery requires treating the disorder
- Supported by: Source 8, Source 9

### Section 6: Scoliosis — Should You Screen?
- Key points: 1-3% prevalence, girls more affected, AAP/POSNA recommend screening at 10-12 (girls) and 13-14 (boys), bracing works when caught early
- Supported by: Source 11, Source 12

### Section 7: When to Talk to Your Doctor
- Key points: Persistent pain during/after activity, missed periods in female athletes, disordered eating signs, back asymmetry, frequent fractures, bone pain at night
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 8, Source 11, Source 13

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Eating disorders affect boys too — RED-S is gender-inclusive; article should not only focus on girls
- Scoliosis screening is controversial: AAP/POSNA recommend it, USPSTF says evidence is insufficient — present both positions honestly
- "Growing pains" vs. concerning bone pain: bilateral, nocturnal, not activity-related = likely benign; unilateral, activity-related, or waking from sleep = needs evaluation
- ACL prevention programs work and are underutilized — FIFA 11+, neuromuscular warm-ups
- Calcium from dairy alternatives (plant milks) varies widely in bioavailability — not all are equivalent to cow's milk
- Vitamin D deficiency rates are higher in teens with darker skin — frame without stigmatization but acknowledge the disparity
- Early sport specialization (single sport before 12) increases overuse injury risk — AAP recommends diversified sport participation
- Amenorrhea in teenage athletes is NEVER normal — it always warrants medical evaluation
