# Ages 5–12 — The School Years

## If you only read one thing, read this

Your child is becoming a person with opinions, friendships, worries, and a life that increasingly happens outside your sight. Your job is shifting — from doing everything for them to teaching them how to do things for themselves. This is good. It's supposed to happen. And it can feel like loss even when it's progress.

**Their body:** They need 9–12 hours of sleep (most aren't getting it), at least 60 minutes of physical activity a day (most aren't getting that either), and a vision check — myopia rates in school-age children have surged globally. If your child squints, holds books close, or complains of headaches, get their eyes checked. [Source: src4, src5, src2]

**Their mind:** Anxiety is the most common mental health issue in this age group. It doesn't always look like worry — it can look like stomachaches, school refusal, anger, or perfectionism. If something feels off for more than two weeks, talk to your pediatrician. [Source: src12]

**School:** Homework has minimal proven academic benefit in elementary school. What matters more: reading together, staying curious, and making sure your child knows that struggle is part of learning, not evidence of failure. [Source: src15]

**You:** The shift from hands-on parenting to coaching is one of the hardest transitions. You'll need to let them fail at small things so they can handle big things later. Helicopter parenting is linked to higher rates of anxiety and depression in children. Giving them age-appropriate independence is not neglect — it's development. [Source: src18]

**When to worry:** Persistent sadness or irritability lasting more than two weeks. Sudden drop in grades. Avoiding school or friends. Any of these warrants a same-week call to your pediatrician. **If your child talks about not wanting to be alive or hurting themselves, this is a crisis — call 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline), text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line), or go to your nearest emergency department immediately.**

Everything else in this article fills in the details. Come back to it whenever you need it.

## Part 1: Your child's body

### Growth

Your child will grow steadily during these years — roughly 4 to 6 centimeters per year — before the dramatic acceleration of the pubertal growth spurt. Girls typically begin their growth spurt around age 9, boys around age 11. This is the calm before the storm, physically speaking. [Source: src1]

**Scoliosis** is worth knowing about. Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis is most common between ages 13 and 15, but screening is recommended at ages 10 and 12 for girls and 13–14 for boys, timed to coincide with peak growth. Watch for uneven shoulders, a hip that seems higher on one side, or clothing that hangs unevenly. If you notice any of these, mention it at your child's next checkup. Screening using the forward bend test is simple and can be done at home. [Source: src1]

**When to ask about growth:** If your child's height is below the 3rd percentile, if they're growing less than 4 centimeters per year after age 6, or if they seem significantly shorter than you'd expect based on your and your partner's height — bring it up with your doctor. Most of the time, the answer is reassuring. In one large study, among children below the 3rd percentile who were growing at a normal rate and had a normal physical exam, only 1.3% had a newly diagnosed medical cause. [Source: src1]

### Vision — the myopia epidemic

This is one of the most important health topics for school-age children, and one many parents haven't heard enough about.

Myopia (nearsightedness) has reached epidemic levels worldwide. In China, 55% of all school-age children are myopic — 35% in primary school, 74% in junior high. Globally, East Asian populations are hardest hit, but the trend is rising everywhere. [Source: src2]

**The single most evidence-based preventive measure is outdoor time.** Research consistently shows that spending 2 or more hours per day outdoors significantly reduces the risk of developing myopia. The mechanism appears related to bright natural light exposure. Compared to 30 minutes of daily outdoor time, 2.3 hours per day reduces the risk of myopia onset by about 50%. This works for prevention — but importantly, outdoor time does not slow progression in children who are already myopic. [Source: src2]

**The 20-20-20 rule** (look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds every 20 minutes) is widely recommended for reducing eye strain from screens. It does help with comfort. However, the evidence that it prevents myopia is weak — animal research suggests breaks need to be at least 5 minutes per hour to have any effect on refractive changes. Still, it's a reasonable habit for digital eye comfort. [Source: src2]

**If your child does develop myopia,** treatments exist to slow progression. Low-dose atropine eye drops (particularly 0.05% concentration) are supported by strong evidence for slowing myopic progression. Orthokeratology (specially designed overnight contact lenses) also shows meaningful benefit. Discuss options with a pediatric ophthalmologist. [Source: src2]

**Get their eyes checked** before first grade, and any time symptoms appear — squinting, sitting too close to screens, headaches after reading, or difficulty seeing the board at school. [Source: src2]

### Teeth

Welcome to the mixed dentition years. Starting around age 6, your child will begin losing primary teeth and gaining permanent ones — a process that continues until roughly age 12. The typical sequence: first molars and central incisors around age 6, lateral incisors next, then canines and premolars, and finally second molars around age 12. [Source: src3]

**Dental sealants** are one of the most effective and underused preventive measures in pediatric dentistry. Applied to the chewing surfaces of newly erupted permanent molars, sealants prevent 80% of cavities in back teeth — where 9 out of 10 cavities occur. Children without sealants have almost three times more cavities than children with them. Ask your dentist about sealants when those first permanent molars come in around age 6, and again when second molars arrive around age 12. [Source: src3]

**Orthodontic evaluation:** The American Association of Orthodontists recommends a first evaluation by age 7. This may seem early, but during mixed dentition, developing problems can be detected — and sometimes early intervention can reduce or eliminate the need for more extensive treatment later. [Source: src3]

**Sports mouthguards:** If your child plays contact sports — or any sport where falls or collisions are possible — a mouthguard is worth the investment. Meta-analysis data shows mouthguard users have dramatically lower rates of dental trauma (7.5–7.8%) compared to non-users (48–59%). Custom-fitted mouthguards offer the best protection; boil-and-bite versions with an ADA seal are a reasonable alternative. [Source: src3]

### Sleep

Children ages 6–12 need 9 to 12 hours of sleep per night. This is not a suggestion — it's the consensus recommendation from the American Academy of Sleep Medicine, endorsed by the AAP. Regularly sleeping less is associated with problems in attention, behavior, learning, and emotional regulation. [Source: src4]

**Screens are the biggest sleep disruptor in this age group.** Screen use before bed raises heart rate, increases alertness, delays sleep onset, and suppresses melatonin production. The content matters too — action-packed or emotionally intense content is worse than calm content. The AAP recommends all screens be turned off at least 30 to 60 minutes before bedtime and kept out of bedrooms entirely. [Source: src4]

**Bedtime autonomy** evolves through these years. At age 5 or 6, you're still directing the bedtime routine. By ages 10 to 12, your child can begin managing their own bedtime with your oversight. The critical factor at every age: consistency. A consistent bedtime matters more than who enforces it. [Source: src4]

### Physical activity

The WHO recommends children ages 5–17 get at least an average of 60 minutes per day of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity. Most of this should be aerobic, with vigorous activities including muscle and bone strengthening at least 3 days per week. Any amount of activity is better than none. [Source: src5]

The benefits go far beyond physical health. Regular activity improves concentration, classroom behavior, academic performance, and reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression. [Source: src5]

**Organized sports readiness** develops gradually:
- **Ages 5–6:** Ready for play-based, non-competitive activities focused on basic motor skills
- **Ages 7–8:** Beginning to understand rules and strategy; appropriate for organized team sports
- **Ages 9–10:** Improved coordination; ready for more complex sports with moderate competition
- **Ages 11–12:** Wide variation in physical maturity; competitive readiness varies significantly between children

[Source: src5]

**Overuse injuries** are a growing problem. Seventy percent of youth athletes quit organized sports by age 13, with injury and burnout among the primary causes. A useful rule of thumb: if your child's weekly hours of sport participation exceed their age in years, or exceed 16 total hours per week, the risk of overuse injury rises significantly. Variety — playing multiple sports rather than specializing in one — is protective. For most sports, there is no evidence that intense training before puberty is necessary for reaching elite levels. [Source: src5, src17]

### Nutrition

School-age children need fuel to grow, learn, and stay active — and this is the stage where eating habits get established for life.

**Breakfast matters.** Strong evidence links eating breakfast with improved cognitive function (especially memory), reduced absenteeism, and better mood. Children with inadequate caloric or micronutrient intake show significantly worse grades, higher absenteeism, and more psychological problems. [Source: src7]

**Calcium is critical now.** Peak bone mass accumulation happens during childhood and adolescence. Children ages 4–8 need 1,000 mg of calcium per day; ages 9–18 need 1,300 mg. Inadequate calcium during growth may increase lifelong osteoporosis risk. [Source: src7]

**Iron needs increase** during growth spurts and especially with the onset of puberty. Iron deficiency — the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide — can cause fatigue, poor concentration, decreased academic performance, and weakened immunity. [Source: src7]

**Childhood overweight and obesity** have reached concerning levels globally — the prevalence of overweight (including obesity) in children ages 5–19 rose from 8% in 1990 to 20% in 2022. In the US, 19.7% of children ages 2–19 have obesity. Prevention focuses on diet and physical activity: limiting sugar-sweetened beverages, reducing sedentary screen time, ensuring daily physical activity, and modeling healthy eating at home. Focus on health behaviors rather than weight or body size — research is clear that parental "fat talk" is strongly associated with children's body dissatisfaction and disordered eating. [Source: src9, src8]

**A note on eating disorders:** These can begin as early as ages 8–10, and cases in children under 12 have nearly doubled in the past decade. In young children, they may present differently — as somatic complaints, selective eating, or ritualistic behaviors around food rather than the patterns we associate with adolescent eating disorders. Never use food as reward or punishment, and model a positive relationship with food and body. [Source: src8]

### Common health issues

**Growing pains** affect up to 37% of children ages 3–12. Despite the name, they're not actually caused by growth — the cause remains unknown. They typically occur in the late afternoon or evening, affect both legs (shins, calves, thighs), and are never in the joints. The hallmark: a completely normal exam, no swelling, and pain that's gone by morning. Gentle massage, stretching, and a warm compress usually help. **Red flags that are NOT growing pains:** pain in a single joint, swelling or redness, limping, fever, morning stiffness, or bone tenderness. [Source: src6]

**Headaches** are common — by age 15, up to 75% of children have experienced a significant one. Migraine affects about 3.8% of children and looks different than in adults: it's often on both sides of the head, shorter in duration (as little as 1–2 hours), and may come with abdominal symptoms. **See your doctor if:** headaches wake your child from sleep, progressively worsen over weeks, come with vomiting or vision changes, or are accompanied by any neurological symptoms. [Source: src6]

**ADHD** is diagnosed in about 11.4% of US children (7 million children ages 3–17). Globally, prevalence is estimated at approximately 5–7%. The evidence on treatment is clear: for children under 6, behavior therapy is the recommended first step. For children 6 and older, the AAP recommends FDA-approved medication and/or evidence-based behavioral therapy, with combined treatment producing the best overall outcomes. [Source: src6]

## Part 2: Your child's mind

### Cognitive development

Between ages 7 and 11, your child enters what developmental psychologists call the **concrete operational stage.** They can now think logically — understanding conservation (that the amount of water doesn't change when you pour it into a differently shaped glass), classification, and multiple perspectives. But they're still reasoning about concrete things. Abstract thinking — hypotheticals, metaphors, philosophical questions — comes later, in adolescence. [Source: src10]

**Reading development** follows a crucial trajectory. Between ages 5 and 7, children are learning to decode — matching sounds to letters, recognizing basic words. By ages 7 to 9, they're developing fluency and beginning to read for comprehension. Then, around grades 3 to 4 (ages 8–10), a critical shift happens: children move from "learning to read" to "reading to learn." Children who are not reading proficiently by the end of third grade are four times more likely to not graduate high school. If your child is struggling with reading, early intervention matters enormously. [Source: src10]

**Executive function** — the mental skills that help children plan, focus attention, remember instructions, and manage multiple tasks — develops in a roughly linear trajectory through ages 5–12. Working memory shows especially rapid development in early childhood, stabilizing around ages 10–12. These skills predict math and reading achievement throughout the school years. Effective interventions include curricula targeting these skills, aerobic exercise, and mindfulness practices. [Source: src10]

### Social life

Friendships become increasingly complex during these years, and increasingly important.

- **Ages 5–7:** Friendships are based on proximity and shared activities — "she's my friend because we sit together."
- **Ages 8–10:** Shared interests, trust, and reciprocity become central. Children start understanding that friendship requires give and take.
- **Ages 9–12:** The need for genuine intimacy in friendships emerges. Children confide in friends, develop loyalty, and begin to experience the pain of betrayal.

[Source: src11]

Social skills learned through friendships — collaboration, compromise, perspective-taking, empathy — are not extras. They're foundational. Rejection hurts, and brain imaging research shows it activates the same neural regions as physical pain. When your child is excluded, they need your support to process the experience — not to have it minimized ("just find other friends") or catastrophized. [Source: src11, src16]

**Bullying** deserves direct attention. According to WHO data from 2024, 1 in 6 school-age children has experienced cyberbullying, and rates have increased since 2018. Victims of cyberbullying show higher rates of depression, anxiety, loneliness, and suicidal ideation than victims of traditional bullying. Evidence-based prevention requires a whole-school approach: social-emotional learning programs, clear reporting systems, and adults who take reports seriously. [Source: src11]

### Emotional development

**Anxiety** is the most common mental health disorder in children. The prevalence of mental, behavioral, and developmental disorders in children ages 3–17 increased from 25.3% to 27.7% between 2016 and 2021. Depression incidence rose approximately 60% and anxiety incidence rose about 31% between 2017 and 2021. [Source: src12]

**Anxiety in children often doesn't look like anxiety.** It can present as:
- Stomach aches or headaches, especially on school mornings
- Refusal to go to school (a red flag associated with social anxiety, generalized anxiety, and depression)
- Perfectionism and meltdowns over mistakes
- Avoidance of new situations or social events
- Irritability and anger

[Source: src12]

**Depression in school-age children differs from adult depression.** The primary symptom may be irritability rather than sadness. Children may not be able to articulate that they feel sad — instead, you'll see declining grades, withdrawal from friends and activities they used to enjoy, changes in sleep or appetite, and somatic complaints. Depression and anxiety frequently co-occur. [Source: src12]

**Self-esteem** typically declines through childhood and into adolescence, which feels counterintuitive but is actually developmentally normal — children are developing more realistic self-assessments. The three domains that matter most: academic self-concept, social acceptance, and physical appearance. Autonomy-supportive parenting — encouraging age-appropriate independence and expressing confidence in your child's ability to make decisions — is associated with healthier self-esteem. [Source: src12]

### Learning differences

**Dyslexia** affects an estimated 5–15% of children, with a pooled meta-analysis estimate of 7.1% in primary school students. It's neurobiological in origin — not caused by laziness, lack of intelligence, or poor instruction. The core deficit is in phonological processing. Signs to watch for: difficulty rhyming, slow to learn letter-sound connections, reversing letters past age 7, avoiding reading, and a large gap between oral ability and written performance. Boys are affected at roughly twice the rate of girls (9.2% vs. 4.7%). [Source: src13]

**ADHD** presents in three forms: predominantly inattentive (difficulty sustaining attention, disorganized, forgetful — often overlooked, especially in girls), predominantly hyperactive-impulsive (fidgeting, excessive talking, difficulty waiting), and combined. These conditions frequently co-occur — ADHD, dyslexia, and dyscalculia share genetic and cognitive underpinnings. [Source: src13]

**When to get evaluated:** If your child is struggling academically or behaviorally and you suspect a learning difference, don't "wait and see." Earlier identification leads to better outcomes. You can request a free evaluation through your school district (under IDEA in the US) or seek a private evaluation through a psychologist. A comprehensive evaluation should include cognitive testing, academic achievement testing, behavioral assessment, and developmental history. [Source: src13]

### Screen time and digital life

The landscape of children's media use has changed faster than the research can keep up — and the guidelines have evolved to reflect this.

**Current reality:** Tweens ages 8–12 average 5.5 hours of screen media per day. Sixty-five percent watch TV daily, 64% play online video games, and 38% have used social media despite most platforms setting a minimum age of 13. [Source: src14]

**The AAP updated its guidelines in 2025** with a significant shift: rather than setting a single time limit for children 6 and older, the focus moved to the quality and context of screen use. The key principle: digital ecosystems designed for children's developmental needs can support learning, while those prioritizing engagement and commercialization encourage problematic prolonged use. The AAP recommends a Family Media Use Plan tailored to your family's needs. [Source: src14]

**What the evidence does show clearly:**
- **4 or more hours per day** of screen time is associated with a threefold increase in risk of depression, social phobia, and generalized anxiety. [Source: src14]
- Screen time is negatively correlated with academic performance. [Source: src14]
- Social media use before age 13 is associated with negative mental health outcomes. Most platforms' minimum age of 13 (based on COPPA) exists for a reason. [Source: src14]

**Practical guidance:** Keep devices out of bedrooms. Screens off 30–60 minutes before bed. Co-view and co-play when possible — family participation with media facilitates learning and lets you know what your child is encountering online. Talk about digital citizenship: evaluating online content critically, understanding that a digital footprint is permanent, and recognizing manipulation. [Source: src14, src4]

## Part 3: School life

### Homework

The research on homework is more nuanced than most people realize. The landmark meta-analysis by Cooper found that homework's academic benefit varies dramatically by age. **In elementary school, homework has minimal direct effect on achievement.** Small amounts — no more than 20 minutes per night — may help develop study habits, but there is no evidence that piling on more homework improves learning for young children. The commonly endorsed "10-minute rule" (10 minutes per grade level per night) is a reasonable guideline. In middle and high school, the correlation with achievement strengthens, but even for older students, excessive homework becomes counterproductive. [Source: src15]

What does matter for young learners: reading together, fostering curiosity, and making sure your child has time to play, rest, and be bored. Unstructured time is not wasted time.

### Extracurriculars

Participation in extracurricular activities is associated with better academic performance, higher self-esteem, improved social skills, and lower rates of substance use. Physical activities carry additional benefits for mental health, sleep, and attention. Arts participation is linked to creativity and emotional expression. These are real, measurable benefits. [Source: src17]

**But overscheduling is real too.** Warning signs: chronic fatigue, declining grades, loss of interest in activities they used to enjoy, irritability, and sleep problems. Children need unstructured free time — not as a reward, but as a developmental necessity. [Source: src17]

**On quitting:** There's an important distinction between quitting because something is hard (a moment to teach persistence) and quitting because of genuine disinterest (a moment to respect autonomy). A reasonable approach: commit to finishing the season or session, then reassess together. [Source: src17]

**On early sport specialization:** A 2024 rapid review of 93 studies involving over 62,000 athletes found that early specialization in a single sport (intensive focus for more than 8 months per year before puberty) is associated with increased injury risk, worse physical performance, no benefit to long-term sport success, and poor psychological outcomes including burnout and social isolation. The recommendation from sports medicine organizations is clear: multisport participation until at least age 12–14. [Source: src17]

### Motivation

**Intrinsic motivation** — curiosity and the desire to master something — predicts deeper learning and sustained engagement. **Extrinsic motivation** — grades, rewards, sticker charts — can actually undermine intrinsic motivation over time. This doesn't mean grades don't matter. It means that how you talk about learning shapes whether your child sees it as something worth doing for its own sake. [Source: src15]

**Growth mindset** — the belief that abilities can be developed through effort — has shown positive effects on motivation and willingness to attempt challenges, particularly in lower-achieving students. It's most effective when the broader school and family culture reinforces growth-oriented norms. Praise effort and strategy, not innate talent. "You worked really hard on that" lands differently than "you're so smart." [Source: src15]

## Part 4: You, the parent

### The parenting shift

This is one of the biggest identity transitions in parenting, and almost nobody warns you about it. You are shifting from **doing things for your child** to **teaching your child to do things for themselves.** The result: you will watch them struggle with things you could do for them in 30 seconds. You will bite your tongue. You will sometimes fail at biting your tongue.

The research on this transition is remarkably clear. **Helicopter parenting** — excessive involvement, overprotection, solving problems your child could solve — is directly associated with lower autonomy, decreased competence, lower self-esteem, and higher rates of anxiety and depression in children. Children of hovering parents miss out on developing problem-solving skills because the problems keep getting solved for them. [Source: src18]

This doesn't mean you step back completely. The sweet spot is **autonomy-supportive parenting**: encouraging independence appropriate to your child's developmental stage while staying available as a resource. The balance point shifts progressively toward more autonomy through the school years. [Source: src18]

**Age-appropriate independence milestones** (these are guidelines, not rules — your child's individual readiness matters more than their age):
- **Ages 5–6:** Dressing themselves, simple chores, playing in the yard with you nearby
- **Ages 7–8:** Walking to school (neighborhood dependent), staying home briefly, basic meal prep
- **Ages 9–10:** Caring for younger siblings briefly, managing homework independently, walking to nearby locations alone
- **Ages 11–12:** Staying home alone for several hours, managing their daily schedule with oversight, beginning to babysit

[Source: src18]

### Difficult conversations

Many parents worry that talking to children about difficult topics — death, divorce, illness, sex, violence in the news — will harm them. The evidence says the opposite. There is no evidence that involving children in sensitive, age-appropriate discussions creates significant problems. There is growing evidence that it helps. What does cause harm: withholding information and leaving children to fill in the blanks with their own imaginations, which often harbor fears more distressing than reality. [Source: src20]

**Talking about death:** Children's understanding evolves. At ages 5–7, they're beginning to grasp that death is permanent but may not understand its universality. By ages 10–12, they have an adult-like understanding of death's finality. Use simple, honest language. Avoid euphemisms like "went to sleep" or "passed away," which confuse young children. Pay attention to their practical concerns — "Who will pick me up from school?" is as real a worry as abstract grief. [Source: src20]

**Sex education** should be progressive and begin earlier than most parents think. The WHO recommends comprehensive sexuality education starting at age 5 — and this is less scary than it sounds. At ages 5–8, it means correct anatomical terms, personal safety, boundaries, consent basics, and age-appropriate information about where babies come from. By ages 9–12, it means puberty changes, reproduction details, healthy relationships, consent, and online safety. Evidence shows comprehensive sex education reduces teen pregnancy, STI rates, and school dropout. [Source: src20]

**Current events:** Ask what your child already knows before providing information. Children often hear more than we think. Filter based on developmental stage, reassure about safety, focus on helpers and what is being done, and limit exposure to news media — especially for younger children. [Source: src20]

### Your own mental health

Parental burnout is real and measurable — defined as chronic exhaustion, inefficacy, and emotional distance in the parental role. A study of over 17,000 parents across 42 countries found that burnout varies significantly by culture, but the consequences are consistent: when parents experience burnout, negative parenting increases, and children develop more anxiety, depression, and behavioral problems. It becomes a vicious cycle. [Source: src19]

The school years bring their own triggers: anxiety about academic performance, comparison to other families, the pressure to get your child into the right activities and the right schools. Parent self-care is not optional — parental stress directly impacts child outcomes. Mutual respect, democratic decision-making at home, and emotional warmth in the parent-child relationship are all associated with better parental mental health. [Source: src19]

### Your child's wider world

Between 50% and 80% of children have meaningful relationships with non-parental adults — teachers, coaches, extended family, mentors, neighbors. These relationships matter. Research shows natural mentoring stimulates social-emotional development, cognitive growth, and positive identity formation. Encourage these connections. A child who has multiple adults in their corner is better equipped for the challenges ahead. [Source: src22]

Free play — unstructured, child-directed time — is not wasted time. It's how children progress from dependence to independence and from external regulation to self-regulation. Guided play, where adults facilitate but don't direct, supports optimal development. Make sure your child's schedule leaves room for it. [Source: src22]

### Preparing for puberty

**Talk about it before it happens.** This is the single most important piece of advice about puberty education. Girls can begin puberty as early as age 8, boys as early as age 9. If you wait until changes are already underway, you've missed the window where preparation reduces anxiety. Start conversations at ages 6–8 for girls and 7–9 for boys. [Source: src23]

**The typical onset ranges:** Girls begin puberty between ages 8 and 13 (breast development is usually the first sign). Boys begin between ages 9 and 14 (testicular enlargement is usually the first sign). Average age of menarche is about 12.5 years. Full pubertal development takes 2 to 5 years. [Source: src23]

**Early puberty is increasing worldwide.** Rates of precocious puberty (onset before age 8 in girls, before age 9 in boys) have risen dramatically in recent decades — the strongest contributing factor is childhood obesity. This is one more reason to prioritize healthy eating and physical activity. [Source: src23]

**What to cover:** Use correct anatomical terms. Normalize the process — this is healthy, expected, and happens to everyone. Cover body changes (hair growth, body odor, skin changes, growth spurts), emotional changes (mood swings, new feelings), and hygiene. Make it an ongoing conversation, not a single awkward "talk." [Source: src23]

## Part 5: Safety

### Bike helmets

Helmets reduce head injury by 48%, serious head injury by 60%, and traumatic brain injury by 53%. Yet fewer than half of children under 14 regularly wear one. This is one of the simplest, most evidence-based safety interventions available. Make it a non-negotiable — every ride, every time. [Source: src21]

### Pedestrian safety

Children under 10 have limited ability to judge traffic speed and distance. Teaching crossing skills in real-world contexts — actually practicing at crosswalks — is more effective than classroom instruction alone. Walk routes together before expecting your child to navigate them independently. [Source: src21]

### Water safety

Drowning risks change as children grow, and swim lessons — while important — do not "drown-proof" a child. The evidence supports multiple layers of prevention: four-sided pool fencing (strongest evidence), life jackets for open water, swim lessons (the WHO recommends basic swimming and water safety training for ages 6–10), close adult supervision, and the presence of lifeguards. Designate a specific adult to watch the water — "everyone is watching" means no one is watching. [Source: src21]

### Home alone readiness

There is no universal legal age for staying home alone — it varies by jurisdiction, with some states specifying ages 10–12. More important than age is readiness: Can your child follow rules consistently? Handle unexpected situations calmly? Reach you by phone? Know what to do in an emergency? Start with short periods and build gradually. [Source: src21]

### Body safety

Traditional "stranger danger" has a fundamental flaw: the majority of child abuse is perpetrated by someone the child knows. A more effective framework teaches children to recognize **behaviors**, not categories of people, that signal danger. Anyone who asks a child for help (adults should ask other adults), asks them to keep secrets from parents, or makes them uncomfortable is a "tricky person" — regardless of whether they're a stranger or a familiar adult. Teach children the correct names for body parts, that their body belongs to them, and that they have the right to say no to any unwanted touch. [Source: src21]

## Part 6: When to call the doctor or seek help

### Physical health — call your doctor if:

- **Headaches** that wake your child from sleep, progressively worsen over weeks, or come with vomiting, vision changes, or neurological symptoms
- **Growth** that seems to have stalled (less than 4 cm/year after age 6) or is significantly below expected for family height
- **Joint pain** that's unilateral, in the joint (not the muscle), associated with swelling, limping, fever, or morning stiffness
- **Vision changes** — squinting, holding books very close, difficulty seeing the board, frequent headaches after near work
- **Persistent abdominal pain** or changes in bowel habits lasting more than two weeks
- **Severe or worsening headaches** — especially if new-onset, affecting sleep, or accompanied by vomiting

[Source: src1, src2, src6]

### Mental health — seek help if:

- **Persistent sadness, irritability, or hopelessness** lasting more than two weeks
- **Anxiety** that interferes with daily life — school refusal, inability to separate from parents, avoidance of age-appropriate activities, physical symptoms (stomach aches, headaches) that improve when the feared situation is avoided
- **Sudden drop in grades** or loss of interest in activities they used to enjoy
- **Social withdrawal** — pulling away from friends and family
- **Talk of not wanting to be alive, or self-harm** — this is a crisis. Call 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline), text HOME to 741741, or go to the nearest emergency department immediately
- **Behavioral changes** that seem out of character and persist
- **Disordered eating** — food restriction, excessive exercise, distorted body image, avoidance of eating with family
- **School refusal** — persistent pattern, not an occasional bad day

Don't wait for certainty. If something feels off, it's enough to make the call. Pediatricians expect these conversations and would rather hear from you early than late.

[Source: src12, src8]

## One last thing

Somewhere between ages 5 and 12, your child will become someone you couldn't have predicted. They'll develop passions you don't share, opinions you didn't teach them, friendships you can't fully see. They'll embarrass you, surprise you, and occasionally say something so perceptive it stops you in your tracks.

They'll also struggle. They'll be left out, get things wrong, face problems you can't fix for them. And the hardest part of this stage isn't any specific challenge — it's learning that your job is no longer to stand between your child and the world. It's to stand behind them while they face it.

You won't do this perfectly. No parent does. But the research is clear on one thing: what matters most isn't the perfect school, the perfect activity schedule, or the perfect parenting strategy. It's the relationship. A child who knows they are loved, who has at least one adult who believes in them, who feels safe coming to you with the hard stuff — that child has what they need.

You're building that every day. In the rides to school, the homework battles, the bedtime conversations, the quiet moments when you're just together. It counts. All of it counts.
