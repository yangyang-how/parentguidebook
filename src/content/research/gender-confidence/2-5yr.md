## Research Brief

Between ages 2-5, children develop a stable sense of gender identity (typically by age 4-6), engage in increasingly gender-typed play, build body confidence through physical mastery, begin asking "boy or girl?" questions about themselves and others, and are ready for foundational consent education. This is also when gender stereotyping peaks — and when adult responses to gender expression matter most.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Gender Identity Becomes Stable
- Key points: Identity by 4-6, constancy by 5-7, expression ≠ identity, questions are normal
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: "Am I a boy or a girl?" is a developmental milestone, not a crisis

### Section 2: Peak Stereotyping — and Why It's Normal
- Key points: Rigidity at 3-5, cognitive rule-learning phase, adult response shapes trajectory
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: When your 4-year-old insists "only girls can wear pink," they're learning rules — and you get to show them rules can bend

### Section 3: Building Body Confidence
- Key points: Appearance awareness at 3-4, physical mastery > appearance praise, body comments matter
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Say "look how fast you ran!" more than "you look so pretty" — one builds confidence, the other builds dependency

### Section 4: Consent Education Basics
- Key points: Ask before touching, stop means stop, body belongs to you, everyday practice
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Tickle games are consent classrooms: "Do you want me to tickle you? Should I stop?"

### Section 5: When Gender Expression Is Non-Typical
- Key points: Occasional cross-gender play is universal, persistent non-conformity warrants consultation (not alarm), support = better outcomes
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 7, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: A boy who loves dresses is exploring. A child who consistently, persistently identifies as a different gender needs support, not correction.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese culture may pathologize gender non-conformity more readily — frame as normal developmental variation
- Consent education may feel culturally foreign to Chinese families where physical affection with elders is expected — frame as complementary
- "Pretty" praise is deeply embedded in Chinese culture for girls (真漂亮) — suggest adding competence-based praise alongside, not eliminating
- Gender rigidity at 3-5 can worry parents who value flexibility — reassure that it's temporary
- The distinction between gender exploration (normal) and gender diversity (needs support) should be clear but not anxiety-inducing
