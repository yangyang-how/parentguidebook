## Research Brief

Ages 6 months to 2 years is when sleep patterns consolidate but battles intensify — night wakings, separation anxiety at bedtime, nap transitions, and the move from crib to toddler bed. This article covers safe sleep beyond infancy, age-appropriate sleep amounts, night waking causes and management, the crib-to-bed transition, and when sleep problems warrant professional help.

- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: How Much Sleep Does My Baby/Toddler Need?
- Key points: 12-16 hrs at 6-12mo, 11-14 hrs at 1-2yr (including naps), nap transitions
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: These numbers include naps — it's total in 24 hours

### Section 2: Night Waking — Why It Happens and What to Do
- Key points: Sleep associations, separation anxiety, teething, developmental leaps, evidence-based strategies
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: 30% of babies this age wake frequently — you're not doing anything wrong

### Section 3: Safe Sleep After 6 Months
- Key points: Room sharing through year 1, rolling is OK, no loose bedding, crib safety
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: The rules relax slightly but the crib stays safe

### Section 4: The Crib-to-Bed Transition
- Key points: Timing (around age 2 or when climbing), safety-proofing the room, managing the new freedom
- Supported by: Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Don't rush it — the crib is the safest place

### Section 5: Night Terrors vs Nightmares
- Key points: Night terrors start around age 2, differ from nightmares, harmless, don't wake the child
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Terrifying for you, forgotten by them — here's how to handle it

### Section 6: When Sleep Problems Need Professional Help
- Key points: Persistent problems affecting development, snoring/apnea, extreme night waking
- Supported by: Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: Most sleep issues are behavioral and fixable — some need a specialist

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Sleep training is controversial in many parenting communities — present evidence-based options without being prescriptive about one method
- Bed sharing is practiced in many cultures despite AAP recommendations against it — acknowledge this sensitively while being clear about the evidence
- Night terrors vs nightmares confusion is very common — clear differentiation helps parents respond appropriately
- Cultural note: In Chinese culture, co-sleeping and extended breastfeeding to sleep are very common — the article should respect these practices while providing safety information
