## Research Brief

The preschool years are a social-emotional explosion: children learn to share, take turns, form their first real friendships, label and discuss emotions, develop self-regulation, and use pretend play as a laboratory for understanding social roles. This article covers friendship formation, emotional labeling and vocabulary, the development of self-regulation, the social function of pretend play, and practical strategies for building these competencies at home.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Friendship — The First Real Relationships
- Key points: First friends at 3-4, activity-based, having a friend is protective, peer learning
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Facilitate friendships by creating opportunities for play, not by managing the relationships

### Section 2: Sharing, Turn-Taking, and the Rules of Social Life
- Key points: True sharing at 3-4 (not 2), turn-taking first, don't force sharing, adult guidance needed
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Your 2-year-old isn't selfish — they literally don't have the cognitive equipment for sharing yet

### Section 3: Naming Feelings — The Power of Emotional Vocabulary
- Key points: Labeling improves regulation, emotion coaching method, basic emotions by age 4-5
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: "You seem frustrated because the tower fell down" is more powerful than "stop crying"

### Section 4: Self-Regulation — The Skill Behind All Other Skills
- Key points: Rapid development ages 3-5, executive function foundations, built through practice not instruction
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Simon Says, Red Light Green Light, and waiting for a turn are literally building your child's prefrontal cortex

### Section 5: Pretend Play — The Social-Emotional Laboratory
- Key points: Not frivolous, develops perspective-taking and conflict resolution, more play = better social skills
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: When your child plays "house" or "doctor," they're practicing being human

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese education culture, academic readiness is often prioritized over social-emotional development in preschool — this article should make the case that SE skills ARE school readiness
- Chinese grandparents may solve social conflicts for children (直接帮忙解决) rather than coaching through them — address the coaching approach
- Emotional suppression is more culturally valued in Chinese culture — distinguish between appropriate regulation and harmful suppression
- Pretend play may be undervalued relative to academic activities (认字, 算术) — present the evidence for its developmental importance
- Gender differences in social-emotional expectations (boys shouldn't cry, girls should be nice) should be addressed
