## Research Brief

Middle childhood (5–12 years) is when identity and belonging shift decisively from family to peers and school. Erikson's industry vs. inferiority stage describes the central task: developing a sense of competence through skills and social comparison. Children develop more realistic self-concepts, become acutely aware of how they measure up to classmates, and derive self-esteem from academic, athletic, and social domains. School belonging — feeling accepted, valued, and connected — becomes a powerful predictor of well-being and academic success.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Academic self-concept formed in elementary school is remarkably persistent — early negative experiences can be hard to reverse
- Children who are "good at everything" may struggle later when they encounter real challenge
- Bullying and social exclusion are direct threats to belonging and identity in this age group
- Neurodivergent children may find the social comparison of this stage especially challenging
- Cultural and socioeconomic factors influence which competencies are valued and thus shape identity
