## Research Brief

Newborns sleep 16-18 hours per day in scattered 2.5-4 hour episodes with no established circadian rhythm. Sleep architecture is dominated by active (REM) sleep, comprising over half of total sleep time. The AAP's 2022 safe sleep guidelines — supine position, firm flat surface, room-sharing without bed-sharing — are the cornerstone of SIDS prevention, reducing risk by up to 50%. Circadian rhythms begin developing around 6-8 weeks and are not established until 3-4 months.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- The AAP explicitly does NOT recommend bed-sharing, but acknowledges some families will do it — safer bed-sharing counseling (no soft bedding, no substances, breastfeeding) is part of the harm-reduction approach
- Swaddling is acceptable but must be discontinued when baby shows signs of rolling (can happen as early as 2 months)
- Inclined sleepers, rockers, and other non-flat surfaces are NOT safe for unsupervised sleep
- Skin-to-skin is safe when the caregiver is awake and alert; risk increases when caregiver is drowsy
- Premature infants are at higher SIDS risk and may need additional monitoring
- Day-night confusion is biologically normal — not a behavioral problem to fix
- Parents should not feel guilty about normal newborn sleep patterns — reassurance is important
