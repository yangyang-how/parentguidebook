## Research Brief

Postpartum physical recovery encompasses healing from childbirth (vaginal or cesarean), pelvic floor rehabilitation, abdominal wall recovery, hormonal shifts, and return to physical activity. ACOG now recommends the postpartum period be treated as an ongoing process rather than a single 6-week visit, with initial contact within 3 weeks and comprehensive evaluation by 12 weeks. WHO guidelines emphasize a "positive postnatal experience" with 63 recommendations covering maternal recovery, mental health screening, and breastfeeding support.

- **Date:** 2018
- **URL:** https://www.acog.org/clinical/clinical-guidance/committee-opinion/articles/2018/05/optimizing-postpartum-care
- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**
> "ACOG recommends that postpartum care be an ongoing process with contact within the first 3 weeks, followed by a comprehensive visit no later than 12 weeks after birth."

> "Components of postpartum care include assessing presence of perineal or cesarean incision pain; providing guidance regarding normal versus prolonged recovery; assessing for presence of urinary and fecal continence, with referral to physical therapy or urogynecology as indicated."

**Claims supported:**

- Pelvic floor dysfunction screening is a standard part of postpartum assessment
- Referral to physical therapy should be provided when incontinence is identified

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Recovery timelines vary enormously by mode of delivery (vaginal vs. cesarean vs. instrumental)
- Many women underreport postpartum symptoms due to normalization of discomfort
- The 6-week "all clear" is a myth — ACOG explicitly moved away from this model
- Diastasis recti prevalence data varies by measurement method and diagnostic criteria
- Exercise recommendations must account for breastfeeding, sleep deprivation, and mental health
- Cultural expectations around postpartum rest (e.g., "sitting the month" in Chinese culture) should be respected and integrated
