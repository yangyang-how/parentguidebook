## Research Brief

The 6-month to 2-year period is the most dramatic for motor development: infants progress from sitting to crawling to independent walking. The anterior fontanelle closes (typically 12-18 months), and physiologic bowlegs (genu varum) are normal and expected. Key parental concerns during this stage include walking timeline (normal range 9-18 months), leg alignment, and the transition from breast milk/formula to solid foods for calcium and vitamin D intake. The AAP recommends evaluation if a child is not walking by 18 months.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Crawling is NOT a milestone anymore per CDC 2022 — many parents worry if baby doesn't crawl, but 4% of typical children skip it
- Walkers and jumpers (baby equipment) do not help babies walk sooner and may delay development — AAP discourages them
- Shoe choice for early walkers: barefoot or soft-soled shoes are best; rigid shoes do not help and may hinder natural foot development
- "W-sitting" is common in toddlers and the clinical significance is debated — avoid alarming parents but mention it if it's the only sitting position used
- Late walkers who are otherwise meeting cognitive and social milestones are less concerning than those with delays across multiple domains
- Regression (losing a previously acquired skill like walking) is always a red flag and warrants urgent evaluation
