# Breathing at 6 Months–2 Years — Croup, Choking, and Airway Emergencies

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — croup and choking are common at this age, and knowing what to do matters.**

**The short version:** Between 6 months and 2 years, your child starts eating solids, crawling, walking, and putting everything in their mouth. This brings two major respiratory concerns: **croup** (a viral illness with a terrifying barking cough that's usually mild) and **choking on food or small objects** (the fourth leading cause of accidental death in children under 3). Bronchiolitis can still occur during the second RSV season. Parents need to know the sound of stridor, the Heimlich technique for each age, and when to go to the ER.

**Key points:**
- Croup affects 3% of children aged 6 months to 3 years; 94% of ER visits go home the same day [Source: src1]
- Choking is the 4th leading cause of accidental death in children under 3 [Source: src5]
- Back blows + chest thrusts for infants under 1; abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) for children over 1 [Source: src8]
- Cough suppressants should NOT be used in children under 6 [Source: src2]

## Normal breathing at 6 months to 2 years

By 6 months, breathing should be more regular and quieter than in the newborn period. The respiratory rate for this age group is about 20 to 30 breaths per minute. Periodic breathing should have resolved. Breathing should look effortless — you shouldn't see the skin pulling in around the ribs or the nostrils flaring with normal breaths.

Your child's immune system is encountering viruses for the first time, so respiratory infections are common — especially once daycare starts. Most are mild upper respiratory infections (colds) that resolve on their own. The key is knowing which symptoms are just a cold and which need medical attention.

## Croup: the barking cough

If you've never heard croup before, it's unmistakable — and alarming. Your child develops a harsh, barking cough that sounds like a seal. They may also have **stridor**: a high-pitched, raspy sound when breathing in. Both are caused by swelling in the voice box (larynx) and windpipe (trachea). [Source: src10]

### What causes it

Parainfluenza virus accounts for about 75% of all croup cases. It's a viral infection — antibiotics don't help. [Source: src1]

### What to expect

Croup often starts with cold symptoms (runny nose, mild cough) for a day or two, then the barking cough appears — usually at night. **Symptoms characteristically worsen at night**, which is why many parents first encounter croup during a terrifying 2 AM wake-up. [Source: src10]

Most cases are mild and resolve in 3 to 5 days. Less than 6% of children who come to the ER with croup need to be admitted. [Sources: src1, src3]

### What helps

**At home (mild croup — barking cough but no stridor at rest):**
- **Stay calm.** Crying and agitation make croup worse. Comfort your child. [Source: src2]
- **Cool night air** can temporarily improve symptoms. The classic "car ride to the ER" often helps because of the cool air — many parents arrive at the hospital with a child who's already improving. [Source: src3]
- **A cool-mist humidifier** in the room may help, though evidence is limited. [Source: src2]
- **Sit with your child upright** — being upright is more comfortable than lying flat.

**What NOT to give:** Cough suppressants should not be used in children under 6. Antibiotics don't work on viral croup. [Source: src2]

**Medical treatment:**
- **Dexamethasone** (a steroid) given as a single oral dose is highly effective and is the first-line treatment. It reduces airway swelling and typically improves symptoms within hours. [Source: src1]
- **Nebulized epinephrine** is used for moderate-to-severe croup in the ER. It provides rapid but temporary relief. Children who receive it need to be observed for 2–4 hours due to the possibility of symptom rebound. [Source: src3]

### When croup becomes an emergency

**🔴 Go to the ER immediately if:**
- **Stridor at rest** — if you hear the high-pitched sound when your child is calm and not crying, this indicates moderate-to-severe airway narrowing [Source: src3]
- **Drooling or difficulty swallowing** — this can indicate epiglottitis (rare since Hib vaccination, but serious)
- **Blue lips or face**
- **Severe retractions** — skin pulling in deeply with each breath
- **Your child looks frightened and is sitting very still, leaning forward** — this posture suggests significant airway compromise

## Stridor vs. wheezing: knowing the difference

These two sounds come from different places in the airway, and telling them apart helps you communicate accurately with your child's doctor: [Source: src4]

| | Stridor | Wheezing |
|---|---|---|
| **Sound** | High-pitched, raspy | High-pitched, whistling |
| **When** | Breathing IN (inspiratory) | Breathing OUT (expiratory) |
| **Where** | Upper airway (throat, voice box) | Lower airway (lungs, bronchioles) |
| **Common causes** | Croup, foreign body | Bronchiolitis, asthma |

**Why this matters:** When you call the doctor or arrive at the ER and say "my child has stridor," that immediately tells them the problem is in the upper airway. It's specific, helpful information. [Source: src4]

## Choking and foreign body aspiration

As your baby starts solid foods (around 6 months) and begins crawling and exploring with their mouth, choking becomes a real and serious risk. Foreign body aspiration is the fourth leading cause of accidental death in children under 3 and the third leading cause in infants under 1. [Source: src5]

### High-risk foods

The most dangerous foods for young children are round, hard, or sticky:
- **Hot dogs** — the #1 choking food for children. Cut lengthwise into strips, then into small pieces. [Source: src5]
- **Grapes** — quarter them lengthwise [Source: src6]
- **Nuts and seeds** — avoid until age 4 [Source: src5]
- **Popcorn** — avoid until age 4 [Source: src5]
- **Hard candy** [Source: src5]
- **Raw carrots** — cook until soft, then cut into appropriate pieces [Source: src5]
- **Chunks of meat or cheese** — cut into pieces no larger than 1/2 inch [Source: src6]
- **Peanut butter** — don't give a large glob; spread thin on bread or crackers [Source: src6]

### Food preparation rules

- Cut all food into pieces no larger than 1/2 inch (about 1 cm) [Source: src6]
- Quarter round foods (grapes, cherry tomatoes, hot dogs) lengthwise [Source: src6]
- Cook hard fruits and vegetables until soft [Source: src6]
- Always supervise eating — no walking, running, or playing while eating [Source: src6]

### Non-food choking hazards

- **Balloons** — a choking risk for children up to age 8 (uninflated latex balloons and pieces of popped balloons) [Source: src6]
- **Coins** — one of the most commonly aspirated non-food objects [Source: src7]
- **Small toy parts** — follow age recommendations on toy packaging [Source: src7]
- **Button batteries** — extremely dangerous if swallowed (not just a choking risk but a chemical burn risk)

### What to do if your child is choking

**For infants under 1 year:** [Source: src8]
1. Place the baby face-down on your forearm, supporting the head
2. Give 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand
3. Turn the baby face-up. Give 5 chest thrusts using two fingers on the breastbone
4. Alternate back blows and chest thrusts until the object comes out or the baby starts coughing/crying
5. If the baby becomes unresponsive, call 911 and begin infant CPR

**For children over 1 year:** [Source: src8]
1. Stand behind the child and wrap your arms around their waist
2. Make a fist and place it above the belly button, below the ribcage
3. Give quick upward thrusts (the Heimlich maneuver)
4. Continue until the object comes out or the child becomes unresponsive
5. If they become unresponsive, call 911 and begin child CPR

> **Every parent and caregiver should learn infant/child CPR.** The AAP recommends CPR training for all parents. Local hospitals, Red Cross chapters, and community centers often offer classes. It takes a few hours and could save your child's life. [Source: src6]

## Ongoing RSV/bronchiolitis risk

Bronchiolitis can still occur during your child's first and second RSV seasons. Second-season infections are typically milder than the first. Treatment remains supportive: hydration, nasal suctioning, and monitoring. [Source: src9]

If your child has not received nirsevimab and is entering their first RSV season, ask your pediatrician whether it's still indicated.

## When to call 911

**Call 911 or go to the ER for:**
- Stridor at rest (not just when crying) [Source: src3]
- Blue lips or face [Source: src4]
- Drooling with inability to swallow [Source: src3]
- Severe retractions — skin pulling in deeply between ribs or below ribcage [Source: src4]
- Sudden choking episode — if your child is coughing forcefully, let them cough. If they cannot cough, cry, or breathe, begin back blows/chest thrusts (under 1) or Heimlich (over 1) immediately and call 911. [Source: src8]
- Your child is unusually limp, lethargic, or unresponsive
