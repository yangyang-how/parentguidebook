# Tummy Time & Flat Head Prevention — What Every New Parent Should Know

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — start early, build the habit, and your baby benefits for months.**

**What is tummy time?** Placing your baby on their stomach while they're awake and you're watching. It's the single most important exercise for your baby in the first six months of life.

**Key facts:**
- Start soon after hospital discharge — even in the first week of life
- Build up to 15–30 minutes total per day by 7 weeks of age
- Strengthens the neck, shoulders, arms, and core — the foundation for every motor milestone to come
- Helps prevent positional plagiocephaly (flat head), which now affects 20–46% of infants
- Always supervised and always while awake — **back to sleep** remains the rule

**What to do:** Start with short sessions (2–3 minutes at a time), several times a day. If your baby fusses, that's normal — try different positions and keep at it. Every minute counts.

## Why tummy time matters

Since the early 1990s, the "Back to Sleep" campaign has saved thousands of lives by teaching parents to place babies on their backs for sleep. The rate of sudden infant death dropped dramatically, and putting babies to sleep on their backs remains one of the most important safety recommendations in pediatrics. [Source: src5]

But this life-saving change came with an unintended side effect. Babies who spend most of their time on their backs — sleeping, lying in car seats, resting in bouncers — don't get the chance to develop the muscles they need for rolling, crawling, sitting, and eventually walking. And their soft skull bones, pressed against a flat surface for hours each day, can develop flat spots. [Source: src3]

Tummy time is the counterbalance. It's how your baby builds strength from the ground up — literally. When a baby lies on their stomach, they have to work against gravity to lift their head, push up on their arms, and eventually reach for things. This effort builds the neck, shoulder, arm, and core muscles that underlie every major motor milestone in the first year. [Source: src3]

A systematic review published in *Pediatrics* found that tummy time was positively associated with gross motor development, the ability to roll, crawl, and move while prone, and the prevention of positional head flattening. [Source: src3] It's not just tradition or intuition — the evidence is clear.

## When to start and how much

**Start soon after hospital discharge.** The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends beginning tummy time in the first days after bringing your baby home. [Source: src5]

Here's a practical progression:

- **Week 1–2:** Start with 1–2 minutes at a time, 2–3 times per day. Your newborn may barely lift their head — that's fine. Even the attempt is building muscle.
- **By 7 weeks:** Work up to a total of 15–30 minutes spread throughout the day. This doesn't need to happen all at once. [Source: src5]
- **By 3–4 months:** Your baby should be able to hold their head up steadily during tummy time and may begin pushing up on their forearms.
- **By 5–6 months:** Many babies can push up on straight arms and may begin rolling from tummy to back. [Source: src1]

The key word is *total* — you're accumulating minutes across the day, not doing a single long session. Five sessions of three minutes each is fifteen minutes. That counts.

## How to do tummy time (practical positions)

If your baby screams every time you put them face-down on the floor, you're not doing anything wrong. Many babies dislike tummy time at first — it's hard work, and they'd rather be on their back where life is easier. Here are positions that can help:

### Chest-to-chest
Lie back on a couch or bed at a slight recline and place your baby tummy-down on your chest, facing you. This is the gentlest way to start because your baby can see your face and feel your warmth. It counts as tummy time. This is especially good for newborns in the first few weeks.

### Lap position
Place your baby tummy-down across your lap while you sit. You can gently pat or rub their back. This gives them a slight incline, making it a bit easier than the flat floor.

### On the floor with a rolled towel
Place a small, firmly rolled towel or receiving blanket under your baby's chest and armpits. This gives them a slight lift and makes it easier to raise their head. Make sure their arms are in front of the roll so they can push against the surface.

### Side-lying
If your baby truly won't tolerate being on their stomach at all, side-lying (with you supporting them so they don't roll onto their back) is a partial alternative. It's not a replacement for full tummy time, but it does engage some of the same muscles.

### Tips that help
- **Time it after a diaper change** — your baby is already undressed and on a surface.
- **Get down on the floor with them** — face-to-face at their level. Talk, sing, make faces. Babies work harder when there's something worth looking at.
- **Use a mirror** — place a small, unbreakable mirror in front of your baby. Most babies are fascinated by the face looking back at them.
- **Try when your baby is alert and content** — right after a nap, not right after a feeding (many babies spit up when placed on their tummies with a full stomach).
- **Stop if they're genuinely distressed** — a few minutes of fussing is normal and productive. Prolonged, escalating crying means it's time to take a break and try again later.

## The connection to motor development

Tummy time isn't just about preventing flat spots. It's the training ground for nearly every motor milestone your baby will reach in the first year.

**Head control (2–4 months):** The ability to hold the head steady is the first major motor milestone, and it's built almost entirely through tummy time. When your baby lifts their head during tummy time, they're strengthening the neck extensors and developing the vestibular (balance) system. [Source: src11]

**Rolling (4–6 months):** Rolling from tummy to back — and later, back to tummy — requires core strength and the coordination of the trunk, shoulders, and hips. Babies who get regular tummy time tend to reach this milestone more readily. [Source: src1]

**Pushing up and sitting (4–6 months):** When a baby pushes up on their arms during tummy time, they're developing the shoulder and arm strength that will later help them prop themselves up for sitting and eventually crawl. By 6 months, most babies can lean on their hands to support themselves while sitting. [Source: src1]

Babies who spend too much time in "containers" — car seats, swings, bouncers, and other devices that hold them in one position — may have fewer opportunities to develop these muscles. This is sometimes called "container baby syndrome," and the simplest antidote is regular time on the floor, both on the tummy and on the back, with freedom to move.

## What is positional plagiocephaly?

Positional plagiocephaly (play-jee-oh-SEF-ah-lee) is a flat spot on the back or side of a baby's head caused by external pressure on the soft skull bones. It's not a bone disease — it's a shape issue that results from spending too much time with the same part of the head pressed against a flat surface.

A newborn's skull is designed to be soft. The skull bones are separated by flexible seams called sutures and soft spots called fontanelles. This flexibility allows the head to pass through the birth canal during delivery — which is why many newborns have a slightly cone-shaped head for the first few days. [Source: src6]

But that same softness means the skull can be molded by sustained pressure. When a baby lies on their back for many hours a day (as they should, for safe sleep), the back of the head can flatten over time. If the baby also has a preferred head-turning direction — which many do — one side may flatten more than the other, leading to an asymmetric shape.

### How common is it?

Before the "Back to Sleep" campaign began in 1992, positional plagiocephaly was relatively uncommon, affecting about 2–5% of infants. Today, the incidence has risen to 20–46%. [Source: src4]

This is important to understand: **positional plagiocephaly is a direct, predictable consequence of the life-saving recommendation to place babies on their backs to sleep.** It's the trade-off. The Back to Sleep campaign prevents sudden infant death; tummy time during waking hours prevents flat spots. Both are necessary. [Source: src4]

### Is it serious?

For the vast majority of babies, positional plagiocephaly is a cosmetic concern that improves on its own as the baby becomes more mobile (rolling, sitting, spending less time on their back). It does not affect brain development. The skull continues to grow and reshape throughout the first year, and most mild-to-moderate asymmetry resolves without any treatment.

That said, some parents understandably worry about how their baby's head looks. If the flattening is significant, there are steps you can take (described below), and in rare cases, a shaping helmet may be recommended.

## Preventing flat spots

The good news is that the same practice that builds motor strength — tummy time — is also the primary strategy for preventing positional plagiocephaly. [Source: src4]

### Tummy time (the most important step)
Every minute your baby spends on their tummy during waking hours is a minute their head is free from the pressure that causes flattening. This alone is the most effective prevention. [Source: src4]

### Repositioning during sleep
While your baby must always sleep on their back, you can alternate which direction their head faces. One practical way: alternate which end of the crib you place your baby's head. Babies tend to turn toward the room (toward light, sound, and activity), so switching their orientation encourages them to turn their head in different directions from night to night.

### Vary the holding position
When you feed, hold, or carry your baby, alternate sides. If you always hold them in your left arm, they always turn their head the same way.

### Limit time in containers
Car seats, bouncers, swings, and strollers all have one thing in common: a flat or curved surface pressing against the back of your baby's head. Use them when needed for safety and convenience, but try not to leave your baby in these devices for extended periods when they could be on the floor instead.

### Encourage looking both ways
If your baby has a preferred direction they turn their head, gently encourage them to look the other way. You can do this by placing interesting things (your face, a toy, a light source) on their less-preferred side.

## When to worry about head shape

Most flat spots are mild and self-correcting. Here's how to think about it:

**Probably fine — keep doing tummy time and repositioning:**
- A slight flat spot on the back of the head that you notice mainly when looking from above
- Mild asymmetry that is improving as your baby becomes more mobile
- Your baby is meeting motor milestones on schedule

**Worth mentioning at the next well-child visit:**
- A noticeable flat spot that isn't improving after several weeks of repositioning and tummy time
- One ear appears shifted forward compared to the other when viewed from above
- The forehead appears more prominent on one side

**See your pediatrician sooner:**
- A flat spot that is getting worse despite consistent repositioning and tummy time
- Your baby has a strong, persistent head-turning preference that you can't redirect (this could be torticollis — tightness in a neck muscle — which needs treatment)
- The asymmetry is severe enough to be visible from the front of the face

### A note about helmets

You may have seen babies wearing shaping helmets (cranial orthotic devices). These work by providing a custom-molded shell that leaves room where the skull needs to grow while applying gentle contact where it doesn't. Helmets are most effective when used between about 4 and 12 months of age, when skull growth is fastest.

However, helmets are typically reserved for moderate-to-severe plagiocephaly that has not responded to repositioning. Most babies do not need them. If your pediatrician thinks a helmet evaluation might be appropriate, they'll refer you to a specialist — usually a pediatric neurosurgeon or craniofacial specialist — who can measure the asymmetry and determine whether a helmet would help.

> **If your baby has a flat spot, this is not something you caused by "not doing enough."** Flat spots happen because babies need to sleep on their backs — and they sleep a lot. You are doing the right thing by following safe sleep guidelines. Adding tummy time and repositioning is the next step, not a correction for something you did wrong.

## Warning signs — when to see a doctor

🔴 **See a doctor within days if:**
- Your baby's head shape is changing rapidly or appears very asymmetric
- Your baby cannot turn their head to one side at all (possible torticollis — a treatable neck muscle condition)
- You notice a ridge forming along any of the skull sutures (this could indicate craniosynostosis, a different condition where skull sutures fuse prematurely, which requires medical evaluation)
- Your baby's fontanelle (soft spot) is bulging, very tense, or sunken — this is not related to plagiocephaly but should be evaluated promptly [Source: src6]

🟡 **Mention at your next visit if:**
- Your baby has a persistent preferred head-turning direction despite your efforts to redirect
- A flat spot is not improving after 2–3 months of consistent repositioning and tummy time
- Your baby is not lifting their head during tummy time by 4 months of age
- Your baby is not rolling in either direction by 6 months [Source: src1]
- You have any questions or concerns about your baby's head shape or motor development — your pediatrician has seen this many times before and can help

## Frequently asked questions

**"My baby hates tummy time. What am I doing wrong?"**
Nothing. Most babies find tummy time difficult, especially at first. It's genuinely hard work for them. Start with very short sessions (even 30 seconds counts in the early weeks), use the gentler positions described above (chest-to-chest, lap), and be patient. Most babies fuss less as they get stronger.

**"My baby was born premature. When should we start tummy time?"**
Ask your baby's medical team. In general, tummy time recommendations for premature babies follow adjusted age (age from the due date, not the birth date). Your neonatologist or pediatrician can give you a personalized timeline.

**"Can tummy time happen on a bed or couch?"**
A firm, flat surface on the floor is safest. Beds and couches are soft and pose a suffocation risk if the baby rolls or turns their face into the surface. If you do chest-to-chest on a couch, stay awake and alert.

**"Is it too late to start tummy time at 3 months?"**
No. Starting later is still better than not starting at all. Your baby may protest more because they haven't built up the neck and shoulder strength yet, but the muscles will develop with practice. Be patient and consistent.

**"My baby already has a flat spot. Will it go away?"**
In most cases, yes. As babies spend more time upright (sitting, being held, eventually standing), the pressure on the back of the head decreases and the skull reshapes. Consistent tummy time, repositioning, and normal motor development resolve the majority of mild-to-moderate flat spots without any additional treatment.
