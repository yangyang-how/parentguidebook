## Research Brief

Between 6 months and 2 years, infant vision matures toward near-adult levels — depth perception solidifies, distance vision strengthens, and eye-hand coordination develops rapidly. This is a critical window for detecting and treating strabismus, amblyopia, and nasolacrimal duct obstruction. AAP and AOA recommend a comprehensive eye assessment between 6 and 12 months, with instrument-based screening at ages 1 and 2 years.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Blocked tear ducts are extremely common and almost always benign — parents should not panic
- However, persistent redness/swelling near the tear duct may indicate dacryocystitis (infection) requiring urgent care
- Intermittent outward eye deviation (exotropia) can be normal in infants but persistent deviation needs evaluation
- Patching compliance is a major challenge in toddlers — atropine drops may be easier for some families
- Some refractive errors in toddlers are normal and self-correcting; not all need glasses immediately
