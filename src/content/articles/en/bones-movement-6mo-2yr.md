# Bones & Movement at 6 Months–2 Years — Sitting, Walking, and Bowlegs

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — most of what you're seeing is normal development unfolding on its own schedule.**

**The short version:** Between 6 months and 2 years, your child goes from sitting upright to walking independently — the most dramatic motor transformation of their life. Most babies walk between 9 and 18 months, and about 4% never crawl at all (which is fine). [Source: src2, src4] Bowlegs are a normal part of infant leg alignment and typically straighten out by age 2. [Source: src8] The anterior fontanelle (soft spot) closes between 12 and 18 months in most children. [Source: src6] The main nutritional shift is from breast milk or formula toward solid foods that supply enough calcium and vitamin D for rapidly growing bones.

**Key points:**
- Walking range is 9 to 18 months; average is around 12 months [Source: src4]
- Crawling was removed from CDC milestone checklists in 2022 — it's not required [Source: src2]
- Bowlegs are physiologically normal until age 2 [Source: src8]
- Anterior fontanelle closes between 12 and 18 months (up to 24 months is still within range) [Source: src6]
- Talk to your pediatrician if your child is not walking by 18 months [Source: src4, src5]

## The big milestones: sitting, crawling, walking

Motor development between 6 months and 2 years follows a general pattern, but the timeline varies widely from child to child. Here's the rough sequence most children follow: [Source: src3]

- **Sitting without support** — around 6 to 9 months [Source: src1, src3]
- **Pulling to stand** — around 9 to 12 months [Source: src1]
- **Cruising** (walking while holding onto furniture) — an intermediate step that many children use before independent walking [Source: src12]
- **Walking independently** — 9 to 18 months, with the average at about 12 months [Source: src4]

### About crawling

If your baby doesn't crawl, don't worry. The CDC removed crawling from its developmental milestone checklists in 2022 because of the wide variation in how — and whether — babies crawl. Approximately 4% of typically developing babies never crawl on their hands and knees at all. [Source: src2]

Some babies scoot on their bottoms, commando-crawl on their bellies, or roll to get where they want to go. Others skip straight from sitting to pulling up and cruising. All of these are normal movement strategies. [Source: src2]

### Motor development isn't linear

Children don't march through milestones in a straight line. Your child may crawl and cruise on the same day, take a few independent steps and then go back to crawling for another week, or use completely different strategies for different situations. This is normal. [Source: src12]

## Is my baby walking late?

The normal range for independent walking is 9 to 18 months. That's a 9-month spread — a baby who walks at 9 months and one who walks at 17 months are both perfectly on track. [Source: src4]

**18 months is the key threshold.** The AAP recommends talking to your pediatrician if your child is not walking by 18 months. [Source: src4, src5] This doesn't necessarily mean something is wrong — some children are simply at the later end of normal — but it warrants a professional evaluation to rule out underlying issues.

A child who isn't walking yet but is meeting other developmental milestones (babbling, responding to their name, engaging socially) is less concerning than one who shows delays across multiple areas. [Source: src1]

### Developmental screening schedule

The AAP recommends standardized developmental screening at 9, 18, and 30 months. These are opportunities for your pediatrician to formally assess whether your child's motor, language, and social development are on track. [Source: src1]

## Bowlegs and funny-looking legs — what's normal

Many parents notice that their baby's legs bow outward and wonder if something is wrong. In nearly all cases, the answer is no.

### Physiologic bowlegs

Most infants are born with bowed legs due to their folded position in the womb. This is called **physiologic genu varum** (medical terminology for normal bowlegs), and it's expected. The bowing is typically most noticeable when your child starts standing and walking. [Source: src8]

The natural progression of leg alignment follows a predictable pattern: [Source: src8]

1. **Birth to 18 months:** Bowlegs (genu varum) — normal
2. **Around age 2:** Legs straighten to neutral alignment
3. **Ages 2 to 4:** Knock-knees (genu valgum) — also normal
4. **By age 7 to 8:** Adult-type straight alignment

Most children with physiologic bowlegs or knock-knees require no treatment beyond observation. Braces and special shoes are not effective for physiologic bowlegs and are not recommended. [Source: src10]

### When bowlegs are not normal

While most bowing is harmless, certain features suggest a pathologic cause: [Source: src9]

- **Asymmetric bowing** — one leg significantly more bowed than the other
- **Bowing that worsens** after age 2 instead of improving
- **Pain or limping**
- **Short stature** combined with bowing

Pathologic causes include **Blount's disease** (a growth disorder of the tibia that causes progressive bowing) and **rickets** (bone softening from vitamin D deficiency). [Source: src9] Both are treatable, especially when caught early.

## The soft spot closes

The **anterior fontanelle** — the diamond-shaped soft spot on top of your baby's head — closes as the skull bones grow together. The typical timeline is 12 to 18 months, though there is considerable variation: about 10% close by 6 months, 25 to 50% close by 12 months, and nearly all close by 24 months. [Source: src6]

### What to watch for

- **Very early closure** (before 3 months) may indicate **craniosynostosis**, a condition where skull bones fuse prematurely. This can restrict brain growth and may need evaluation. [Source: src6]
- **Very late closure** (still open well after 18 months) may be associated with **rickets**, **hypothyroidism**, or other conditions worth investigating. [Source: src6]
- **A bulging fontanelle** (tense and raised when your baby is calm and upright) can indicate increased pressure inside the skull and warrants prompt medical attention.
- **A sunken fontanelle** can indicate dehydration.

Your pediatrician checks the fontanelle at every well-child visit. If you notice anything unusual between visits, bring it up.

## Nutrition for growing bones

Bone is living, growing tissue — not the static scaffold it might seem. During this period of rapid growth, your child's skeleton needs adequate calcium and vitamin D to mineralize properly. [Source: src11]

### Calcium

Calcium needs increase significantly as your child transitions from milk to solids: [Source: src7]

| Age | Daily calcium target |
|---|---|
| 7 to 12 months | 260 mg |
| 1 to 3 years | 700 mg |

Good sources include whole milk (after age 1), yogurt, cheese, fortified cereals, and calcium-set tofu.

### Vitamin D

Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption and bone mineralization. Without enough of it, bones can become soft and weak — this is **rickets**, and it still occurs in children who don't get adequate vitamin D. [Source: src7]

| Age | Daily vitamin D target |
|---|---|
| Birth to 12 months | 400 IU |
| 1 to 3 years | 600 IU |

Breast milk contains very little vitamin D, which is why the AAP recommends supplementing 400 IU daily for all breastfed infants. After age 1, fortified whole milk and a varied diet usually provide enough, but your pediatrician may recommend continued supplementation depending on your child's diet and sun exposure. [Source: src7]

## When to talk to your doctor

Most of what you see during this stage is normal variation. But certain signs warrant a conversation with your pediatrician:

**Motor milestones:**
- **Not walking by 18 months** — the standard evaluation threshold [Source: src4, src5]
- **Persistent toe walking** — occasionally walking on toes is normal in early walkers, but if your child consistently walks on tiptoes beyond the first few months of walking, mention it [Source: src5]
- **Asymmetric gait** — dragging one leg, tilting consistently to one side, or favoring one arm over the other [Source: src5]
- **Regression** — if your child was standing or walking and then loses that ability, this always warrants urgent evaluation [Source: src5]

**Bone and alignment:**
- **Bowlegs worsening after age 2** or bowing that is clearly asymmetric [Source: src9]
- **Pain or reluctance to bear weight**

**Fontanelle:**
- **Bulging fontanelle** when your child is calm and upright [Source: src6]
- **Fontanelle still widely open** well past 18 months [Source: src6]
