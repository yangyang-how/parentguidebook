## Research Brief

School-age years (5-12) mark the period when myopia (nearsightedness) most commonly emerges and progresses, driven by a combination of genetic predisposition, increased near work, and reduced outdoor time. Global myopia prevalence has risen dramatically in recent decades, particularly in East and Southeast Asia. Strong evidence shows that increased outdoor time (2+ hours/day) has a significant protective effect against myopia onset, likely through dopamine-mediated mechanisms related to bright light exposure. Screen time is associated with myopia risk, though the causal mechanism may be displacement of outdoor time rather than screens themselves. Blue light-blocking glasses have no scientific evidence supporting eye health benefits (per AAO). Convergence insufficiency affects 4-17% of school-age children and can cause reading difficulties that mimic attention problems. Sports eye injuries are a leading cause of childhood blindness, but 90% are preventable with proper protective eyewear.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Myopia is NOT just an inconvenience — high myopia (>-6D) significantly increases lifetime risk of retinal detachment, glaucoma, and macular degeneration. Slowing progression matters for long-term eye health
- The "outdoor time" protective effect is about LIGHT EXPOSURE, not physical activity — reading outdoors is protective too. This is an important distinction for parents
- Blue light glasses are a marketing phenomenon with no AAO support. The article should state this clearly without being preachy. The concern about screens is displacement of outdoor time, not the light itself
- Convergence insufficiency is frequently misdiagnosed as ADHD or a learning disability — children who struggle with reading should have a comprehensive eye exam, not just a standard acuity screening
- Standard school vision screenings (Snellen chart) test distance acuity only and miss many conditions including convergence insufficiency, hyperopia, and mild amblyopia
- The 20-20-20 rule (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds) is practical advice for reducing eye strain, though evidence for preventing myopia is limited
- Myopia management (atropine, ortho-K, etc.) is an evolving field — the article should present options neutrally and emphasize discussing with an eye care professional
- Sports eye protection should be positioned as normal equipment (like a helmet), not an overreaction
- Children who already wear glasses may assume their regular glasses protect them during sports — they do NOT (regular lenses can shatter on impact)
