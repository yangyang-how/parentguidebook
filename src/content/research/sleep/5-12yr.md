## Research Brief

School-age children need 9-12 hours of sleep but increasingly don't get it — homework, activities, screens, and social pressures compete with bedtime. This article covers recommended sleep duration, the impact of insufficient sleep on school performance, screen time management, building independent sleep habits, and recognizing sleep disorders in this age group.

## Sources

- **Date:** 2016
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4877308/
- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**
> "Children 6 to 12 years of age should sleep 9 to 12 hours per 24 hours on a regular basis."

> "Sleeping the recommended hours is associated with improved attention, behavior, learning, memory, emotional regulation, quality of life, and mental and physical health."

> "Not sleeping enough is associated with attention, behavior, and learning problems."

### Source 2: Youth Screen Media Habits and Sleep (PMC)
- **Publication:** Child and Adolescent Psychiatric Clinics of North America
- **Date:** 2018
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5839336/
- **Type:** systematic-review

**Key passages:**
> "90% of studies found an association between screen media use and delayed bedtime and/or decreased total sleep time."

> "Limiting screen time during the 30 to 60 minutes before bedtime yields modest benefits in terms of sleep quality and duration."

**Claims supported:**

- Blue light suppresses melatonin production
- A screen-free buffer before bed is evidence-based advice

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: How Much Sleep Does a School-Age Child Need?
- Key points: 9-12 hours, only 5% of kids meet all health guidelines, sleep is the most impactful
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your child probably isn't getting enough — here's what "enough" looks like

### Section 2: The Hidden Cost of Lost Sleep
- Key points: Attention, behavior, academic performance, emotional regulation, even 30 min matters
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: That extra 30 minutes of homework might cost more than it gains if it cuts sleep

### Section 3: Screens and Sleep — The Evidence
- Key points: 90% of studies show harm, blue light, interactive vs passive, no screens in bedroom
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The single most effective thing you can do is remove screens from the bedroom

### Section 4: Building Good Sleep Habits
- Key points: Consistent schedule (weekdays AND weekends), bedtime routine still matters, parent-set bedtimes
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Yes, your 10-year-old still benefits from a parent-set bedtime

### Section 5: When It's Not Just "Growing Pains"
- Key points: Sleep apnea mimicking ADHD, restless legs as "growing pains," sleepwalking, anxiety-related insomnia
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: If your child has attention problems, sleep might be the real issue

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Sleep-deprived children look HYPER, not sleepy — this is counterintuitive and causes frequent misdiagnosis as ADHD
- Weekend sleep catch-up doesn't compensate for weekday sleep debt — consistency matters more than total hours
- Caffeine in sodas, energy drinks, and even chocolate can affect children's sleep more than parents realize
- Cultural note: In China, homework loads are heavy and late-night studying is common — the article should address the sleep-study tension directly
