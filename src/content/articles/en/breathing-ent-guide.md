# Breathing & ENT — From First Breaths to Clear Airways

**⏱️ Quick Summary (60 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — know these signs and when to act.**

**The short version:** Respiratory issues are the most common reason children visit the doctor. Most are minor — colds, coughs, stuffy noses — and resolve on their own. But a few conditions require quick recognition and action. This guide covers everything from normal newborn breathing to asthma, from ear infections to choking emergencies, so you know what's routine, what needs a doctor, and what needs an ER.

**Key things every parent should know:**
- Newborns breathe 30–60 times per minute — much faster than adults. Periodic breathing with pauses up to 10 seconds is normal.
- Young children get 6–8 colds per year. Antibiotics don't help. Saline, honey (age 1+), and time do.
- A barky "seal" cough at night is usually croup — treatable, but know when it's an emergency.
- Snoring is not just annoying — in children, it can signal enlarged adenoids or sleep apnea, which affects behavior, learning, and growth.
- Learn the signs of respiratory distress: skin pulling in around the ribs, nasal flaring, blue lips. These mean "go now."

**When to worry:** Breathing faster than normal for age. Retractions (skin pulling in with each breath). Nasal flaring. Grunting. Blue or gray color around the lips. Stridor (high-pitched sound when breathing in). Any of these = call your doctor or go to the emergency department immediately.

## Newborn breathing: what's normal, what's not

Your newborn breathes differently from you. Their normal breathing rate is 30 to 60 breaths per minute — roughly two to three times faster than an adult's. If you sit and count, it can feel alarmingly fast. It's not. [Source: src1]

You'll also notice something called **periodic breathing**: a pattern where your baby breathes rapidly for a stretch, then pauses for up to 10 seconds, then starts again. This looks terrifying at 3am when you're watching the monitor. But pauses of up to 10 seconds are normal in the first few weeks — it's the immature nervous system working things out. [Source: src2]

**Obligate nasal breathing:** Babies up to about 3 months old breathe primarily through their nose. They don't instinctively switch to mouth breathing when their nose is stuffy. This means even mild congestion — normal congestion, the kind that comes with dry air or a bit of mucus — can make them sound like a tiny freight train. Nasal saline drops and gentle suction with a bulb syringe are your first-line tools. [Source: src3]

**Sneezing:** Your newborn will sneeze a lot. This is not a cold. It's how they clear their tiny nasal passages. Think of it as the nose's self-cleaning cycle. [Source: src3]

**What's NOT normal:**
- Pauses in breathing longer than 20 seconds, or shorter pauses accompanied by a blue/dusky color or a drop in heart rate — this is pathological apnea. [Source: src2]
- Grunting with every exhale (not just during a bowel movement).
- The skin between or below the ribs pulling in with each breath (retractions).
- Nostrils flaring wide with each breath.
- Blue or gray color around the lips, inside the mouth, or at the fingertips.

Any of these signs means call your doctor or go to the emergency department right away. Don't wait to see if it gets better. [Source: src1]

## The common cold — the most frequent visitor

If there's one certainty in parenting, it's this: your child will get colds. Many colds. Relentlessly, mercilessly many colds.

**How many is normal:**
- Infants and preschoolers: 6 to 8 colds per year — up to 10 to 12 if they're in daycare [Source: src4]
- School-age children: 5 to 6 per year
- Teenagers: about 4 per year (adult level)

Most colds are caused by rhinovirus (about 50% of them), with coronavirus, adenovirus, and parainfluenza making up most of the rest. [Source: src4]

### Why antibiotics don't help

Colds are caused by viruses. Antibiotics kill bacteria, not viruses. Giving antibiotics for a cold does not make it go away faster, does not prevent complications like ear infections or sinusitis, and does cause side effects — diarrhea, allergic reactions, and the growing problem of antibiotic resistance. [Source: src5]

This can be frustrating when your child is miserable and you want to do *something*. But "something" in this case means comfort measures, not a prescription.

### What actually helps

- **Nasal saline irrigation** — using saline drops or spray up to 6 times a day can speed recovery and reduce the need for fever medications and decongestants. [Source: src6]
- **Honey** (age 1 and older only — never for infants under 1) — evidence supports it for cough relief. A spoonful before bed can help. [Source: src6]
- **Acetaminophen or ibuprofen** — for fever and discomfort. Follow age-appropriate dosing. Ibuprofen is only for children 6 months and older.
- **Humidifier** in the bedroom — adds moisture to help with congestion.
- **Fluids and rest** — the boring advice that actually works.
- **OTC cough and cold medicines** — the AAP recommends against these for children under 6. They don't work well in young children and carry risks. [Source: src6]

### When it's not just a cold

Most colds resolve in 7 to 10 days. See your doctor if: [Source: src5]
- Fever lasts more than 3 days, or returns after seeming to improve
- Your child complains of ear pain or has ear discharge
- Sinus pain or pressure lasting more than 10 days without improvement
- Rapid or labored breathing
- Wheezing
- High fever (above 40°C / 104°F) at any point

## Croup — the barky cough

Few sounds in parenting are as startling as a croup cough at 2am. It's a harsh, barking sound — often compared to a seal — accompanied by a hoarse voice and sometimes a high-pitched whistling sound when your child breathes in (stridor). [Source: src7]

**What it is:** Croup is a viral infection (usually parainfluenza virus) that causes swelling in the upper airway — the voice box and windpipe. Because young children have small airways, even a little swelling narrows the passage significantly. [Source: src7]

**Who gets it:** Most common between 6 months and 3 years old, though it can occur up to age 6. Symptoms tend to be worst at night and usually last 3 to 7 days. [Source: src7]

**Treatment:**
- **Mild croup** (barky cough but no stridor at rest): Cool mist humidifier or taking the child outside into cool night air can help. A single dose of oral dexamethasone (a steroid) is recommended even for mild cases — it reduces swelling and works within 2 to 3 hours, with effects lasting 24 to 48 hours. [Source: src7]
- **Moderate to severe croup** (stridor at rest, visible retractions): This needs emergency care. Treatment includes dexamethasone plus nebulized epinephrine in the emergency department. [Source: src7]

**Go to the ER if your child has:** [Source: src7]
- Stridor (high-pitched breathing sound) while resting — not just when crying
- Significant retractions — the skin pulling in at the neck, between ribs, or below the ribcage
- Blue or dusky color
- Drooling or inability to swallow
- Extreme agitation or unusual sleepiness

## RSV and bronchiolitis

**RSV** (respiratory syncytial virus) is the leading cause of bronchiolitis and pneumonia in infants. Nearly all children are infected with RSV by age 2, but it's most dangerous in the youngest — especially babies under 6 months. [Source: src8]

**Bronchiolitis** is the resulting illness: it starts looking like a cold (runny nose, mild cough), then progresses over a few days to wheezing, faster breathing, and sometimes difficulty feeding. The peak age for bronchiolitis is 2 to 6 months. [Source: src8]

### Treatment is supportive only

Here's something many parents find surprising: there is no medication that treats bronchiolitis. Studies have shown that albuterol (the inhaler medication), steroids, and antibiotics do not help. Treatment is supportive — nasal suctioning, keeping your child hydrated, and oxygen if needed. Some children with bronchiolitis need hospitalization for hydration and oxygen support, but most recover at home. [Source: src8]

### Prevention — a real breakthrough

The good news is that RSV prevention has changed dramatically: [Source: src8]

- **Nirsevimab (Beyfortus):** A long-acting monoclonal antibody given as a single injection to protect infants during their first RSV season. Recommended for babies under 8 months entering their first RSV season, if the birthing parent didn't receive the RSV vaccine during pregnancy.
- **Abrysvo (Pfizer):** A maternal vaccine given during pregnancy at 32–36 weeks gestation. It passes protective antibodies to the baby before birth.
- Most infants need either the maternal vaccine or the infant antibody — not both.
- RSV season typically runs October through March.

Ask your OB or pediatrician about which option is right for your family.

## Adenoids and tonsils — the hidden troublemakers

This section gets extra detail because enlarged adenoids are one of the most common — and most underdiagnosed — causes of problems in young children. They can affect breathing, sleep, hearing, behavior, and even facial development. Many parents have never heard of adenoids until their child is having surgery on them.

### What are adenoids?

Adenoids are a patch of lymphoid tissue (similar to tonsils) tucked behind the nose, at the very back of the nasal passage where it meets the throat. You can't see them by looking in your child's mouth — they're hidden up above the roof of the mouth. They're part of the immune system and help fight infections in young children. [Source: src9]

Adenoids grow from birth, reach their largest size between ages 3 and 7, and typically shrink on their own by puberty. The problem is what happens when they get too big during those peak years. [Source: src9]

### Signs of enlarged adenoids

Adenoid hypertrophy (enlargement) is extremely common and is the most common cause of nasal obstruction in children. Here's what to watch for: [Source: src9]

- **Chronic mouth breathing** — your child habitually breathes through their mouth, especially during sleep. Their mouth hangs open.
- **Snoring** — often loud and nightly, not just when they have a cold.
- **Sleep apnea** — pauses in breathing during sleep, gasping, restless sleep, unusual sleep positions (hyperextended neck, sleeping sitting up).
- **Nasal voice** — a stuffy, "talking through the nose" quality even when they don't have a cold.
- **Recurrent ear infections** — enlarged adenoids physically block the Eustachian tubes (the tubes connecting the middle ear to the throat), trapping fluid and leading to repeated infections.
- **Chronic congestion** — runny nose or stuffy nose that never fully clears.
- **"Adenoid facies"** — this sounds alarming, but it's a medical term for facial changes caused by chronic mouth breathing: an elongated face, perpetually open mouth, and sometimes dental changes. These can be reversible if the airway obstruction is treated early. [Source: src9]
- **Bad breath** — from chronic mouth breathing and mucus accumulation.

### The connection to ears

This is crucial to understand: enlarged adenoids and ear infections are intimately connected. The adenoids sit right next to the openings of the Eustachian tubes. When they're swollen, they physically block drainage from the middle ear. Fluid gets trapped, bacteria grow, and the result is recurrent ear infections or chronic fluid buildup (effusion) that can affect hearing. [Source: src9]

If your child has recurrent ear infections, ask about the adenoids. They may be the root cause.

### The connection to sleep and behavior

Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) affects 2 to 4% of children, and the number one cause in children is enlarged adenoids and tonsils. [Source: src9] This isn't just about snoring — untreated sleep apnea in children can lead to behavioral problems, difficulty concentrating, hyperactivity, poor school performance, and even growth issues. We cover this in more detail in the sleep-disordered breathing section below.

### When surgery is recommended

Adenoidectomy (removal of adenoids) — often combined with tonsillectomy as a "T&A" — is recommended when: [Source: src10]

- **Obstructive sleep apnea** from adenoid/tonsillar hypertrophy
- **Recurrent acute ear infections** — 3 or more in 6 months, or 4 or more in 12 months — with adenoid hypertrophy
- **Chronic middle ear fluid** lasting more than 3 months, especially with hearing loss
- **Chronic sinusitis** not improving with medical treatment
- **Nasal obstruction** causing significant quality-of-life impact

### Outcomes — and the immune system question

The results are generally excellent. Adenotonsillectomy improves sleep apnea symptoms in approximately 85% of children. [Source: src11]

**"But won't removing them weaken my child's immune system?"** This is the most common question parents ask, and the answer is no. Studies consistently show that adenoidectomy and tonsillectomy do not weaken the immune system. The adenoids and tonsils are a small part of a much larger immune network — removing them does not leave your child defenseless. [Source: src11]

The procedure itself is safe and brief — typically about 15 minutes. Serious complications are rare. The primary bleeding rate is about 0.3%. Children with severe OSA may need a follow-up sleep study to confirm the apnea has resolved. [Source: src11]

## Ear infections

Ear infections are the bread and butter of pediatric visits. About 80% of children will have at least one by age 5, and they're the most common reason children are prescribed antibiotics. [Source: src12]

### Two types to understand

**Acute otitis media (AOM):** A bacterial infection behind the eardrum. Symptoms include ear pain (babies may pull at their ears, be fussy, or have trouble sleeping), fever, and sometimes ear discharge if the eardrum ruptures. This is the classic "ear infection." [Source: src12]

**Otitis media with effusion (OME):** Fluid behind the eardrum *without* active infection. This often follows an ear infection or can occur on its own. The child may not have pain, but the fluid can muffle hearing — which is why a child with OME may seem to not listen or may speak louder than usual. OME can persist for weeks to months. [Source: src12]

### Antibiotics — when and when not

Not every ear infection needs antibiotics. The AAP guidelines offer a **watch-and-wait option** for certain children: [Source: src13]

**Watch-and-wait is appropriate when:**
- The child is 24 months or older with mild symptoms and a one-sided infection
- The child is 6–23 months with mild symptoms, a one-sided infection, and no ear drainage
- Pain has been present less than 48 hours, fever is below 39°C (102.2°F), and pain is controlled with pain relievers

**Immediate antibiotics are needed when:**
- The child is under 6 months
- Symptoms are severe — significant ear pain, ear pain lasting 48+ hours, or fever at or above 39°C (102.2°F)
- Both ears are infected in a child aged 6–23 months
- There is ear drainage (ruptured eardrum)

The reassuring news: about 70% of ear infections resolve on their own within 2 to 3 days, and about 80% within a week. [Source: src13] When antibiotics are needed, the first choice is amoxicillin.

### Ear tubes

For children with recurrent ear infections (3 or more in 6 months, or 4 or more in a year) or persistent middle ear fluid lasting more than 3 months with hearing loss, **tympanostomy tubes** (ear tubes) may be recommended. [Source: src14]

These are tiny tubes placed through the eardrum during a brief procedure. They allow fluid to drain and air to circulate in the middle ear, breaking the cycle of infection and fluid buildup. The tubes typically fall out on their own after 6 to 18 months. It's one of the most common childhood surgeries — about 667,000 per year in the US. [Source: src14]

## Asthma

Asthma is the most common chronic respiratory condition in children, affecting about 8% of children in the US — roughly 6 million. [Source: src15]

### The diagnosis challenge in young children

Here's something many parents don't realize: asthma is genuinely difficult to diagnose in children under 5 or 6. The gold standard test (spirometry — blowing into a tube that measures lung function) can't be reliably performed by young children. So in young children, the diagnosis is clinical — based on the pattern of symptoms, risk factors, and how the child responds to treatment. [Source: src16]

To make things more complicated, wheezing is very common in young children. About 30% of children wheeze before age 3, but only about 30% of those wheezers will go on to develop asthma. The rest outgrow it. [Source: src16]

The **Modified Asthma Predictive Index** helps doctors assess which wheezing children are more likely to develop asthma. Risk is higher if the child has recurrent wheezing plus one major factor (a parent with asthma, eczema, or allergic sensitization to airborne allergens) or two minor factors (allergic rhinitis, wheezing apart from colds, or elevated eosinophils in blood work). [Source: src16]

### Common triggers

- **Viral infections** — the most common trigger in young children [Source: src17]
- **Allergens** — dust mites, pet dander, mold, pollen, cockroach allergens
- **Environmental irritants** — cold air, exercise, tobacco smoke, air pollution, strong odors, cleaning products

Environmental triggers account for up to 80% of asthma flare-ups in urban children. [Source: src17]

### Treatment: the step-wise approach

Asthma treatment follows a step-by-step approach, increasing medication as needed: [Source: src18]

- **Step 1 (intermittent):** Quick-relief inhaler (albuterol) as needed — for children who only wheeze occasionally
- **Step 2 (mild persistent):** Low-dose inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) daily — medications like budesonide or fluticasone — plus quick-relief inhaler as needed
- **Step 3–4 (moderate-severe):** Higher doses of ICS, possibly combined with a long-acting bronchodilator (LABA); specialist referral recommended

For children 5 and under, inhalers should be used with a **spacer and face mask** — this ensures the medication actually reaches the lungs instead of the back of the throat. [Source: src18]

### The asthma action plan

Every child with asthma should have a written asthma action plan, shared with the school and all caregivers. It uses a traffic-light system: [Source: src18]
- **Green zone (doing well):** Daily medications, no symptoms — keep doing what you're doing
- **Yellow zone (caution):** Symptoms increasing — add or increase quick-relief medication, may need to adjust daily medications
- **Red zone (emergency):** Severe symptoms — use quick-relief inhaler, seek immediate medical care

### Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction

About 90% of children with asthma experience exercise-induced symptoms — chest tightness, coughing, or wheezing during or after exercise. But this also occurs in some children without an asthma diagnosis. [Source: src19]

Prevention: 2 puffs of the quick-relief inhaler 15 to 30 minutes before exercise. A warm-up period also helps. Cold, dry air makes it worse — which is why symptoms are often more noticeable in winter outdoor sports. [Source: src19]

**Important:** Asthma should not stop a child from being active. With proper management, children with asthma can participate fully in sports and physical activity.

## Allergies

### Allergic rhinitis — the chronic stuffy nose

Allergic rhinitis (hay fever) affects an estimated 10 to 20% of children and becomes more common with age — from about 5% at age 3 to nearly 15% by the teenage years. [Source: src20]

Classic symptoms include nasal congestion, sneezing, clear runny nose, and itchy nose, eyes, or throat. You might notice the **"allergic salute"** (your child repeatedly pushing their nose upward with the palm of their hand) or **"allergic shiners"** (dark circles under the eyes from chronic congestion). Chronic congestion can lead to mouth breathing. [Source: src20]

### The allergic march

Many allergic children follow a pattern called the **allergic march**: eczema appears first (often in infancy), followed by food allergies, then allergic rhinitis, then asthma. Not every child follows this pattern, but if your child has eczema and food allergies, be aware that respiratory allergies may develop later. [Source: src20]

### Testing and treatment

Allergy testing can be done at any age. The two main methods are skin prick testing and blood tests (serum-specific IgE). Testing is recommended when symptoms significantly affect quality of life or when immunotherapy is being considered. [Source: src21]

**Immunotherapy** (allergy shots or sublingual drops/tablets) is the only treatment that can actually modify the underlying allergic disease — not just mask symptoms. It typically takes 3 to 5 years of treatment but can provide long-term relief and may even prevent the progression from allergic rhinitis to asthma. It's generally started at age 5 and older. [Source: src22]

## Choking and foreign body aspiration

Choking is the fourth leading cause of accidental death in children overall, and the third leading cause in infants under 1 year. Most choking incidents occur in children under 3 years old. [Source: src23]

### Why young children are at highest risk

Children between ages 1 and 3 are at the greatest risk because they're exploring everything by mouth, they don't yet have molars to properly chew food, and their swallowing coordination is still developing. [Source: src23]

### The most dangerous items

**Foods:** Peanuts and other nuts (most common), hot dogs (the most fatal food item — responsible for 17% of food aspiration deaths), whole grapes, popcorn, raw carrots, hard candy, and seeds. [Source: src24]

**Non-food items:** Latex balloons (the most fatal non-food item — 29% of aspiration deaths), small toy parts, coins, button batteries, and pen caps. [Source: src24]

### Emergency response

**For infants under 1 year:** [Source: src25]
1. Place the baby face-down on your forearm (supporting the head), resting your forearm on your thigh.
2. Give **5 firm back blows** between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
3. If the object doesn't come out, turn the baby face-up and give **5 chest compressions** (using two fingers in the center of the chest, just below the nipple line).
4. Alternate between back blows and chest compressions until the object is dislodged or the baby becomes unresponsive.
5. **Do NOT use abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) on infants** — their organs are too fragile.

**For children over 1 year:** [Source: src25]
1. Stand behind the child and wrap your arms around their waist.
2. Make a fist with one hand and place it just above the navel, below the ribcage.
3. Grasp your fist with your other hand and give quick, upward **abdominal thrusts** (Heimlich maneuver).
4. Repeat until the object is expelled or the child becomes unresponsive.

**Call 911 if:** The object is not dislodged, the child becomes unresponsive, or the child is turning blue.

The AAP recommends that all parents and caregivers take a CPR and choking first aid course. [Source: src25]

### Prevention

- Cut food into small pieces for young children. Cut round foods (grapes, cherry tomatoes) lengthwise, not across. Quarter hot dogs lengthwise. Avoid giving hard, round, or sticky foods to children under 4. [Source: src26]
- Supervise all meals — no eating while running, playing, or riding in the car.
- Keep small objects out of reach. Check toy packaging for age recommendations.
- No latex balloons for young children. [Source: src26]

## Sleep-disordered breathing — when snoring isn't just snoring

Many parents dismiss snoring in children as harmless or even cute. But habitual snoring — snoring on most nights — affects 7 to 12% of children, and about 2 to 4% of children have obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). The peak age for pediatric OSA is 2 to 6 years, driven primarily by adenoid and tonsillar enlargement. [Source: src27]

### Why it matters: the behavior connection

This is one of the most underappreciated facts in pediatric health: **sleep-disordered breathing can look like ADHD.** [Source: src28]

Studies show that 25 to 57% of children with ADHD also have sleep-disordered breathing. In one study, nearly 78% of children with ADHD had significant snoring, and 50% were found to have OSA on a sleep study. Sleep-disordered breathing can cause or worsen inattention, hyperactivity, behavioral problems, and learning difficulties. Some children who are diagnosed with ADHD actually have untreated sleep apnea as the underlying cause. [Source: src28]

The AAP recommends screening children with hyperactive or inattentive behavior for sleep-disordered breathing. [Source: src28]

**If your child snores regularly and has any of the following, talk to your pediatrician:**
- Witnessed pauses in breathing, gasping, or choking during sleep
- Restless sleep or unusual sleep positions
- Daytime sleepiness or difficulty waking
- Behavioral problems, irritability, or difficulty concentrating
- Poor growth

### Diagnosis and treatment

The gold standard for diagnosing OSA is an overnight sleep study (polysomnography), which monitors airflow, oxygen levels, brain waves, and heart rate during sleep. [Source: src29]

**Treatment:** [Source: src30]
- **First-line:** Adenotonsillectomy — cures OSA in approximately 85% of otherwise healthy children
- **If surgery isn't appropriate or doesn't fully resolve the problem:** CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure)
- **If obesity is a contributing factor:** Weight management
- **For mild residual OSA:** Nasal corticosteroid sprays and montelukast

## When to go to the ER — respiratory emergencies

Learn these signs. You may never need them, but if you do, you'll need to act fast.

### Signs of respiratory distress — call 911 or go to the ER immediately

- **Retractions:** Skin pulling in at the neck, between the ribs, or below the ribcage with each breath. This means the child is working very hard to breathe. [Source: src31]
- **Nasal flaring:** Nostrils widening with each breath. [Source: src31]
- **Grunting:** A sound with each exhale — the body's attempt to keep airways open. [Source: src31]
- **Tachypnea:** Breathing much faster than normal for age (see table below). [Source: src31]
- **Cyanosis:** Blue or gray color around the lips, inside the lips, or at the fingernails. [Source: src31]
- **Stridor:** A high-pitched sound when breathing in — suggests the upper airway is narrowing. [Source: src31]
- **Altered consciousness:** Unusually sleepy, difficult to wake, or confused. [Source: src31]
- **Inability to speak or cry:** In older children, this suggests severe obstruction. [Source: src31]
- **Tripod positioning:** Leaning forward, hands on knees — a sign of severe breathing difficulty. [Source: src31]

### Specific emergencies to recognize

- **Croup emergency:** Stridor at rest (not just when crying), drooling or unable to swallow, severe retractions, blue color. [Source: src32]
- **Asthma emergency:** Quick-relief inhaler not helping, child can't complete sentences, lips are blue, rapid breathing at rest. [Source: src32]
- **Epiglottitis:** Rare but life-threatening. High fever, drooling, muffled voice, child leaning forward in a "sniffing position." Call 911. **Do NOT try to examine the throat.** [Source: src32]
- **Anaphylaxis:** Sudden wheezing or stridor combined with hives, swelling, or stomach symptoms. Give epinephrine (EpiPen) if available and call 911. [Source: src32]
- **Foreign body obstruction:** Can't cough, cry, or speak. Begin choking rescue maneuvers immediately and call 911. [Source: src32]

## Normal respiratory rates by age

Use this as a reference. If your child is consistently breathing faster than the upper range for their age — especially at rest — that's a reason to call the doctor.

| Age | Normal breaths per minute |
|-----|--------------------------|
| Newborn (0–1 month) | 30–60 |
| Infant (1–12 months) | 30–60 |
| Toddler (1–3 years) | 24–40 |
| Preschool (4–5 years) | 22–34 |
| School-age (6–12 years) | 18–30 |
| Adolescent (13–18 years) | 12–20 |

[Source: src33]

**How to count:** Watch your child's chest rise and fall while they're resting or sleeping (not crying or just finishing exercise). Count the number of breaths in 30 seconds and multiply by 2.
