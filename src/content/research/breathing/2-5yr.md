## Research Brief

Ages 2-5 are when recurrent wheezing raises the question of asthma — but diagnosing asthma in preschoolers is challenging because spirometry is not feasible. The NAEPP and GINA guidelines use clinical criteria (frequency of episodes, pattern of symptoms, family history, atopy) to guide diagnosis and treatment. Viral respiratory infections remain the most common trigger. Croup can still occur. Inhaled corticosteroids are the cornerstone of controller therapy, and every child with asthma needs a written action plan.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Many preschoolers wheeze with viral infections and NEVER develop asthma — parents should not assume the worst
- The modified Asthma Predictive Index (mAPI) is a useful clinical tool but not perfect
- Inhaler technique in toddlers requires a spacer with face mask — proper technique instruction is critical
- Montelukast (Singulair) has an FDA black box warning for neuropsychiatric effects — parents should discuss risks/benefits
- "He only wheezes when sick" does not mean it's not asthma — episodic viral wheezing is a recognized phenotype
- Preschool/daycare settings are high-risk for viral triggers — this is expected, not a failure of management
- Secondhand smoke is the single most important modifiable environmental risk factor
