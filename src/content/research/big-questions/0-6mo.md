## Research Brief

Newborns don't ask philosophical questions — but they are already doing the foundational cognitive work that will make philosophy possible. Wonder and curiosity are present from birth, expressed through sensory exploration, preferential looking, and habituation studies that reveal sophisticated expectations about how the world works. This article covers the origins of wonder, how sensory exploration is proto-inquiry, what infant cognition research tells us about early meaning-making, and how responsive caregiving nurtures the curiosity that will eventually become "Why?"

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Born Curious
- Key points: Curiosity is innate, habituation shows expectation, preferential looking reveals reasoning
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Your baby staring at the ceiling fan isn't zoning out — they're investigating

### Section 2: Sensory Exploration as Proto-Inquiry
- Key points: Mouthing, shaking, dropping = hypothesis testing, systematic exploration
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: When your baby drops their spoon for the 20th time, they're running a gravity experiment

### Section 3: Object Permanence — The First Big Question
- Key points: "Where did it go?" begins at 4-8 months, foundation for understanding absence and loss
- Supported by: Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Peek-a-boo is your baby's first philosophical inquiry: "Do things still exist when I can't see them?"

### Section 4: Nurturing Wonder
- Key points: Responsive caregiving, following baby's interests, narrating exploration
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 5, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: The single most powerful way to nurture your baby's curiosity is to notice what they notice

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese 早教 (early education) culture may focus on directed learning rather than curiosity-following — present evidence for child-led exploration
- Some parents may feel silly narrating to a baby who can't understand words — validate and explain why it matters
- The connection between infant exploration and later philosophical thinking is real but should not be overstated
- Wonder and curiosity should not be confused with stimulation — babies also need calm and rest
