## Research Brief

Between 6 months and 2 years, the cognitive foundations for big questions are being built: object permanence reaches completion ("Where did it go?"), cause-and-effect understanding deepens, and symbolic thought begins — the capacity to use one thing to represent another, which will eventually enable abstract thinking about God, death, and meaning. This article covers these cognitive milestones as the scaffolding for later philosophical thinking.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: "Where Did It Go?" — Object Permanence Completes
- Key points: A-not-B error, tracking invisible displacements, full permanence by 18-24 months
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Your toddler searching frantically for a hidden toy is doing philosophy — they're testing whether reality persists beyond perception

### Section 2: "What Makes What Happen?" — Cause and Effect
- Key points: Active experimentation, button-pushing, dropping, pulling, causal understanding by 12-18 months
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: The relentless experimentation isn't destructive — it's empirical inquiry with small hands

### Section 3: The Birth of Symbol — When a Banana Becomes a Phone
- Key points: Symbolic thought at 18-24 months, pretend play, abstract thinking foundation
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: When your child pretends a block is a car, they've just crossed a cognitive Rubicon

### Section 4: "What's That?" — The First Questions
- Key points: Pointing at 9-12 months, vocabulary explosion, naming and categorizing
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Answer their pointing. Name what they see. You're not just teaching vocabulary — you're teaching them the world can be understood.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese parents may not recognize cause-effect play (opening/closing, dropping) as cognitive development — validate and explain
- The connection between these mundane milestones and later philosophical capacity should be drawn without overstating
- "All gone" (没了) is a concept Chinese toddlers grasp early — leverage this cultural touchpoint
- Adults who dismiss toddler repetitive behavior as annoying miss its cognitive significance
