# Sports Overuse Injuries — Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's Disease, and Growing Athletes

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know. Most overuse injuries in young athletes are self-limiting and manageable at home, but some need medical evaluation.**

**What are overuse injuries?** Injuries that happen gradually from repetitive stress on bones, muscles, and tendons — not from a single fall or collision. In growing children, the growth plates (areas of developing cartilage near the ends of bones) are the weakest link, making young athletes more vulnerable than adults.

**Key facts:**
- Osgood-Schlatter disease (knee pain) and Sever's disease (heel pain) are the two most common overuse injuries in young athletes — both are self-limiting
- Growth plates are softer than surrounding bone and ligament, so repetitive stress hits them first
- Early sport specialization (one sport, year-round, before age 12) significantly increases overuse injury risk
- Most overuse injuries respond well to activity modification, ice, and stretching — not complete rest
- Female athletes have 2-9x higher risk of ACL injuries in jumping and pivoting sports

**What to do:** If your child has persistent pain during or after sports that isn't improving with rest, or if pain is affecting how they move, see a doctor. Most of these conditions have excellent long-term outcomes when managed properly.

## Why growing bodies are vulnerable

Here's something that surprises many parents: a child's skeleton is not simply a smaller version of an adult's. Children and adolescents have **growth plates** — areas of soft, developing cartilage near the ends of long bones where new bone is being formed. These growth plates are the engine of skeletal growth, and they're also the weakest structural link in the musculoskeletal chain. [Source: src1]

In an adult, the tendons and ligaments that attach muscles to bones are typically weaker than the bone itself — so an adult who overloads a joint is more likely to strain a tendon or sprain a ligament. In a growing child, the growth plate is weaker than both the tendon and the bone. The same repetitive forces that would cause a tendinitis in an adult may instead damage the growth plate in a child. [Source: src1]

This is especially relevant during growth spurts. When bones grow rapidly, the muscles and tendons haven't caught up yet — they're relatively tighter, pulling harder on their attachment points. That increased tension, combined with the repetitive loading of organized sports, creates the perfect conditions for overuse injuries. [Source: src2]

The good news: growth plates eventually close and harden into solid bone (this happens at different ages for different bones, generally completing by the late teens). Once that happens, these particular vulnerabilities disappear. Most childhood overuse injuries are self-limiting — they resolve when growth is complete. [Source: src3]

## Osgood-Schlatter disease — the classic "sports knee"

If your child plays a sport that involves running, jumping, or quick direction changes and starts complaining about knee pain — particularly right below the kneecap — Osgood-Schlatter disease is one of the most likely culprits. It's one of the most common causes of knee pain in active adolescents. [Source: src3]

### What's happening

The quadriceps muscle (the big muscle on the front of the thigh) connects to the shinbone via the patellar tendon, which attaches at a small bump called the **tibial tubercle** — the bony point you can feel just below the kneecap. In a growing child, this attachment site is a growth plate made of soft cartilage. Every time the child runs, jumps, or kicks, the quadriceps pulls on that growth plate. Do it enough times, and the growth plate becomes irritated and inflamed. [Source: src3]

The result: pain, swelling, and sometimes a visible bump at the top of the shin, just below the knee. The bump is actually new bone forming in response to the pulling — and it may become permanent, though it's usually painless once growth is complete. [Source: src3]

### Who gets it

Osgood-Schlatter typically occurs in boys between ages 11 and 14 and in girls between ages 9 and 12 — the difference tracks with when each sex enters their pubertal growth spurt. It's especially common in sports like soccer, basketball, track, ballet, and gymnastics — anything with lots of running and jumping. [Source: src3]

### The important reassurance

Children and teens with Osgood-Schlatter disease almost always recover with no lasting health effects. It does not cause permanent damage to the bones or knee joint. [Source: src4] It's painful and frustrating, but it is self-limiting — meaning it resolves on its own when the growth plate closes and hardens into solid bone.

### Managing it at home

The standard approach is **activity modification, not complete rest.** [Source: src4]

- **Reduce** activities that cause pain (especially jumping and deep knee bending), but don't necessarily stop all activity
- **Ice** the area for 15-20 minutes after activity
- **Stretch** the quadriceps and hamstrings regularly — tightness in these muscles increases the pulling force on the growth plate
- **Over-the-counter pain relief** (ibuprofen or acetaminophen) as needed for flare-ups
- **A patellar tendon strap** or knee pad can reduce pressure on the tender area during activity
- **Gradual return** — when pain allows, ease back into full participation rather than jumping straight to the previous training load

Most children can continue playing their sport at a reduced level. Complete rest is rarely necessary and can actually be counterproductive — the goal is finding the level of activity that allows participation without worsening symptoms. [Source: src4]

## Sever's disease — the heel pain equivalent

If Osgood-Schlatter is the knee version, Sever's disease (calcaneal apophysitis) is the heel version — and the mechanism is remarkably similar. [Source: src5]

### What's happening

The Achilles tendon connects the calf muscle to the heel bone (calcaneus). In a growing child, the heel bone has its own growth plate. The calf muscle and Achilles tendon pull on this growth plate with every step, jump, and sprint. When the pulling is repetitive and forceful enough, the growth plate becomes inflamed. [Source: src5]

### Who gets it

Sever's disease affects children between the ages of 8 and 15, with a higher rate of occurrence among boys. It's especially common in children who run and jump on hard surfaces — track, soccer, basketball, and gymnastics are frequent culprits. It often flares up at the beginning of a new sport season, when a child suddenly increases their activity level. [Source: src5]

### Managing it

The approach mirrors Osgood-Schlatter management:

- **Activity modification** — reduce running and jumping on hard surfaces
- **Ice** after activity
- **Calf and Achilles stretching** — tight calf muscles are a major contributor
- **Supportive shoes** with good heel cushioning; avoid flat shoes and cleats when possible during flare-ups
- **Heel cups or gel inserts** can reduce impact on the growth plate
- **Gradual return** as symptoms allow

Like Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's disease resolves when the growth plate fuses — usually by age 15-16. It does not cause lasting damage. [Source: src5]

## Stress fractures — when bone can't keep up with demand

Stress fractures are a different category of overuse injury, and they deserve more respect than growth plate conditions. A stress fracture is a small crack in a bone that develops when muscles become fatigued and can no longer absorb the shock of repeated impact, transferring more force to the bone than it can handle. The body can't build new bone fast enough to keep up with the breakdown, and eventually a tiny crack develops. [Source: src1]

In young athletes, stress fractures most commonly occur in the shinbone (tibia), the bones of the foot (metatarsals), and sometimes the lower back (pars interarticularis — particularly in gymnasts, dancers, and cricket fast bowlers). [Source: src1]

### Warning signs of a stress fracture

- Pain that starts during activity and gets worse with continued activity
- Pain that improves with rest but returns when activity resumes
- Tenderness at a specific point on the bone (not spread over a wide area)
- Sometimes mild swelling

Stress fractures require medical evaluation. Unlike Osgood-Schlatter or Sever's disease, continuing to train through a stress fracture can make it significantly worse — a small crack can become a complete fracture. Treatment typically involves 4-8 weeks of modified activity, sometimes a walking boot, and a gradual return to sport. [Source: src1]

## The early specialization problem

There's a growing body of evidence — and a clear position from the American Academy of Pediatrics — that **early sport specialization is a risk factor for overuse injuries in young athletes.** [Source: src1]

Early specialization means intensive, year-round training in a single sport before age 12. The concern is straightforward: the same muscles, tendons, and growth plates get loaded in the same patterns over and over, without the variety of movement that comes from playing different sports. A child who plays soccer in the fall, basketball in the winter, and swims in the summer loads their body in diverse ways and gives overused areas time to recover. A child who plays soccer year-round, with travel teams and camps filling every gap, puts the same structures under constant stress. [Source: src1]

The AAP recommends that children under 12 participate in multiple sports rather than specializing in one. They also recommend at least 1-2 days off per week from organized training, and at least 2-3 months per year away from a single sport. [Source: src6]

This can be a difficult recommendation to follow in a culture that often rewards early specialization with travel team spots, college scholarships, and competitive advantage. But the evidence is clear: young athletes who specialize early are more likely to experience overuse injuries, more likely to burn out, and — perhaps counterintuitively — are not more likely to reach elite levels than those who diversify early and specialize later. [Source: src6]

> **A note for parents navigating pressure:** You may hear from coaches, other parents, or even your child that taking time off from their primary sport will put them "behind." The evidence doesn't support this. Many elite professional athletes played multiple sports through adolescence. The most important thing you can give a young athlete is a body that's healthy enough to keep playing — not a resume of year-round single-sport training starting at age 8.

## ACL injuries — why girls are at higher risk

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) is one of the major stabilizing ligaments of the knee. ACL tears are one of the most significant sports injuries in adolescents, often requiring surgery and 6-12 months of rehabilitation. [Source: src7]

Here's the statistic that gets attention: **female adolescents participating in jumping and pivoting sports have a 2 to 9 times greater chance of experiencing an ACL injury compared to male athletes in the same sports.** [Source: src7]

### Why the difference?

The reasons are biomechanical, not a matter of toughness or conditioning. Female athletes tend to have: [Source: src7]

- A wider pelvis, which changes the angle of force at the knee
- Greater joint laxity (looseness)
- Different neuromuscular firing patterns — a tendency to land from jumps with the knees straighter and closer together (a "knock-knee" pattern), which puts more stress on the ACL
- Hormonal factors that may affect ligament stiffness during the menstrual cycle

### Prevention works

The most important thing parents should know: **ACL injury prevention programs are effective and underutilized.** Neuromuscular training programs — structured warm-up routines that include balance exercises, plyometrics, and strengthening with an emphasis on proper landing and cutting mechanics — have been shown to significantly reduce ACL injury risk. [Source: src7]

The **FIFA 11+** program is one of the best-studied examples. Originally designed for soccer, its principles apply across jumping and pivoting sports. It takes about 20 minutes as a warm-up and includes: [Source: src7]

- Running exercises at various speeds
- Strength exercises for the core, hips, and legs
- Balance and coordination drills
- Plyometric exercises with a focus on proper landing technique (knees tracking over toes, soft landings, controlled deceleration)

If your child plays a sport with significant ACL injury risk (soccer, basketball, volleyball, lacrosse, skiing), ask their coach whether the team uses a neuromuscular warm-up program. If not, it's worth raising the topic. These programs don't require special equipment and can replace a team's existing warm-up routine. [Source: src7]

## Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)

Overuse injuries don't happen in isolation. They're more common — and heal more slowly — in young athletes who aren't fueling their bodies adequately. **Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)**, formerly known as the "female athlete triad," describes what happens when an athlete's energy intake doesn't match their energy expenditure. [Source: src8]

The classic pattern involves disordered eating, menstrual irregularity (in female athletes), and low bone density — but RED-S affects athletes of all genders and impacts nearly every body system, including bone health, immune function, growth, and mental health. [Source: src8]

Under-fueling can lead to stress fractures, delayed growth, poor muscle development, and prolonged recovery from injuries. [Source: src8] An athlete who keeps getting stress fractures or whose overuse injuries aren't healing on a normal timeline should be evaluated for energy availability — they may simply not be eating enough for the amount of training they're doing.

> **For parents:** Amenorrhea (missed periods) in a teenage athlete is **never** normal and always warrants medical evaluation. It's often dismissed as a side effect of training, but it's actually a warning sign that the body is under-fueled and shutting down non-essential functions — including the bone-building processes that are critical during adolescence.

## When to see a doctor

### 🔴 See a doctor within days if:

- **Sudden inability to bear weight** on a leg or foot after activity
- **Sudden knee giving way or a "pop" felt during a cutting or pivoting movement** — possible ACL injury
- **Visible deformity or significant swelling** in a joint after activity
- **Pain that wakes your child from sleep** and is not related to activity (bone pain at night, unrelated to exercise, can rarely indicate something more serious) [Source: src1]
- **Missed periods** in a female athlete who has previously had regular menstrual cycles [Source: src8]

### 🟡 Schedule a doctor's visit if:

- **Persistent pain during or after activity** that hasn't improved after 2 weeks of rest and home management
- **A limp** during or after activity, even if the child says it "doesn't hurt that much"
- **Pain at a specific point on a bone** (not a joint or muscle) that's tender to touch — possible stress fracture
- **Knee or heel pain** that's limiting participation in sports despite home treatment measures
- **Recurring injuries** — an athlete who keeps getting hurt may have an underlying issue (biomechanical, nutritional, or training-related)
- **Gradual decrease in performance** combined with fatigue, mood changes, or weight loss — possible RED-S [Source: src8]
- **Any pain that's getting worse over time** rather than staying the same or improving

### When it's likely fine to manage at home

- Mild knee pain below the kneecap in an active 10-14 year old that improves with activity modification and ice (likely Osgood-Schlatter) [Source: src4]
- Mild heel pain in an active 8-15 year old that improves with rest, stretching, and supportive shoes (likely Sever's disease) [Source: src5]
- General muscle soreness that appears 1-2 days after a new or more intense activity and resolves within a few days (delayed onset muscle soreness — normal)
- Mild pain during a growth spurt that occurs in both legs at night and responds to stretching ("growing pains")

## Return-to-sport guidelines

One of the most common questions parents ask — and one of the hardest to answer definitively — is "when can my child go back to playing?" Here are general principles:

**The golden rule:** Pain is the guide. A child who is still in significant pain during activity is not ready for full return, regardless of how many weeks have passed. [Source: src4]

**Gradual progression, not sudden return.** After a period of rest or reduced activity, the return should be stepped:

1. **Pain-free daily activities** — walking, stairs, normal school activities without pain
2. **Light activity** — easy jogging, gentle sport-specific movements
3. **Moderate activity** — increased intensity, sport-specific drills at moderate effort
4. **Full practice** — complete team practice without restriction
5. **Full competition** — game-intensity play

Each step should last at least a few days, and the child should be pain-free at each level before progressing. If pain returns at any step, drop back to the previous level. [Source: src4]

**After a stress fracture**, return is slower and should be guided by a healthcare provider. Most stress fractures need 4-8 weeks before starting a gradual return, and imaging confirmation of healing may be needed.

**After ACL surgery**, return to full sport typically takes 9-12 months and should be guided by a physical therapist and orthopedic surgeon, with functional testing to confirm readiness. [Source: src7]

## What you can do as a parent

**Encourage diverse play.** Before age 12 especially, multi-sport participation protects against overuse injuries and builds a broader athletic foundation. [Source: src6]

**Watch the training load.** A simple guideline: weekly training hours shouldn't exceed the child's age. A 10-year-old training more than 10 hours per week in a single sport is at increased risk. [Source: src6]

**Feed the athlete.** Growing bodies that are also training need adequate calories, calcium (1,300 mg/day for ages 9-18), and vitamin D (600 IU/day). An under-fueled athlete is an injury-prone athlete. [Source: src9]

**Prioritize warm-ups.** A proper warm-up that includes dynamic stretching and neuromuscular exercises is one of the most effective injury prevention tools available — and it's free. [Source: src7]

**Listen to pain.** "No pain, no gain" does not apply to growing bodies. Pain is information. A child who says something hurts is telling you something important, even if nothing looks visibly wrong.

**Model rest.** If your child sees that rest and recovery are valued — not viewed as weakness or laziness — they're more likely to speak up when something hurts rather than pushing through.
