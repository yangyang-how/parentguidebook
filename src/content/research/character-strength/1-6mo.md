## Research Brief

Months 1-6 is when the infant's temperament becomes visible and early self-regulation begins. Babies start showing persistence (reaching for objects), frustration tolerance, and social engagement. This article covers temperament vs character, how responsive caregiving during this period builds emotional resilience, the emergence of self-soothing, and how parents can support developing strength without pushing too hard.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Your Baby's Personality Is Showing
- Key points: Temperament emerges, some babies are intense, some are mellow, all are normal
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Your baby's temperament is not a report card on your parenting

### Section 2: Building the Stress Response System
- Key points: Cortisol regulation, how soothing shapes biology, "toxic stress" vs manageable stress
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Every time you calm your crying baby, you're teaching their body how to manage stress

### Section 3: The First Self-Soothing
- Key points: Thumb-sucking, looking away, emerging at 3-6 months, co-regulation precedes self-regulation
- Supported by: Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Your baby is starting to develop their own calming strategies — built on the foundation of yours

### Section 4: Serve and Return Gets Richer
- Key points: Social smiling, cooing conversations, reciprocal play, building persistence
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: When your baby coos and you coo back, you're building character. Literally.

### Section 5: Temperament Is Not Destiny
- Key points: Difficult + responsive parenting > easy + unresponsive, environment shapes expression
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your "challenging" baby may become your most resilient child

## Red Flags & Nuances

- "Just let them cry" vs "respond to every cry" is a false dichotomy — the evidence supports responsive care without requiring perfection
- Temperament labels can become self-fulfilling prophecies — the article should present temperament as a starting point, not a verdict
- Cultural note: In Chinese culture, early self-regulation is highly valued — frame it as emerging from responsive care, not from toughening up
