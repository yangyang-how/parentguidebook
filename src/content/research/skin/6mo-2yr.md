## Research Brief

The 6-month to 2-year period is marked by ongoing eczema management (affects 5-20% of children), the emergence of the eczema-food allergy connection (the "atopic march"), and increasing exposure to viral skin infections like hand-foot-mouth disease and molluscum contagiosum. Sun protection becomes relevant as infants become mobile. Early allergen introduction around 6 months, particularly for infants with eczema, is now recommended to reduce food allergy risk.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Non-blanching rashes (petechiae/purpura) require urgent medical evaluation regardless of age
- Eczema herpeticum (herpes infection of eczematous skin) is a medical emergency — clustered blisters, fever, rapid spread
- Food allergy testing should be guided by clinical history, not done as a screening test for all eczema patients
- Molluscum is often worse in children with eczema due to impaired skin barrier
- Sunscreen is recommended from 6 months; before 6 months, shade and clothing are preferred (AAP)
- Steroid phobia remains a major barrier to effective eczema treatment — parents need clear guidance on safe use
