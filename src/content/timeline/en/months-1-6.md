## If you only read one thing, read this

The survival fog of the first month is starting to lift. You're not imagining it — things are getting easier. Your baby is becoming more human and less potato: they're smiling at you on purpose now, they're starting to reach for things, and their sleep is slowly (slowly) consolidating into longer stretches. You're finding a rhythm. It won't be perfect, and it won't be permanent — babies change the rules as soon as you learn them — but it's there.

**The big milestones:** By 2 months, your baby will smile at you and mean it. By 4 months, they'll laugh, reach for toys, and hold their head steady. By 6 months, they'll be rolling, sitting with support, babbling, and probably putting everything they can grab into their mouth. They'll roughly double their birth weight by 5–6 months. [Source: src11, src15]

**Feeding evolves:** Breastfeeding gets easier as supply regulates. Cluster feeding typically improves by 3–4 months. Around 6 months, you'll introduce solid foods — and current evidence says introducing common allergens early (not delaying them) actually reduces allergy risk. [Source: src28, src36]

**Sleep gets better, then worse, then better again:** Around 3–4 months, many babies hit the "4-month sleep regression" — which isn't really a regression but a permanent shift in how their brain handles sleep. It's rough for 2–6 weeks, and then sleep gradually improves. Stop swaddling as soon as your baby shows signs of rolling. [Source: src41, src42]

**You:** If you're struggling emotionally, this is not just a first-month problem. Postpartum depression and anxiety can develop or worsen anytime in the first six months — in any parent or caregiver, regardless of who gave birth. If you're not feeling like yourself, that's worth a conversation with your doctor. It's not weakness. It's one of the most common complications of having a baby, and treatment works. [Source: src55, src57]

## Part 1: Your baby right now

### What they can do now

Your baby is changing faster in these months than they will at any other point in their life. Here's what to expect.

**Vision:** At 1 month, your baby could barely see past your face. By 3 months, their eyes work together to track objects across a room. By 5 months, they see the world in three dimensions — depth perception is online. By 6 months, they have good color vision and can distinguish between shades of red, blue, and yellow. They'll use their eyes to guide their hands to reach for things. If their eyes are still crossing or wandering after 3–4 months, mention it to your pediatrician — by this age, the eyes should consistently track together. [Source: src1, src2]

**Hearing and communication:** By 2 months, your baby starts cooing — those long, vowel-heavy sounds (ahh, ooh) that mark their first experiments with their voice. By 4–6 months, cooing evolves into babbling: chains of consonant-vowel sounds (ba-ba, da-da, muh-muh). They're not saying words yet, but they're practicing the building blocks of language. They'll also start responding to your tone of voice — they notice when you sound happy, upset, or silly. Talk to them. Narrate what you're doing. Research shows that conversational turns — the back-and-forth of you speaking, them cooing, you responding — matter more for brain development than raw word count. [Source: src4, src5, src6, src54]

**Red flags for hearing:** If your baby doesn't react to loud sounds, doesn't turn toward sounds by 4 months, has no cooing or vowel sounds by 3–4 months, or isn't babbling by 6 months, bring it up with your pediatrician. Early hearing screening follows the 1-3-6 rule: screened by 1 month, diagnostic test by 3 months if needed, and connected with services by 6 months. Early intervention before 6 months makes a significant difference in language outcomes. [Source: src7]

**Motor milestones:** These are the big, visible changes that make this period so exciting:

- **2 months:** Briefly holds head up when held or during tummy time. Moves both arms and legs. [Source: src11]
- **3–4 months:** Head control improves dramatically. Starts reaching for and swiping at objects. Raises head confidently during tummy time. [Source: src8, src9]
- **4–5 months:** Grasps objects — rattles, toys, your hair, your food. Brings everything to their mouth (this is exploration, not hunger). Holds head steady without support. [Source: src8, src9, src11]
- **5–6 months:** Rolls from tummy to back (and eventually back to tummy). Pushes up with straight arms during tummy time. Starts leaning on hands to support themselves while sitting. Transfers objects between hands. [Source: src8, src11]

**About tummy time:** The AAP recommends starting from birth — 2–3 sessions of 3–5 minutes daily, increasing gradually. Work up to 15–30 minutes total per day by 7 weeks. It strengthens neck muscles, helps prevent flat spots on the head, and builds the foundation for rolling and crawling. Always supervised, always while awake. Many babies protest at first. That's normal. Short, frequent sessions work better than forcing long ones. [Source: src12]

**A note on milestone timelines:** The CDC's 2022 updated milestones are set at the 75th percentile — meaning most children achieve them by the listed age, but plenty of healthy babies are earlier or later. There is wide individual variation, especially for motor milestones. If your baby hasn't hit a milestone by the expected age, mention it to your pediatrician, but don't panic. One milestone in isolation rarely tells the whole story. [Source: src10, src11]

**Social and emotional development:** The social smile — your baby's first deliberate, in-response-to-you smile — typically appears around the end of the second month, and it's one of the most rewarding moments of early parenthood. By 3–4 months, your baby clearly prefers their primary caregivers: big smiles and animated cooing for you, more reserved stares for strangers. They're intrigued by other children. They laugh. They have distinct moods and preferences. They're becoming a person.

Attachment is forming through every feeding, every response to their cry, every time you pick them up. The AAP emphasizes "early relational health" — the quality of your early relationship is the foundation for all of their development. You don't need to be perfect. You need to be responsive — most of the time. [Source: src49, src50]

### Growth and body changes

**Weight and length:** After the first-month weight dip, babies gain steadily. Most will double their birth weight by 5–6 months. Growth isn't linear — it comes in spurts, typically around 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months (each lasting a few days). During growth spurts, your baby may be fussier, hungrier, and cluster-feed intensely. This is normal and temporary. [Source: src15, src17]

What matters more than any single measurement is the trajectory. A baby at the 15th percentile who stays at the 15th percentile is growing normally. The pediatrician is watching the curve, not the number. The WHO growth charts (recommended by the AAP for children 0–24 months) reflect healthy growth across diverse populations. [Source: src18]

**Skin changes:**

- **Cradle cap** (seborrheic dermatitis): Thick, yellowish, scaly patches on the scalp. Common from birth through the first few months. Rarely itchy or uncomfortable despite looking alarming. Shampoo frequently and brush gently with a soft brush. Usually clears by 6–12 months without treatment. [Source: src19, src20]
- **Eczema** (atopic dermatitis): Affects up to 15–20% of children, and about 60% develop symptoms in the first year. In infants, it most commonly appears on the cheeks, trunk, and limbs. First-line treatment for infants under 2 is topical corticosteroids for active flares, plus regular moisturizing to repair the skin barrier. Talk to your pediatrician about a management plan. [Source: src21]
- **Drool rash:** As drooling increases (especially with teething), saliva can irritate the skin around the mouth and chin. An absorbent bib changed frequently, plus a barrier cream like petroleum jelly, helps prevent it. [Source: src19]
- **Diaper rash:** Caused by prolonged contact with wet or soiled diapers. Frequent changes, air drying, and a thick layer of barrier cream at each change are the standard approach. [Source: src19]

**Teething:** The first tooth typically appears around 6 months, but the range is wide — some babies start as early as 3–4 months, others not until after 12 months. The lower front teeth almost always come first.

Here's what the evidence actually says about teething symptoms: a prospective study in *Pediatrics* defined the "teething period" as an 8-day window around tooth emergence. Within that window, babies may have increased biting, drooling, gum-rubbing, irritability, wakefulness, and mild temperature elevation. **But true fever (above 38°C / 100.4°F) is not caused by teething.** If your baby has a real fever, it's from something else — don't dismiss it as "just teething." [Source: src22, src23]

For pain relief: a cool teething ring or clean washcloth to chew on, and weight-appropriate acetaminophen (for babies 2 months and older) or ibuprofen (over 6 months only) with your pediatrician's guidance. **Avoid** teething tablets with belladonna, gels with benzocaine (the FDA has warned against both), and amber teething necklaces (choking and strangulation risk, no proven benefit). [Source: src22]

### Feeding evolution

**Breastfeeding:** By now, you've moved past the steep learning curve of the first weeks. Supply is regulated by demand — your body has learned how much your baby needs. Cluster feeding, those marathon evening sessions, typically improves by 3–4 months. Feeding frequency gradually shifts from every 1–3 hours to every 2–4 hours as your baby gets more efficient. [Source: src28]

During growth spurts, your baby may temporarily want to feed constantly again. This isn't a sign that your supply has dropped — it's your baby placing an order for more. A few days of increased demand tells your body to increase production. The system works. [Source: src17, src28]

**Formula feeding:** Amounts increase gradually: from about 90–120 mL (3–4 oz) per feeding in the first month, to roughly 180–240 mL (6–8 oz) per feeding at 4–5 feedings per day by 6 months. A useful rule of thumb: approximately 75 mL (2.5 oz) per day per pound of body weight. By 2–4 months (or when your baby weighs more than 5.4 kg / 12 lbs), many formula-fed babies begin to need fewer nighttime feeds — but whether to drop night feeds entirely should be guided by your pediatrician based on your baby's individual growth. [Source: src14]

**Signs of formula intolerance:** Excessive spitting up, persistent fussiness after feeds, blood or mucus in stool, or skin rashes (hives, eczema flares). Cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA) affects up to 3% of infants. If your pediatrician suspects intolerance, they may recommend a hydrolyzed ("hypoallergenic") formula. Symptoms typically resolve within 4–6 weeks of switching. [Source: src32]

**Vitamin D:** All breastfed and partially breastfed infants need 400 IU of vitamin D daily, starting in the first few days of life. Breast milk alone doesn't provide enough. Formula-fed babies consuming about 1 liter per day of fortified formula get adequate vitamin D from the formula itself. Here's the uncomfortable truth: only 27% of infants meet supplementation guidelines. If you've been forgetting the drops, you're in the majority — but start now. [Source: src38, src39]

**Introducing solids (~6 months):** Most major guidelines agree: introduce complementary foods at approximately 6 months (not before 4 months). But age alone isn't the deciding factor — look for readiness signs:

- Sits up alone or with support and controls head and neck
- Has lost the tongue-thrust reflex (no longer pushes food out with their tongue)
- Has roughly doubled their birth weight (at least about 6 kg / 13 lbs)
- Shows interest in food — watches intently, opens mouth when food approaches
- Can swallow food rather than pushing it back out

[Source: src34, src35]

**Allergen introduction — the paradigm shift:** For decades, parents were told to delay allergenic foods. The evidence now says the opposite. The LEAP study demonstrated an **81% relative risk reduction** in peanut allergy when peanut was introduced early (4–11 months) compared to delayed introduction (5 years) in at-risk infants. Since these guidelines were published, peanut allergy rates have dropped 27%. [Source: src36, src37]

The current approach: introduce common allergens (peanut, egg, dairy, sesame) early, not late. For high-risk babies (severe eczema or egg allergy), peanut can be introduced as early as 4–6 months after other solids are tolerated. For moderate-risk babies (mild to moderate eczema), around 6 months. There is no evidence that delaying allergenic foods prevents allergies — waiting longer may actually increase the risk. [Source: src36]

### Sleep

**The landscape:** By 3–4 months, sleep begins consolidating into longer stretches, especially at night. Total sleep drops slightly to 12–16 hours per day (including naps). By 5–6 months, many babies sleep through the night — though "many" is not "all," and "through the night" often means a 6-hour stretch, not the 8 you're dreaming of. [Source: src40]

**The 4-month sleep regression:** If your baby was sleeping well and suddenly isn't, welcome to the most well-documented sleep disruption of infancy. Around 3–4 months, your baby's sleep architecture permanently matures — they develop lighter sleep stages that they didn't have before. The result: shorter naps, more night wakings, difficulty falling asleep, and general overtiredness for everyone.

It lasts 2–6 weeks. It's not all babies — there's considerable individual variation. And it's not actually a regression — it's a one-time developmental change. Their sleep is growing up. It feels like a setback, but it's progress. [Source: src41]

**Safe sleep updates for this age:**

- **Stop swaddling** as soon as your baby shows signs of attempting to roll — typically around 3–4 months, but possibly earlier. A swaddled baby who rolls to their stomach is at increased risk of suffocation. Wearable sleep sacks that restrict arm movement should also stop. Sleep sacks that allow free arm movement are fine to continue. [Source: src42]
- All other safe sleep rules from the first month still apply: on their back, on a firm flat surface, nothing else in the crib, room sharing (not bed sharing) recommended for at least the first 6 months. [Source: src27]
- Pacifiers at nap and bedtime may reduce SIDS risk. [Source: src27]

**About sleep training:** There is insufficient evidence to support behavioral sleep interventions (like "cry it out") for infants under 6 months, and mainstream guidelines do not recommend them at this age. Young infants should not be left to cry themselves to sleep. If you're considering sleep training, the evidence-supported window begins at 6 months, and we'll cover it in the next article. For now, respond to your baby. That's the right call. [Source: src43, src44]

### Vaccinations

Your baby has three major vaccination visits in this period: at 2 months, 4 months, and 6 months. Here's the 2025 CDC/AAP recommended schedule:

| Vaccine | 2 months | 4 months | 6 months |
|---------|----------|----------|----------|
| Hepatitis B (HepB) | 2nd dose | — | 3rd dose |
| Rotavirus (RV) | 1st dose | 2nd dose | 3rd dose* |
| DTaP (diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis) | 1st dose | 2nd dose | 3rd dose |
| Hib (Haemophilus influenzae type b) | 1st dose | 2nd dose | 3rd dose** |
| PCV (pneumococcal conjugate) | 1st dose | 2nd dose | 3rd dose |
| IPV (inactivated poliovirus) | 1st dose | 2nd dose | 3rd dose |

*Rotarix (RV1) is a 2-dose series; RotaTeq (RV5) is a 3-dose series.
**Hib schedule varies by product (3 or 4 dose series). [Source: src24, src25]

**Common reactions:** Soreness, redness, and swelling at the injection site are the most common. About 25% of babies develop a low-grade fever after DTaP, lasting 24–48 hours. Mild drowsiness (~30%), fussiness (~30%), and decreased appetite (~10%) are also typical. These side effects usually resolve within a couple of days. A lukewarm sponge bath, extra fluids, and acetaminophen (with your pediatrician's guidance) can help. [Source: src26]

**One reassuring note:** Evidence suggests that infant vaccinations may actually help protect against SIDS. [Source: src27]

## Part 2: You, the parent

The first month was about survival. These months are about starting to find yourself again — though that process is slower and messier than anyone prepares you for.

### Physical recovery

**Pelvic floor:** Recovery takes much longer than the initial healing from birth. Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) can begin in the immediate postpartum period and are worth doing consistently. A systematic review found that pelvic floor muscle training reduces urinary incontinence by 37% and pelvic organ prolapse by 56%. If you're experiencing incontinence, heaviness, or pelvic pressure, targeted exercises — ideally guided by a pelvic floor physiotherapist — can make a significant difference. [Source: src58, src59]

**Diastasis recti (abdominal separation):** A small separation typically recovers within 4–8 weeks. Larger separations may take 6–12 months and may require specialist physiotherapy. The key exercises are pelvic floor and deep core work — not sit-ups or crunches, which can make it worse. Avoid heavy lifting and movements that cause visible "doming" of the abdomen in early recovery. [Source: src60]

**Return to exercise:** After an uncomplicated vaginal delivery, ACOG says you can begin exercising within a few days — or as soon as you feel ready. After a cesarean or complications, wait for clearance. Start with 20–30 minutes per day of walking or gentle stretching, and gradually progress. [Source: src61]

**Postpartum hair loss:** Around 3–4 months after delivery, you may notice alarming amounts of hair coming out. This is telogen effluvium — falling estrogen levels cause a larger-than-normal proportion of hair to shift from growing to shedding phase (normally about 10% of hair is shedding; postpartum, this jumps to 30% or more). Shedding peaks around 4 months postpartum and can continue for up to 5 months. Most people regain normal hair fullness within 6–9 months after shedding stops — by the baby's first birthday, most have recovered fully. If hair hasn't regained fullness after a year, see your doctor. [Source: src62]

### Mental health

**This is not just a first-month concern.** The first 6 months after delivery is a high-risk period for depression. PPD affects 10–20% of postpartum individuals, and here's the statistic that should change how we screen for it: **50% of cases go undiagnosed.** [Source: src55]

**Postpartum anxiety (PPA)** affects about 14% of postpartum individuals and can occur alongside depression or on its own. Symptoms include excessive worry, racing thoughts, inability to sit still, difficulty sleeping even when the baby sleeps, physical symptoms (dizziness, nausea, heart racing), and intrusive thoughts about harm coming to your baby. ACOG recommends screening with validated tools (EPDS, GAD-7, PHQ-9) at prenatal and postpartum visits. [Source: src56]

**Paternal and partner depression:** This is real, it is underrecognized, and it matters. A comprehensive meta-analysis found depression in 8–10% of fathers in the first year postpartum — with rates of about 9% in the first month, 8% at 1–3 months, and 9% at 3–6 months. Risk factors include unemployment, low social support, financial strain, history of mental illness, and — notably — when the other parent has PPD, the risk increases significantly. The National Perinatal Association recommends screening partners at least twice in the first year. [Source: src57]

**When to seek help:** If sadness, anxiety, or emotional numbness persists beyond two weeks; if you're having difficulty bonding with your baby, functioning daily, eating, or sleeping (beyond normal newborn-related sleep disruption); if you're withdrawing from people you normally lean on; if irritability feels out of proportion; or if you're having thoughts that you or your family would be better off without you. These are not signs of failure. They're signs of a medical condition that responds well to treatment. Tell someone — your partner, a friend, your doctor. [Source: src55]

### Return to work

If you're returning to work during this period, here's what the research says and what might help:

**Breastfeeding impact:** Women returning to full-time work within 3 months have significantly lower rates of breastfeeding at 12 months (12%) compared to those who don't return to work (34%). Part-time return falls in between (20%). This isn't a judgment — it's data to help you plan. [Source: src30]

**Practical steps:** Start pumping practice and bottle introduction a few weeks before your return date. Build a frozen supply. In the US, the PUMP Act requires employers to provide break time and a private space (not a bathroom) for expressing milk for 1 year after birth. [Source: src29]

**Childcare transitions:** Research shows that parents report receiving surprisingly little support during the transition to childcare. Gradual transitions — short visits that lengthen over time — are recommended. Before about 6 months, most babies adapt relatively easily to new caregivers as long as care is consistent and responsive. The more robust separation anxiety typically emerges around 9 months. [Source: src51, src63]

**Guilt is normal.** Whether you're returning to work because you need to, because you want to, or some complicated mixture of both — the guilt is almost universal, and it doesn't mean you're making the wrong choice. Children thrive in quality childcare. They also thrive with a parent who has meaningful work and a sense of identity beyond caregiving. There is no single right answer here.

### Identity and relationships

**With your partner:** The data hasn't changed since the first month — the transition to parenthood puts real stress on relationships. Meta-analyses show significant declines in relationship satisfaction from pregnancy through the first year postpartum. The biggest predictor remains violated expectations about division of labor: when one partner ends up doing significantly more childcare and housework than they expected, satisfaction drops. The stronger and more important the pre-birth expectations, the more negative the impact when they aren't met. [Source: src64, src65]

One finding worth knowing: a study on early parenthood found that when a parent experiences joy and delight in their child, it positively influences not just their own relationship satisfaction but their partner's as well. The good moments buffer the hard ones. [Source: src66]

**If you have older children:** Regression is common and normal — toilet training accidents, wanting a bottle, baby talk, increased clinginess. This is how children process the seismic shift of no longer being the youngest. Don't punish it. Give them dedicated one-on-one time. Involve them in baby care in age-appropriate ways. Praise the grown-up behavior you want to see. [Source: src68]

**Your identity:** Parenthood reorganizes your sense of self. Research links parental identity and psychological need satisfaction — when parents feel competent and connected in their new role, relationship satisfaction and well-being are better preserved. If you're struggling with the identity shift, that's not a character flaw. It's a developmental transition on par with adolescence, happening in a culture that barely acknowledges it. [Source: src67]

## Part 3: When to call the doctor

### For the baby

Call the same day — or go to the emergency department — if your baby has any of the following:

- **Fever:** Rectal temperature at or above 38°C (100.4°F). **Under 3 months, fever is a medical emergency — go to the emergency department immediately.** Between 3–6 months, call your doctor the same day.
- **Breathing difficulty:** Persistent fast breathing, pauses longer than 10 seconds, skin pulling in around the ribs or at the neck, nasal flaring, any blue or dusky color around the lips or face.
- **Feeding refusal:** Skipping 2 or more consecutive feedings, or consistently taking much less than usual.
- **Dehydration signs:** Fewer than 6 wet diapers per day (after the first week). No wet diaper for 8 or more hours. Dry mouth. Sunken soft spot.
- **Vomiting:** Forceful or projectile vomiting (not the same as spit-up). Any green (bilious) vomit.
- **Stool changes:** Blood or mucus in stool. White or clay-colored stool.
- **Inconsolable crying:** More than 3 hours, not responding to any soothing.
- **Lethargy:** Unusually difficult to wake, floppy, weak cry.
- **Eyes:** Consistently crossed or misaligned after 3–4 months. White or cloudy pupil. Eyes not following a moving object by 3 months.
- **Developmental concerns:** No social smile by end of 2 months. No cooing by 3–4 months. Not turning toward sounds by 4 months. Not reaching for objects by 5 months. No babbling by 6 months.
- **"Something seems off"** — you still don't need a specific symptom. Your instinct is valid.

[Sources: src1, src4, src7, src11, src22]

### For you

Call your doctor if you experience:

- Baby blues lasting beyond 2 weeks, or getting worse instead of better
- Persistent sadness, anxiety, or emotional numbness that interferes with daily functioning
- Difficulty bonding with the baby
- Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby
- Racing thoughts, panic, inability to sleep even when the baby is sleeping
- Heavy or worsening postpartum bleeding
- Fever above 38°C (100.4°F)
- Breast pain with redness, warmth, or fever (possible mastitis)
- Incontinence or pelvic pain that isn't improving
- Hair loss that hasn't resolved by the baby's first birthday
- Feeling detached, numb, or unable to care for yourself or the baby

Partners: the above applies to you too. You can develop depression and anxiety regardless of whether you gave birth. [Source: src55, src56, src57]

## One last thing

Somewhere between the first month and the sixth, you stop being someone who just had a baby and start being someone who is raising a child. The shift is gradual enough that you might not notice it happening — until one day you realize you handled something (a blowout, a 3am feeding, a stranger's unsolicited opinion) with a competence that would have felt impossible eight weeks ago.

You still won't have it figured out. The baby will change again — new sleep pattern, new feeding challenge, new developmental leap that rearranges everything you just got used to. That's the deal. The rhythm you find is never permanent. But you're getting better at finding new ones.

Some days will be genuinely wonderful. Your baby will laugh for the first time, and it will be the best sound you've ever heard. They'll reach for your face with both hands. They'll fall asleep on your chest and you'll sit very still, not because you have to, but because you don't want to move.

Other days will be hard — the teething, the sleep regression, the relentlessness of it, the feeling that you're losing yourself in the service of someone who can't even say thank you yet.

Both are real. Both are these months.

And the fact that you're still here, still reading, still trying to learn how to do this well — that tells us plenty about the kind of parent you are.

You're doing better than you think.
