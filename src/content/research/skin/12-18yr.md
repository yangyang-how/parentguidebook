## Research Brief

Adolescent skin health is dominated by acne vulgaris, which affects the vast majority of teenagers and has significant psychological impact. Evidence-based treatment follows a stepped approach from topical retinoids and benzoyl peroxide to oral antibiotics and isotretinoin for severe cases. Sun safety and tanning bed avoidance are critical — 60% of teens think tanned people look better, and indoor tanning dramatically increases melanoma risk. Body image concerns, skin-related self-esteem, and the psychosocial burden of visible skin conditions are central themes for this age group.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Acne that causes scarring or significant psychological distress warrants early specialist referral — don't delay
- Isotretinoin requires pregnancy prevention due to severe teratogenicity — counseling is mandatory
- Never use antibiotics alone for acne — always pair with benzoyl peroxide to prevent resistance
- Indoor tanning beds should be strongly discouraged — many states now ban or restrict teen access
- Acne can be an early sign of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) in adolescent girls — consider if irregular periods present
- Social media pressure around "perfect skin" can worsen the psychological impact of acne
- Some acne treatments increase sun sensitivity (retinoids, doxycycline) — sun protection is even more important
