# Food Allergies in Children — Early Introduction, Recognition, and Emergency Response

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — the science on when to introduce allergens has reversed, and knowing how to recognize a severe reaction can save your child's life.**

**The short version:** About 1 in 13 children has a food allergy. [Source: src4] The biggest shift in allergy science in the past decade: introducing common allergens **early** (around 6 months) dramatically reduces the risk of developing allergies — the old advice to delay was wrong. If your child does have a food allergy, recognizing the signs of anaphylaxis and knowing how to use an epinephrine auto-injector are essential skills for every caregiver.

**Key takeaways:**
- Early introduction of peanut and egg (around 6 months) **reduces** allergy risk — the LEAP trial showed an 81% reduction [Source: src1]
- The old advice to delay allergens has been completely reversed [Source: src2]
- Anaphylaxis requires immediate epinephrine — every minute of delay reduces survival odds [Source: src4]
- Regular, ongoing exposure after introduction is necessary to maintain tolerance [Source: src3]

---

## The science has changed — introduce allergens early

If you grew up hearing that babies should avoid peanuts, eggs, and other common allergens until age 1, 2, or even 3, that advice has been completely reversed based on strong evidence. [Source: src2]

### The LEAP trial: the study that changed everything

The landmark LEAP (Learning Early About Peanut Allergy) trial enrolled over 600 infants who were at high risk for peanut allergy — those with severe eczema, egg allergy, or both. Half were randomized to consume peanut-containing food regularly starting between 4–11 months of age; the other half avoided peanut entirely.

The results were striking: infants who ate peanut early had an **81% lower rate of peanut allergy** by age 5 compared to those who avoided it. [Source: src1] Follow-up data showed that this protection persisted — children who consumed peanut early continued to show tolerance through 12 years of age. [Source: src1]

### It's not just peanuts

Multiple additional studies (LEAP-On, EAT) have confirmed that early introduction works for multiple allergens, including egg. [Source: src3] And the evidence goes further: **delaying introduction of allergenic foods beyond 6–12 months may actually increase allergy risk** rather than decrease it. [Source: src3]

The old approach — avoiding allergens to prevent allergies — did the opposite of what was intended.

### Current guidelines (2024–2025)

The AAP and other major medical organizations now recommend:

- **Introduce peanut and egg around 6 months of age** (not before 4 months)
- **Introduce other common allergens** (milk, wheat, soy, tree nuts, fish, shellfish, sesame) around the same time
- **Pre-screening with skin or blood tests before introduction is no longer recommended** for most babies
- **Early introduction is especially important** for babies with eczema or a family history of food allergy — these children are at higher risk and benefit most from early exposure

[Source: src2]

**Real-world impact:** Since early introduction guidelines were adopted, emerging data suggest peanut allergy diagnoses have begun to decline. [Source: src2] The science works.

## How to introduce allergens safely

### Step-by-step approach

1. **Start when your baby is already eating some solid foods** — they should be tolerating at least a few non-allergenic foods comfortably before you introduce allergens.

2. **Choose a time when you can observe your baby** for at least 10–15 minutes after the first taste. Do this at home, during the day, when your baby is healthy.

3. **Offer a small amount of one allergenic food:**
   - **Peanut:** Mix a thin layer of smooth peanut butter into a puree your baby already likes, or dissolve peanut butter powder in breast milk/formula. Never give whole peanuts or chunks — these are choking hazards.
   - **Egg:** Offer a small amount of well-cooked scrambled egg.
   - **Dairy:** A small amount of plain yogurt or a thin smear of cheese.
   - **Wheat:** Offer wheat-based infant cereal or soft cooked pasta.

4. **Watch and wait** 10–15 minutes before offering more.

5. **Introduce one new allergen at a time**, with 3–5 days between new allergens. This way, if a reaction occurs, you know which food caused it.

6. **Once introduced, keep offering the food regularly.** Occasional exposure is what maintains tolerance. A one-time introduction followed by months of avoidance is not effective. [Source: src3]

### The big 9 allergens

These nine foods are responsible for the vast majority of food allergies: [Source: src4]

1. **Milk**
2. **Eggs**
3. **Peanuts**
4. **Tree nuts** (almonds, cashews, walnuts, etc.)
5. **Fish**
6. **Shellfish**
7. **Wheat**
8. **Soy**
9. **Sesame**

## Recognizing an allergic reaction

Allergic reactions to food typically occur within minutes to 2 hours after eating the trigger food. They range from mild to life-threatening.

### Mild to moderate reactions

- **Skin:** Hives (raised, red, itchy welts), redness, eczema flare, swelling around the mouth
- **Mouth/throat:** Itching or tingling in the mouth, mild lip swelling
- **Stomach:** Stomach pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
- **Nose:** Runny nose, sneezing

A mild reaction does not always mean a severe one will follow — but it does mean this food is one your child's immune system is reacting to. Contact your pediatrician to discuss next steps, which may include allergy testing and developing a management plan.

### Anaphylaxis — this is an emergency

**🔴 Signs of anaphylaxis (call emergency services immediately):**

- **Swelling of the tongue, lips, or throat** — any difficulty swallowing
- **Difficulty breathing** — wheezing, persistent coughing, shortness of breath
- **Voice changes** — hoarseness, high-pitched breathing sounds
- **Dizziness, feeling faint, or loss of consciousness**
- **Pale or blue skin** — check fingernails and gums in children with darker skin tones
- **Rapid drop in blood pressure** — the child may become limp or unresponsive
- **Multiple body systems involved** — for example, hives PLUS vomiting PLUS difficulty breathing

[Source: src4]

Anaphylaxis can progress rapidly. **Do not wait to see if it gets better.**

## Emergency response: what to do

If your child has a known food allergy and you carry epinephrine auto-injectors:

1. **Use epinephrine at the first sign of a severe reaction.** Do not wait. Do not give antihistamines first and "see if that works." Epinephrine is the only effective treatment for anaphylaxis. [Source: src4]

2. **Call emergency services** immediately after giving epinephrine — a second wave of symptoms (biphasic reaction) can occur hours later, and your child needs medical observation.

3. **Lay the child down** with legs elevated (unless they're having difficulty breathing, in which case let them sit up).

4. **Give a second dose of epinephrine** if symptoms don't improve within 5–10 minutes.

**Important:** Always carry **two** epinephrine auto-injectors. One may not be enough.

### Make sure everyone knows

Every person who cares for your child — babysitters, grandparents, teachers, coaches, friends' parents — should know:
- Which food(s) your child is allergic to
- What a reaction looks like
- Where the epinephrine auto-injectors are
- How to use them

Practicing with a trainer device removes the hesitation that delays treatment in an emergency.

## Living with a food allergy

### At home

- **Read every label, every time.** Ingredients change. A product that was safe last month may not be safe this month.
- **Learn the hidden names** for common allergens. Milk, for example, can appear as casein, whey, lactalbumin, and other names.
- **Establish safe zones** — designated allergen-free areas or utensils if needed for severe allergies.
- **Teach your child** about their allergy as early as they can understand. Even young children can learn "I can't eat that — it makes me sick."

### At school and social events

- **Provide a written allergy action plan** to the school, signed by your child's doctor.
- **Ensure epinephrine is available at school** — many schools require two auto-injectors on file.
- **Talk to other parents** before playdates and birthday parties. Most parents are happy to accommodate once they know.
- **Teach your child to ask** before accepting food from others.

### The emotional side

Having a food allergy — or parenting a child with one — can be isolating and anxiety-inducing. Food is social, and being excluded from shared meals, birthday cakes, and holiday traditions is hard for children. Acknowledge those feelings. Help your child develop strategies for navigating social situations with confidence rather than fear.

If food allergy anxiety is significantly affecting your child's quality of life or your own, a therapist who specializes in chronic health conditions can help.

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Call emergency services immediately:**
- Any signs of anaphylaxis — breathing difficulty, throat swelling, loss of consciousness, multiple body systems involved [Source: src4]
- Use epinephrine first, then call

**🔴 Call your doctor promptly:**
- Any allergic reaction in a baby or child who has never had one before
- Hives, swelling, or vomiting after eating a new food

**🟡 Mention at your next pediatric visit:**
- Family history of food allergies and questions about when to introduce allergens
- Mild reactions (rash, stomach upset) after specific foods
- Your child's eczema is severe and you want guidance on allergen introduction
- Questions about allergy testing, outgrowing allergies, or new treatments

> **A note on outgrowing allergies:** Many children outgrow milk, egg, wheat, and soy allergies by school age. Peanut, tree nut, fish, and shellfish allergies tend to be more persistent — but even some of these can be outgrown. Your allergist can help you determine when it's appropriate to retest and, if safe, attempt a supervised oral food challenge.
