## Research Brief

Building a support network during pregnancy is one of the most protective investments a family can make. This article covers prenatal classes as community builders, finding parent communities before birth, building a support network, the role of doulas, and cultural birth practices that create community support.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Build Your Village Before the Baby Comes
- Key points: Prenatal network building, second trimester timing, four key components
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: The time to build your support network is before you need it desperately

### Section 2: Prenatal Classes as Community
- Key points: Education + friendship, connections persist for years, social support predicts lower PPD
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Go to the class for the information. Stay for the friendships.

### Section 3: Doulas and Birth Support
- Key points: Improved outcomes, emotional/physical/informational support, community doula programs
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: A doula is not a luxury — it's evidence-based support that improves outcomes

### Section 4: Cultural Practices as Community Support
- Key points: 坐月子, cuarentena, cultural frameworks provide structured support, integrate with evidence
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Your culture probably has a postpartum support tradition for a reason. Honor it AND update it.

### Section 5: Finding Resources
- Key points: Community resources, online communities (curated > open), awareness gap
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: There are more resources available than you know. Ask your doctor, your library, and 211.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- 坐月子 provides structured community support — this should be respected and leveraged as a strength
- Chinese immigrant families may lose their birth community support — building new networks is critical
- WeChat groups for Chinese expecting parents are a major support resource — acknowledge but note misinformation risk
- Doula culture is less established in Chinese communities — introduce the concept
- Cultural practices should be integrated with evidence, not replaced by it
