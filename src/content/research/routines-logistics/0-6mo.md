## Research Brief

The first six months are logistical chaos — feeding schedules (or lack thereof), sleep routines, diaper/bathing logistics, pediatrician visit schedules, and managing visitors all compete for a parent's depleted cognitive resources. This article covers the feeding schedule vs on-demand debate, early sleep routines, daily care logistics, the well-visit schedule, and managing the flood of visitors and "helpers."

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Feeding — Schedule or On-Demand?
- Key points: On-demand for newborns, patterns emerge by 3-4 months, responsive feeding builds trust
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Watch the baby, not the clock. The schedule will come.

### Section 2: Sleep Logistics
- Key points: 14-17 hours in short stretches, routines from 6-8 weeks, parent sleep management
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: You can start a bedtime routine at 8 weeks. You can't start sleep training until 4-6 months. There's a difference.

### Section 3: The Care Routine as Connection
- Key points: Diapering/bathing are learning moments, narrate routines, bath 2-3x/week
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Every diaper change is a conversation. Every bath is a bonding session.

### Section 4: The Visit Schedule
- Key points: 5 visits in 6 months, bring questions, vaccinations and screening
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Write your questions down before the appointment. You will forget them at 2 AM.

### Section 5: Managing Visitors and the Mental Load
- Key points: Visitor boundaries, helpful vs unhelpful helpers, mental load sharing
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: The best visitor brings a casserole and does the dishes. The worst holds the baby and gives advice.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese families, 坐月子 provides a structured first-month routine — acknowledge its value while noting where it may conflict with AAP guidance
- Grandparent visitors in Chinese families may stay for months — different boundary dynamics than Western drop-in visitors
- Mental load distribution is especially challenging in Chinese families where gender roles may be more traditional
- On-demand feeding may conflict with Chinese scheduling preferences — present the evidence clearly
