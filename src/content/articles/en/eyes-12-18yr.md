# Your Teen's Eyes — Myopia, Screens, and Taking Charge of Eye Health

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — myopia often progresses during the teen years, and slowing it down now prevents serious problems later.**

**The short version:** Adolescence brings unique eye health challenges: myopia (nearsightedness) often worsens during the growth spurt years, heavy screen use causes digital eye strain in over 60% of teens, and contact lens hygiene becomes a real concern. The good news is that evidence-based treatments can slow myopia progression, and most screen-related symptoms are manageable.

**Key points:**
- Myopia is considered a global epidemic — and it often progresses fastest during the teen years [Source: src2]
- Over 60% of adolescents experience digital eye strain from device use [Source: src7]
- Blue-light-blocking glasses have no proven benefit for eye health (the AAO does not recommend them) [Source: src8]
- 80–120 minutes of outdoor time daily is protective against myopia [Source: src4]

**When to worry:** Squinting at distant objects. Headaches after screen use. Needing to sit closer to see the board. Rapid changes in prescription.

---

## Myopia: the teen epidemic

Myopia (nearsightedness) is now recognized as a high-priority cause of visual impairment by the AAO and AAPOS. It's not just about needing glasses — high myopia (worse than -6.00 diopters) significantly increases the lifetime risk of retinal detachment, glaucoma, cataracts, and other sight-threatening conditions. [Source: src2]

Myopia often first appears in childhood and frequently progresses during the teen years. Here's what many families don't realize: **myopia progression doesn't necessarily stop at 18.** Some teens continue to worsen through their early 20s. [Source: src5]

This is why slowing progression matters — it's not just about keeping the prescription manageable now, it's about reducing the risk of serious eye disease decades later.

### What drives myopia

Two modifiable factors stand out in the research:

**Near work:** Extended time spent reading, studying, or using screens at close range is a risk factor for myopia development and progression. The intensity and duration of near work both matter. [Source: src4]

**Lack of outdoor time:** This is the big one. Research consistently shows that 80–120 minutes of outdoor time per day is protective against myopia onset and progression. It's not about exercise specifically — it's about exposure to bright outdoor light, which appears to slow eye growth. [Source: src4]

> **For parents:** You can't always control how much homework your teen has. But you can encourage outdoor time and breaks from screens. These are the two levers within your control — and they're backed by strong evidence. [Source: src4]

## Slowing myopia down

If your teen's myopia is progressing, there are now several evidence-based interventions that can slow it:

**Low-dose atropine drops:** Applied once daily, low-dose atropine (typically 0.01–0.05%) can slow myopia progression. Side effects at low doses are minimal. [Source: src3]

**Orthokeratology (Ortho-K):** Special rigid contact lenses worn overnight that temporarily reshape the cornea, providing clear vision during the day without glasses or contacts. Also shown to slow myopia progression. Particularly popular with teen athletes. [Source: src3]

**MiSight and dual-focus contact lenses:** MiSight is the first FDA-approved contact lens specifically designed to slow myopia progression in children aged 7–16. Other dual-focus and extended-depth-of-focus lenses also have evidence supporting myopia control. [Source: src3]

**Outdoor time:** Not a treatment in the traditional sense, but 80–120 minutes daily outdoors is one of the most effective preventive measures and complements any of the above. [Source: src4]

**What about regular glasses?** Standard single-vision glasses or contacts correct your teen's vision but do nothing to slow progression. If myopia is worsening, ask your eye doctor about management options. [Source: src5]

> **Treatment should be individualized.** The right approach depends on your teen's rate of progression, lifestyle, and risk factors. Discuss options with a pediatric ophthalmologist or an optometrist experienced in myopia management. [Source: src5]

## Screen time and digital eye strain

Over 60% of adolescents experience digital eye strain (DES), with the risk notably higher among those using devices for more than 2 hours daily. [Source: src7]

**Common symptoms:**
- Headaches (the most common — reported by about 72% of affected teens) [Source: src11]
- Blurred vision [Source: src11]
- Tearing [Source: src11]
- Dry, tired eyes [Source: src6]
- Neck and shoulder pain [Source: src6]

### What actually helps

**The 20-20-20 rule:** Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet (6 meters) away for at least 20 seconds. This gives the focusing muscles in the eyes a break. [Source: src6]

**Proper ergonomics:** Screen at arm's length, slightly below eye level. Good room lighting (not working in the dark with a bright screen). [Source: src6]

**Reducing screen time before bed:** This matters — but for sleep, not for eye damage. Digital eye strain is associated with poor sleep quality in teens. [Source: src7]

### What doesn't help

**Blue-light-blocking glasses:** Despite heavy marketing, there is no scientific evidence that blue-light-blocking filters prevent digital eye strain or protect the eyes. The AAO does not recommend them. Screen light does not damage the eyes. [Source: src8]

Blue light does affect circadian rhythm — but the solution is reducing screen time before bed, not buying special glasses. [Source: src8]

## Contact lenses for teens

Many teens want to switch from glasses to contact lenses. This is fine — when they're mature enough to handle the responsibility.

**Daily disposable lenses** are the safest option for teens. No cleaning, no storage cases, no buildup of deposits. Put them in, take them out, throw them away. [Source: src3]

**The hygiene conversation matters.** Contact lens-related infections can be serious. The rules are non-negotiable:
- Wash hands before touching lenses
- Never sleep in lenses unless specifically prescribed for overnight wear
- Never swim or shower in lenses
- Replace lenses on schedule — no stretching
- If eyes are red, painful, or vision changes, remove lenses and see a doctor

> **For parents:** Teens tend to be overconfident about hygiene. Having a direct conversation about the risks — including the possibility of permanent vision damage from infection — is more effective than assuming they'll follow the instructions. [Source: src5]

## Allergies and nasal congestion

Allergic rhinitis affects up to 40% of adolescents and often overlaps with eye symptoms — itchy, watery, red eyes (allergic conjunctivitis). But untreated nasal congestion also has indirect effects: it can contribute to sleep-disordered breathing, fatigue, and poor academic performance. [Source: src9]

If your teen has chronic stuffiness, it's worth treating — not just for comfort, but because the downstream effects on sleep and concentration are real. Nasal corticosteroid sprays are the first-line treatment for moderate-to-severe allergic rhinitis. [Source: src9]

## Vision screening for teens

The AAP Bright Futures schedule recommends vision screening at ages 12 and 15. At this age, the focus shifts from detecting amblyopia (that window is largely closed) to catching uncorrected refractive errors — myopia that may have worsened, or new issues that have emerged. [Source: src1]

**Signs your teen may need an eye check:**
- Squinting at the board at school
- Holding phone or books very close
- Headaches, especially after reading or screen time
- Declining grades that might be related to not seeing clearly
- Complaints about blurry distance vision

If your teen already wears glasses or contacts, annual eye exams are recommended to track progression and update the prescription. [Source: src10]

## A note about teens taking ownership

Adolescence is when your child should begin transitioning toward managing their own health. For eye care, this means:
- Understanding their own prescription and what it means
- Knowing when their next appointment is
- Taking responsibility for contact lens hygiene
- Recognizing symptoms that need attention (not just ignoring headaches)

This transition doesn't happen overnight, and your teen still needs your support and oversight. But the goal is that by the time they leave home, they can manage their eye health independently. [Source: src5]
