## Research Brief

School age shifts the confidence challenge — parents must navigate homework battles, advocate for their child at school, decide when to push vs let go, and parent a child who is increasingly their own person (and may be very different from the parent). This article covers academic confidence, school advocacy, the push-pull balance, and parenting a child whose interests, temperament, or abilities differ from your own.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The Homework Question
- Key points: Structure and support not content, battles are about power, emotional support > subject knowledge
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: You don't need to know 4th-grade math. You need to sit nearby, show interest, and hand them a snack.

### Section 2: Advocating at School
- Key points: Know your rights, document concerns, request meetings, advocacy is learnable
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: If something's not right at school, your child needs you to speak up. Here's how.

### Section 3: When to Push, When to Let Go
- Key points: Encouragement vs pressure, autonomy respect, pushing beyond readiness backfires
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The hardest question in parenting: "Am I helping them grow or breaking their spirit?" The answer is in their face.

### Section 4: Parenting a Child Who's Different From You
- Key points: Temperament mismatch, adjusting approach, learning your child's actual nature
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Your bookworm child of two athletes is not a disappointment. They're themselves.

### Section 5: Growing Your Confidence
- Key points: New domains require new skills, acknowledging uncertainty, peer mentorship
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 7, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: "I don't know, but I'll find out" is the most honest and effective parenting sentence at this age

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese academic parenting culture places enormous pressure on parents to ensure academic success — confidence is directly tied to grades
- Immigrant Chinese parents may feel confident in Chinese education systems but lost in Western ones — specific navigation guidance is valuable
- The "push vs let go" dilemma is culturally loaded — Chinese culture generally defaults to push
- Parenting a child who doesn't share the parent's values (e.g., academic interest) is a significant source of Chinese family conflict
