# Bones & Movement at 2–5 Years — Running, Jumping, and First Fractures

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to Know — most musculoskeletal changes at this age are normal development, not problems.**

**The short version:** Between ages 2 and 5, children go from wobbly walkers to running, jumping, climbing, hopping machines. Knock-knees are a normal phase that peaks around ages 3–4 and resolves by 7–8. Falls are inevitable, and fractures are the fourth most common injury in children under 6 — but children's bones are flexible, so fractures tend to be incomplete (greenstick or buckle types) and heal in 3 weeks to 2 months. Calcium and vitamin D from food are the best investments in your child's growing skeleton.

**Key points:**
- Motor skills explode: running by 2, tricycle by 3, hopping on one foot by 4 [Source: src1]
- Knock-knees are physiologically normal from ages 3–7 [Source: src4]
- Fractures are the 4th most common injury in children under 6 [Source: src7]
- Children's fractures heal faster than adults' — typically 3 weeks to 2 months [Source: src5]
- Calcium needs: 700 mg/day (ages 1–3), 1,000 mg/day (ages 4–8) [Source: src10]

## The movement explosion

Something clicks between ages 2 and 5. Your child goes from cautious steps to full-speed chaos in what feels like overnight. This is normal — and it's one of the most dramatic developmental leaps in childhood.

**By age 2:** Most children can run, kick a ball, and walk up stairs with support. [Source: src1]

**By age 3:** Running becomes easy and fluid. Pedaling a tricycle is a new skill. Climbing stairs step by step, with one foot meeting the other on each step, is typical. [Source: src1, src3]

**By age 4:** Hopping on one foot appears. Catching a bounced ball becomes possible. Coordination improves noticeably — your child can run and dodge obstacles, change direction, and start to play simple chasing games. [Source: src1]

**By age 5:** Standing on one foot for 10 or more seconds, hopping, and possibly skipping. Stair climbing with alternating feet (one foot per step, like an adult) is the norm. [Source: src1, src3]

Speed, strength, and coordination all improve dramatically through the preschool years. [Source: src2] This is a period of constant physical practice — running, climbing, jumping off things, and testing the limits of what their bodies can do.

> **For parents:** These milestones describe what 75% or more of children can do by a given age. [Source: src1] If your child isn't there yet, it doesn't automatically signal a problem — but bring it up at the next well-child visit if you're concerned.

## Knock-knees — the new normal

Many parents notice their preschooler's legs look... off. The knees angle inward. The ankles seem too far apart. It looks wrong, but it's almost always normal.

Here's the typical leg alignment timeline: most toddlers start with mild bowlegs. By age 2, the legs straighten. Then from roughly age 3 to 7, the knees swing inward — this is **genu valgum**, or knock-knees. It peaks around ages 3–4, then gradually corrects. By age 7–8, most children achieve straight adult-type alignment. [Source: src4]

This is physiologic — it's how normal leg development works. No treatment is needed. No special shoes, no braces, no orthotics.

### When knock-knees are not normal

Talk to your doctor if you notice: [Source: src4]
- **Asymmetry** — one leg looks significantly different from the other
- **Pain** — knock-knees shouldn't hurt
- **Worsening after age 4** — the angle should be improving, not getting worse
- **Extreme severity** — if the gap between the ankles is large and seems to be growing

These could point to conditions like Blount's disease, rickets, or metabolic bone disease — uncommon, but worth ruling out.

## Falls and fractures — what parents need to know

Active preschoolers fall. A lot. Most falls result in nothing more than tears and a bruise. But sometimes a bone breaks — and fractures are the **fourth most common injury in children under age 6**. [Source: src7]

### Why children's fractures are different

Children's bones are not just smaller versions of adult bones. They're more flexible, more porous, and still growing. This flexibility means they often bend or buckle rather than snapping completely. [Source: src5]

**Greenstick fractures:** The bone bends and cracks on one side but doesn't break all the way through — like bending a green twig. [Source: src5]

**Buckle (torus) fractures:** The bone is compressed and buckles outward on one side. It's weakened but not completely broken. [Source: src5]

These incomplete fracture patterns are the most common types in young children. [Source: src5] The wrist (distal radius) is the single most common fracture site. [Source: src6]

### How fast do they heal?

Fast. The average recovery time for a pediatric fracture is **3 weeks to 2 months**. [Source: src5] Children's bones have a rich blood supply and active growth, which accelerates healing well beyond what adult bones can manage.

### Playground safety

US emergency rooms treat more than **200,000 children under age 14 every year** for playground-related injuries. [Source: src8] Monkey bars and climbing structures are common culprits. Falls are the primary mechanism. [Source: src6, src8]

This does not mean you should keep your child off the playground. Active play is essential for motor development, coordination, and bone strength. But a few practical steps help:

- **Surface matters.** Playgrounds with rubber matting, wood chips, or sand beneath equipment absorb falls better than concrete or packed dirt.
- **Height matters.** Equipment designed for preschoolers is lower to the ground for a reason.
- **Supervision matters.** A nearby adult can't prevent every fall, but can intervene before a risky climb or catch a child losing grip.

> **For parents:** A fracture in an active preschooler who fell off a climbing frame is a normal childhood event — it doesn't mean you did something wrong. Children's bones are built to heal from exactly these kinds of injuries.

## Feeding growing bones

The skeleton your child builds now matters for decades. Childhood is the critical period of bone accretion — the calcium deposited during these years determines adult bone mass, which is a significant predictor of fracture risk later in life. [Source: src12]

### Calcium

- **Ages 1–3:** 700 mg per day [Source: src10]
- **Ages 4–8:** 1,000 mg per day [Source: src10]

That translates to roughly 2–3 servings of dairy per day. [Source: src10] One cup of milk has about 300 mg of calcium, so two to three cups covers it for most children.

**If your child doesn't drink much milk:** Yogurt, cheese, calcium-fortified orange juice, fortified plant milks, tofu made with calcium sulfate, and leafy greens (like broccoli and kale) all contribute. [Source: src10]

Picky eating is extremely common at ages 2–5, which can make hitting calcium targets a challenge. Focus on variety and consistency rather than perfection at any single meal.

### Vitamin D

Children ages 1 and older need **600 IU of vitamin D per day**. [Source: src11] Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption — without enough of it, dietary calcium can't do its job.

Children who drink less than about 1 liter (roughly 4 cups) of vitamin D–fortified milk per day may need a supplement. [Source: src11] Talk to your pediatrician about whether supplementation makes sense for your child.

### Diet first, supplements second

The AAP recommends that food be the primary source of calcium and vitamin D, with supplements used only when dietary intake falls short. [Source: src9] A balanced diet that includes dairy (or fortified alternatives), fruits, vegetables, and protein will cover most children's bone-building needs.

## When to talk to your doctor

**🟡 Schedule an appointment if you notice:**
- Knock-knees that are worsening after age 4 or persist past age 7–8 [Source: src4]
- One leg looking significantly different from the other (asymmetric alignment) [Source: src4]
- Your child limps persistently or refuses to use a limb after a fall [Source: src5]
- Repeated fractures from minor falls — this can occasionally point to underlying bone conditions
- Your child seems significantly behind peers in motor milestones (not running by 3, not climbing stairs by 4) [Source: src1]
- Pain in the legs, knees, or hips that doesn't resolve with rest

**🔴 Seek immediate care if:**
- A limb looks deformed, swollen, or your child cannot move it after a fall
- Your child cannot bear weight on a leg after an injury
- You see bone pushing against or breaking through the skin

### A note about growth plates

Children's bones have growth plates (physes) — areas of developing cartilage near the ends of long bones. A fracture that involves a growth plate can potentially affect future bone growth if not properly treated. [Source: src6] If your doctor suspects a growth plate injury, imaging and follow-up are important — don't skip the follow-up appointment even if your child seems fine.
