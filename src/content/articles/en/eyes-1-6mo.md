# Your Baby's Eyes at 1–6 Months — Tracking, Color, and What to Watch For

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — early detection of eye problems changes outcomes.**

**The short version:** Between 1 and 6 months, your baby's vision undergoes rapid development. They go from barely focusing to tracking objects, recognizing faces, and beginning to see in color. This is also the window when serious but treatable conditions — strabismus, amblyopia, congenital cataracts — are best caught.

**Key milestones at a glance:**
- **6–8 weeks:** Should make eye contact and begin to fix gaze on faces [Source: src5]
- **2–3 months:** Starts following moving objects with their eyes [Source: src3]
- **4–5 months:** Color vision improves significantly; depth perception begins [Source: src3]
- **6 months:** Eye movements are faster and more accurate; color vision near-complete [Source: src3]

**When to worry:** No eye contact by 8 weeks. Eyes still crossing after 3–4 months. A white spot in the pupil (any age). One eye always turning in or out.

---

## How vision develops from 1 to 6 months

Your baby is building their visual system from the ground up. At 1 month, they see the world in blurry patches of light and dark. By 6 months, they can track a toy across the room, distinguish your face from a stranger's, and see nearly the full range of colors you can. [Source: src3]

This isn't passive — their brain is actively wiring new connections between the eyes and the visual cortex every day. What you do matters: talking to your baby, showing them colorful objects, and giving them things to reach for all feed this process. [Source: src4]

## Month by month: what to expect

### 6–8 weeks: Eye contact

This is a big one. By about 6 to 8 weeks, your baby should be making eye contact with you — fixing their gaze on your face, especially during feeding. If your baby doesn't seem to look at your face or make eye contact by 8 weeks, mention it to your pediatrician. Poor eye contact at this age warrants further assessment. [Source: src5]

Fixation and tracking should also be improving — your baby may start to follow a slowly moving object or your face as it moves from side to side. Before 2 months, it's normal for one or both eyes to wander or deviate temporarily. [Source: src9]

### 2–3 months: Tracking and focus

Your baby can now follow a moving toy with their eyes more steadily. They're drawn to bright colors, bold patterns, and faces. This is when those first real smiles often appear — your baby is seeing you more clearly and responding. [Source: src3]

**What you can do:** Slowly move a colorful rattle from side to side, about 30 cm from your baby's face. Let them follow it with their eyes. Change direction occasionally. [Source: src4]

### 4–5 months: Color and depth

By 4 months, color vision has developed significantly — your baby can now tell apart similar shades, not just high-contrast patterns. Around 5 months, something remarkable happens: the brain starts combining the slightly different images from each eye into a single three-dimensional picture. This is when depth perception begins. [Source: src3]

**Important milestone:** By about 4 months, your baby's eyes should be moving together consistently. Occasional crossing before this was normal. After this, consistent crossing or one eye drifting is something to discuss with your doctor. [Source: src4]

### 6 months: Near-complete color vision

By 6 months, your baby's eye movements are faster and more accurate. Color vision is essentially fully developed. They can see objects at farther distances and focus well. Their visual system is now sophisticated enough to support the big physical milestones coming next — reaching, sitting, crawling. [Source: src3]

## What the doctor checks at well-baby visits

During your baby's routine checkups between 1 and 6 months, the doctor should be checking their eyes. Here's what they look for:

**Red reflex test:** The doctor shines a light into each eye with an ophthalmoscope. Both eyes should show an even, symmetric red glow — like the "red-eye" effect in photos. An absent, white, or asymmetric red reflex is a red flag for serious conditions including congenital cataracts, retinoblastoma, and high refractive errors. The AAP recommends this test at every well-child visit. [Sources: src1, src2]

**Fixation and following:** The doctor checks whether your baby can fix their gaze on an object (like a small toy or the doctor's face) and follow it as it moves. This assesses both visual acuity and eye muscle coordination. [Source: src1]

**External eye exam:** Looking at the eyes, eyelids, and tear ducts from the outside — checking for redness, swelling, discharge, or structural abnormalities. [Source: src1]

**Pupil response:** Checking that both pupils constrict equally when light is shone on them. [Source: src1]

> **For parents:** These checks take less than a minute and don't hurt. Most babies don't mind them at all. But they can catch life-threatening conditions early — which is why they matter so much. [Source: src2]

## Warning signs parents should watch for

Most eye variations in young babies are normal. But some signs need prompt attention:

**🔴 See a doctor within days:**
- **A white spot in the pupil** — could indicate retinoblastoma (a rare but treatable eye cancer) or congenital cataracts. This is always urgent. [Source: src4]
- **No eye contact by 8 weeks** — may indicate a vision or neurological problem. [Source: src5]
- **One eye that is always turned in or out** — intermittent crossing before 3–4 months can be normal, but constant deviation at any age is not. [Source: src9]

**🟡 Mention at next visit:**
- **Excessive tearing** — often a blocked tear duct, which usually resolves on its own but should be monitored. [Source: src4]
- **Extreme sensitivity to light** — may indicate elevated intraocular pressure. [Source: src4]
- **Red or encrusted eyelids** — may indicate infection. [Source: src4]

> **A note for parents of premature babies:** If your baby was born before 32 weeks or weighed less than 2,000g, they should already be in a retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) screening program. If not, ask your baby's medical team. Use your baby's corrected age when comparing to the milestones above. [Source: src7]

## Conditions detected in this window

The reason early screening matters so much is that several serious conditions are best caught — and most successfully treated — during the first 6 months.

**Strabismus (eye misalignment):** The most common cause of amblyopia in children under 3. If one eye consistently turns in, out, up, or down, the brain may start ignoring that eye's input, leading to permanent vision loss in that eye if untreated. Early detection dramatically improves outcomes. [Source: src6]

**Amblyopia ("lazy eye"):** Reduced vision in one eye because the brain favors the other. Amblyopia becomes increasingly difficult to treat after age 7–9. Treatment is simpler and more effective the earlier it starts. [Source: src7]

**Congenital cataracts:** A clouding of the eye's natural lens present at birth. If not detected early, cataracts can permanently obstruct vision development and lead to irreversible vision loss. [Source: src10]

**Retinoblastoma:** A rare childhood eye cancer with a 90% cure rate if detected early — but fatal without treatment. The red reflex test is the primary screening tool. [Source: src10]

## When to see an eye specialist

Your pediatrician handles routine screening, but some situations call for a referral to a pediatric ophthalmologist:

- An abnormal red reflex at any visit [Source: src2]
- Persistent eye crossing or deviation after 3–4 months [Source: src9]
- No fixation or following behavior by 3 months [Source: src5]
- Any family history of retinoblastoma, childhood cataracts, or significant eye disease [Source: src7]
- Your baby was premature and needs ROP follow-up [Source: src7]

Don't wait for a referral if something feels wrong. You can ask your pediatrician directly, or in some regions, self-refer to an ophthalmologist. Early is always better — weeks matter with these conditions.
