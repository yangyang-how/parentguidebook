## Research Brief

Newborn skin in the first month undergoes rapid adaptation to extrauterine life. The skin barrier is competent at birth in full-term neonates but continues maturing over the first 1-2 years. Common benign conditions include erythema toxicum neonatorum (30-70% of newborns), milia, miliaria, neonatal acne, cradle cap, and diaper dermatitis, while jaundice-related skin yellowing affects 60% of term and 80% of preterm infants.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Jaundice appearing within the first 24 hours is always pathological and requires urgent investigation
- Erythema toxicum can look alarming but is completely benign — important to reassure parents
- Skin of color may make jaundice harder to detect visually; bilirubin testing is more reliable
- Premature infants have significantly more fragile skin barriers and require extra gentle care
- Herpes simplex vesicles can mimic benign neonatal pustulosis — any grouped vesicles need urgent evaluation
- Vernix should not be aggressively removed; it provides antimicrobial and moisturizing benefits
