## Research Brief

Unintentional injury is the leading cause of death for children ages 1-17 in the United States — more than all diseases combined. Yet most childhood injuries are preventable through age-appropriate safety measures. This overview covers why injury prevention matters, the developmental basis for age-specific risks, the balance between supervision and childproofing, and the evidence that prevention programs work.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The Numbers That Matter
- Key points: #1 cause of child death, top causes by age, prevention works
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: This isn't about being paranoid — it's about knowing which dangers are real at which age

### Section 2: Developmental Risk — New Skills = New Dangers
- Key points: Motor milestones create predictable risks, children can't assess risk, environmental modification
- Supported by: Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Every time your child learns to do something new, a new category of danger opens

### Section 3: Supervision + Childproofing = Protection
- Key points: Both needed, environmental strategies more reliable, supervision graduated by age
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: You can't watch them every second. That's why you also change the environment.

### Section 4: The Big Three: Sleep, Cars, and Water
- Key points: 3,500 sleep deaths/year, car crashes #1 for 1-13, drowning #1 for 1-4
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 9, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: Safe sleep, car seats, and water supervision — these three things prevent the most deaths

### Section 5: Prevention Works
- Key points: 30-year decline in injury rates, proven interventions, product safety standards
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 4, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Childhood safety isn't about fear — it's about knowing the simple things that dramatically reduce risk

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese families may co-sleep more commonly — safe sleep messaging needs cultural sensitivity while being medically clear
- Product safety standards differ between US and China — imported products may not meet US standards
- Water safety is critical in both cultures — drowning risk is global
- In Chinese culture, grandparents as caregivers may not be up-to-date on current safety guidelines
- Cultural practices (bed-sharing, carrying in arms vs car seats) may conflict with evidence-based safety recommendations
