## Research Brief

From 6 months to 2 years, mobility transforms the safety landscape. Crawling, cruising, and walking open up an entire world of hazards: poisons, drowning, burns, choking, and furniture tip-overs become primary concerns. This article covers childproofing for mobile children, poison prevention, drowning prevention, burn and scald prevention, choking hazards, and furniture anchoring.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Mobility = Danger (Childproofing for Mobile Kids)
- Key points: Update childproofing at each milestone, gates/locks/covers, re-assess regularly
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: The moment your baby moves, your home needs to change

### Section 2: Poison Prevention
- Key points: Under-6s are 50% of exposures, lock everything, Poison Control number
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Program 1-800-222-1222 into your phone right now. You may need it at 2 AM.

### Section 3: Drowning Prevention
- Key points: #1 cause of death for 1-4, any standing water, touch supervision, swim lessons from 1
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: A toddler can drown in a bucket. Every water source in your home is a potential danger.

### Section 4: Burns, Choking, and Tip-Overs
- Key points: Scalding prevention, choking foods/objects, furniture anchoring, button batteries
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5, Source 6, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Hot coffee, a grape, and an unanchored dresser — these are the everyday objects that send toddlers to the ER

### Section 5: Windows and Falls
- Key points: Window guards, no climbable furniture near windows, ongoing vigilance
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Toddlers climb. Windows open. Install guards before both things happen at once.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese households, traditional medicine products and herbal supplements may be additional poison risks
- Hot water for tea is a common scalding risk in Chinese homes — specifically address
- Button batteries in Chinese-made toys and electronics may be less securely housed
- Grandparent medications are a major poison risk — address specifically for multi-generational households
- Furniture anchoring awareness is lower in some Chinese households
