## If you only read one thing, read this

Your child is not a baby anymore — and they will remind you of this loudly, frequently, and at inconvenient times. The preschool years are when a small human with big feelings learns to use words instead of screams, a toilet instead of a diaper, and a fork instead of their fist. It takes longer than you think. That's normal.

**Development:** Your child will go from about 300 words to thousands, from parallel play to real friendships, from needing you for everything to insisting they can do it themselves (they usually can't — yet). Every child moves at their own pace. The range of normal is enormous.

**Eating:** Picky eating peaks between ages 2 and 6. It takes 8–15 exposures to a new food before a child may accept it — most parents give up after 3–5. Keep offering, without pressure. Your job is to decide what, when, and where. Their job is to decide whether and how much. [Source: src1]

**Sleep:** They need 10–14 hours total (including naps). Most children drop their last nap between ages 3 and 5. A consistent bedtime routine is the single most evidence-backed sleep intervention. [Source: src2]

**Safety:** Drowning is the number one cause of death for children ages 1–4. Pool fencing, constant supervision, and swim lessons (starting as early as age 1) are your primary defenses — but no single measure is enough on its own. [Source: src3]

**Discipline:** The evidence on spanking is clear and overwhelming: it increases aggression, defiance, and mental health problems. It does not work. What does work: clear boundaries, natural consequences, and about four positive interactions for every correction. [Source: src4]

**You:** Parental burnout is a real, measurable condition — not a personal failure. It affects up to 9% of parents in Western countries. You cannot pour from an empty cup, and refilling it is not selfish. [Source: src5]

## Part 1: Your child right now

### Body

**Growth:** The explosive growth of infancy is over. Your child is now growing about 5–7 cm (2–3 inches) per year in height and gaining roughly 2 kg (4–5 lbs) per year. Steady tracking along their growth curve matters more than any single measurement. If they cross two major percentile lines on the growth chart — either direction — your pediatrician will want to investigate. [Source: src6]

**Vision:** Between ages 3 and 5, your child should have a vision screening to check for amblyopia ("lazy eye"), which affects 2–3% of children. This matters because treatment works best before age 7 — the earlier it's caught, the better the outcome. And there's something you can do right now that costs nothing: let them play outside. Children who spend 1–2 hours outdoors daily have significantly lower risk of developing myopia. One leading hypothesis is that bright natural light stimulates dopamine release in the retina, which may help the eye develop properly — though the precise mechanism is still being studied. [Source: src7]

**Teeth:** By roughly age 3, your child will have all 20 primary teeth. Dental caries (cavities) is the most common chronic disease of childhood — more common than asthma — with untreated decay in about 13% and overall caries experience in 23% of children ages 2–5. Prevention is straightforward: brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste (a rice-grain smear under age 3, a pea-sized amount from ages 3–6), and have your pediatrician or dentist apply fluoride varnish every 6 months. Children lack the dexterity to brush effectively on their own until about age 6–8, so you'll need to do it or supervise closely. [Source: src8]

Prolonged pacifier use beyond age 2–3 increases the risk of dental malocclusion (bite problems), and continued use during sleep beyond early infancy is associated with higher rates of ear infections. Thumb-sucking usually stops on its own between ages 2 and 4; if it persists past age 4, talk to your dentist. [Source: src8]

**Motor skills:** At 2, they run (somewhat chaotically) and kick a ball. At 3, they pedal a tricycle and climb well. At 4, they hop on one foot and catch a bounced ball. At 5, they skip, balance on one foot for 10 seconds, and throw overhand. Fine motor skills follow a similar progression: from scribbles (age 2) to circles (age 3) to recognizable human figures (age 4) to writing letters (age 5). [Source: src9]

A note on handedness: most children show a preference by the preschool years, and it continues strengthening through age 10. But a very early, strong hand preference — established before 18 months — especially with increased muscle tone on one side, is a red flag that warrants evaluation. [Source: src9]

**Common illnesses:** Your preschooler will get 6–8 colds per year. In daycare, up to 12. Each one can last 10–14 days, and the cough may linger for up to 6 weeks. This is normal. Their adenoids are at their largest between ages 3 and 5 and will naturally shrink afterward. If your child snores persistently, breathes through their mouth at rest, or has recurrent ear infections, mention it to your pediatrician — enlarged adenoids or tonsils may be the cause. [Source: src10]

**Growing pains:** About 24–37% of children ages 4–6 experience bilateral leg pains, usually in the evening or at night. Despite the name, there is no evidence these are caused by actual growth. They're real pain, but they're benign and self-limiting. Stretching, massage, and heat packs help. Red flags that suggest something else: pain that's one-sided, in a joint, accompanied by swelling, limping, fever, or weight loss. [Source: src10]

### Mind

**Language:** This is where the fireworks happen. At age 2, your child has roughly 200–300 words and is stringing together two-word combinations ("more milk," "no bath"). By age 3, they have about 1,000 words and are using 3–5 word sentences. By 5, they command 2,000–5,000 words and use adult-like grammar — all without a single lesson. They learn by listening to you. [Source: src11]

Grammatical errors like "I goed" or "two mouses" are actually signs of learning — your child has figured out the rules and is applying them logically, just not to the irregular exceptions yet. Don't correct them; simply model the correct form naturally ("Yes, you went to the park!"). [Source: src11]

**Stuttering:** Normal disfluency — repeating whole words ("I-I-I want that") or using lots of filler words — is common between 18 months and 7 years, especially when children are excited or tired. Up to 65–80% of children who stutter recover naturally, most within 15 months of onset. But watch for these signs of clinical stuttering: repeating parts of words ("w-w-want"), stretching sounds out ("ssssnake"), visible tension or struggle, or your child becoming frustrated or avoiding speaking. A family history of persistent stuttering, later onset (after age 3.5 combined with other risk factors), or stuttering lasting more than 6–12 months are reasons to seek evaluation. Early speech therapy makes a real difference. [Source: src11]

**Bilingual children:** If your family speaks more than one language — keep going. Bilingual children learn at comparable overall rates. Their vocabulary in each individual language may be smaller, but their total vocabulary across both languages is comparable to monolingual peers. Code-switching (mixing languages in a sentence) is not confusion; it's a sign of linguistic sophistication. [Source: src11]

**Cognitive leaps:** Between ages 4 and 5, something remarkable happens: your child develops theory of mind — the understanding that other people have thoughts, beliefs, and knowledge different from their own. Before this, they genuinely believe that if they know something, everyone knows it. After, they can understand that someone can hold a mistaken belief. This is also when deliberate lying becomes possible — which, counterintuitively, is a cognitive milestone. [Source: src12]

Magical thinking is characteristic of this age. Your child may believe their thoughts caused an event, that stuffed animals have feelings, or that wishing hard enough makes things real. This is normal — it's imagination under construction, not confusion. [Source: src12]

**Pre-literacy:** Shared reading (dialogic reading — where you ask questions, point to pictures, let them "read" to you) is the single strongest predictor of early literacy. By age 5, most children recognize several letters, can count to 10 or beyond, and may write their own name. But here's what the research actually says about school readiness: social-emotional skills — the ability to regulate emotions, follow directions, and interact with peers — are more predictive of success than knowing the alphabet. [Source: src12, src13]

### Emotions and behavior

**Tantrums:** They peak around age 3. In one study, 84% of children ages 3–5 had at least one tantrum in the past month. Only 4% had daily tantrums — that level may warrant evaluation. The decline happens because your child is developing better tools: more words, more emotional vocabulary, more self-regulation. [Source: src14]

Your child's emotional regulation develops in stages. At 2, they rely almost entirely on you to calm down. By 3–4, they start using their own strategies — distraction, self-talk, comfort objects. By 4–5, they can sometimes delay gratification and talk themselves through frustration. This is a years-long process, not a switch that flips. [Source: src14]

**Aggression:** Physical aggression — hitting, biting, pushing — is most common around ages 2–3 and declines as language develops. Biting is especially common in toddlers who don't have the words yet. This is normal. What's not normal: aggression that's getting worse over time, self-harm during tantrums, tantrums lasting more than 25 minutes regularly, or aggression toward both self and others (this last pattern is the strongest predictor of later behavioral disorders). [Source: src14]

**Fears:** Ages 2–4 bring fears of loud noises, animals, the dark, and monsters. Ages 4–6 add fears of "bad guys," being alone, and death. These are developmentally appropriate. The right response: validate ("I understand that's scary"), provide gentle exposure, model calm behavior. The wrong response: dismiss ("there's nothing to be scared of") or force confrontation. [Source: src15]

About 7–10% of preschoolers have anxiety that crosses from normal into clinical territory — anxiety that is excessive, persistent, and interferes with daily life. Separation Anxiety Disorder accounts for about half of childhood anxiety referrals. If your child avoids age-appropriate activities, has frequent physical symptoms (stomachaches, headaches) related to worry, or needs constant reassurance, talk to your pediatrician. Cognitive behavioral therapy is the gold-standard treatment and is effective even in preschoolers. [Source: src15]

### Social

Your child's social life follows a predictable arc. At age 2, they play beside other children, not with them (parallel play). Around 3–3.5, they start interacting during play (associative play). By 3.5–4.5, they begin cooperative play — shared goals, role assignment, real collaboration. [Source: src16]

**Sharing:** Expecting a 2-year-old to share is expecting something they are developmentally unable to do. "Mine" is their dominant concept, and it's supposed to be — they're establishing ownership before they can voluntarily give it up. By 3, sharing with support becomes possible. By 4–5, it becomes more consistent. Forced sharing backfires. Modeling and positive reinforcement work better. [Source: src16]

**Friendships:** By age 3, children drift toward preferred playmates. By 4–5, real friendships form based on shared interests. These early friendships are based more on proximity and shared activities than deep emotional bonds — but their quality predicts later social competence. [Source: src16]

### Sleep

**Total sleep needs:** Ages 1–2 need 11–14 hours per day (including naps). Ages 3–5 need 10–13 hours. [Source: src2]

**Nap transitions:** Most children drop from two naps to one around 12–18 months, and drop their last nap between ages 3 and 5. About half of 4-year-olds still nap; by 5, most don't. Signs they're ready to drop the nap: they sleep well at night without it, or they have difficulty falling asleep at naptime or bedtime. [Source: src2]

**Nightmares vs. night terrors:** Nightmares happen during REM sleep (second half of the night). Your child wakes up, remembers the dream, and can be comforted. Night terrors happen during deep non-REM sleep (first third of the night). Your child appears awake but isn't — they may scream and thrash, won't recognize you, and won't remember the episode. Night terrors affect 3–6% of children and peak between ages 3 and 7. Don't try to wake them — just keep them safe. [Source: src2]

**Bedtime resistance:** Common, normal, and maddening. A consistent bedtime routine is the single best tool: 3–4 calming activities (bath, brush teeth, read, sing) lasting 20–45 minutes in the same sequence every night. The evidence is dose-dependent — the more consistent the routine, the better the outcomes: earlier bedtime, shorter time to fall asleep, fewer night wakings, more total sleep, and benefits that extend beyond sleep to language, literacy, and emotional regulation. [Source: src2]

**Melatonin:** If sleep problems persist despite good sleep habits, talk to your pediatrician before reaching for melatonin. It's unregulated in the US, which means what's on the label may not match what's in the bottle. Most children who need it respond to a low dose (0.5–1 mg). There are no studies of melatonin in typically developing children, and no long-term safety data. It should only be considered after healthy sleep habits have been established and found insufficient. [Source: src2]

### Nutrition

**Picky eating:** This is the hill most parents of preschoolers are prepared to die on. Don't. Picky eating peaks between ages 2 and 6 and is evolutionarily adaptive — it protects newly mobile children from eating things that might poison them. The research is clear: it takes 8–15 exposures to a new food before acceptance, the exposures must involve actual tasting (not just looking), and they must be pressure-free. [Source: src1]

The most evidence-supported approach is Ellyn Satter's Division of Responsibility: the parent decides what food is offered, when, and where. The child decides whether to eat and how much. This model supports children's self-regulation of hunger, produces wider food acceptance long-term, and reduces mealtime conflict. Trust the model. Don't negotiate, bribe, or force. [Source: src1]

**When it's more than picky eating:** ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder) is different from typical pickiness. It doesn't improve with age and can result in weight loss, nutritional deficiencies, or significant functional impairment. Red flags: eating fewer than 20 foods, losing weight, severe anxiety around new foods, or nutritional deficiencies. The average delay between onset and diagnosis is 2 years. If this sounds like your child, bring it up with your pediatrician. [Source: src1]

**Key nutrients to watch:**
- **Iron:** 7 mg/day (ages 1–3), 10 mg/day (ages 4–8). Excess milk intake is the most common cause of iron deficiency in toddlers — it fills them up and displaces iron-rich foods. [Source: src17]
- **Calcium:** 700 mg/day (ages 1–3), 1,000 mg/day (ages 4–8). [Source: src17]
- **Vitamin D:** 600 IU/day for all children after age 1. Supplement if they drink less than 32 oz of milk per day. [Source: src17]

**Milk:** After age 2, talk to your pediatrician about transitioning to low-fat (1%) or skim milk — this is especially recommended for children at risk of overweight, though some guidelines support whole milk through age 5 for children at healthy weight. Limit to 2 cups per day (ages 2–3) or 2.5 cups per day (ages 4–5). More than that crowds out other important foods. [Source: src17]

**Juice:** Maximum 4 oz per day (ages 1–3), 4–6 oz per day (ages 4–6). Whole fruit is always better. No juice in bottles or sippy cups for sipping throughout the day. [Source: src17]

### Screen time

The AAP recommends no more than 1 hour per day of high-quality programming for children ages 2–5, and co-viewing whenever possible. Well-designed programs can genuinely improve cognitive, literacy, and social outcomes for children ages 3–5. But children still learn language best from live interactions with you. [Source: src18]

What matters as much as the clock: background TV reduces play quality, shortens attention spans, and impairs parent-child interaction — even when no one is actively watching it. Turn it off when it's not in use. [Source: src18]

The 2025 AAP update moves toward a more nuanced framework — the "5 C's" — considering the Child, Content, Context, Co-use, and Continual monitoring, rather than strict time limits alone. But for preschoolers, the 1-hour guideline remains a reasonable anchor. [Source: src18]

## Part 2: Developmental milestones

### Toilet training

There is no magic age. There is readiness.

Signs your child is ready: they stay dry for at least 2 hours, show interest in the toilet or underwear, can follow simple directions, can pull pants up and down, communicate the need to go, and have regular bowel movements. These signals typically appear between 18 and 24 months, but the US average for beginning training is between ages 2 and 3, with an average completion age of about 28.5 months. Girls typically train earlier than boys. [Source: src19]

**Methods:** The AAP recommends a child-oriented approach — start when readiness signs appear, follow your child's lead, and keep it unregimented. A more structured method (scheduled sitting, increased fluids, positive reinforcement) may produce faster results but can also produce more stress. There is no level-1 evidence proving one method superior. The one thing every expert agrees on: never punish accidents. Praise successes. [Source: src19]

**Nighttime dryness** is a separate developmental process. It requires brain-bladder maturity — specifically, sufficient ADH (antidiuretic hormone) production during sleep. About 85% of children are dry at night by age 5. But 15–20% of 5-year-olds still wet the bed, and this is not considered a clinical problem until age 5 at the earliest. Bedwetting resolves on its own at about 15% per year without treatment. It's 2–3 times more common in boys. Don't wake children at night to use the toilet, don't restrict fluids, and don't punish. [Source: src19]

**Regression** is common and usually triggered by stress: a new sibling, a move, illness, starting preschool. Return to a supportive, pressure-free approach. It's temporary. [Source: src19]

### Preschool and kindergarten readiness

The question most parents ask: "Is my child ready for school?" The question the research actually answers: "Can my child regulate their emotions, follow two-step directions, and interact positively with other children?"

Teachers consistently rank readiness factors as: physical nourishment and rest, verbal communication, enthusiasm and curiosity, ability to follow directions, and sensitivity to others' feelings — not academic knowledge. A child who can sit in circle time, manage frustration, and play cooperatively with peers is often better prepared than one who can read but can't do those things. [Source: src13]

Play-based preschool programs show equal or better long-term outcomes compared to academically focused ones. And "redshirting" — delaying kindergarten entry by a year — shows mixed to no benefit in the research. Any initial age advantage tends to fade by third grade. [Source: src13]

## Part 3: You, the parent

### Discipline

Let's address the most contentious topic first: **spanking.** The AAP's 2018 policy statement explicitly recommends against corporal punishment. The meta-analytic evidence is not ambiguous: 13 of 17 effect sizes linked spanking to increased risk of harmful outcomes. Spanking is associated with increased aggression, increased defiance (the opposite of its intended effect), increased risk of mental health disorders, and cognitive problems. The effect sizes do not substantially differ from those of physical abuse. Spanking teaches children that physical force is an acceptable way to solve problems. [Source: src4]

**What works instead:**

- **Authoritative parenting** — warm and firm — produces the best outcomes across studies: least conduct problems, highest prosocial behavior, best overall adjustment. This is not permissive (warm with no boundaries) or authoritarian (strict with no warmth). It's both, together. [Source: src4]
- **Natural consequences** — when safe and appropriate. Refusing a coat means feeling cold. The lesson sticks better than a lecture. [Source: src4]
- **Time-outs** — can work when done correctly (brief, 1 minute per year of age, boring but safe location, followed by reconnection), but research shows about half of preschoolers cannot explain why they were put in time-out. "Time-in" — staying with the child and co-regulating — is an emerging evidence-based alternative for younger preschoolers. [Source: src4]
- **Specific praise** — "You put your shoes on all by yourself!" is more effective than "Good job!" Aim for roughly 4 positive interactions for every correction. [Source: src4]

### Mental health

**Burnout** is not a buzzword. Parental burnout — emotional exhaustion in the parental role, emotional distancing from children, and loss of fulfillment — has been measured across 42 countries. Prevalence: about 5% globally, up to 9% in Western, individualistic cultures where intensive parenting norms dominate. Risk factors include perfectionism, high expectations, lack of support, and parenting alone. [Source: src5]

**Decision fatigue** is real. Parents — and the research consistently shows this falls disproportionately on mothers — carry an enormous invisible workload: scheduling, planning, remembering, coordinating, monitoring. This "cognitive labor" or "mental load" is linked to reduced life satisfaction, partner dissatisfaction, and role overload. [Source: src5]

**Social media comparison** makes it worse. Negative emotions from comparing yourself to other parents (envy, worry, inadequacy) correlate with higher burnout. The gap between feeling you should do everything and feeling you can't is one of the strongest correlates of parental burnout. [Source: src5]

**Protecting yourself:** Maintain your identity beyond parenthood. Keep at least one hobby, one friendship, one thing that is yours. Social support — online or offline — is associated with lower burnout. You are not being selfish. You are being functional. [Source: src5]

### Relationships

**With your partner:** The data has not changed since the first-month article. Most couples experience a decline in relationship satisfaction after becoming parents. The biggest predictor of trouble is still the perception of unfair division of labor. After the birth of a child, women take on 2 or more additional hours of work per day compared to about 40 extra minutes for men. The gap in division of labor is not present before birth — it emerges after. [Source: src20]

Father involvement in childcare strengthens couple satisfaction. Same-gender couples distribute cognitive labor more equitably on average. The strongest predictor of a mother's relationship satisfaction is not strict equality but the perception that the division is fair to both partners. [Source: src20]

Have the conversation. Revisit it regularly. And if regular dedicated couple time sounds impossible — even 15 minutes of genuine connection daily makes a measurable difference. Coparenting quality (working as a team) is distinct from relationship quality but strongly linked to it. [Source: src20]

**If you have or are expecting another child:** Children over age 2 show greater decreases in attachment security to parents after a new sibling arrives. Common reactions include regression, aggression toward the baby, and attention-seeking — but also surprising leaps in independence. Research does not support a "crisis model." Most firstborns adjust without lasting problems. What helps: individual attention for the older child, validating their feelings, avoiding comparison. [Source: src21]

## Part 4: Safety

### Drowning

Drowning is the number one cause of death for children ages 1–4 in the United States. It can happen in only a few inches of water — in buckets, bathtubs, wading pools — in under 2 minutes. It is silent — not the splashing and yelling you see in movies. [Source: src3]

**Prevention is layered — no single measure is enough:**
- **Pool fencing:** Four-sided isolation fencing around all pools is the single most effective prevention measure.
- **Supervision:** Constant, dedicated adult supervision. Not checking your phone by the pool. One designated adult, watching the water, within arm's reach for young children.
- **Swim lessons:** The AAP recommends starting at age 1 for many children. Formal lessons reduce drowning risk in children ages 1–4. But swim lessons do not "drown-proof" a child. They are one layer, not a guarantee.
- **CPR training:** Learn it. Hope you never use it.
- **Bathtubs:** Never leave a child unattended, even briefly.
- **Empty standing water** — buckets, wading pools, bathtubs — when not in use. [Source: src3]

### Poisoning

Approximately 3 million poisoning exposures occur per year in the US, many in children under 5. The most dangerous substances: medications (especially in grandparents' homes, which may not be childproofed), cleaning products, liquid nicotine, and lamp oil. Prevention: safety latches on cabinets, medications stored up high and locked, products in original labeled containers. Post the Poison Control number where you can see it: **1-800-222-1222** (US). [Source: src3]

### Car seats

Every transition to a less protective car seat increases injury risk. Keep your child in each stage as long as possible:
- **Rear-facing:** Until they exceed the weight or height limit of their seat (many allow rear-facing to 40+ lbs).
- **Forward-facing with harness:** From when they outgrow rear-facing through at least age 4 (many harness seats accommodate up to 65 lbs).
- **Booster seat:** From when they outgrow the harness through at least age 8 or until 4 feet 9 inches tall. [Source: src3]

## Part 5: When to call the doctor

### Developmental red flags

Talk to your pediatrician if your child:
- **By age 2:** Does not use at least 50 words. Does not combine two words ("want milk"). Does not follow simple instructions. Does not point to things or show interest in other children. Loses skills they previously had.
- **By age 3:** Speech is difficult for strangers to understand. Does not play pretend. Does not follow 2-step instructions ("Pick up the toy and put it on the table"). Has daily tantrums. Avoids eye contact. Does not notice or respond to other children.
- **By age 4:** Cannot retell a favorite story. Does not follow 3-step instructions. Does not understand "same" and "different." Does not engage in pretend play. Cannot draw a circle. Shows extreme separation anxiety.
- **By age 5:** Cannot write their first name. Cannot count to 10. Cannot hold a conversation with back-and-forth exchanges. Cannot dress or use the toilet independently. Shows persistent, intense fears that interfere with daily life. [Source: src9, src11, src12]

### Medical red flags

**Call emergency services (911) immediately if your child has:**
- **Breathing difficulty with danger signs:** Retractions (skin pulling in around the ribs), blue or dusky color around the lips or face, or severe respiratory distress. Do not wait.

**Call the same day if your child has:**
- **Breathing concerns:** Persistent wheezing or rapid breathing without the danger signs above.
- **Dehydration:** No urine for 8 or more hours, dry mouth, no tears when crying, sunken eyes.
- **High fever** with severe symptoms: stiff neck, severe headache, persistent vomiting, or rash that doesn't blanch when pressed.
- **Sudden severe abdominal pain** — especially in the lower right.
- **Persistent vomiting** — especially if green (bilious) or containing blood.
- **Unusual bruising or bleeding** — bruises in unusual locations (torso, face), or bruising that seems disproportionate.
- **Vision concerns:** One eye turning in or out consistently, squinting, holding objects very close.
- **"Something seems off."** You don't need a specific symptom. Parental instinct is a valid reason to call. Your pediatrician would rather hear from you and reassure you than not hear from you at all.

## One last thing

The preschool years are loud. They are chaotic. They are full of "why" — relentless, recursive, philosophically exhausting "why."

They are also full of astonishing leaps. Your child will say something that stops you in your tracks — a complete sentence that didn't exist six months ago, an observation about the world that reveals a mind assembling itself in real time, a moment of empathy that shows a person forming where there used to be only need.

You will lose your patience. You will raise your voice and wish you hadn't. You will wonder if you're doing this right. You will google something at 2am that makes you feel worse.

Here's what the evidence says, across every study, every meta-analysis, every longitudinal dataset: what matters most is not perfection. It's presence. Warmth. Consistency. Repair after rupture. Showing up again tomorrow.

Your child does not need a perfect parent. They need a good-enough one who keeps trying. You are already that parent.
