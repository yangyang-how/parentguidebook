## Research Brief

The school-age years are when education becomes formal, structured, and high-stakes. This article covers homework (how much is too much?), effective parent-teacher communication, identifying and supporting learning disabilities, gifted education, navigating school transitions, and addressing bullying in the school context. The evidence on homework is surprisingly clear: for elementary school, the benefits are minimal; for middle school, moderate.

- **Publication:** Review of Educational Research / various

- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7084327/

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Homework — How Much Is Actually Helpful?
- Key points: 10-minute rule, minimal benefit in elementary, moderate in middle, quality > quantity
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Less homework than you think is more helpful than you'd expect

### Section 2: Parent-Teacher Partnerships
- Key points: Proactive communication, specific questions, strengths-based, regular contact
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: The best parent-teacher relationships are built before there's a problem

### Section 3: Learning Disabilities — Early Identification Matters
- Key points: 5-15% of children, dyslexia/dyscalculia/dysgraphia, your rights under IDEA, early intervention
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: If your child is struggling despite effort, they may not need to try harder — they may need to learn differently

### Section 4: Supporting Gifted Learners
- Key points: Unchallenged gifted kids face risks, multi-dimensional identification, acceleration evidence
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: A bored gifted child is not thriving — they need appropriate challenge

### Section 5: School Transitions
- Key points: Temporary disruption is normal, preparation helps, most adjust within 1-2 months
- Supported by: Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: If your child is struggling after changing schools, give it two months before panicking

### Section 6: Bullying in the School Context
- Key points: 20% affected, whole-school approaches, academic and health effects, what parents can do
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: If your child is being bullied, they need advocacy, not advice to "toughen up"

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese academic culture places enormous weight on homework and test performance — the homework research may be surprising and needs careful framing
- In China, the "double reduction" (双减) policy has reduced homework but parents often supplement with tutoring — address both systems
- Learning disabilities are under-diagnosed in Chinese populations due to stigma and different educational frameworks
- Gifted education in Chinese culture often means more academics, not enrichment — present alternative models
- Bullying dynamics in Chinese schools (ranking-based, teacher-involved) differ from Western patterns
- Parent-teacher communication in Chinese culture may be more hierarchical — encourage partnership model
