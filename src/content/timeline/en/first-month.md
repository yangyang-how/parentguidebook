# The First Month

## If you only read one thing, read this

Your baby needs three things from you: **food, warmth, and you.** That's it. Everything else — the schedules, the apps, the gear, the advice from everyone with an opinion — is extra.

**Feeding:** Every 2–3 hours, breast or formula. Both are fine. If your baby has 6 or more wet diapers a day after the first week, they're getting enough.

**Sleep:** On their back, on a firm flat surface, in your room but not your bed. Nothing else in the crib — no blankets, pillows, or toys.

**When to call the doctor:** Temperature at or above 38°C / 100.4°F (always urgent under 3 months). Won't eat for 2 or more feedings in a row. Breathing looks labored — ribs pulling in, nostrils flaring, grunting with every breath. Skin turns blue or dusky around the lips. Or if you just feel something is wrong — that counts too.

**You:** If you're crying more than the baby, if you feel nothing when you expected to feel everything, or if you're having thoughts that scare you — tell someone. A partner, a friend, your doctor. This is not weakness. This is biology. It happens to the majority of new parents, and help exists and works.

Everything else in this article is for when you have more time. You can stop here and come back later. You haven't missed anything essential.

## Part 1: Your baby right now

### What they can see, hear, and feel

Your baby entered the world with some remarkable abilities, and some very limited ones.

**Vision:** They can see about 20–30 centimeters clearly — roughly the distance between your face and theirs when you're holding or feeding them. Beyond that, the world is a blur of light and shadow. They're drawn to high-contrast patterns (black and white, light and dark) and to the edges of things. But most of all, they're drawn to faces — and especially to yours. [Source: src1]

Their eyes may wander, drift, or occasionally cross. This is normal in the first two months. The six small muscles around each eye are brand new on the job, still learning to work together. If one eye *constantly* turns in or out (not just occasionally), or if this continues past 3–4 months, mention it to your doctor. [Source: src1]

**Hearing:** Your baby has been hearing your voice since before they were born — they recognize it. They'll startle at loud sounds (you'll see their arms fly out — that's the Moro reflex, completely normal) and quiet down at familiar ones. Talk to them, sing to them. They don't understand the words, but they understand the voice. [Source: src2]

**Touch:** Of all the senses, touch is the most developed at birth. Skin-to-skin contact — your bare chest against their bare body — is one of the most powerful things you can do. It regulates their temperature, heart rate, and breathing. It calms them. It helps establish feeding. It's not a luxury or a nice-to-have. It's medicine. [Source: src3]

### Their body — what's normal, what's not

Newborn bodies do some surprising things. Most of them are completely normal and resolve on their own. Here's what you're likely to see:

**Skin:**
- **Peeling and flaking** — especially in the first one to three weeks. Their skin is adjusting to air after nine months in fluid. No cream needed. [Source: src4]
- **Milia** — tiny white bumps, often on the nose and cheeks. Present in up to 40% of newborns. Disappear on their own within a few weeks. Don't squeeze them. [Source: src4]
- **Baby acne** — small red bumps, usually appearing around two to four weeks. Not caused by anything you did. Clears up within a few months without treatment. [Source: src4]
- **Jaundice** — a yellowish tint to the skin, most noticeable in natural light. Appears around day two to three, peaks around day four to five, and usually resolves by day ten to fourteen. Mild jaundice is very common and usually harmless. **But:** jaundice appearing in the first 24 hours is never normal — call your doctor immediately. And if jaundice deepens (spreading to the arms, legs, or whites of the eyes) or your baby becomes hard to wake or feed, call your doctor the same day. [Source: src5]

**Breathing:**
Your newborn breathes faster than you — 40 to 60 breaths per minute is normal (adults breathe 12–20). They may have **periodic breathing**: a pattern of rapid breaths, then a pause of up to 10 seconds, then rapid breaths again. This looks alarming but is normal — it's their immature nervous system working things out. They'll also sneeze a lot. This isn't a cold — it's how they clear their tiny nasal passages.

**What's not normal:** Pauses longer than 10 seconds. Grunting with every breath (not just when pooping). The skin between or below their ribs pulling in with each breath (retractions). Nostrils flaring. Blue or dusky color around the lips or face. Any of these = call your doctor or go to the emergency department right away. [Source: src6]

**Umbilical cord stump:**
Keep it clean and dry. No need for alcohol — current guidelines recommend dry care. Fold the diaper below it so it stays aired out. It usually falls off within about three weeks. A small amount of oozing or a slight odor is normal as it dries. **Call the doctor if:** redness or swelling spreads outward from the base, there's foul-smelling discharge, or there's active bleeding. [Source: src7]

**Weight:**
It's normal — and expected — for newborns to lose weight in the first few days. Breastfed babies may lose up to 7–10% of their birth weight; formula-fed babies around 5–7%. The lowest point is usually day two or three. After that, they should start gaining, and most babies regain their birth weight by day 10–14. If your baby hasn't regained by two weeks, your doctor will want to evaluate feeding. [Source: src8]

**Temperature:**
Normal range is 36.5–37.5°C (97.7–99.5°F). Their hands and feet may feel cool — this is normal due to immature circulation, not a sign of being cold. **A rectal temperature at or above 38°C (100.4°F) in a baby under three months is always an emergency.** Don't wait, don't take it again to "make sure." Call your doctor or go to the emergency department. For newborns, fever may be the only sign of a serious infection. [Source: src9]

### Feeding

Here is the most important thing about feeding: **however you feed your baby, your baby is being fed.** Breast milk, formula, or a combination — all nourish your baby completely. The best feeding method is the one that works for your family. [Source: src10]

**If you're breastfeeding:**
- Expect to feed 8–12 times in 24 hours. Yes, that's almost constantly. This is normal, especially in the first weeks.
- **Cluster feeding** — marathon sessions where your baby seems to want to eat every 30 minutes, often in the evenings — is normal and expected. It's not a sign that you don't have enough milk. It's your baby building your supply and filling up before a longer stretch of sleep. [Source: src11]
- The latch may take practice. Some pain in the first few days is common as you both learn, but breastfeeding should not be excruciating. If it is, a lactation consultant can make a real difference — often a small adjustment in positioning changes everything. [Source: src12]
- Around day three to five, your milk "comes in" — your breasts may feel very full and hard (engorgement). This is temporary and improves as your body adjusts to your baby's needs. [Source: src11]

**If you're formula feeding:**
- Feed on demand — watch your baby's cues, not the clock. In the first month, feedings are typically every two to four hours.
- A rough guideline: about 60–90 mL (2–3 oz) per feeding in the first few weeks, gradually increasing. Your baby will tell you when they've had enough by turning away or falling asleep. [Source: src13]

**How to know they're getting enough (for any feeding method):**
- **Diapers:** By day five, look for at least 6 wet diapers in 24 hours. In the first few days, it's fewer: roughly 1 wet diaper on day 1, 2 on day 2, and so on.
- **Stools:** Transitioning from dark meconium (black/green) to yellow, seedy stools by day three or four.
- **Weight:** Gaining steadily after the first-week dip.
- **Behavior:** Alert when awake, good muscle tone, strong cry. [Source: src13]

**If you're struggling:** You are not alone, and it's not your fault. Breastfeeding difficulties are the norm, not the exception — ACOG reports that early breastfeeding challenges are among the most common reasons mothers stop before they planned to. Help is available: lactation consultants, breastfeeding clinics, warm lines, and peer support groups. And if you choose to switch to formula, or to supplement, or to stop — that is a valid choice, and your baby will thrive. [Source: src12]

### Sleep

Your newborn sleeps 14–17 hours a day. It will not feel like it, because those hours come in fragments — stretches of 20 minutes to three hours, scattered randomly across day and night. They have no circadian rhythm yet. They don't know night from day. This is normal, and it resolves on its own over the first few months. [Source: src14]

**Safe sleep — the three basics:**

1. **Alone** — in their own sleep space. Room sharing (baby in a crib or bassinet next to your bed) is recommended and reduces the risk of SIDS by up to 50%, but bed sharing is not recommended. [Source: src15]
2. **On their back** — for every sleep, every time, every caregiver. Even if they seem to sleep better on their stomach. Even if your mother says "we did it and you turned out fine." The back-to-sleep recommendation has cut SIDS deaths by more than 50% since it was introduced. [Source: src15]
3. **In a crib** — a firm, flat surface with a fitted sheet. Nothing else. No blankets, no pillows, no stuffed animals, no bumper pads, no weighted sleep products. [Source: src15]

**The reality of sleep deprivation:** We'll talk about this more in Part 2 (it's as much a parent survival issue as a baby issue). For now: the fragmented sleep of a newborn is one of the hardest parts of the first month. It's not sustainable, and it's not supposed to be permanent. You will sleep again.

## Part 2: You, the parent

This section is about you. Not your baby — you. Because in the fog of new parenthood, your own needs can become invisible. They shouldn't be. You matter in this equation. A baby needs a parent who is okay, and "okay" doesn't mean perfect.

### Your body

**If you gave birth vaginally:** You may have soreness, swelling, and possibly stitches. Sitting can be uncomfortable. Ice packs, sitz baths, and a peri-bottle (a small squeeze bottle of warm water used while urinating) are your friends. Most soreness improves significantly within the first two weeks.

**If you had a C-section:** You had major abdominal surgery. Full recovery takes six to eight weeks. You'll be sore at the incision site. You'll need help getting up, holding the baby, and doing basically everything for the first week or two. Accept the help. Healing is not laziness. [Source: src16]

**Bleeding (lochia):** Postpartum bleeding is normal for four to six weeks. It starts heavy and red, gradually becoming lighter and turning pink, then yellowish-white. **Call your doctor if:** you soak through a pad in less than an hour, pass clots larger than a golf ball, or the bleeding suddenly becomes heavy again after lightening. [Source: src16]

**The hormonal crash:** Within days of delivery, your estrogen and progesterone levels plummet — a steeper hormonal drop than at any other point in your life. This is the chemistry behind the exhaustion, the emotional volatility, the crying for no reason. It's not you being dramatic. It's physiology. [Source: src17]

**When to see your own doctor:** The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends a check-in within three weeks after birth — not just the traditional six-week visit. If anything concerns you before then, don't wait. You are also a patient. [Source: src16]

### Your mind

**Baby blues** are so common they're almost universal — 50% to 85% of new parents experience them. They typically peak around day three to five (when the hormonal crash hits hardest) and resolve within two weeks. Symptoms include crying spells, mood swings, feeling overwhelmed, irritability, and difficulty sleeping even when the baby sleeps. This is the baby blues. It's miserable, but it passes. [Source: src17]

**When it doesn't pass — or when it's more than blues:**

Postpartum depression (PPD) and postpartum anxiety (PPA) are different from the blues. They're more intense, last longer than two weeks, and they interfere with your ability to function. They affect 10–20% of mothers, and about 8–10% of fathers and non-birthing partners — often unrecognized. [Source: src17, src18]

Signs to watch for in yourself or your partner:
- Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness that doesn't lift
- Anxiety so intense it makes it hard to eat, sleep, or focus on anything other than worry
- Difficulty bonding with the baby — feeling disconnected, going through the motions
- Withdrawing from people you normally lean on
- Anger or irritability that feels out of proportion
- Feeling like your baby or your family would be better off without you
- Inability to care for yourself or the baby

**This is not weakness.** It's a medical condition with biological roots — the same way diabetes or a thyroid disorder is. Treatment works: therapy, medication (certain SSRIs have well-established safety profiles during breastfeeding — your doctor can help you choose), or both. There's even a newer oral medication specifically for postpartum depression. [Source: src17]

**If you recognize yourself in any of the above, please tell someone. Your partner, a friend, your doctor — anyone.** You can also contact the Postpartum Support International helpline. You don't have to feel this way, and you won't always.

**Intrusive thoughts:** This one is rarely talked about but incredibly common. Up to 70–100% of new parents experience unwanted, frightening thoughts — vivid images of dropping the baby, of something terrible happening, of harm coming to your child. These thoughts can be shocking and deeply upsetting.

Here's what the research says: **these thoughts are a feature of the postpartum brain, not a bug.** Your protective instincts are on high alert, and your anxiety system is scanning for threats — including imagined ones. Having the thought is not the same as wanting to act on it. If the thoughts horrify you, that's actually a sign they're ego-dystonic — meaning they're the opposite of what you want. [Source: src19]

**When to seek help about intrusive thoughts:** If they become persistent, if you feel compelled to act on them (rather than horrified by them), if you start avoiding your baby to prevent the thoughts, or if they come with other PPD/PPA symptoms.

**The identity earthquake:** Nobody fully prepares you for this: you are not just "adding a baby to your life." Your entire identity is being reorganized. The word for this is **matrescence** — a developmental transition as profound as adolescence, but one that society barely acknowledges. You may grieve your former freedom, your body, your career rhythm, your spontaneity, your sense of self. This grief is normal. It does not mean you regret your baby. It means you're human, adjusting to one of the biggest changes a person can go through. [Source: src20]

**About bonding:** Some parents feel an overwhelming rush of love the moment they hold their baby. Others feel... not much. Exhaustion, maybe. Relief. A sense of responsibility. But not the cinematic flood. **Both experiences are normal.** Bonding is not a light switch — for many parents, it's a gradual process that builds over weeks and months through the daily acts of caring: feeding, holding, rocking, talking. If the bond feels slow, you haven't failed. You're building it right now. [Source: src20]

### Your relationships

**With your partner:** The data is stark — 67% of couples experience a decline in relationship satisfaction in the first three years after their first baby. The biggest predictor of trouble? Not sleep deprivation, not money — it's the **perception of unfair division of labor.** Have the conversation about who does what, early and often. And recognize that at 3am, everything feels more broken than it actually is. Sleep deprivation makes every disagreement feel existential. Sometimes the most helpful thing either partner can say is: "We're both exhausted. This fight is about sleep, not about us." [Source: src21]

**With visitors:** You are allowed to say "not yet." You are allowed to say "please wash your hands." You are allowed to say "we're not taking visitors for two weeks." Your recovery and your baby's first days are not a social event. The people who love you will understand.

**If you're doing this alone:** Nothing in this article should make you feel like you're missing something essential. Single parents, solo parents, parents whose partners are away — you are not doing it wrong. You are doing the work that sometimes takes two people, and that requires not heroism but **a support network.** A friend who brings dinner. A neighbor who holds the baby while you shower. A phone line to call at 2am. If you don't have this network yet, building one is not a luxury — it's a survival strategy. Your pediatrician's office, local parent groups, and postpartum support organizations can help you find your people.

## Part 3: When to call the doctor

### For the baby

Call the same day — or go to the emergency department — if your baby has any of the following:

- **Fever:** Rectal temperature at or above 38°C (100.4°F). In a baby under three months, this is always treated as potentially serious. Don't wait.
- **Feeding refusal:** Skipping two or more consecutive feedings, or consistently taking much less than usual.
- **Dehydration signs:** Fewer than 6 wet diapers per day after day 5. No wet diaper for 8 or more hours. Dry mouth. Sunken soft spot on the head.
- **Jaundice worsening:** Yellowing spreading to the arms, legs, or whites of the eyes, especially with increasing sleepiness or poor feeding.
- **Breathing difficulty:** Persistent rate above 60 breaths per minute. Pauses longer than 10 seconds. Skin pulling in around the ribs or at the neck with each breath. Nasal flaring. Any blue or dusky color.
- **Vomiting:** Forceful or projectile vomiting (not the same as normal spit-up). Any green (bilious) vomit.
- **Umbilical cord:** Redness or swelling spreading outward from the base. Foul-smelling discharge. Active bleeding.
- **Stool changes:** Blood or mucus in stool. White or clay-colored stool.
- **Inconsolable crying:** More than three hours, not responding to any soothing.
- **Lethargy:** Unusually difficult to wake, floppy, weak cry.
- **"Something seems off"** — you don't need a specific symptom. Parental instinct is a valid reason to call. For newborns, the threshold to call is low. Doctors expect it and would rather hear from you than not.

[Sources: src9, src22, src23]

### For you

Your health matters too. Call your doctor if you experience:

- Heavy bleeding — soaking through a pad in less than one hour
- Fever above 38°C (100.4°F)
- Redness, warmth, swelling, or discharge from a C-section incision or perineal stitches
- Pain in your chest or difficulty breathing
- Severe headache that doesn't improve, or vision changes
- Calf pain or swelling in one leg
- Baby blues that last beyond two weeks or get worse instead of better
- Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby
- Feeling detached, numb, or unable to care for yourself or the baby

[Source: src16]

## One last thing

You don't have to love every moment. Some moments in the first month are magical — the first time they grab your finger, the weight of them sleeping on your chest, that milk-drunk face. Other moments are terrible — the screaming that won't stop, the 4am diaper blowout, the loneliness of being the only one awake in the world.

Both are real. Both are the first month.

You don't have to feel bonded yet. You don't have to have it figured out. You don't have to do it alone. You don't have to be perfect. You don't have to enjoy the hard parts. You definitely don't have to "cherish every moment" — anyone who says that has forgotten what this part is actually like.

You just have to show up. Feed them, hold them, keep them warm and safe. The rest builds itself, day by day, feed by feed, sleepless night by sleepless night.

And the fact that you're reading this — at whatever hour, in whatever state, on whatever day of this strange and beautiful and exhausting new life — already tells us something about the kind of parent you are.

You're doing fine.
