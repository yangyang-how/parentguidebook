## Research Brief

School-age children (5-12) experience steady bone mineral accrual that accelerates dramatically with the onset of puberty. This is a critical window: physical activity at ages 5-8 can produce bone mineral content 6-14% higher than sedentary peers, with benefits persisting into adulthood. Overuse injuries (Osgood-Schlatter, Sever's disease) emerge as children participate in organized sports. Growth plate injuries require special attention. The CDC/WHO recommend 60 minutes daily of moderate-to-vigorous activity including bone-strengthening exercises 3 times per week.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- "Growing pains" are real but should be bilateral and occur at night, not during activity — unilateral pain during activity is not growing pains
- Early sport specialization (single sport, year-round, before age 12) increases overuse injury risk — AAP recommends multi-sport participation
- Growth plate injuries can affect future bone growth — any injury near a joint in a growing child deserves medical attention
- Bone pain that wakes a child at night or is not related to activity may indicate something more serious (rare but important)
- Gender differences emerge: girls enter puberty earlier and have earlier peak bone accrual — this affects injury patterns and nutritional timing
- Temporary clumsiness during growth spurts is normal — not a sign of motor delay in school-age children
- Screen time displacing physical activity is a modern concern for bone health in this age group
