# Skin Domain Research — All Ages

Compiled: 2026-03-25

## 1. Newborn Skin (0–3 months)

### Vernix caseosa
- White, waxy coating present at birth — composed of water (~80%), lipids (~10%), proteins (~10%)
- Functions: antimicrobial barrier, moisturizer, temperature regulation, wound healing
- WHO and evidence-based guidelines recommend leaving vernix intact to absorb naturally
- Should not be vigorously rubbed off — delays bathing 24 hours if possible
- Source: WHO 2022 postnatal care guidelines; RCH Melbourne neonatal skin care guideline

### Newborn skin peeling
- Normal in first 1–2 weeks, especially in post-term babies
- Outer skin layer (stratum corneum) adapts from amniotic fluid to air
- No treatment needed; moisturizer optional
- Source: Johns Hopkins Medicine, HealthyChildren.org (AAP)

### Milia
- Tiny white/yellow bumps (1–2 mm) on nose, cheeks, chin, forehead
- Present in ~40–50% of newborns
- Caused by trapped keratin beneath skin surface
- Resolve spontaneously within weeks to months
- No treatment needed — do not squeeze or pick
- Source: AAFP 2008 "Newborn Skin: Common Rashes"; Johns Hopkins

### Erythema toxicum neonatorum (ETN)
- Most common pustular eruption in newborns
- Incidence: 40–70% of term newborns
- Appears within first 2–5 days of life
- Red blotchy patches with small yellow/white pustules
- Self-resolving within 5–14 days; may recur
- No treatment needed; benign
- More common in term infants >2500g; rare in preterm
- Source: Contemporary Pediatrics; AAFP 2008

### Mongolian spots (dermal melanocytosis)
- Blue-gray flat patches, usually on buttocks and lower back
- Present in >80% of Asian, Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous newborns; ~10% of white newborns
- Caused by melanocytes in deeper skin layers
- Usually fade by age 3–5; some persist
- Benign — but should be documented to prevent misidentification as bruises
- Source: AAP, HealthyChildren.org

### Baby acne (neonatal acne)
- Affects ~20% of newborns
- Red bumps/pustules on cheeks, nose, forehead
- Appears at 2–4 weeks, resolves by 3–4 months
- Caused by maternal hormones still circulating; possibly Malassezia yeast
- No treatment usually needed; gentle cleansing with water
- If severe/persistent past 6 weeks → consider infantile acne (different condition, may need treatment)
- Source: AAP, AAD

### Cradle cap (infantile seborrheic dermatitis)
- Thick, yellowish, greasy scales on scalp
- Affects up to 70% of infants in first 3 months
- Caused by overactive sebaceous glands + Malassezia yeast
- Not itchy, not contagious, not caused by poor hygiene
- Treatment: gentle brushing with soft brush, regular shampooing, mineral/baby oil to loosen scales
- Usually resolves by 12 months
- Persistent cases: medicated shampoo (ketoconazole) or mild topical steroid per pediatrician
- Source: Cleveland Clinic, Nemours KidsHealth, AAP

### Diaper rash (irritant diaper dermatitis)
- Affects up to 50% of infants
- Peak incidence: 9–12 months
- Caused by prolonged contact with urine/stool, friction, moisture
- Prevention: frequent diaper changes, barrier cream (zinc oxide/petroleum jelly), air drying
- Treatment: barrier creams, antifungal if candidal (satellite lesions, bright red)
- When to call doctor: rash doesn't improve in 2–3 days, blisters/pus, fever, spreading beyond diaper area
- Source: AAP HealthyChildren.org, Mayo Clinic

## 2. Infant/Toddler Skin (3 months – 3 years)

### Eczema / Atopic dermatitis (AD)
- Most common chronic skin condition in children
- Prevalence: 15–25% of children worldwide; up to 25% in developed countries
- Onset: 60% develop by age 1; 90% by age 5
- Pathophysiology: defective skin barrier (filaggrin mutations in 20–30% of AD patients) + immune dysregulation + microbiome changes
- AAP 2025 clinical report: "Atopic Dermatitis: Update on Skin-Directed Management"
- Key management:
  - Daily moisturization (within 3 minutes of bathing — "soak and seal")
  - Lukewarm baths, gentle fragrance-free cleanser
  - Topical corticosteroids for flares (low-potency for face/folds, medium for body)
  - Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) for sensitive areas
  - Proactive therapy: intermittent TCS/TCI 2x/week to prevent flares
  - Newer: crisaborole (PDE4 inhibitor), ruxolitinib (JAK inhibitor) topical
  - Systemic: dupilumab approved for moderate-severe AD age 6mo+
- Moisturizer evidence: NNT ~5 for preventing flares with daily moisturization
- Steroid fears: "steroid phobia" is a major barrier; low-potency TCS used correctly is safe
- Food allergy connection: ~30% of children with moderate-severe AD have food allergies; but elimination diets without testing are NOT recommended; food allergy testing should be guided by clinical history
- Source: AAP Pediatrics 2025; AAD guidelines; WHO; systematic review WAO Journal 2024

### Drool rash
- Red, irritated skin around mouth, chin, cheeks
- Common during teething (4–7 months onward)
- Prevention: barrier cream (petroleum jelly), gentle wiping, bib use
- Treatment: barrier cream, gentle moisturizer
- Source: AAP HealthyChildren.org

### Hives (urticaria)
- Raised, itchy welts; can appear anywhere on body
- Affects ~15–25% of children at some point
- Acute: most common in children, usually viral trigger, resolves <6 weeks
- Chronic: >6 weeks, less common in children (0.1–3%)
- Treatment: oral antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine)
- When to call 911: hives + difficulty breathing, tongue/throat swelling, dizziness = anaphylaxis
- Source: AAP Pediatrics in Review; Boston Children's Hospital; Cedars-Sinai

### Viral rashes
- **Roseola (HHV-6):** high fever 3–5 days → rash appears as fever breaks; age 6–24 months; self-limiting
- **Hand-foot-and-mouth disease (Coxsackievirus):** painful mouth sores + rash on palms/soles/buttocks; common in under-5; self-limiting 7–10 days; contagious
- Source: AAP, CDC

## 3. Preschool/School-Age Skin (3–12 years)

### Warts (verrucae)
- Caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) — common, benign
- Affect ~10–20% of school-age children
- Common warts on hands; plantar warts on feet
- Most resolve spontaneously within 2 years
- Treatment if bothersome: OTC salicylic acid, cryotherapy, duct tape occlusion
- Source: AAD, BMJ Best Practice

### Molluscum contagiosum
- Small, flesh-colored, dome-shaped bumps with central dimple
- Caused by poxvirus; spread by direct contact
- Common in children ages 1–10
- Self-resolving in 6–12 months (can take up to 4 years)
- Treatment often not necessary; options include cryotherapy, curettage, cantharidin
- Source: AAD, CDC

### Impetigo
- Superficial bacterial skin infection (Staph aureus or Group A Strep)
- Most common bacterial skin infection in children ages 2–5
- Two forms: non-bullous (honey-colored crusts) and bullous (large blisters)
- Highly contagious; spread by direct contact
- Treatment: topical mupirocin or retapamulin for limited disease; oral antibiotics (cephalexin, dicloxacillin) for widespread
- Keep home from school/daycare until 24 hours after starting antibiotics
- Source: AAP Red Book, AAD

### Ringworm (tinea)
- Fungal infection — NOT caused by a worm
- Tinea corporis (body): red, scaly, ring-shaped patches
- Tinea capitis (scalp): scaling, hair loss, common in prepubertal children
- Tinea capitis REQUIRES oral antifungal (griseofulvin or terbinafine); topical alone insufficient for scalp
- Tinea corporis: topical antifungal (clotrimazole, terbinafine) for 2–4 weeks
- Source: AAD, AAP

### Head lice (pediculosis capitis)
- Affects 6–12 million US children/year, ages 3–11
- Not a sign of poor hygiene; does not transmit disease
- Spread by head-to-head contact (NOT commonly from shared hats/brushes)
- AAP position: "no-nit" policies for school exclusion are NOT recommended
- Treatment: OTC permethrin 1% or pyrethrins as first-line; prescription options (ivermectin, spinosad, malathion) for resistant cases
- Repeat treatment in 7–10 days to kill newly hatched nymphs
- Source: AAP Pediatrics 2015 (updated guidance); AAP Pediatrics in Review 2024

### Sunburn prevention (all ages)
- See dedicated sun protection section below
- Source: AAP, AAD, WHO

## 4. Teen Skin (12–18 years)

### Acne vulgaris
- Most common skin condition in adolescents
- Prevalence: ~85% of teens ages 12–24
- Pathophysiology: excess sebum + follicular plugging + C. acnes bacteria + inflammation
- Types: comedonal (blackheads/whiteheads), inflammatory (papules/pustules), nodulocystic
- AAD 2024 updated guidelines:
  - Mild: benzoyl peroxide + topical retinoid
  - Moderate: add topical antibiotic (with benzoyl peroxide to prevent resistance)
  - Moderate-severe: oral antibiotics (doxycycline preferred; limit to 3 months)
  - Severe/scarring/treatment-resistant: oral isotretinoin (strong recommendation)
  - New: trifarotene, clascoterone topical
  - Combined oral contraceptives and spironolactone for hormonal acne in females
- Isotretinoin: highly effective (complete clearance in 85%+); requires iPLEDGE program; side effects include dry skin/lips, teratogenicity, rare mood changes (no causal link established to depression in recent meta-analyses)
- Frequent lab monitoring during isotretinoin NOT recommended for healthy young patients (AAD 2024)
- Source: AAD JAAD 2024; Guideline Central summary

### Skin picking (excoriation disorder)
- Often linked to acne — picking at real or perceived blemishes
- Can worsen scarring significantly
- May indicate anxiety, OCD, or body-focused repetitive behavior
- Approach: address underlying anxiety, cognitive-behavioral therapy (habit reversal training)
- Source: AAD, APA

### Body image and skin
- Acne significantly impacts self-esteem, social withdrawal, depression
- Studies show acne causes quality-of-life impairment comparable to asthma and epilepsy
- Early, effective acne treatment reduces psychological burden and scarring risk
- Source: British Journal of Dermatology; AAD

## 5. Sun Protection — All Ages

### Sunscreen age guidelines
- **Under 6 months:** AAP recommends avoiding direct sun; use shade + clothing; if unavoidable, small amounts of mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide/titanium dioxide) on exposed areas (face, hands)
- **6 months and older:** Broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen; reapply every 2 hours and after swimming/sweating
- Mineral (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) vs chemical: mineral recommended for young children (sits on skin, less absorption)
- Source: AAP HealthyChildren.org; AAD

### UVA vs UVB
- UVB: causes sunburn, directly damages DNA → primary cause of skin cancer
- UVA: penetrates deeper, causes aging/wrinkles, also contributes to cancer
- "Broad spectrum" means protection against both
- Source: AAD, Skin Cancer Foundation

### SPF explanation
- SPF 15: blocks ~93% of UVB
- SPF 30: blocks ~97% of UVB
- SPF 50: blocks ~98% of UVB
- Higher SPF gives diminishing returns; proper application matters more than SPF number
- Most people apply only 25–50% of the recommended amount
- Source: AAD

### Clothing and shade
- UPF-rated clothing provides reliable protection
- Wide-brimmed hat, sunglasses with UV protection
- Seek shade during peak hours (10 AM – 4 PM)
- Source: AAP, AAD

### Vitamin D balance
- AAP recommends 400 IU/day vitamin D for infants, 600 IU/day for children 1+
- Vitamin D should come from diet/supplementation, not unprotected sun exposure
- Source: AAP 2014 vitamin D guidelines

### Sunburn treatment
- Cool compresses, aloe vera, ibuprofen/acetaminophen for pain
- Extra fluids for hydration
- Blistering sunburn: do NOT pop blisters; see a doctor if severe/extensive
- Any sunburn in a child under 1 year: contact pediatrician
- Source: AAP, AAD

## 6. Birthmarks

### Infantile hemangiomas
- Most common benign tumor of infancy — up to 4–5% of infants
- More common in: females (3:1), premature babies, white infants
- Growth pattern: appear in first weeks, grow rapidly 1–3 months, plateau, then slowly involute (shrink)
- 90% resolve by age 4 with minimal residua; some leave residual skin changes
- Most require NO treatment — just monitoring
- When to treat/refer (by 1 month of age if high-risk):
  - Near eye (risk to vision — amblyopia)
  - Airway (subglottic hemangioma)
  - Large facial hemangiomas (PHACE syndrome screening)
  - Ulcerating hemangiomas (painful, risk of scarring/infection)
  - Liver or multiple (>5) cutaneous hemangiomas
- Treatment: oral propranolol (first-line, 2–3 mg/kg/day); topical timolol for small/superficial
- AAP Clinical Practice Guideline 2019
- Source: AAP Pediatrics 2019; AAFP summary

### Port wine stains (capillary malformation)
- Flat, pink-to-purple patches; present at birth; do NOT fade
- Affect ~0.3% of newborns
- Facial port wine stain in V1 distribution: screen for Sturge-Weber syndrome (glaucoma, seizures)
- Treatment: pulsed dye laser (can lighten; best results when started early)
- Source: AAD, AAP

### Cafe-au-lait spots
- Light brown, flat patches
- 1–2 spots: common in general population (10–20% of people)
- **6+ spots >5mm (prepubertal) or >15mm (postpubertal): screen for neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1)**
- NF1 additional features: axillary/inguinal freckling, neurofibromas, Lisch nodules
- If ≥3 cafe-au-lait spots, warrants clinical evaluation
- Source: JAMA Dermatology; NIH NF1 diagnostic criteria; Stanford Children's Health

## 7. Allergic Skin Reactions

### Contact dermatitis
- Irritant (more common) vs allergic (delayed-type hypersensitivity)
- Common triggers in children: nickel (jewelry, belt buckles), poison ivy/oak, fragrances, preservatives, rubber
- Presents: red, itchy, sometimes blistering rash at site of contact
- Treatment: remove trigger, topical corticosteroids, cool compresses
- Patch testing if recurrent/unclear trigger
- Source: TRiP Utah pediatric guide; AAD

### Hives / urticaria
- See section 2 above
- Acute: ~80% of cases in children are idiopathic or viral
- Source: AAP Pediatrics in Review

### Angioedema
- Deeper swelling (lips, eyelids, hands, feet, genitals)
- Often accompanies urticaria
- When dangerous: tongue, throat, airway involvement → anaphylaxis
- Treatment: antihistamines for mild; IM epinephrine for anaphylaxis
- Source: AAP, ACAAI

### When to use epinephrine
- Any signs of anaphylaxis: hives + breathing difficulty, throat tightness, vomiting, dizziness, hypotension
- Administer IM epinephrine (EpiPen Jr for <30kg; EpiPen for ≥30kg) into outer thigh
- Call 911 immediately after administration
- Biphasic reactions can occur — observe in ER for 4+ hours
- Source: ACAAI, AAP anaphylaxis guidelines

## 8. When to See a Dermatologist

### Red flags for urgent evaluation
- Rapidly spreading rash with fever (rule out cellulitis, meningococcemia, Kawasaki)
- Petechiae/purpura (non-blanching spots) — may indicate serious infection or blood disorder
- Blistering rash covering >10% body surface
- Suspected Stevens-Johnson syndrome or toxic epidermal necrolysis

### Persistent rashes
- Eczema not responding to appropriate topical treatment after 2–4 weeks
- Any rash that worsens despite treatment
- Rashes causing significant sleep disruption or quality-of-life impact

### Unusual moles — ABCDE rule
- **A — Asymmetry:** one half doesn't match the other
- **B — Border:** irregular, ragged, or blurred edges
- **C — Color:** uneven distribution; multiple colors
- **D — Diameter:** >6mm (larger than a pencil eraser)
- **E — Evolving:** changing in size, shape, or color
- Pediatric melanoma is rare but does occur; any changing, bleeding, or symptomatic mole warrants evaluation
- Source: AAD, Skin Cancer Foundation

### Other reasons to consult
- Extensive or rapidly growing birthmarks
- Hair loss (alopecia areata, tinea capitis)
- Skin infections not responding to first-line treatment
- Suspected drug reactions
- Any skin finding causing significant parental concern
