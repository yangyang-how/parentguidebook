## Research Brief

Adolescence (12-18 years) is marked by continued myopia progression, heavy digital device use, and emerging independence in eye care. Myopia is now considered an epidemic globally, and evidence-based interventions (atropine, orthokeratology, dual-focus lenses) can slow progression. Digital eye strain affects over 60% of teen device users, though blue-light-blocking glasses have no proven benefit. Sports eye injury prevention and contact lens safety are also key concerns for this age group.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- High myopia (>-6.00 diopters) significantly increases lifetime risk of serious eye diseases — this is why slowing progression matters
- Blue-light-blocking glasses are heavily marketed but have NO evidence of benefit for eye health (AAO position)
- Blue light DOES affect sleep — but the solution is reducing screen time before bed, not special glasses
- Contact lens compliance is a real concern in teens — poor hygiene can cause serious infections
- Myopia progression does not always stop at 18 — some teens may need continued management into their 20s
- The article should empower teens to take ownership of their eye health without being preachy
