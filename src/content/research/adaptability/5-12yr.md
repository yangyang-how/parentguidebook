## Research Brief

School-age children face an expanding range of transitions — changing schools, shifting friend groups, family changes (divorce, remarriage, new siblings), and the increasing expectation to cope independently. This article covers school transitions, the development of coping strategies, building flexibility as a learnable skill, changing friend groups, and supporting children through family-level changes.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: School Transitions
- Key points: Temporary dips, buffering strategies, one semester adjustment
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: If grades drop after changing schools, wait a semester before panicking

### Section 2: Building Coping Skills
- Key points: Expanding repertoire, active > avoidant, explicitly teachable, growth mindset
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 6, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: "What are your options?" is the most powerful question you can ask a stuck child

### Section 3: Navigating Friend Changes
- Key points: Friendship shifts are normal, repair > avoidance, social competence through change
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Losing a friend hurts. Learning to make a new one is growth.

### Section 4: Family Changes (Divorce, Blended Families)
- Key points: Honesty and routine, parentification risk, blended family takes 2-5 years, loyalty conflicts
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Your child doesn't need you to fix the situation. They need you to be honest, stable, and present.

### Section 5: Executive Function and Flexibility Training
- Key points: EF continues developing, strategy games and activities, school-based programs
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Chess, music, sports, and problem-solving games aren't just hobbies — they're adaptability training

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese academic culture may prioritize stability over adaptability — address the value of learning to handle change
- Divorce and blended families carry significant stigma in Chinese culture — present evidence sensitively
- Parentification is particularly common in Chinese families where children are expected to be responsible early
- "Just deal with it" (忍一忍) is a common Chinese coping approach — present evidence for active coping strategies
- Some children's difficulty adapting may signal anxiety disorders — include when to evaluate
