# Night Terrors, Sleepwalking, and Nightmares — A Parent's Guide to Parasomnias

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — parasomnias are common in childhood, almost always harmless, and typically resolve on their own.**

**The short version:** If your child has ever screamed inconsolably in the middle of the night with their eyes open but seemingly unable to see you — and then had no memory of it the next morning — that was almost certainly a night terror. Night terrors affect about one-third of children, peaking around age 3.5, and they are harmless despite looking terrifying. Sleepwalking affects up to 15% of children, peaking around ages 8 to 12. Nightmares are different from both — they occur during REM sleep, the child wakes fully, and comfort helps. Understanding which is which changes your response completely.

---

## What are parasomnias?

Parasomnias are unusual behaviors that occur during sleep. In children, the most common are:

- **Night terrors** (also called sleep terrors)
- **Sleepwalking** (somnambulism)
- **Nightmares**

Night terrors and sleepwalking are both **disorders of arousal** — they happen when the brain gets partially stuck between deep non-REM sleep and wakefulness. The child is neither fully asleep nor fully awake. Nightmares are different — they occur during REM sleep and involve a full awakening.

This distinction matters because the response to each is completely different.

## Night terrors

### What they look like

Night terrors are among the most alarming things a parent can witness. Your child may:

- Scream or cry inconsolably
- Appear terrified — wide-eyed, sweating, heart racing
- Sit up or thrash in bed
- Be completely unresponsive to your voice or touch
- Push you away if you try to comfort them
- Have their eyes open but seem to "not see" you

The episode typically lasts 5 to 15 minutes, though it can feel much longer.

### What's actually happening

Your child is not awake. They are stuck in a partial arousal from deep non-REM sleep, which occurs in the first third of the night — typically 1 to 2 hours after falling asleep. [Source: src3] Their body is activated (screaming, thrashing, autonomic arousal) but their conscious brain is not engaged. They are not experiencing fear the way it looks from the outside.

The child will not remember the episode the next morning. [Source: src1] If you ask them about it, they will have no idea what you are talking about.

### How common are they?

Night terrors affect about one-third of children. They peak at approximately 3.5 years of age and typically resolve spontaneously by age 6 to 7. [Source: src1]

### What to do during a night terror

1. **Do not try to wake your child.** Attempting to wake a child during a night terror usually prolongs the episode and increases agitation. [Source: src1]
2. **Stay nearby and keep them safe.** Make sure they cannot fall out of bed or hit something.
3. **Do not restrain them** unless they are about to hurt themselves.
4. **Wait it out.** The episode will end on its own, and your child will settle back into quiet sleep.
5. **Do not discuss it the next morning** unless your child brings it up. They don't remember it, and bringing it up may cause unnecessary worry.

### What makes night terrors more frequent

Sleep deprivation and irregular sleep schedules are the strongest triggers. [Source: src1] Other triggers include:

- Illness or fever
- Stress or emotional upheaval
- A full bladder
- Sleeping in an unfamiliar environment
- Noise or other disruptions during sleep

**The best prevention is consistent, adequate sleep.** Getting enough total sleep and keeping bedtime regular reduces the frequency of night terrors more than anything else. [Source: src1]

### Are night terrors a sign of emotional problems?

In the vast majority of cases, no. Most children with night terrors do not have underlying psychiatric conditions. [Source: src2] Night terrors are a feature of normal brain development, not a symptom of psychological distress.

That said, the relationship between night terrors and emotional stress is bidirectional — significant emotional stress can increase frequency, and the disrupted sleep can worsen daytime behavior, creating a cycle. [Source: src2] If night terrors are frequent and your child is also going through a stressful period, addressing the stress may help with the sleep.

### Scheduled awakening — for severe cases

For children who have night terrors at a predictable time most nights, a technique called **scheduled awakening** can help. About 15 to 30 minutes before the terror typically occurs, gently rouse your child — just enough that they shift position or mumble, not enough to fully wake them. This disrupts the sleep cycle that leads to the partial arousal.

This technique has been shown to reduce or eliminate night terrors in many children. It requires consistency — you need to do it every night for several weeks. But for families dealing with nightly episodes, it can be effective.

## Sleepwalking

### What it looks like

A sleepwalking child may:

- Get out of bed and walk around the house
- Have their eyes open but appear glassy or unfocused
- Perform routine actions (opening doors, going to the bathroom, getting dressed)
- Speak incoherently or not at all
- Be difficult to wake
- Have no memory of the episode the next morning

Sleepwalking occurs during deep non-REM sleep, typically in the first few hours of the night — the same sleep stage as night terrors. [Source: src3]

### How common is it?

Sleepwalking affects up to 15% of children, peaking between ages 8 and 12. [Source: src3] It runs strongly in families — if both parents have a history of sleepwalking, a child has about a 60% chance of sleepwalking too.

Most children outgrow sleepwalking by adolescence.

### Safety is the priority

The child is not in distress during sleepwalking — unlike night terrors, there is no screaming or apparent fear. The main concern is safety. A sleepwalking child can open doors, walk down stairs, or leave the house.

**Safety measures:**
- Lock exterior doors and windows (consider alarms on doors)
- Gate stairways
- Remove tripping hazards from pathways
- Do not leave sharp objects or breakable items accessible
- Consider a bell or alarm on the child's bedroom door so you are alerted when they get up

**Do not try to forcefully wake a sleepwalking child.** Gently guide them back to bed. They may be confused or agitated if fully woken.

### When sleepwalking needs medical attention

Most sleepwalking is harmless and outgrown. See your pediatrician if:

- Episodes are frequent (multiple times per week)
- Your child is at risk of injury during episodes
- Sleepwalking persists into mid-adolescence
- The sleepwalking involves dangerous behaviors (leaving the house, attempting to cook)
- Your child seems excessively sleepy during the day

## Nightmares

### How they're different from night terrors

Nightmares are the sleep disturbance parents are most familiar with — and they are fundamentally different from night terrors.

| | Night terrors | Nightmares |
|---|---|---|
| **When in the night** | First 1-2 hours (deep non-REM sleep) | Second half of the night (REM sleep) |
| **Awareness** | Not awake — does not recognize you | Fully awake — alert, oriented, seeks comfort |
| **Memory** | No memory the next morning | Remembers the dream, often vividly |
| **What you see** | Screaming, thrashing, inconsolable | Child wakes crying, calls for you |
| **Duration** | 5-15 minutes, then back to quiet sleep | Variable — may have difficulty returning to sleep |
| **Your response** | Don't wake them; keep them safe | Comfort, reassure, stay present |
| **Peak age** | Around 3.5 years | From age 3 onward, common through childhood |

[Source: src3]

### How to help with nightmares

Unlike night terrors, nightmares are genuinely distressing for the child. They wake up frightened, they remember what scared them, and they need your reassurance.

**What helps:**
- **Stay with them** until they feel safe.
- **Validate their feelings.** "That does sound scary. I'm right here and you're safe." Telling a child "there's nothing to be scared of" rarely helps — they just experienced something frightening, and dismissing it doesn't make the feeling go away.
- **A nightlight** can help children who are afraid of the dark.
- **A comfort object** — a stuffed animal or blanket they associate with safety.
- **Avoid scary content** close to bedtime — stories, shows, or games with frightening elements can feed nightmares.

### When nightmares need attention

Occasional nightmares are a normal part of childhood. But frequent nightmares — especially if they follow a traumatic experience, prevent your child from sleeping, or are accompanied by significant daytime anxiety — may warrant evaluation. Persistent, distressing nightmares can be a feature of anxiety disorders or post-traumatic stress.

## The connection between sleep and parasomnias

One theme runs through all childhood parasomnias: **insufficient sleep makes them worse.**

Night terrors become more frequent when children are overtired or on irregular schedules. [Source: src1] Sleepwalking episodes increase with sleep deprivation. [Source: src3] Even nightmares may be more frequent when sleep quality is poor.

This means the most effective prevention strategy for all three is the same: consistent, adequate sleep.

- Maintain a regular bedtime and wake time
- Ensure your child is getting the recommended total hours for their age
- Keep screens out of the bedroom
- Address any underlying sleep issues (snoring, difficulty falling asleep, restless sleep)

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Call your doctor promptly:**
- Sleepwalking that puts your child at serious risk of injury (going outside, near windows, attempting dangerous activities)
- Episodes that are extremely frequent (nightly or multiple times per night)
- Any parasomnia that is accompanied by seizure-like activity (rhythmic jerking, tongue biting, incontinence)
- New onset of parasomnias in a teenager who did not have them as a young child

**🟡 Mention at your next visit:**
- Night terrors occurring more than once or twice per week
- Sleepwalking that persists beyond age 13-14
- Nightmares that are frequent enough to cause significant sleep anxiety or avoidance
- Any parasomnia you're unsure about — better to describe it than to guess
- Your child seems excessively sleepy during the day despite adequate time in bed

> **A note for worried parents:** Watching your child scream inconsolably while they stare through you with glassy eyes is one of the most unsettling experiences in parenting. It helps enormously to know what you're seeing: a brain temporarily stuck between sleep stages. Your child is not suffering, not having a psychological crisis, and will not remember it. The hardest part of a night terror is being the parent watching it happen. The reassurance isn't just for your child — it's for you too. This is normal. It will pass. And your child is fine.
