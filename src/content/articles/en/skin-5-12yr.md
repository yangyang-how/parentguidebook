# Your Child's Skin at 5–12 Years — Lice, Ringworm, Warts, and Sun Safety

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — most school-age skin conditions are common, treatable, and not dangerous.**

**The short version:** The school-age years bring a new landscape for your child's skin. Shared classrooms, sports teams, sleepovers, and locker rooms create ideal conditions for the transmission of skin infections — head lice (which peak at ages 4 to 11), ringworm, warts, and scabies are all common. [Source: src1] Eczema may persist but often improves during these years. Sun protection becomes more important as your child spends increasing time outdoors for sports and play, and childhood sunburns have a direct impact on adult skin cancer risk. [Source: src7] This is also the age when visible skin conditions start to affect self-esteem — a dimension worth paying attention to.

**Normal:** Occasional warts, a bout of head lice, mild eczema flares, insect bite reactions during summer.
**Not normal:** Rapidly spreading rash with fever, non-blanching spots, large area of hot red swelling (possible cellulitis), sudden worsening of eczema with painful blisters.

## Head lice — facts over fear

Head lice are one of the most emotionally charged topics in school-age parenting, and most of that emotion is out of proportion to the actual medical significance.

### What you need to know

Head lice are most common in children aged 4 to 11 years. [Source: src1] They spread primarily through direct head-to-head contact — not commonly through shared hats, brushes, or helmets. Lice cannot survive long away from a human scalp. They do not jump or fly.

**They are not a sign of poor hygiene.** Lice do not prefer dirty hair over clean hair. They do not transmit disease. The primary symptom is itching, caused by an allergic reaction to lice saliva.

### Treatment

**Only treat when live lice are confirmed** — finding nits (eggs) alone, without live lice, is not sufficient reason to start treatment. Overtreating drives resistance. [Source: src3]

**First-line treatment:** Over-the-counter permethrin 1% lotion or pyrethrin-based products. Apply according to package directions and repeat in 7 to 10 days to kill newly hatched nymphs. [Source: src3]

**If OTC products fail** after two properly applied treatments: prescription options include ivermectin lotion, spinosad, or malathion. Dimethicone-based products are a non-pesticide alternative that works by physically coating and suffocating the lice. [Source: src3]

**Nit removal:** After treatment, use a fine-toothed nit comb to remove nits from the hair. This is time-consuming but helps confirm treatment success and removes visible evidence.

### School policies

The AAP does not support mandatory "no-nit" policies that exclude children from school for nits remaining after treatment. A child who has been treated for lice should be allowed to return to school the same day. The social stigma and lost school time from lice cause more harm than the lice themselves. [Source: src3]

## Fungal infections — ringworm and athlete's foot

### Ringworm on the body (tinea corporis)

Ringworm appears as red, scaly, ring-shaped patches with a clearing center and an active, sometimes raised border. [Source: src2] It spreads through direct contact with infected skin, pets (cats and dogs can carry it), or contaminated surfaces. It is common in school-age children, particularly those involved in contact sports.

**Treatment:** Over-the-counter topical antifungal cream — clotrimazole or terbinafine — applied twice daily for 2 to 4 weeks. Continue treatment for at least one week after the rash appears to have resolved to prevent recurrence. [Source: src2]

**School attendance:** Children with ringworm on the body can attend school while being treated. Cover the area with clothing or a bandage if possible. [Source: src11]

### Ringworm on the scalp (tinea capitis)

This is a critically different condition that requires a different approach. Scalp ringworm causes scaling, itching, and patchy hair loss. The fungus infects the hair shaft itself, making it unreachable by topical creams.

**Oral antifungal medication is required.** Topical treatment alone is insufficient for scalp ringworm. [Source: src2] [Source: src11] If your child has patchy hair loss with scaling, see your pediatrician.

Avoid sharing combs, brushes, hats, and hair accessories during treatment.

### Athlete's foot (tinea pedis)

As children begin using shared showers, pools, and locker rooms, athlete's foot becomes more common. It appears as itchy, peeling, or cracked skin between the toes or on the soles.

**Treatment:** Over-the-counter topical antifungal cream. Encourage your child to dry their feet thoroughly after washing, wear sandals in shared showers, change socks daily, and choose breathable shoes.

## Warts, molluscum, and scabies

### Warts

Warts are caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) and are extremely common in school-age children. Common warts appear on the hands as rough, raised bumps; plantar warts grow on the soles of the feet and can be painful when walking. [Source: src4]

**The good news:** Most warts resolve on their own within two years as the immune system clears the virus. [Source: src6]

**If treatment is desired:**
- Over-the-counter salicylic acid — applied daily, it takes weeks of consistent use. Patience is required.
- Cryotherapy (freezing) at the doctor's office — another option, though not dramatically faster than salicylic acid
- For warts that are painful, rapidly growing, or near the eyes or genitals, see your pediatrician [Source: src6]

### Molluscum contagiosum

Small, dome-shaped bumps with a central dimple, caused by a poxvirus. Still common in this age group, especially in children with eczema. Treatment is usually unnecessary — the bumps resolve on their own in 6 to 12 months. [Source: src6]

### Scabies

Scabies is caused by a microscopic mite that burrows into the skin. It spreads through prolonged skin-to-skin contact and causes intense itching, especially at night. Characteristic signs include tiny burrow tracks (thin, wavy lines) in the web spaces between fingers, on wrists, and in skin folds.

**Treatment:** Permethrin 5% cream applied to the entire body from the neck down, left on for 8 to 14 hours, then washed off. The entire household should be treated simultaneously to prevent re-infestation. Bedding and clothing should be washed in hot water. [Source: src4]

Itching from scabies can persist for several weeks after successful treatment — this is a residual allergic reaction to the dead mites, not a sign that treatment failed.

## Sun safety for active kids

Sun protection during childhood is not just about preventing sunburn today. A history of one or more sunburns in childhood significantly increases the risk of developing skin cancer as an adult, including both basal cell carcinoma and melanoma. [Source: src7]

### Building the habit

School-age children are old enough to start taking some responsibility for sun protection, but they need the habit modeled and reinforced by adults.

**Sunscreen:**
- Broad-spectrum, water-resistant, SPF 30 or higher [Source: src7]
- Apply to all exposed skin 15 minutes before going outside
- Reapply every 2 hours and immediately after swimming or heavy sweating [Source: src7]
- Most people apply only 25-50% of the recommended amount — more is better than less

**Beyond sunscreen:**
- Protective clothing — UPF-rated clothing provides more reliable protection than a regular T-shirt (which offers roughly UPF 5)
- Wide-brimmed hat protecting face, ears, and neck
- Sunglasses with 100% UV protection
- Seek shade during peak UV hours (10 AM to 4 PM) [Source: src8]

### Sports-specific advice

- Apply sunscreen before practice, not just games
- Reapply during breaks
- Water sports and snow sports both carry high UV exposure (water and snow reflect UV rays)
- Schools and sports programs should incorporate sun-safe policies — the CDC recommends that schools provide shade, allow sunscreen use, and schedule outdoor activities outside peak UV hours when possible [Source: src9]

## Eczema in school-age children

If eczema has persisted from earlier childhood, it is typically concentrated in the flexural areas — elbow creases, behind the knees, wrists, and ankles. Many children see improvement during the school-age years, though some continue to have significant disease.

**Management updates:**
- Updated AAP guidance emphasizes trigger avoidance: low humidity, harsh soaps, certain detergents, and contact allergens [Source: src10]
- Daily moisturization remains the foundation
- Proactive therapy (applying anti-inflammatory topicals to previously affected areas twice weekly) continues to be recommended to prevent flares
- Newer therapies are available for children whose eczema isn't controlled by standard topical treatment

### When skin affects confidence

This is the age when children become acutely aware of how they look — and how they compare to peers. Visible skin conditions like eczema, warts, or molluscum can affect self-esteem, participation in activities (especially swimming), and social interactions.

**What you can do:**
- Take your child's concerns seriously — don't minimize how a skin condition makes them feel
- Effective treatment reduces the visible impact and the emotional burden
- If eczema or another condition is causing your child to avoid activities or affecting their mood, that is a reason to escalate treatment
- Consider involving your child in their own skin care routine — age-appropriate responsibility builds a sense of control

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Seek medical attention promptly:**
- **Hot, red, rapidly spreading area of skin with fever** — possible cellulitis (bacterial skin infection spreading into deeper tissue)
- **Non-blanching rash** — spots that don't fade with pressure, especially with fever or an unwell child
- **Sudden cluster of painful blisters on eczematous skin** — possible eczema herpeticum (emergency)
- **Large area of blistering or peeling skin** — especially if the child seems unwell
- **Warts near the eyes or genitals** — these need medical evaluation, not home treatment

**🟡 See your pediatrician soon:**
- Head lice not responding to two rounds of over-the-counter treatment
- Patchy hair loss with scaling (likely scalp ringworm — needs oral treatment)
- Ringworm on the body not improving after 2 weeks of topical antifungal
- Eczema flares that are disrupting sleep or school attendance
- Intense nighttime itching with burrow-like tracks (possible scabies)
- A mole that is changing size, shape, or color
- Any skin condition affecting your child's emotional well-being

> **A note about lice and shame:** If your child comes home with head lice, they are not dirty. You are not a negligent parent. Head lice are a nuisance, not a hygiene failure. Treat it, notify close contacts, and move on. The embarrassment surrounding lice causes more suffering than the lice ever will.
