## Research Brief

School-age children bring concrete operational thinking to abstract questions — they can think logically about death, fairness, justice, and God in ways that are more sophisticated than preschoolers but still grounded in concrete experience. This article covers maturing death understanding, fairness and justice reasoning (Kohlberg's conventional stage), religious and philosophical education, the P4C movement in schools, media portrayals of big themes, and how to support ongoing dialogue as children's thinking deepens.

- **Type:** systematic-review

**Key passages:**

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Death Gets Real
- Key points: Full understanding by 7-8, personal experience, open discussion reduces anxiety, grief support
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Your child can now understand that death is permanent. That's scary for both of you. Talk about it anyway.

### Section 2: Fairness, Justice, and "The Rules"
- Key points: Conventional morality, equity vs equality, peer discussion, ethical practice
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 5, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Dinner table debates about fairness are moral development in action

### Section 3: Faith, Meaning, and Stories
- Key points: Mythic-Literal stage, narrative-based meaning, literalism is normal, religious education
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Your child may take religious stories very literally. That's not naivety — it's how this age processes meaning.

### Section 4: Philosophy for Children
- Key points: P4C improves thinking, academic transfer, community of inquiry, practical application at home
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Ask your child "What do you think?" more often. Then listen. Then ask "Why do you think that?"

### Section 5: Media as Philosophical Stimulus
- Key points: Movies/books present big themes, co-viewing essential, broaden perspective
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: After the movie, ask "Was that fair?" or "What would you have done?" — turn entertainment into thinking

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Death avoidance in Chinese culture (避讳) is particularly strong — evidence for open discussion may face resistance
- Chinese education emphasizes correct answers over inquiry — P4C approach may be unfamiliar but valuable
- Religious education is sensitive — China has a secular state education system but diverse family practices
- Including children in funerals is uncommon in some Chinese families — present evidence gently
- "Right and wrong" framing is culturally preferred in China over "let's discuss the dilemma" — bridge the gap
