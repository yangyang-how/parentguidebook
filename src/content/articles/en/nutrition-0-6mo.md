# Feeding Your Baby (0–6 Months) — Breast Milk, Formula, and the One Supplement Every Baby Needs

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — the basics of feeding in the first six months are straightforward, but a few things need to happen early.**

**The short version:** For the first six months, your baby needs only one thing: milk. Breast milk or infant formula provides complete nutrition. The single most important supplement — regardless of how you feed — is vitamin D (400 IU daily, starting in the first few days of life). Beyond that, feeding is about following your baby's cues, not the clock.

**Key takeaways:**
- Breast milk and formula are both complete nutrition for the first 6 months [Source: src1, src2]
- Start vitamin D drops (400 IU daily) within the first few days of life — for all babies [Source: src3]
- Newborns feed 8–12 times per day; crying is a **late** hunger cue, not an early one [Source: src6]
- Most "low milk supply" is perceived, not actual — true primary insufficiency affects only 5–15% of women [Source: src10]
- No water, juice, or other liquids before 6 months

## Breast milk or formula — both are valid

This is worth saying clearly at the top: the best way to feed your baby is the way that works for your family.

The WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life, followed by continued breastfeeding with appropriate complementary foods up to 2 years or beyond. [Source: src1] The AAP aligns with this recommendation and supports breastfeeding for 2 years or beyond as mutually desired. [Source: src2]

Breast milk has well-documented health benefits. For infants, it is protective against infections and is associated with improved immune function. For the breastfeeding parent, it reduces the risk of breast and ovarian cancer, and type 2 diabetes. [Source: src9] These benefits are real and supported by strong evidence.

At the same time, the AAP explicitly recognizes that families may face barriers to breastfeeding and supports all families in their infant feeding decisions. [Source: src2] Infant formula is a safe, complete source of nutrition that has supported the healthy growth of millions of children. If you breastfeed, wonderful. If you formula-feed, wonderful. If you do a combination, wonderful. If you planned to breastfeed and it didn't work out, you have not failed your child.

The evidence is clear that **how** you feed matters far less than **that** you feed — responsively, reliably, and with love.

## How much, how often

### Following your baby's cues

The most important feeding principle in the first six months is **responsive feeding** — watching your baby's hunger and fullness signals rather than following a rigid schedule. [Source: src6]

**Early hunger cues (act on these):**
- Rooting — turning head toward anything that touches the cheek
- Sucking on hands or fists
- Increased alertness and activity
- Opening mouth, smacking lips

**Late hunger cue (you've missed the earlier ones):**
- Crying [Source: src6]

A crying baby is a hungry baby who has been trying to tell you for a while. Once a baby is crying from hunger, they're often too upset to latch or feed well. Learning the early cues makes feeding calmer for everyone.

**Fullness cues (stop here):**
- Turning away from the breast or bottle
- Closing mouth
- Relaxed hands (previously clenched)
- Falling asleep during feeding

Never force a baby to finish a bottle. [Source: src8] Their internal hunger and fullness signals are remarkably well-calibrated — trust them.

### Breastfeeding frequency and volume

Newborns typically feed **8–12 times per day** in the first weeks. [Source: src6] That's roughly every 2–3 hours, around the clock. This frequency is normal and helps establish your milk supply.

You cannot see how much a breastfed baby is drinking, which can be anxiety-inducing. The best indicators that your baby is getting enough:

- **Wet diapers:** At least 6 per day by day 5
- **Stool frequency:** Several stools per day in the first weeks (frequency decreases after the first month — this is normal)
- **Weight gain:** Your pediatrician will track this at well-visits
- **Feeding behavior:** Baby feeds actively, you can hear swallowing, and baby seems satisfied after most feeds

### Formula feeding amounts

For formula-fed babies, the general guideline is about **2.5 ounces of formula per pound of body weight per day**, up to a maximum of approximately 32 ounces (960 mL) per day. [Source: src8]

As a rough guide by age:

| Age | Amount per feeding | Feedings per day |
|---|---|---|
| First weeks | 2–3 oz (60–90 mL) | 8–12 |
| 1 month | 3–4 oz (90–120 mL) | 7–8 |
| 2–3 months | 4–5 oz (120–150 mL) | 6–7 |
| 4–6 months | 6–8 oz (180–240 mL) | 4–6 |

[Source: src8]

These are averages. Some babies eat more at some feedings and less at others. Watch your baby, not the ounce markings.

### Paced bottle feeding

Whether you're giving formula or expressed breast milk, **paced bottle feeding** helps your baby maintain their natural self-regulation:

- Hold the baby in a semi-upright position (not lying flat)
- Hold the bottle horizontally so milk doesn't pour in by gravity
- Let the baby draw milk actively rather than flooding their mouth
- Pause periodically to let them decide if they want more

This approach mimics the pace of breastfeeding and helps prevent overfeeding.

## Vitamin D — the one supplement every baby needs

Regardless of how you feed your baby, this is the one supplement that deserves your attention: **all breastfed and partially breastfed infants should receive 400 IU of vitamin D daily, beginning in the first few days of life.** [Source: src3]

Formula-fed infants who consume less than about 33 ounces (1 liter) of formula per day also need supplementation. [Source: src3]

**Why breast milk isn't enough for vitamin D:** Human milk typically contains only about 25–50 IU per liter of vitamin D — far less than what babies need. [Source: src4] This isn't a flaw in breast milk. It reflects the reality that humans evolved getting vitamin D primarily from sun exposure, and modern life doesn't provide enough sun for infants (nor should babies be in direct sunlight for long periods).

Without supplementation, breastfed infants are at risk for vitamin D deficiency, which can cause rickets — a condition where bones become soft and weak. Rickets is rare but entirely preventable with a daily vitamin D drop. [Source: src4]

**How to give it:** Infant vitamin D drops are available over the counter. Most deliver 400 IU in a single drop. You can place it on the nipple before nursing, on a pacifier, or directly in the baby's mouth. It has no significant taste.

**An alternative approach:** Research shows that maternal high-dose vitamin D supplementation (4,000–6,400 IU per day) can achieve adequate vitamin D levels in breast milk, potentially replacing the need for infant drops. [Source: src7] However, adherence to infant drops is low in many populations — as low as 20–30% in some studies [Source: src7] — which is one reason some providers suggest the maternal supplementation route instead. Discuss with your healthcare provider which approach works best for your family.

## "Am I making enough milk?"

This fear is nearly universal among breastfeeding parents — and usually unfounded.

**Perceived insufficient milk supply is the most common reason for early breastfeeding cessation.** Yet true primary lactation insufficiency — where the body genuinely cannot produce enough milk — affects only about **5–15% of women.** [Source: src10]

Most cases of low supply are secondary to suboptimal breastfeeding management: feeding too infrequently, poor latch, or supplementing with formula without pumping to maintain supply. [Source: src10] These are fixable problems, not permanent conditions.

### Signs your baby IS getting enough milk

- At least 6 wet diapers per day by day 5
- Steady weight gain after the initial newborn weight loss (babies typically regain birth weight by 10–14 days)
- Active feeding with audible swallowing
- Baby seems satisfied after most feeds (not constantly hungry)

### Signs that might indicate a real supply issue

- Fewer than 6 wet diapers per day after day 5
- Weight loss continuing after 2 weeks or failure to regain birth weight
- Baby is constantly fussy and unsatisfied after feeding
- No audible swallowing during feeds

### What actually increases milk supply

The most reliable way to increase supply is straightforward: **remove more milk, more often.** Milk production works on supply and demand — the more milk is removed (by nursing or pumping), the more the body produces. [Source: src10]

What does **not** reliably increase supply: special teas, cookies, supplements marketed as "galactagogues." The evidence for these is weak. The evidence for frequent, effective milk removal is strong.

## Getting help when feeding is hard

Struggling with feeding doesn't mean you're doing something wrong. Many common feeding challenges have specific, solvable causes.

**When to seek help:**

- **Pain during breastfeeding** — a good latch should not hurt after the first few seconds. Persistent pain usually indicates a latch problem that a lactation consultant can assess.
- **Cracked or bleeding nipples** — almost always a latch issue. A lactation consultant can help.
- **Baby struggling to latch or making clicking sounds** — may indicate tongue-tie (ankyloglossia), which restricts tongue movement. Ask your pediatrician for an evaluation.
- **Baby arching, crying, or refusing to eat** — may indicate reflux or other physical discomfort.
- **Concerns about weight gain** — if your pediatrician flags slow weight gain, they can help determine the cause and solution.

**Who to ask for help:**

- **Lactation consultant (IBCLC)** — for latch, supply, and breastfeeding technique issues. Ask your hospital or pediatrician for a referral. Many are covered by insurance.
- **Pediatrician** — for weight gain concerns, reflux, or suspected tongue-tie
- **Feeding therapist** — usually a speech-language pathologist, for persistent oral motor difficulties

**Combination feeding is an option.** Some families breastfeed and supplement with formula, either by choice or necessity. This is a valid approach — it doesn't have to be all or nothing. If you're combining, pumping when you give a bottle helps maintain your milk supply.

## What NOT to give before 6 months

- **Water** — babies get all the hydration they need from breast milk or formula. Extra water can dilute their blood sodium levels, which is dangerous.
- **Juice** — no nutritional benefit, and the sugar is not appropriate for infants.
- **Solid foods** — your baby's digestive system and swallowing coordination are not ready before approximately 4–6 months.
- **Cow's milk** — it does not provide the right balance of nutrients for infants and can be difficult to digest.
- **Honey** — never before 12 months due to botulism risk.

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Call your doctor promptly:**
- Your baby has not had a wet diaper in 8 or more hours
- Weight loss continues beyond 2 weeks or your baby has not regained birth weight by 2–3 weeks
- Your baby is excessively sleepy and difficult to wake for feedings
- Signs of dehydration — dry mouth, no tears when crying, sunken fontanelle (soft spot)
- Your baby refuses to feed entirely
- Projectile vomiting after most feedings (not just spit-up — projectile vomiting travels across the room)
- Blood in your baby's stool

**🟡 Mention at your next pediatric visit:**
- Frequent spit-up that doesn't seem to bother the baby (likely normal reflux)
- Concerns about how much your baby is eating
- Questions about whether your baby needs vitamin D drops
- Fussiness that seems related to feeding but is not severe
- You're interested in combination feeding and want guidance on how to balance it

> **A note on the early weeks:** The first days and weeks of feeding can feel chaotic and overwhelming. Your baby is learning to eat. You are learning to feed them. It's messy, tiring, and imperfect — and that's normal. If you and your baby are figuring it out together, you are doing it right. The people who make it look easy on the internet are either lying or have forgotten.
