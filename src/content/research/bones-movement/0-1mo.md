## Research Brief

In the first month of life, the newborn skeleton is largely cartilaginous with approximately 270 bones (vs. 206 in adults), connected by flexible sutures and fontanelles that allowed passage through the birth canal. Movement at this stage is dominated by primitive reflexes (Moro, grasp, rooting) rather than voluntary motor control. The most common musculoskeletal concern is birth-related clavicle fracture. Vitamin D supplementation (400 IU/day) should begin within the first few days of life for breastfed infants.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- An asymmetric Moro reflex (one arm does not respond) can indicate clavicle fracture or brachial plexus injury — this needs medical evaluation
- Fontanelle assessment: a bulging fontanelle may indicate increased intracranial pressure; a deeply sunken fontanelle may indicate dehydration — neither is a bone issue per se but parents check the "soft spot"
- Skull molding after vaginal delivery is normal and resolves within days — parents should not be alarmed
- "Floppy baby" (hypotonia) at birth can indicate neuromuscular conditions — this overlaps with bone/movement concerns
- Vitamin D supplementation messaging should not discourage breastfeeding — frame as "breast milk is perfect nutrition PLUS vitamin D drops"
