## Research Brief

Between 6 months and 2 years, routines become the backbone of daily life — nap transitions, mealtime schedules, bedtime rituals, daycare drop-off procedures, travel logistics, and the challenge of maintaining consistency across multiple caregivers. This article covers nap transitions, building mealtime and bedtime routines, daycare drop-off strategies, travel with a baby/toddler, and cross-caregiver consistency.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Nap Transitions
- Key points: 3→2 at 6-9 months, 2→1 at 12-18 months, temporary disruption, readiness signs
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: When your baby starts fighting the third nap, it's not regression — it's time to drop it

### Section 2: Mealtime Routines
- Key points: Scheduled meals > grazing, consistency, self-feeding, family meals when possible
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: The mess of self-feeding is the price of independence. It's worth it.

### Section 3: Bedtime Routines
- Key points: 20-30 minutes, screen-free before bed, same sequence, same time
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The bedtime routine is the most valuable 20 minutes of your day

### Section 4: Daycare Drop-Off and Travel
- Key points: Consistent goodbye, no sneaking, travel with portable routines, jet lag recovery
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: A good goodbye routine takes 30 seconds and prevents 30 minutes of tears

### Section 5: Consistency Across Caregivers
- Key points: Same basic routines, written guides, "consistent enough," managing resistance
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Write your routine down and give it to every caregiver. It's not controlling — it's helpful.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese grandparent caregivers may have very different routine preferences — written guides help bridge the gap
- Travel between China and abroad disrupts routines significantly — jet lag and cultural adjustment compound
- Mealtime routines in Chinese culture include specific foods and customs — leverage existing cultural practices
- Transition warnings may be less common in Chinese parenting — present evidence for their effectiveness
