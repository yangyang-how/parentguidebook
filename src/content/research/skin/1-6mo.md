## Research Brief

The 1-6 month period is when atopic dermatitis (eczema) most commonly first appears, with 45% of cases starting before 6 months. Infantile seborrheic dermatitis (cradle cap) is also prevalent during this window. Differentiating between the two conditions can be challenging due to overlapping onset periods. Emollient use is the cornerstone of skin care, though evidence on whether emollients can prevent eczema onset is mixed.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Eczema that fails to respond to appropriate treatment may be misdiagnosed — consider scabies, contact dermatitis, or immunodeficiency
- Infected eczema (weeping, crusting, sudden worsening) needs medical attention; eczema herpeticum is a medical emergency
- Parents often confuse cradle cap with eczema — reassurance about cradle cap's benign nature is important
- Evidence on emollient prevention of eczema is genuinely mixed — avoid making definitive prevention claims
- Steroid phobia is very common among parents; article should address it without dismissing parental concerns
- Skin of color: eczema may appear gray, purple, or dark brown rather than red; hypopigmentation may follow flares
