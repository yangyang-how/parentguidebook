## Research Brief

School-age children face an expanding world of stressors — academic pressure, social dynamics, bullying, family changes — and their mental health concerns become increasingly visible but also increasingly complex. This article covers childhood anxiety and depression (which often co-occur), school refusal, the impact of bullying on mental health, ADHD as a common comorbidity, family therapy approaches, and building emotional vocabulary as a protective factor.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Anxiety — The Most Common and Most Missed
- Key points: 7-9% of children, often mistaken for shyness or perfectionism, USPSTF screening recommendation, somatic complaints
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The child with the stomachache every Monday morning might not be faking

### Section 2: Depression in Children Looks Different
- Key points: 4.4% prevalence, irritability > sadness at this age, declining grades, social withdrawal
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: Childhood depression doesn't look like adult depression — it looks like anger, defiance, or just "not caring"

### Section 3: School Refusal — More Than Just Not Wanting to Go
- Key points: 1-5% affected, anxiety-driven, somatic complaints, rapid return matters
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Forcing a child back to school without addressing the anxiety doesn't work. Keeping them home indefinitely makes it worse.

### Section 4: Bullying and Its Aftermath
- Key points: 20% experience bullying, mental health consequences, cyberbullying amplification, both parties need help
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Ask your child regularly, specifically, whether they're being bullied. Don't wait for them to tell you.

### Section 5: ADHD and Comorbidity
- Key points: 9.8% prevalence, comorbidity is the norm, multi-setting diagnosis, treatment by age
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: If your child has ADHD, the question isn't just "do they have ADHD" — it's "what else might be going on too"

### Section 6: Building Emotional Intelligence as Protection
- Key points: Emotional vocabulary, naming feelings, family therapy, CBT for children, parent involvement
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 8, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: Teaching your child 50 words for feelings is one of the cheapest, most effective mental health interventions that exists

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Academic pressure in Chinese culture (学业压力) is a primary stressor for school-age children — the article should address this directly
- In Chinese culture, seeking mental health treatment for a child carries enormous stigma — "face" concerns may prevent families from getting help
- ADHD is under-diagnosed in Chinese populations due to cultural expectations of obedience and the stigma of psychiatric labels
- Bullying dynamics in Chinese schools may differ (academic ranking-based bullying, teacher-mediated shaming) — acknowledge cultural variation
- The 8-10 year treatment delay is a crisis — every article should emphasize early identification
