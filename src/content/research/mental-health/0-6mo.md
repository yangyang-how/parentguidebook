## Research Brief

The first six months postpartum are the highest-risk window for parental mental health crises. This article covers postpartum depression vs baby blues, postpartum anxiety (often missed), screening at pediatric well-visits, paternal depression, the impact of parental mental health on infant bonding and development, and when/how to get help. The AAP's recommendation to screen mothers at pediatric visits was a landmark shift in recognizing that infant and maternal health are inseparable.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Baby Blues vs Postpartum Depression — How to Tell the Difference
- Key points: Baby blues (80%, resolves in 2 weeks) vs PPD (10-15%, persists), 2-week rule
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Feeling overwhelmed after birth is nearly universal. Feeling overwhelmed at 6 weeks is a signal to get help.

### Section 2: Postpartum Anxiety — The Condition Nobody Talks About
- Key points: 15-20% of mothers, intrusive thoughts, hypervigilance, often dismissed as "normal worry"
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: If you can't sleep even when the baby sleeps, that's not dedication — that may be anxiety

### Section 3: How Parental Mental Health Affects Your Baby
- Key points: Disrupted interaction, reduced engagement, cognitive effects, buffering by other caregivers
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: Getting help for yourself IS helping your baby

### Section 4: Fathers and Partners Get It Too
- Key points: 8-10% of fathers, peaks at 3-6 months, higher when partner also affected, EPDS works
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Partners: if you're struggling, you're not weak — you're experiencing something with a name and a treatment

### Section 5: When It's an Emergency
- Key points: Postpartum psychosis (1-2 per 1,000), rapid onset, hallucinations/delusions, hospitalization needed
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: If a new parent suddenly seems confused, hears things, or seems disconnected from reality — this is a medical emergency. Call 911.

### Section 6: Getting Help — Screening and Resources
- Key points: AAP screens at well-visits, ACOG screens postpartum, hotlines, therapy options
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 4, Source 7, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: Here are the concrete resources. Use them.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, the 坐月子 (postpartum confinement) period can both protect (rest, support) and isolate (limited autonomy, family pressure). The article should acknowledge both.
- Chinese families may attribute PPD to being "spoiled" or "not tough enough" — this needs direct, compassionate correction
- Paternal depression is virtually unrecognized in Chinese culture. This section may face cultural resistance but is important.
- Postpartum psychosis is sometimes confused with "possession" in some cultural contexts — the article should be clear this is a medical condition
- Screening at pediatric visits is genius because it catches mothers who may not have their own postpartum follow-up
