# Eating Disorders & Bone Health — The Hidden Skeletal Impact

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — talk to your doctor if you notice warning signs. If your child has an eating disorder and is experiencing bone pain or fractures, seek care promptly.**

**What's the connection?** Eating disorders can cause rapid, severe bone loss during the exact years when your child's body is supposed to be building bone for life. Up to 90% of young people with anorexia nervosa have low bone density, and significant bone loss can occur in less than a year. [Source: src1]

**Key facts:**
- Your child builds roughly 90% of their lifetime bone mass by age 18-20 — eating disorders disrupt this critical window [Source: src3]
- Bone loss from eating disorders happens through multiple pathways: hormone disruption, nutritional deficiency, and stress hormones [Source: src2]
- Missed menstrual periods in teenage girls are never "just stress" — they signal that bone health may be at risk [Source: src2]
- Male athletes and boys with eating disorders are affected too — this is not only a girls' issue [Source: src5]
- Bone recovery requires treating the eating disorder itself — calcium supplements alone are not enough [Source: src2]

**What to do:** If your child shows signs of disordered eating, excessive exercise, food restriction, or missed periods — or if they've had a stress fracture — talk to their doctor. Early intervention protects bones that are still being built.

## Why adolescence is the critical window

To understand why eating disorders are so dangerous to bone health, you first need to understand what's happening inside your child's skeleton during these years.

Your child's bones are not finished products. They are under construction — actively, rapidly, urgently under construction. Between the ages of roughly 10 and 18, the skeleton is laying down the mineral density that will have to last an entire adult lifetime. About 90% of peak bone mass is achieved by age 18-20. [Source: src3]

The pace of this construction is staggering. During the two years surrounding peak pubertal growth — around age 12.5 for girls and age 14 for boys — adolescents accumulate over 25% of their total adult bone mass. A quarter of the skeleton, built in just two years. [Source: src4]

Think of it as a bone bank account. These years are when the largest deposits are being made. The balance your child reaches by their late teens and early twenties is essentially the maximum they'll ever have. After about age 30, everyone begins to slowly lose bone. The higher the peak, the more there is to lose before bone density drops into a danger zone. [Source: src3]

Half of a woman's total body calcium and up to two-thirds of a man's are deposited during puberty. [Source: src6] This is why anything that interferes with nutrition, hormones, or physical health during these years doesn't just cause a temporary setback — it can permanently lower the ceiling of what the skeleton can achieve.

This is the biological backdrop against which eating disorders do their damage.

## How eating disorders destroy bone

Eating disorders attack bone health through multiple, simultaneous pathways. This is not a simple matter of "not getting enough calcium." The mechanisms are complex, interconnected, and alarmingly efficient at dismantling bone. [Source: src2]

**Low estrogen.** When the body doesn't receive adequate energy, it responds by reducing the production of reproductive hormones — most critically, estrogen in girls and testosterone in boys. Estrogen is not just a reproductive hormone; it is one of the skeleton's most important protectors. It slows the rate at which bone is broken down and helps regulate the cycle of bone renewal. When estrogen drops, bone breakdown accelerates while new bone formation stalls. [Source: src2]

**Reduced IGF-1.** Insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1) is a hormone that directly stimulates bone formation. It's one of the key signals telling the skeleton to build new bone tissue. Dietary restriction suppresses IGF-1 production, which means the "build more bone" signal gets turned down at exactly the age when it should be turned up to maximum. [Source: src2]

**Elevated cortisol.** The body perceives chronic energy deficit as a threat — because it is one. In response, it increases production of cortisol, the stress hormone. Cortisol at chronically elevated levels actively promotes bone loss. It interferes with calcium absorption, suppresses bone-building cells (osteoblasts), and activates bone-destroying cells (osteoclasts). [Source: src1]

**Direct nutritional deficiency.** Beyond the hormonal effects, inadequate intake means the raw materials for bone construction — calcium, vitamin D, protein — are simply not available in sufficient quantities. You cannot build a house without bricks, and you cannot build bone without nutrients. Fewer than 15% of adolescent girls in the United States already meet the recommended daily intake of 1,300 mg of calcium even without an eating disorder. [Source: src7] Restricting food intake makes an already precarious situation dramatically worse.

These pathways don't operate in isolation. They compound each other. Low estrogen accelerates bone loss. Low IGF-1 slows bone formation. High cortisol does both. Nutritional deficiency removes the building materials. The result is a skeleton under siege from multiple directions at once.

## Anorexia nervosa and bone density

The numbers are stark, and they deserve to be stated plainly.

Up to 90% of young people with anorexia nervosa have low bone mineral density. [Source: src1]

More than 50% of girls with anorexia nervosa show significant bone loss in less than one year. [Source: src1]

Young people with anorexia have approximately three times the fracture risk of their peers. [Source: src1]

Those who develop an eating disorder at a younger age experience more severe bone health consequences, because they are unable to reach the peak bone mass their skeleton was designed to achieve. [Source: src1] A 13-year-old who develops anorexia is not just losing existing bone — they are losing the bone they were supposed to build over the next five to seven years. That window does not reopen.

This is not meant to frighten anyone. It is meant to convey why eating disorders are a medical emergency for the skeleton, and why early recognition and treatment matter so profoundly. Every month of active illness during these critical years is a month of bone deposits that may never be made.

## RED-S and the Female Athlete Triad

Not all disordered eating looks like a textbook eating disorder. In young athletes, the problem often hides behind words like "dedication," "discipline," and "commitment to the sport."

The **Female Athlete Triad** describes the interconnection of three conditions that frequently occur together in female athletes: disordered eating (or low energy availability), menstrual irregularity, and low bone density. [Source: src5]

The concept has since been broadened to **Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)**, which recognizes that the consequences of under-fueling affect far more than just these three areas — and that male athletes are vulnerable too. RED-S describes what happens when an athlete's energy intake is chronically insufficient for the energy demands of their sport and their growing body. [Source: src5]

The sequence often looks like this: A young athlete — a runner, gymnast, dancer, swimmer, wrestler, or participant in any sport with aesthetic or weight-class pressures — begins restricting food intake. This might be deliberate dieting, or it might be unintentional under-fueling driven by increasing training volumes without matching increases in food. The body, receiving insufficient energy, starts shutting down functions it considers non-essential for immediate survival. Reproductive function is among the first to go. [Source: src5]

Under-fueling can lead to stress fractures, delayed growth, poor muscle development, and prolonged recovery from training and injuries. [Source: src5] These are not abstract risks. They are the daily reality for too many young athletes whose adults — coaches, parents, and even medical providers — fail to recognize what is happening.

### Amenorrhea is a red flag, not a badge of honor

When a teenage girl who has already begun menstruating stops having periods — or when periods never start despite reaching the expected age — this is called amenorrhea. In the context of athletic training, there is a persistent and dangerous myth that missing periods is a normal consequence of hard training, or even a sign of peak fitness.

**It is neither. Amenorrhea in a teenage athlete is never normal, and it always warrants medical evaluation.** [Source: src5]

Missing periods means the body has suppressed estrogen production because it doesn't have enough energy to support both reproduction and the demands being placed on it. And as we've discussed, low estrogen directly translates to bone loss. When teenage girls miss periods frequently, they may not absorb the amount of calcium needed to reach peak bone mass. [Source: src2]

If a coach, trainer, or even another parent tells you that missed periods are "just part of training," that advice is medically incorrect and potentially harmful to your child's long-term skeletal health.

### Male athletes are affected too

RED-S is not limited to female athletes. Boys and young men who restrict energy intake — whether for weight-class sports like wrestling, for aesthetic reasons in gymnastics or diving, or for perceived performance benefits in distance running — experience their own hormonal disruptions. Low testosterone in male athletes impairs bone health in ways parallel to low estrogen in female athletes. [Source: src5]

Because eating disorders in boys and men are less often recognized and more heavily stigmatized, male athletes with RED-S may go undiagnosed for longer. The bone consequences are just as real.

## Bone recovery requires treating the eating disorder

There is no shortcut around this fact: **bone health cannot be restored while an eating disorder remains active.** [Source: src2]

Calcium supplements do not fix the problem. Vitamin D supplements do not fix the problem. These are necessary nutrients, but they cannot overcome the hormonal disruption caused by chronic energy deficit. You cannot deposit money into a bank account if the bank is closed.

Weight restoration — reaching and maintaining a healthy body weight with adequate nutrition — is the single most important intervention for bone health in someone with an eating disorder. When adequate energy is consistently available, the body can resume normal hormone production, IGF-1 levels can recover, cortisol can normalize, and the skeleton can begin receiving the signals and materials it needs to rebuild. [Source: src2]

This does not mean bone recovery is instant or complete. Some degree of bone loss from an eating disorder may be permanent, particularly if the illness occurred during the peak bone-building years and lasted for an extended period. But recovery is possible, and it begins with treating the eating disorder. [Source: src1]

For adolescents, there is cautious reason for optimism: because the skeleton is still actively growing, young people may have greater capacity for bone recovery than adults — if treatment begins early and is sustained. [Source: src2]

## What parents can watch for

Eating disorders are skilled at hiding. They exploit shame, secrecy, and the cultural noise around diet and exercise. But there are signs you can learn to recognize.

### 🔴 See a doctor soon

These signs suggest a potentially serious situation that should be evaluated by a healthcare provider:

- **Stress fractures**, especially more than one, or in an unusual location — a stress fracture in a teenager can be the first visible evidence of weakened bone [Source: src1]
- **Missed periods** (in a girl who has previously menstruated) for three or more consecutive months [Source: src2]
- **Periods that never start** despite reaching age 15-16 [Source: src5]
- **Noticeable weight loss** or failure to gain weight/height as expected during puberty
- **Fainting, dizziness, or heart rate changes** — these suggest the body is under significant physiological stress
- **Your child tells you** they have a problem with eating, or someone else expresses concern — take this seriously every time

### 🟡 Watch closely and talk to your child

These patterns may or may not indicate an eating disorder, but they deserve your attention:

- **Increasingly rigid food rules** — cutting out entire food groups, refusing to eat foods they used to enjoy, elaborate rituals around eating
- **Exercising beyond what their sport requires** — training alone outside of practice, inability to take rest days, exercising despite injury or illness
- **Preoccupation with body weight, shape, or size** that seems to cause real distress
- **Eating very differently in front of others** than when alone (you may notice discrepancies between what they say they ate and what's actually gone from the kitchen)
- **Wearing baggy clothing** to hide weight changes
- **Withdrawing from meals with family** or friends
- **Mood changes** — increased irritability, anxiety, or depression, particularly around food or exercise
- **Fatigue and frequent illness** that seems disproportionate to their activity level

No single sign on this list is diagnostic. Many teenagers go through phases of picky eating or body-consciousness that are developmentally normal. What should heighten your concern is a pattern — multiple signs, increasing intensity, or changes that persist over weeks and months rather than days.

## How to approach the conversation

If you suspect your child may have disordered eating, the way you bring it up matters enormously. Eating disorders are tangled up with shame, control, identity, and fear. A conversation that feels like an accusation or an interrogation will almost certainly push your child further into secrecy.

**Lead with what you've observed, not what you suspect.** "I've noticed you seem really tired lately, and you haven't been eating much at dinner. I'm worried about you." This is very different from "I think you have an eating disorder."

**Express concern for their wellbeing, not their weight or appearance.** "I want to make sure your body is getting what it needs to be healthy" focuses on health. "You're getting too thin" focuses on appearance, which is exactly the lens an eating disorder uses.

**Listen more than you talk.** Your child may not be ready to acknowledge what's happening. That's okay. Planting the seed that you've noticed and you care — without demanding a confession — can be powerful. They know the door is open.

**Avoid blame.** Not blame toward them ("Why are you doing this to yourself?"), not blame toward yourself ("Where did I go wrong?"), and not blame toward anyone else in the initial conversation. Blame shuts people down. Concern opens them up.

**Don't try to be the treatment.** Your role is to notice, to care, to connect your child with professional help, and to be a steady, non-judgmental presence throughout recovery. You are not expected to be the therapist, the nutritionist, or the doctor. You are expected to be the parent — and that is enough.

If your child denies a problem but you remain concerned, trust your instincts. You can still bring it up with their pediatrician, even without your child's agreement. Doctors are trained to screen for eating disorders sensitively, and an outside perspective can sometimes reach a teenager in ways a parent's concern cannot.

## Getting help

Eating disorders are treatable medical conditions. Recovery is possible. But treatment is most effective when it involves a team — not a parent trying to manage it alone.

**Start with your child's pediatrician or primary care provider.** They can assess physical health, check bone density if warranted (using a DXA scan), screen for hormonal disruption, and refer to specialists.

**A treatment team typically includes:**
- A medical provider monitoring physical health
- A therapist specializing in eating disorders (family-based treatment, or FBT, is the gold standard for adolescents)
- A registered dietitian experienced in eating disorder recovery
- Sometimes a psychiatrist if medication is appropriate

**Bone-specific considerations:**
- A DXA scan can measure bone mineral density to establish a baseline
- In children and adolescents, DXA results should be interpreted using age- and sex-adjusted Z-scores — not the T-scores used for adults [Source: src8]
- Weight restoration and resumption of normal menstrual function (in girls) are the primary treatments for eating disorder-related bone loss [Source: src2]
- The role of medications like bisphosphonates (commonly used for osteoporosis in adults) is limited and still being studied in adolescents [Source: src2]

You are not alone in this, and you did not cause this. Eating disorders arise from a complex interaction of genetic vulnerability, brain chemistry, personality traits, and environmental pressures. They are not a choice, and they are not a reflection of your parenting. What matters now is what happens next — and the fact that you are reading this article suggests you are already paying attention. That matters more than you know.
