## Research Brief

Adolescent routines shift from parent-managed to self-managed — time management, increasing autonomy over daily structure, study habits, part-time job logistics, transportation/driving, and college preparation logistics all require teens to take ownership while parents provide scaffolding.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Time Management — The Skill Nobody Teaches
- Key points: Predicts success, rarely taught, tools need guided introduction
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Hand your teen a planner. Then show them how to use it. Then step back.

### Section 2: Autonomy Over Routines
- Key points: Core developmental task, autonomy + accountability, both over-control and under-involvement harmful
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Your teen needs to manage their own schedule — and face the consequences when they don't

### Section 3: Study Habits
- Key points: Dedicated space, scheduled blocks, phone away, spaced practice, early habits persist
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The study habits your teen builds by 10th grade are the ones they'll carry through college

### Section 4: Sleep Schedule
- Key points: 8-10 hours needed, consistent timing, caffeine/screens/irregularity are the enemies
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: A consistent bedtime is not babyish. It's the single most impactful routine for a teenager.

### Section 5: Work, Driving, and College Logistics
- Key points: ≤15-20 hrs work, driving safety, college prep timeline, teen owns the process
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 6, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Let them manage the college application. Let them manage the job. That's the real preparation.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese academic culture often means routines are dominated by study schedules — balance is especially important
- Part-time work is less common for Chinese teens focused on academics — present evidence for moderate work benefits
- College preparation logistics differ enormously between US and Chinese systems
- Chinese parents may be more involved in managing teen schedules — present evidence for gradual autonomy transfer
- Sleep deprivation is severe among Chinese high school students — the evidence on sleep and performance is critical here
