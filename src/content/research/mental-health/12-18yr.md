## Research Brief

Adolescence is when mental health conditions reach crisis proportions — suicide is the second leading cause of death for ages 10-24, depression and anxiety rates have surged (especially post-pandemic), and the window between symptom onset and treatment is devastatingly long. This article covers teen depression, suicide prevention, self-harm, the impact of social media on mental health, substance use as self-medication, access to care barriers, and crisis resources. The tone must be urgent without being alarmist.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The Numbers Are Alarming — And They're Getting Worse
- Key points: Suicide is #2 cause of death, 22% seriously considered it, post-pandemic surge
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: This is not about "kids these days." This is a public health emergency.

### Section 2: Recognizing Depression and Anxiety in Teens
- Key points: Teen depression looks like irritability/withdrawal, screening recommendations, 50% go untreated
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 9, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: A "moody teen" who stops seeing friends, sleeps all day, and doesn't care about anything isn't just being a teenager

### Section 3: Suicide — The Conversation We Must Have
- Key points: Asking doesn't increase risk, warning signs, screening tools, what to do right now
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Asking your teen "are you thinking about hurting yourself?" will not put the idea in their head. Not asking might cost their life.

### Section 4: Self-Harm — Understanding, Not Judging
- Key points: 15-20% of teens, emotional regulation function, not attention-seeking, risk factor for suicide, DBT
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: If you discover your teen is self-harming, your first response matters enormously. Lead with concern, not punishment.

### Section 5: Social Media and the Teen Brain
- Key points: >3 hrs/day doubles risk, comparison mechanism, passive vs active, sleep displacement
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: The problem isn't that your teen has a phone — it's what's happening in the 3+ hours they spend scrolling

### Section 6: Substance Use as Self-Medication
- Key points: 15% use illicit substances, 60% co-occurring mental health, self-medication cycle
- Supported by: Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: When a teen starts using substances, the question is not just "what are they taking?" but "what are they trying to not feel?"

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Suicide rates among Chinese-American adolescents are a specific concern — cultural factors include academic pressure, family conflict about identity, and stigma against seeking help
- In China, adolescent mental health services are severely under-resourced, and stigma is a major barrier
- The social media conversation must avoid moral panic while taking the evidence seriously
- LGBTQ+ youth face dramatically higher suicide risk — this must be included even though it's sensitive in Chinese cultural contexts
- Medication stigma is especially strong in Chinese families — the article should present evidence for medication when appropriate
- Crisis resources listed are US-based; Chinese equivalents should be included for the ZH version
