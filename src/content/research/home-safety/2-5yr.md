## Research Brief

Preschoolers are mobile, curious, and increasingly independent — but their judgment and impulse control are still immature. This article covers water safety (drowning remains the #1 killer at this age), playground safety, pedestrian safety, gun safety and storage, window guards, and the emerging independence that requires graduated freedom.

## Sources

- **Publication:** American Academy of Pediatrics
- **Date:** 2024
- **URL:** https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/148/2/e2021052227/179629/Prevention-of-Drowning
- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Water Safety Remains #1
- Key points: Drowning still leading cause, pool fencing 83% reduction, Water Watcher, swim lessons
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: If you have a pool, a fence saves more lives than any other single safety measure

### Section 2: Playground Safety
- Key points: 200,000+ ER visits, falls are primary, surfacing matters, age-appropriate equipment
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Check the surface under the playground. Concrete + fall = ER visit.

### Section 3: Pedestrian and Driveway Safety
- Key points: Under-10 can't judge traffic, driveway back-overs, hand-holding
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Walk behind your car before backing up. Every single time.

### Section 4: Gun Safety
- Key points: Lock/unload/separate ammo, ask at friends' homes, 3-year-olds can fire
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: The most important playdate question you'll ever ask: "Do you have guns in your home, and how are they stored?"

### Section 5: Fire, Windows, and Car Seats
- Key points: Fire escape practice, window guards, blind cords, car seat transitions
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 7, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Practice the fire escape plan until your child can do it without you directing them

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Gun safety section is US-specific — Chinese audience may not relate but safety around any weapons applies
- Water safety is universal — pools, ponds, and bathtubs are risks in every culture
- Playground standards differ between US and China — general principles apply regardless
- Car seat use compliance is lower in some Chinese communities — cultural education is needed
- Driveway safety applies globally but vehicle types and driving patterns differ
