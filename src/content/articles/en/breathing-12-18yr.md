# Your Teen's Breathing — Asthma, Vaping, and Airway Health

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — vaping is the defining respiratory threat for this generation, and asthma self-management is a skill teens need to build.**

**The short version:** Adolescence brings changes to asthma patterns (puberty can make it better or worse), growing independence in health management, and a major new threat: vaping. E-cigarettes cause real, documented respiratory harm — bronchitis, airway irritation, worsened asthma, and in severe cases, acute lung injury (EVALI). Meanwhile, exercise-induced bronchoconstriction affects up to 20% of teens and is often underdiagnosed because teens stop exercising rather than seeking help. Allergic rhinitis affects up to 40% of adolescents and impacts sleep and school performance more than most families realize.

**Key points:**
- Vaping causes bronchitis, worsened asthma, and can cause acute lung injury [Sources: src1, src7]
- Asthma patterns often change during puberty [Source: src5]
- Medication adherence drops significantly during adolescence [Source: src5]
- Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction affects up to 20% of people — even those without asthma [Source: src3]
- Allergic rhinitis affects up to 40% of teens and impairs sleep and academic performance [Source: src9]

## Asthma during puberty

If your teen has asthma, puberty may change their pattern. Some teens see their asthma improve — they have fewer attacks, need less medication, and feel like they're "growing out of it." Others see it worsen, with new triggers and more frequent symptoms. Both are normal. [Source: src5]

**What stays the same:** The fundamentals of asthma management — controller medication, trigger avoidance, and having a current action plan — don't change just because your teen is getting older.

**What changes:** Your teen should be transitioning toward managing their own asthma. This means: [Source: src5]
- Knowing their medications (what they take, why, and when)
- Recognizing their own symptoms and knowing when to act
- Carrying their rescue inhaler
- Refilling prescriptions (with your help initially)
- Communicating with their doctor directly (not through you)

### The adherence problem

Medication adherence drops significantly during adolescence. Teens forget doses, skip controller medication because they "feel fine," or stop using their inhaler because they don't want to look different in front of peers. [Source: src5]

**This is normal teen behavior — but it's risky.** Controller medications work by preventing inflammation over time. Stopping them because symptoms aren't present right now is like stopping sunscreen because you're not currently burned.

> **For parents:** You can't force a teen to take their medication, but you can have honest conversations about what happens when they don't. Framing it as "this protects your ability to play your sport / perform in band / hang out with friends without an attack" tends to resonate more than "because I said so." [Source: src5]

## Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction in teens

Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB) causes shortness of breath, coughing, chest tightness, and wheezing during or 5–15 minutes after physical activity. It affects up to 20% of people — including many who don't have a formal asthma diagnosis. [Source: src3]

**The underdiagnosis problem:** Teens with EIB often just stop exercising. They quit the team, skip PE, avoid running with friends — and never mention why. The breathing difficulty gets dismissed as being "out of shape," and nobody investigates further. [Source: src4]

**This is a significant concern** because avoiding exercise leads to worse outcomes: social isolation, weight gain, declining fitness, and paradoxically, worse airway health. Regular exercise actually reduces airway inflammation over time and decreases bronchial hyperresponsiveness. Avoidance makes everything worse. [Source: src4]

### Management

**Pre-exercise rescue inhaler:** SABA taken 10–15 minutes before exercise prevents EIB in most teens. [Source: src3]

**Non-medication strategies:** [Source: src4]
- Proper warm-up (10–15 minutes of gradually increasing intensity)
- Breathing through the nose or wearing a face covering in cold, dry air
- Choosing warm, humid environments when possible (swimming is often well-tolerated)
- Avoiding exercise on high-pollution or high-pollen days

**If symptoms persist despite SABA:** Daily ICS may be needed, especially for teens who exercise frequently. The goal is full participation in whatever activities they choose, not avoidance. [Source: src4]

> **For parents:** If your teen has quit a sport or started avoiding physical activity, ask why. If the answer involves breathing difficulty during exercise, that's not laziness — that's an undiagnosed or undertreated condition. Get them evaluated. Sport is not contraindicated in teens with EIB; regular exercise is recommended by international guidelines. [Source: src4]

## Vaping and your teen's lungs

This is the section that matters most for this generation.

### The damage is real

E-cigarettes are not harmless. Research shows that vaping: [Source: src1]
- Causes respiratory problems including bronchitis and airway irritation
- Increases asthma attacks and school absences in teens with asthma
- Leads to more severe asthma attacks in teens who already have asthma

Teens with pre-existing asthma who vape are **particularly vulnerable** because their airways are already inflamed. Vaping adds insult to injury. [Source: src2]

### EVALI: when vaping causes acute lung injury

E-cigarette or vaping product use-associated lung injury (EVALI) emerged as a serious concern in 2019. Symptoms include cough, shortness of breath, chest pain, fever, and gastrointestinal symptoms — and can mimic pneumonia. [Source: src7]

While vitamin E acetate in THC-containing products was identified as the primary cause of the 2019 outbreak, **nicotine-only products also carry respiratory risks.** The long-term effects of inhaling heated chemicals from any e-cigarette are not fully understood. [Source: src7]

### What teens notice themselves

In surveys, young adults who vape report declining exercise endurance, persistent cough, wheezing, and shortness of breath. Many recognize these symptoms but continue vaping anyway — nicotine addiction is powerful. [Source: src8]

### What parents can do

- **Talk about it directly.** Teens are more likely to listen if you state facts without lecturing. "Vaping causes documented lung damage — here's what the research shows" lands better than "vaping is bad."
- **Watch for signs:** persistent cough, declining fitness, dental erosion (from the acidity of vape aerosol), new or worsening asthma symptoms, and the devices themselves (small, USB-shaped, sometimes disguised as pens or highlighters)
- **Know that many teens hide it.** Vaping devices are easy to conceal, and many teens vape at school or at friends' houses
- **If your teen is already vaping:** approach it as an addiction issue (nicotine is genuinely addictive), not a moral failing. Cessation support is available — talk to your teen's doctor.

> **The uncomfortable truth:** E-cigarettes were marketed as "safer than smoking." For adults trying to quit cigarettes, that may have a grain of truth. For teens who never smoked cigarettes, vaping is adding a new lung-damaging habit where there was none before. It's not harm reduction — it's harm introduction. [Source: src1]

## Allergies, sinusitis, and nasal health

Allergic rhinitis (hay fever, chronic stuffiness) affects up to 40% of adolescents. It's often dismissed as "just allergies," but untreated nasal congestion has cascading effects: [Source: src9]

- **Sleep-disordered breathing** — chronic mouth breathing and snoring affect sleep quality
- **Fatigue** — poor sleep means poor daytime functioning
- **Academic performance** — drowsy, stuffy teens don't concentrate well
- **Mood** — chronic discomfort and poor sleep affect emotional regulation

**Treatment works.** Nasal corticosteroid sprays are the first-line treatment for moderate-to-severe allergic rhinitis. They're safe for daily use, available over the counter, and dramatically improve quality of life. Non-drowsy antihistamines help with eye and nose symptoms. [Source: src9]

> **For parents:** If your teen is always congested, always tired, and struggling in school, allergies may be a bigger factor than you think. This is a treatable problem with real impact — don't dismiss it as something they'll "grow out of." [Source: src9]

## When to see the doctor

**🔴 Go to the ER:**
- Rescue inhaler not working after repeated use
- Severe difficulty breathing — your teen can't speak in full sentences
- Blue or gray lips, fingernails, or face
- Chest pain with shortness of breath (especially in a teen who vapes)
- Cough producing blood
- Any symptoms that might suggest EVALI in a teen who vapes

**🟡 Schedule an appointment:**
- Persistent cough lasting more than 4 weeks
- Exercise intolerance that's new or worsening
- Needing rescue inhaler more than twice a week
- Declining fitness or endurance
- Chronic nasal congestion affecting sleep or school performance
- Suspicion of vaping with new respiratory symptoms
- Asthma that's been stable but is worsening
