# 6 Months to 2 Years

## If you only read one thing, read this

Your baby is about to become a person who *moves.* In the next eighteen months, they'll go from sitting up to walking, from babbling to talking, from milk to actual food. It's the fastest transformation in human development, and you have a front-row seat.

**Movement:** The range of normal is enormous. Some babies walk at 9 months, others at 17 months — both are fine. If they aren't walking by 18 months, talk to your pediatrician. Don't use baby walkers — they delay motor and mental development. [Source: src3]

**Food:** Start solids around 6 months. Introduce common allergens (peanut, egg, dairy) early — this *reduces* allergy risk. No honey before 12 months. At 12 months, switch from formula to whole cow's milk (16–24 oz per day, not more). Continue breastfeeding as long as you and your child want to. [Source: src7, src8, src11]

**Teeth:** Brush with a rice-grain smear of fluoride toothpaste from the first tooth. First dentist visit by age 1. [Source: src2]

**Sleep:** At 6–11 months they need 12–16 hours a day; at 1–2 years, 11–14 hours (both including naps). Naps will consolidate from three to two to one. Twenty to thirty percent of babies and toddlers still wake at night — this is more common than most people admit. [Source: src12, src13]

**When to call the doctor:** Any fever above 38°C (100.4°F) under 3 months remains an emergency. After 3 months, fever plus concerning symptoms (lethargy, difficulty breathing, refusal to drink, rash that doesn't blanch). A febrile seizure — terrifying to watch, but the prognosis is excellent: it does not cause brain damage. [Source: src6]

**You:** Parental burnout peaks around 12–18 months. If you're running on empty, emotionally distant from your child, or losing pleasure in parenting — that's not laziness, it's burnout, and it has a name and a treatment. Tell your doctor.

Everything else below is for when you're ready. You can stop here and come back later.

## Part 1: Your baby right now

### From sitting to walking — the motor revolution

At 6 months, your baby probably sits with support or is learning to sit alone. By their second birthday, they'll be running, climbing, and making you question every piece of furniture you own. Here's the typical progression — but remember, "typical" has a wide lane:

- **6–8 months:** Sits without support. Rolls in both directions. Pivots on their belly to reach things.
- **8–10 months:** Crawling begins — though some babies skip crawling entirely (army-crawl, scoot on their bottom, or go straight to pulling up). This is a normal variation, not a delay. [Source: src3]
- **9–12 months:** Pulling to stand. Cruising — walking while holding onto furniture, looking enormously pleased with themselves.
- **10–14 months:** Standing independently. Those first wobbly, Frankenstein-monster steps.
- **12–18 months:** Walking without holding on. May start running. Climbs on and off furniture. [Source: src3]

The WHO data on walking independently spans from 8 to 18 months (1st to 99th percentile). That's a ten-month range of perfectly normal. Your neighbor's baby who walked at 9 months is not more advanced than yours who walked at 14 months — they just walked earlier. [Source: src3]

**When to talk to your pediatrician:**
- Not pulling to stand by 12 months
- Not walking independently by 18 months
- Persistent toe-walking beyond age 2
- Any loss of skills they previously had (at any age — regression always warrants evaluation) [Source: src3]

**A note on baby walkers:** The AAP advises against them. They don't help babies learn to walk — they actually delay both motor and mental development. They're also a leading cause of infant injuries (falls down stairs, access to hazards). Skip them. [Source: src3]

**Flat feet** are normal in babies and toddlers. The arch develops between ages 2 and 6. No special shoes needed — in fact, barefoot time on safe surfaces is best for developing foot muscles. [Source: src3]

### The communication explosion

This is one of the most remarkable things you'll witness. In eighteen months, your child goes from "babababa" to "more milk, please" (or, more realistically, "MORE MILK" while banging the table).

**What to expect:**
- **4–7 months:** Babbling — repetitive consonant-vowel chains ("babababa," "mamamama"). This isn't language yet, but it's the foundation.
- **7–9 months:** Babbling gets more complex, with varied intonation that sounds almost like sentences. Responds to their name.
- **10–12 months:** First intentional words — "mama" and "dada" used with meaning. They understand roughly 50 words even if they say only a handful. [Source: src4]
- **12–15 months:** 3–5 words used consistently. Points at things they want or find interesting. Follows simple commands ("Give me the cup").
- **15–18 months:** Vocabulary grows to 10–25 words. Uses gestures alongside words.
- **18–24 months:** The vocabulary explosion. Fifty or more words. Two-word combinations appear ("go outside," "more cracker"). They may add several new words per day. By 24 months, a stranger can understand about half of what they say. [Source: src4, src16]

**Bilingual families:** Bilingual children hit one-word and two-word milestones at the same age as monolingual children. Their total conceptual vocabulary across both languages is broadly comparable to a monolingual child's, though vocabulary in each individual language may be smaller. Mixing languages in one sentence (code-switching) is normal and actually a sign of linguistic competence — not confusion. Bilingualism does not cause speech delay. [Source: src4]

**When to talk to your pediatrician:**
- No babbling by 7 months
- No words by 12 months
- Not responding to their name by 9 months
- Not pointing by 12 months
- Fewer than 50 words by 24 months
- No two-word combinations by 24 months
- Any loss of language they previously had [Source: src4]

About 15–20% of 2-year-olds are "late talkers." Roughly half to two-thirds of them catch up by age 3–4 without intervention. But late talking can also be an early sign of developmental language disorder, autism, or hearing loss — so always get it evaluated. Don't "wait and see" past 24 months. [Source: src16]

### Feeding — the big transition

Between 6 months and 2 years, your child goes from an all-liquid diet to eating at the family table. It's messy, it's slow, and it's one of the most anxiety-producing topics in parenting. Here's what the evidence says.

**Starting solids (~6 months):**
Begin with smooth, single-ingredient purees or soft finger foods. Both traditional spoon-feeding and baby-led weaning (where the infant self-feeds soft finger foods from the start) are safe approaches. A 2016 randomized trial published in *Pediatrics* found no significant difference in choking risk between the two methods when done with appropriate foods. [Source: src7]

**Texture progression:**
- ~6 months: smooth purees
- ~7–8 months: thicker purees, mashed foods with soft lumps
- ~9–10 months: finely chopped foods, soft finger foods
- ~12 months: modified table foods, soft pieces

Don't delay lumpy textures beyond 9 months — research links this to later feeding difficulties. [Source: src7]

**Gagging vs. choking — know the difference:**
- **Gagging:** Normal protective reflex. Noisy — coughing, sputtering, eyes watering. Face may redden. This is your baby's safety system working correctly.
- **Choking:** Airway obstruction. Silent — unable to cry or cough. Face may turn blue. This is an emergency. [Source: src7]

**High-risk choking foods to avoid:** Whole grapes, hot dog rounds, whole nuts, raw carrots, popcorn, hard candy, large chunks of meat, and large globs of nut butter. Cut round foods lengthwise. [Source: src7]

**Allergen introduction — a major shift in guidelines:**
Early introduction of allergenic foods is now *recommended* — a reversal of pre-2015 guidance. The landmark LEAP trial showed an approximately 86% relative risk reduction in peanut allergy when peanut was introduced at 4–11 months in high-risk infants. Real-world data suggests meaningful population-level declines since these guidelines were adopted. [Source: src8]

Introduce peanut (as thinned peanut butter or peanut flour mixed into purees — never whole peanuts), egg, dairy, tree nuts, wheat, soy, fish, and sesame early and regularly. For high-risk infants (severe eczema or egg allergy), consider allergy testing before peanut introduction. For everyone else, introduce at home around 6 months with other solids. [Source: src8]

**Signs of an allergic reaction:** Hives, rash, swelling around the mouth, vomiting, excessive crying, back arching. Severe (anaphylaxis): difficulty breathing, widespread hives, throat swelling — use epinephrine immediately and call emergency services. [Source: src8]

**Key nutrition rules:**
- Continue breast milk or formula alongside solids through 12 months
- No honey before 12 months (botulism risk)
- No added salt or sugar for infants
- Offer a diverse range of nutrient-dense foods [Source: src7]

**The cow's milk transition (12 months):**
At 12 months, you can introduce plain, pasteurized whole cow's milk as a primary drink. Whole milk — not reduced-fat — because the fat content supports brain development through age 2. About 16 oz per day (2 cups) is a good target; don't exceed 24 oz (3 cups), as excess cow's milk is associated with iron deficiency. [Source: src11]

Only fortified soy milk is nutritionally equivalent to cow's milk. Other plant milks (oat, almond, rice, coconut) are not recommended as primary replacements — they lack adequate protein and nutrients. Toddler formulas are unnecessary for most children and often contain added sugars. [Source: src11]

**Weaning:**
The WHO and AAP (updated 2022) both support breastfeeding for 2 years or beyond, as mutually desired. There is no single "right" time to wean, and no single "right" approach. Gradual weaning — dropping one feed at a time — generally works better than abrupt weaning for both parent and child. Weaning can trigger genuine grief and sadness in mothers; this is normal and valid. [Source: src9]

**Picky eating (~18 months onward):**
Food neophobia — the fear of new foods — typically emerges around 18–24 months. Up to 20% of children ages 2–3 are described as picky eaters. This is thought to be an evolutionary protective mechanism, and it usually peaks around age 2–3 and improves. [Source: src10]

The evidence-based approach is the Satter Division of Responsibility: the parent decides *what* food is offered, *when* meals happen, and *where*; the child decides *whether* to eat and *how much.* Don't force, bribe, or restrict. Continue offering new foods — it may take 15 or more exposures before a child accepts something. [Source: src10]

**Iron at 12 months:** Toddlers need 7 mg of iron per day. The transition from iron-fortified formula to table foods is a risk period, especially for picky eaters. Excess cow's milk (above 24 oz/day) both displaces iron-rich foods and impairs iron absorption. Your pediatrician should screen for iron deficiency at 12 months. [Source: src10]

### Teeth

The first teeth — usually the lower central incisors — appear around 6–10 months, though some babies don't get a tooth until after their first birthday. The full sequence: lower central incisors first, then upper central incisors (8–12 months), lateral incisors (9–16 months), first molars (13–19 months), canines (16–23 months), and second molars (23–33 months). A full set of 20 primary teeth is complete by roughly age 2.5–3. [Source: src2]

**Brushing:** Start with the first tooth. Use a child-sized soft-bristled brush with a rice-grain-sized smear of fluoride toothpaste. Brush twice daily. Parents should do the brushing — children don't have the dexterity to brush effectively until about age 6–7. [Source: src2]

**Fluoride varnish** can be applied by your pediatrician once the first tooth erupts (typically around 6 months), every 6 months through age 5. [Source: src2]

**First dentist visit:** By age 1, or within 6 months of the first tooth, whichever comes first. [Source: src2]

### Sleep

**How much they need:**
- 4–11 months: 12–16 hours per day (including naps)
- 1–2 years: 11–14 hours per day (including naps) [Source: src12]

**Nap transitions:**
- 6–9 months: 2–3 naps per day
- 12–18 months: transition from 2 naps to 1 (usually the morning nap drops first)
- 18–24 months: 1 nap per day, typically midday, lasting 1–2 hours [Source: src12]

Nap transitions can take several weeks. Expect some crankiness while they adjust.

**Night waking:** By 6 months, many babies can sleep 6–8 hour stretches. But 20–30% of infants and toddlers continue to wake at night, and this is also within the range of normal. The difference between a "good sleeper" and a "bad sleeper" is often whether the baby can self-soothe back to sleep between sleep cycles — not whether they wake up at all. [Source: src13]

**Common causes of disrupted sleep:**
- Separation anxiety (peaks around 8–10 months and again at ~18 months)
- Teething discomfort (though the evidence that teething causes major sleep disruption is weaker than commonly believed)
- Motor milestone practice — babies may stand up in the crib and then not know how to get back down
- Illness, especially ear infections [Source: src13]

**Sleep training:** Studies on graduated extinction ("Ferber method") and bedtime fading show improvements in infant sleep without adverse effects on attachment, cortisol levels, or behavioral development. The AAP does not endorse one specific method but supports parents in finding an approach that works for their family. [Source: src13]

**Safe sleep reminders:**
- Continue back-to-sleep positioning until your child naturally rolls both ways
- The highest SIDS risk is 1–4 months; 90% of SIDS occurs before 6 months; risk drops substantially after 12 months
- After 12 months, soft objects in the crib pose less risk, but wearable blankets remain a good option
- Room-sharing (not bed-sharing) is recommended for at least the first 6 months, ideally 12 months
- Safe sleep practices should continue throughout infancy [Source: src14]

**Bedtime routines matter:** Evidence shows consistent routines — bath, pajamas, books, a song, cuddles — improve infant and toddler sleep. Twenty to forty-five minutes, ending in the room where the child sleeps. Predictability signals the brain to prepare for sleep. [Source: src12]

### Common illnesses

Between 6 months and 2 years, your baby's immune system is meeting the world for the first time. They will get sick. Frequently. Here are the most common illnesses and what to know about each.

**RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus):**
The leading cause of infant hospitalization in the United States. Over half of infants are infected in their first year of life. Prevention has improved dramatically: nirsevimab (brand name Beyfortus) — a single-dose monoclonal antibody — is now the recommended prevention for all infants. RSV hospitalization rates have dropped significantly since these prevention products became available. Ask your pediatrician. [Source: src6]

**Ear infections (Acute Otitis Media):**
The most common reason young children receive antibiotics. Twenty-three percent of children have at least one ear infection by age 1; 60% by age 3. Watchful waiting is appropriate for many uncomplicated cases. If your child has three or more infections in 6 months or four or more in 12 months, your pediatrician may discuss ear tubes — the most common surgery in children. [Source: src6]

**Croup:**
Most common in children 6 months to 3 years. You'll know it by the barking cough that sounds like a seal, a hoarse voice, and stridor (a high-pitched noise when breathing in). It's worse at night. Duration is typically 3–5 days. Cold night air is a traditional clinical recommendation that many parents find helpful. Your pediatrician may prescribe a single dose of dexamethasone, which begins working in about 30 minutes. [Source: src6]

**Febrile seizures:**
Affect 2–5% of children ages 6 months to 5 years, with a peak at 12–18 months. A febrile seizure is a seizure triggered by fever. If your child has one, it will be one of the most frightening things you ever witness. But here is what the evidence says: **simple febrile seizures do not cause brain damage, intellectual disability, or death.** The prognosis is excellent. If your child has a first-time febrile seizure, seek immediate medical evaluation. About 30–50% of children who have one will have another, but the AAP does not recommend anticonvulsant treatment for simple febrile seizures — the focus should be on identifying and treating the fever's cause, not the seizure itself. [Source: src6]

**Hand-Foot-Mouth Disease (HFMD):**
Extremely common in daycare settings. Caused by coxsackievirus. Symptoms: fever, sore throat, painful mouth sores, and a rash of flat or slightly raised red spots on the hands and feet. Duration: 7–10 days. No specific treatment — pain management with acetaminophen or ibuprofen (ibuprofen is safe from 6 months on). It looks awful, but it resolves on its own. [Source: src6]

### Skin

**Eczema** affects up to 25% of children, with about 60% developing it in the first year. In infants, it typically appears on the face, cheeks, and scalp; by toddlerhood, it shifts to the creases of elbows and knees. There's no cure, but it's controllable: daily moisturizing, avoiding triggers (fragrances, harsh soaps), short lukewarm baths, and topical corticosteroids for active flares. [Source: src5]

**Sun protection:** From 6 months on, apply broad-spectrum SPF 30+ mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide — less irritating for babies) to all exposed skin. Reapply every 2 hours. Protective clothing, wide-brimmed hats, and shade remain your first line of defense. [Source: src5]

### Emotional development — tantrums begin

Tantrums typically start between 12 and 18 months, peak at 2–3 years, and gradually taper by age 4–5. They are normal, expected, and not a sign of bad parenting or a difficult child. [Source: src17]

**Why they happen — the neuroscience:** Your toddler's amygdala (the brain's emotion and stress center) is developing faster than their prefrontal cortex (the part responsible for impulse control and emotional regulation). The prefrontal cortex won't be fully mature until their mid-twenties. Right now, it's barely online. Your toddler literally *cannot* regulate big emotions the way an adult can. The hardware isn't built yet. [Source: src17]

**What to do:**
- Stay calm — your child mirrors your emotional state
- Name the feeling: "You're frustrated because you wanted the red cup"
- Redirect or distract when possible
- Be consistent in your responses
- Prevention helps: predictable routines, adequate sleep and food, offering choices ("red cup or blue cup?")
- After the tantrum: brief, calm reconnection — don't lecture [Source: src17]

**When to be concerned:** Tantrums lasting more than 25 minutes regularly, self-injury during tantrums, persistent aggression toward others, or tantrums increasing in frequency after age 4. [Source: src17]

**Separation anxiety** peaks around 8–10 months and again at about 18 months — this is linked to their strengthening attachment bonds and developing memory. They're deeply bonded to you and aware when you leave, but they can't yet predict that you'll return. It usually fades by age 2. [Source: src13, src15]

### Screen time

**Under 18 months:** The AAP recommends avoiding screen media other than video calls with family. Video chat is explicitly exempted — research suggests infants can learn from live video interaction, unlike passive video. [Source: src18]

**18–24 months:** If you choose to introduce screens, select high-quality content and watch together — co-viewing and interacting makes a significant difference. [Source: src18]

**Why the guidelines exist:** Children under 2 learn better from live interaction than from screens. Screen time in this age range is associated with reduced language skills (because it displaces parent-child interaction), shorter nighttime sleep, and potential attention difficulties. Even background TV — when the child isn't watching — reduces the quality and quantity of parent-child interaction. [Source: src18]

## Part 2: You, the parent

The first months were about survival. Now you're past the immediate crisis — and that's when the slower, deeper challenges can surface.

### Mental health

**Late-onset postpartum depression and anxiety:** PPD and PPA don't always appear in the first few weeks. They can onset or persist well beyond 6–12 months postpartum. An estimated 10–15% of parents have clinically significant depression or anxiety symptoms at 12+ months. "Self-blame" has been identified as a distinct bridge symptom in the 13–24 month period — the voice that says everything you're doing is wrong. If you hear that voice constantly, it may be depression talking, not reality. [Source: src19]

**Parental burnout (12–18 months):** This is different from PPD, though they can overlap. Burnout is characterized by emotional exhaustion, emotional distancing from your child, and a loss of pleasure in parenting. It can develop from postpartum depression or independently, and the two can reinforce each other. Risk factors include perfectionism, lack of support, high parenting demands, and poor sleep — which describes the situation of essentially every parent of a toddler. [Source: src19]

**Being "touched out":** The clinical term for that moment when one more tiny hand grabbing your face makes you want to scream. It's a real phenomenon — a profound loss of bodily autonomy where your body feels like communal property. It can be both a symptom of and a trigger for postpartum anxiety and depression. It's more common with breastfeeding toddlers and co-sleeping. Management: communicate your boundaries to your partner, carve out physical space for yourself, and know that needing to not be touched does not make you a bad parent. [Source: src19]

**The identity shift:** One in five mothers reports that the motherhood identity shift — where personal identity becomes deeply intertwined with the caregiver role — is a trigger for postpartum depression. Fathers also experience identity disruption, though it's less studied. Your brain processes this identity shift similarly to grief, which is why it can feel like loss even when you're not losing anything. Maintaining pre-parenthood activities, relationships, and interests isn't selfish — it's protective. [Source: src19]

### Relationships

The research is consistent: most couples experience a significant decline in relationship satisfaction in the first two years after a baby. Women's satisfaction tends to decline more than men's. The single strongest predictor of dissatisfaction is the perception of unfair division of labor — not sleep deprivation, not money, not reduced intimacy. [Source: src20]

**What helps (evidence-based):**
- Regular structured conversations about the relationship — even 15–20 minutes daily
- Expressing gratitude specifically for parenting contributions ("Thank you for handling bath time so I could rest")
- Constructive conflict: problem-solving and negotiation, not criticism and stonewalling
- Seeking counseling proactively, not as a last resort [Source: src20]

### Return to work and childcare

If you're returning to work, here's what you should know: quality of parenting is not diminished by working. Research consistently shows this. What matters is the quality of your interactions during the time you're together, not the quantity of hours. Consistent morning and evening rituals, responsive caregiving when you're present, and reading together daily go a long way. [Source: src21]

**Childcare quality indicators to look for:**
- Staff-to-child ratios (NAEYC standards: 4:1 for infants, 6:1 for toddlers)
- Consistent caregivers with low turnover
- NAEYC accreditation (the gold standard)
- Responsive interactions — do the caregivers talk to the children, get down to their level, respond warmly? [Source: src21]

### Physical health — yours

**Cumulative sleep deprivation:** Parents average 5.5–6 hours of fragmented sleep per night in the first year. Cumulative sleep debt impairs emotional regulation, immune function, and cardiovascular health. Poor maternal sleep continuity directly reduces sensitivity toward the infant. This isn't a character flaw — it's neuroscience. If you can find ways to improve your sleep (trading off with a partner, accepting help, adjusting the baby's sleep schedule), prioritize it. [Source: src22]

**Back pain:** Your baby's weight roughly triples by 12 months [Source: src1], and you're carrying them constantly. Alternate carrying sides, use structured carriers that distribute weight across your hips and shoulders, and strengthen your core. If pain persists beyond 2 weeks or radiates, see a provider. [Source: src22]

**Ongoing postpartum recovery:** Diastasis recti (separation of abdominal muscles) affects about 60% of postpartum women — if the gap is wider than 2 finger-widths at 3+ months postpartum, a physiotherapist can help. Pelvic floor dysfunction (incontinence, prolapse) is not "just part of being a parent" — it's a treatable condition. Pelvic floor physical therapy can evaluate and address these issues. [Source: src22]

## Part 3: Safety

Your baby is becoming mobile, and your home just became a completely different environment. Childproofing is not a one-time event — it's staged, because your child's abilities change every few months.

**By mobility stage:**

- **Rolling/sitting (5–8 months):** Clear floor-level hazards. Keep small objects out of reach. Anything within arm's length from a seated baby is fair game.
- **Crawling (7–10 months):** Outlet covers. Cabinet locks. Stair gates at the top AND bottom of every staircase. Secure all furniture to the walls — furniture and TV tip-overs are a leading cause of injury and death in young children. [Source: src23]
- **Pulling to stand/cruising (9–12 months):** Remove tablecloths (they'll pull everything on top of themselves). Pad sharp corners. Secure tall furniture and TVs.
- **Walking (10–18 months):** Doorknob covers. Toilet locks. Move all cleaning products and medications to high, locked cabinets.
- **Climbing (18–24+ months):** Re-secure everything higher. Window locks and guards on all windows above the first floor. Remove anything a toddler could use as a step stool near counters or windows. [Source: src23]

**The most common injuries:**
- Falls: the number one cause of injury at every age in this range
- Burns: pulling pots off stoves, grabbing hot items at table height, bathwater (set your water heater to 49°C / 120°F maximum)
- Poisoning: increases dramatically with walking and climbing — medications are the number one cause
- Drowning: the number one cause of death for ages 1–4. Children can drown in as little as one inch of water. Never leave a child unattended near any water — bathtub, pool, bucket
- Choking: peaks from 6 months to 3 years with the introduction of solids [Source: src23]

**Three actions every household should take:**
1. Anchor all furniture and TVs to the wall
2. Post the Poison Control number where everyone can see it (US: 1-800-222-1222)
3. Every caregiver learns infant/toddler CPR and choking first aid [Source: src23]

**Car seat guidelines:** Keep your child rear-facing as long as possible — until they reach the maximum height or weight for their seat. Most convertible seats allow rear-facing to 40–50 pounds, which typically means age 2–4. The AAP recommends extended rear-facing because it provides significantly better protection for a young child's head, neck, and spine in a crash. Don't put bulky winter coats under the harness — dress in thin layers and cover with a blanket over the straps. [Source: src24]

## Part 4: When to call the doctor

### For your child

**Call the same day or go to the emergency department if:**

- **Fever:** Above 38°C (100.4°F) in a baby under 3 months — always urgent. In older infants: high fever (above 39°C / 102.2°F) lasting more than 24 hours, or any fever with lethargy, rash, or difficulty breathing.
- **Breathing difficulty:** Rapid breathing, ribs pulling in, nasal flaring, wheezing, or blue/dusky color.
- **Dehydration:** Fewer than 6 wet diapers per day (after the newborn period). No wet diaper for 8+ hours. Dry mouth. Sunken soft spot.
- **Vomiting:** Forceful or projectile vomiting. Any green (bilious) vomit. Vomiting with signs of dehydration.
- **Seizure:** First-time seizure of any kind. (If your child has had a simple febrile seizure before and your pediatrician has given you guidance, follow that plan — but call to report it.)
- **Rash:** Any rash that doesn't blanch when pressed (petechiae) — could indicate a serious infection.
- **Allergic reaction:** Hives, facial swelling, difficulty breathing after eating a new food.
- **Falls:** Fall from a height greater than their own, or any fall with loss of consciousness, vomiting, or behavior change.
- **Lethargy:** Unusually difficult to wake, floppy, weak cry, not interested in surroundings.
- **Something seems off:** You don't need a specific symptom. Parental instinct is a valid reason to call.

[Sources: src6, src8]

### For you

**Call your own doctor if you experience:**

- Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness lasting more than 2 weeks
- Anxiety that interferes with eating, sleeping, or caring for your child
- Emotional exhaustion and detachment from your child (burnout)
- Thoughts of harming yourself or your baby
- Inability to care for yourself
- Persistent back pain, incontinence, or pelvic pain (treatable — not something to endure in silence)
- If you just don't feel right — this counts too

[Sources: src19, src22]

## One last thing

Six months to two years is the stage where parenting shifts from crisis mode to marathon mode. The emergencies get rarer, but the demands don't let up. You're not just keeping a baby alive anymore — you're raising a person. A person who has opinions about which cup to use and who can climb onto the kitchen table but not down from it.

Some of this will be extraordinary. The first time they reach for you specifically, not just any warm body. The moment they say your name and mean it. The look on their face when they take their first independent steps and realize they did that.

Some of it will be grinding. The ninth tantrum before noon. The food they loved yesterday that is now personally offensive to them. The fact that you haven't been to the bathroom alone in months.

Both are real. Both are this stage.

You don't have to enjoy every moment. You don't have to have it figured out. You don't have to do this without help. You are allowed to be tired. You are allowed to miss your old life and love your new one at the same time. You are allowed to put the baby in a safe place and step outside for two minutes when you need to breathe.

The research on what children need most is remarkably consistent and remarkably simple: responsive, consistent caregiving. Not perfect caregiving. Not Instagram caregiving. Just a person who shows up, pays attention, and tries.

You're doing that right now.
