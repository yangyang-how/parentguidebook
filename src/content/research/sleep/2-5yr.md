## Research Brief

Ages 2-5 is when bedtime battles peak, naps phase out, and parasomnias (night terrors, sleepwalking, nightmares) are most common. This article covers age-appropriate sleep needs, managing bedtime resistance, the nap-to-no-nap transition, nightmares vs night terrors, and sleep hygiene foundations for preschoolers.

## Sources

- **Date:** 2016

- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**
> "Children 1-2 years should sleep 11 to 14 hours per 24 hours (including naps)."

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: How Much Sleep Does a Preschooler Need?
- Key points: 10-13 hours including naps (ages 3-5), consequences of insufficient sleep
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: If your 4-year-old is a tornado by 5pm, sleep might be the answer

### Section 2: The Bedtime Battle
- Key points: Limit-setting type insomnia, "one more" requests, consistent routine, firm limits with warmth
- Supported by: Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: This is the most common sleep problem at this age — and the most fixable

### Section 3: Nightmares and Night Terrors
- Key points: How they differ, when they happen, how to respond, when they resolve
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Night terrors look terrifying but are harmless. Nightmares are less dramatic but more distressing for the child.

### Section 4: The Great Nap Transition
- Key points: Most kids drop naps between 3-5, signs of readiness, quiet time as alternative
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: When your child stops napping, bedtime gets easier — trade one battle for another

### Section 5: Screens and Sleep
- Key points: Screen-free wind-down, blue light effects, practical alternatives
- Supported by: Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: The bedtime iPad is making bedtime worse, not better

### Section 6: When Snoring Isn't Cute
- Key points: Sleep apnea in preschoolers, adenoid/tonsil enlargement, when to get evaluated
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Healthy children don't snore regularly — if yours does, mention it to the doctor

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Bedtime resistance is developmental, not defiance — but it still needs limits
- Night terrors: parents should NOT try to wake the child — just keep them safe
- Sleep apnea in preschoolers is often misdiagnosed as ADHD — behavioral symptoms overlap significantly
- Cultural note: Sam's daughter had adenoid hypertrophy — this topic is personally relevant to the project. Handle with extra care and specificity.
