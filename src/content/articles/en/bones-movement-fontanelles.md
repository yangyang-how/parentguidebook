# Fontanelles (Soft Spots) — What Every Parent Should Know

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know. Most fontanelle questions are routine — but a bulging or deeply sunken soft spot needs prompt medical attention.**

**What are fontanelles?** The soft, membrane-covered gaps between the bones of your baby's skull. They allow the head to flex during birth and give the brain room to grow rapidly in the first two years of life.

**Key facts:**
- Every baby is born with six fontanelles — the two you can feel are the large one on top (anterior) and the smaller one at the back (posterior)
- The posterior fontanelle closes by about 6-8 weeks; the anterior fontanelle typically closes between 12-18 months (though some close earlier or later)
- Normal touching, washing, and gentle brushing will not hurt your baby's soft spot — there is a tough membrane protecting the brain underneath
- A bulging fontanelle can signal increased pressure inside the skull — this is urgent
- A sunken fontanelle can signal dehydration — this needs attention

**What to do:** Learn what normal looks and feels like so you can recognize changes. If the soft spot is noticeably bulging (especially with fever or vomiting) or deeply sunken, contact your doctor.

## What are fontanelles and why do babies have them?

Run your fingers gently across the top of your baby's head. Somewhere near the front, you'll feel a soft, slightly yielding area — maybe diamond-shaped, maybe pulsing faintly. That's the anterior fontanelle, and it's supposed to be there.

A baby's skull is not one solid piece of bone like an adult's. It's made up of several separate bone plates connected by flexible, fibrous tissue called sutures. Where multiple sutures meet, there are wider gaps covered by a tough membrane. These gaps are the fontanelles — what most people call the "soft spots." [Source: src1]

They exist for two critical reasons:

**First, to get through the birth canal.** During a vaginal delivery, the skull bones need to overlap and shift to fit through the narrow passage. This is called **molding**, and it's why many newborns arrive with a slightly elongated or cone-shaped head. It looks alarming if you're not expecting it, but it's completely normal and resolves within a few days as the bones gently shift back. [Source: src2]

**Second, to make room for the brain.** A baby's brain triples in size during the first two years of life. If the skull were a solid, fused shell at birth, there would be no room for this explosive growth. The fontanelles and sutures act like expansion joints, allowing the skull to enlarge gradually as the brain grows underneath. The bone plates grow outward from their edges and eventually meet, closing the gaps through a process called intramembranous ossification. [Source: src1]

Think of it this way: your baby's skull is brilliantly engineered — flexible enough for birth, expandable enough for brain growth, and strong enough to protect what matters most.

## The six fontanelles

Most parents know about "the soft spot" — singular. In reality, every baby is born with **six fontanelles**. [Source: src1]

### The two you can feel

**Anterior fontanelle (front soft spot)** — This is the one everyone talks about. It's located on the top of the head, toward the front, where the two frontal bones and two parietal bones meet. It's diamond-shaped and typically measures about 2-3 centimeters across at birth, though there's a wide normal range. This is the largest fontanelle and the last to close. [Source: src1]

**Posterior fontanelle (back soft spot)** — Located at the back of the head where the two parietal bones meet the occipital bone. It's much smaller — roughly triangular, often only about 1 centimeter across. Many parents never notice it. Some babies are born with this one already nearly closed. [Source: src1]

### The four you probably won't notice

**Mastoid fontanelles (x2)** — One on each side, behind the ears, where the temporal, parietal, and occipital bones meet. These are small and close relatively quickly. [Source: src1]

**Sphenoid fontanelles (x2)** — One on each side, near the temples, where the frontal, parietal, temporal, and sphenoid bones meet. Also small and not typically noticeable to parents. [Source: src1]

Your pediatrician may check these during well-baby visits, but for day-to-day parenting, the anterior and posterior fontanelles are the two that matter most.

## When do they close?

Each fontanelle closes on its own timeline as the surrounding bones grow and meet:

| Fontanelle | Location | Typical closure |
|---|---|---|
| Posterior | Back of head | 6-8 weeks [Source: src3] |
| Mastoid (x2) | Behind ears | 6-18 months [Source: src1] |
| Sphenoid (x2) | Near temples | ~6 months [Source: src1] |
| Anterior | Top of head | 12-18 months [Source: src4] |

The anterior fontanelle gets the most attention because it stays open the longest and has the widest range of normal closure times. Research shows that about 10% of babies have it closed by 6 months, 25-50% by 12 months, and virtually all by 24 months. [Source: src4]

**Early closure** (before 3 months) can sometimes indicate **craniosynostosis** — a condition where skull sutures fuse prematurely, potentially restricting brain growth. Your pediatrician monitors head circumference at every well-baby visit specifically to catch this. [Source: src4]

**Late closure** (still open after 18-24 months) may be associated with rickets (vitamin D deficiency), hypothyroidism, or other conditions. Again, your pediatrician tracks this as part of routine care. [Source: src4]

In most cases, a fontanelle that closes on the early or late end of normal is just that — normal. But it's one of many reasons those well-baby checkups matter.

## What normal fontanelles look and feel like

Knowing what's normal makes it much easier to spot what isn't. Here's what to expect:

**By sight:** The anterior fontanelle should look relatively flat — roughly level with the surrounding bone. When your baby is calm and upright, you may see a very slight inward curve. When your baby is lying down, it may appear slightly more level or even a tiny bit raised. You might notice a faint pulsing — that's normal. You're seeing the pulse of blood flow through the vessels beneath the membrane. [Source: src1]

**By touch:** It feels soft but not fragile. There's a tough, fibrous membrane (called the dura mater) beneath the skin protecting the brain. You can gently feel the edges of the surrounding bone plates. The fontanelle itself will feel slightly yielding, like pressing on a taut drum skin. [Source: src1]

**During crying:** When your baby cries hard, strains, or vomits, the fontanelle may temporarily bulge or feel tense. This is normal — it's just a transient increase in pressure from the exertion. It should return to normal when your baby calms down. A fontanelle that only bulges during intense crying is not the same as one that is persistently bulging. [Source: src1]

## "Am I going to hurt the baby if I touch it?"

This is the question nearly every parent has — and many are too nervous to ask.

**No. Normal, gentle touching will not hurt your baby.**

The fontanelle is not an unprotected hole in the skull. Beneath the skin, there is a thick, tough membrane that effectively shields the brain. You can wash your baby's head, gently brush hair over the fontanelle, and touch it during normal care without any risk. [Source: src2]

Think about it this way: if fontanelles were so fragile that gentle contact could cause injury, the human species would have had a serious design problem for the last several hundred thousand years. Babies bump their heads. Other children poke at them. They press their own heads against crib mattresses every night. The membrane is there precisely because nature anticipated all of this.

That said, you should avoid pressing hard on the fontanelle or applying sustained pressure to it — not because it's likely to cause damage from a casual touch, but because there's simply no reason to push firmly on any part of a baby's head. Treat it with the same gentle care you treat the rest of your baby, and you're doing fine.

## Skull molding during delivery

If your baby arrived via vaginal delivery and you noticed an oddly shaped head — elongated, lopsided, or slightly cone-shaped — that was skull molding at work. [Source: src2]

During labor, the bones of the skull overlap at the sutures and fontanelles, allowing the head to compress and fit through the birth canal. The result can look startling: some babies emerge looking like they're wearing an invisible tall hat. [Source: src2]

**This is completely normal and temporary.** Within a few days to a week, the bones settle back into position, and your baby's head rounds out. The process is the same one that made fontanelles necessary in the first place — a skull designed to flex, adapt, and then recover.

Babies born via cesarean section generally don't show molding, since they bypass the birth canal. Babies born after a long labor or with the assistance of vacuum extraction or forceps may show more pronounced molding or temporary swelling. In all cases, the shape resolves on its own. If it doesn't, or if you notice a progressively unusual head shape in the weeks after birth, mention it to your pediatrician — that could be positional plagiocephaly (flat spots from sleeping position) rather than molding, and it has its own set of solutions. [Source: src5]

## Warning signs: when the fontanelle tells you something is wrong

The fontanelle is more than a growth accommodation — it's also a window into your baby's health. Changes in how it looks or feels can be early signals of conditions that need attention.

### 🔴 Bulging fontanelle — seek medical care promptly

A fontanelle that is **persistently tense, swollen, or visibly raised above the level of the surrounding bone** — even when your baby is calm and upright — can be a sign of **increased intracranial pressure** (too much pressure inside the skull). [Source: src1]

Possible causes include:
- **Meningitis** (infection of the membranes surrounding the brain)
- **Encephalitis** (brain inflammation)
- **Hydrocephalus** (excess fluid in or around the brain)
- **Intracranial hemorrhage** (bleeding inside the skull)

**What to watch for:**
- The soft spot feels firm and bulges outward when the baby is calm and upright
- The bulging is new and persistent (not just during crying or straining)
- Accompanied by fever, vomiting, excessive sleepiness, irritability, or seizures
- The baby seems unusually lethargic or difficult to wake

**What to do:** If you notice a persistently bulging fontanelle — especially with fever, vomiting, lethargy, or any change in your baby's behavior — **contact your doctor immediately or go to the emergency department.** This combination of signs warrants urgent evaluation. [Source: src1]

> **Important context:** A brief bulge during hard crying, coughing, or vomiting is normal. The concern is when the fontanelle stays bulging even after the baby has calmed down. If you're unsure whether what you're seeing is normal, it's always better to have it checked than to wait.

### 🟡 Sunken fontanelle — get medical attention soon

A fontanelle that appears **noticeably concave or sunken** — like a shallow dip or valley compared to the surrounding bone — can be a sign of **dehydration**. [Source: src1]

This is most commonly seen when a baby:
- Has been vomiting or having diarrhea
- Has not been feeding well
- Has a fever and has been losing fluids

**Other signs of dehydration to watch for alongside a sunken fontanelle:**
- Fewer wet diapers than usual (fewer than 6 in 24 hours for babies over one week old)
- Dry lips and mouth
- No tears when crying
- Unusual sleepiness or fussiness
- Dark-colored urine

**What to do:** A mildly sunken fontanelle alone, especially if your baby is feeding well, having normal wet diapers, and acting like themselves, may not be cause for alarm — slight variation in appearance is normal. But a clearly sunken fontanelle combined with reduced feeding, fewer wet diapers, or illness should prompt a call to your doctor the same day. If your baby seems very lethargic, can't keep fluids down, or shows multiple signs of dehydration, seek care right away. [Source: src1]

## When to see a doctor — a simple checklist

**Seek urgent care (same day or emergency):**
- ✅ Fontanelle is persistently bulging when the baby is calm and upright
- ✅ Bulging fontanelle accompanied by fever, vomiting, seizures, or unusual lethargy
- ✅ Baby had a significant head injury and the fontanelle appears abnormal afterward

**See your doctor soon (within a few days):**
- ✅ Fontanelle appears noticeably sunken, especially if the baby has been ill
- ✅ You notice signs of dehydration (fewer wet diapers, dry mouth, no tears)
- ✅ The fontanelle feels very different than it usually does — and the change persists

**Mention at the next well-baby visit:**
- ✅ You think the fontanelle feels like it's closing very early (baby under 3 months)
- ✅ The fontanelle still seems wide open and your baby is over 18 months
- ✅ You notice an unusual head shape that isn't resolving or seems to be getting worse
- ✅ You have any questions about what you're feeling or seeing — there are no silly questions about your baby's health

## What your pediatrician checks (and why)

At every well-baby visit, your pediatrician does three things related to the fontanelle and skull:

1. **Measures head circumference** — plotted on a growth chart, this tracks brain growth over time. A head that's growing too fast might suggest fluid buildup; too slow might suggest the sutures are closing prematurely. [Source: src4]

2. **Palpates the fontanelle** — feeling for size, tension, and whether the edges of the bone are overlapping or separating. [Source: src1]

3. **Assesses head shape** — looking for asymmetry that might indicate positional plagiocephaly or, rarely, craniosynostosis. [Source: src5]

These checks take seconds and are part of what makes routine well-baby visits so valuable. Many conditions related to fontanelles and skull growth are caught during these visits long before parents notice anything at home.

## Common questions

**"My baby's soft spot is pulsing — is that normal?"**
Yes. The pulsing you see is the normal pulsation of blood vessels beneath the membrane. It's a sign of good blood flow and is completely expected.

**"The soft spot seems bigger/smaller than my friend's baby's — is something wrong?"**
There's a wide range of normal fontanelle sizes. The anterior fontanelle can be anywhere from about 1 to 4 centimeters across at birth and still be perfectly healthy. What matters more than absolute size is the trend over time, which your pediatrician tracks at well-baby visits. [Source: src1]

**"My baby is 6 months old and I can barely feel the soft spot anymore. Is it closing too early?"**
Possibly, but probably not. About 10% of babies have the anterior fontanelle closed by 6 months, and this is within the range of normal as long as head circumference is growing appropriately. [Source: src4] Your pediatrician will monitor head growth to make sure the brain has room to develop.

**"My baby is 15 months old and the soft spot is still open. Should I worry?"**
No. The anterior fontanelle commonly closes anywhere between 12 and 18 months, and some remain partially open until 24 months. As long as your baby is developing normally and head circumference is on track, a fontanelle that's still open at 15 months is completely within the expected range. [Source: src4]

**"Can I wash my baby's hair over the soft spot?"**
Absolutely. Gentle washing, shampooing, and rinsing over the fontanelle are all safe. The membrane beneath the skin is tough enough to handle routine hygiene.
