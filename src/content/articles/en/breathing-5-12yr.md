# Breathing at 5–12 Years — Asthma, Sports, and Managing It at School

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — well-managed asthma should not limit your child's activities.**

**The short version:** School age is when asthma management gets clearer and more empowering. Children 5 and older can now perform spirometry for a definitive diagnosis. Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB) becomes a major concern as children join organized sports — but the answer is treatment, not avoidance. Exercise is recommended for asthmatic children because it actually improves outcomes. Your child needs an action plan at school, and every state allows students to self-carry inhalers.

**Key points:**
- Spirometry is now possible and recommended for diagnosis and monitoring [Source: src1]
- Exercise is RECOMMENDED for children with asthma — it improves control and lung function [Sources: src4, src5]
- Pre-exercise SABA (rescue inhaler) 10–15 minutes before activity prevents most EIB episodes [Source: src4]
- All 50 US states allow students to self-carry and self-administer rescue inhalers at school [Source: src7]
- Cough-variant asthma (chronic cough without wheezing) is often missed at this age [Source: src1]

## Asthma diagnosis gets clearer: spirometry

One of the biggest changes at age 5 is that your child can now do spirometry — the gold standard breathing test for asthma. Instead of relying on clinical patterns alone (as with younger children), doctors can now measure exactly how well the lungs are working. [Source: src1]

**What spirometry measures:**
- **FEV1** — how much air your child can forcefully exhale in one second
- **FEV1/FVC ratio** — the proportion of total lung capacity that can be exhaled in one second

Your child blows into a tube as hard and as long as they can — like blowing out birthday candles that won't go out. The test is painless and takes about 15 minutes. [Source: src2]

**When to test:**
- At initial evaluation if asthma is suspected [Source: src1]
- Every 1–2 years after diagnosis to monitor control [Source: src1]
- When stepping up or down treatment [Source: src2]
- If symptoms change significantly [Source: src2]

> **For parents:** If your child was diagnosed with asthma before age 5 based on clinical criteria alone, spirometry at age 5–6 is a good opportunity to confirm the diagnosis objectively. Some children diagnosed early turn out not to have asthma — their airways grew, the wheezing stopped, and their lung function is normal. That's good news worth confirming. [Source: src1]

## Asthma severity and the stepwise approach (ages 5–11)

The NAEPP guidelines have specific treatment steps for children aged 5–11, which differ from the 0–4 age group: [Source: src3]

| Step | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Step 1** (Intermittent) | Rescue inhaler (SABA) as needed |
| **Step 2** (Mild persistent) | Low-dose inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) daily |
| **Step 3** (Moderate persistent) | Low-dose ICS + LABA, *or* medium-dose ICS |
| **Step 4** (Moderate-severe) | Medium-dose ICS + LABA |

**Key difference from younger children:** At Step 3, a long-acting beta agonist (LABA) can now be added alongside ICS. This combination is often more effective than simply increasing the ICS dose. [Source: src3]

**The goal** is to use the least medication needed to keep symptoms well-controlled, then step down when possible. "Well-controlled" means: symptoms no more than 2 days per week, nighttime symptoms no more than once per month, no activity limitation, and rescue inhaler use no more than 2 days per week. [Source: src2]

ICS remains the preferred controller medication at every step. It's the most effective single treatment for persistent asthma at this age. [Source: src3]

## Exercise and sports: yes, your child can play

This is the biggest myth to bust: **"My child has asthma, so they can't play sports."**

The opposite is true. International guidelines recommend regular physical exercise for children with asthma. Research consistently shows that exercise improves disease control, lung function, and quality of life. Cardiovascular fitness actually reduces the frequency and severity of asthma symptoms over time. [Sources: src4, src5, src10]

Many Olympic athletes have asthma or exercise-induced bronchoconstriction. Having asthma is not a reason to sit on the sidelines — it's a reason to have a management plan so your child can participate fully.

### Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB)

EIB is narrowing of the airways during or shortly after exercise. It causes coughing, chest tightness, shortness of breath, and wheezing — typically starting during activity or 5–15 minutes afterward. [Source: src4]

**How common is it?** EIB can occur in children with asthma but also in children without any other asthma symptoms. [Source: src4]

**Prevention strategies:**

**Medication (first-line):** A puff of the rescue inhaler (SABA) 10 to 15 minutes before exercise prevents EIB in most children. This is the most common and effective approach. [Sources: src4, src9]

**Non-medication strategies (complementary):** [Source: src6]
- **Proper warm-up** — 10–15 minutes of gradually increasing activity before full exertion
- **Breathing through the nose** or wearing a face covering in cold air
- **Avoiding cold, dry air** — winter sports, ice rinks, and running outdoors in cold weather are higher-risk for EIB
- **Choosing activities wisely** — swimming is often well-tolerated because the air above the pool is warm and humid (though chlorine sensitivity can be an issue for some children)

**When pre-exercise SABA isn't enough:** If your child needs the rescue inhaler frequently for exercise, or if it's not controlling symptoms well, daily ICS may be a better approach. Talk to your doctor about stepping up treatment rather than accepting limited activity. [Source: src9]

> **For parents:** If your child has stopped exercising or avoids PE class because of breathing symptoms, that's not a child managing their asthma — that's a child with uncontrolled asthma. The goal is always full participation, with medication support as needed. Don't let asthma become an excuse to be sedentary — the evidence says that's the worse outcome. [Source: src5]

## Asthma at school

Your child spends 6+ hours a day at school. Asthma management needs to follow them there.

### The school action plan

Give the school nurse a copy of your child's asthma action plan — the same green/yellow/red zone plan your doctor created. Make sure it includes: [Source: src7]
- Which medications your child takes daily (if any)
- What rescue medication to give and when
- Emergency contact information
- When to call 911

### Self-carry laws

All 50 US states have laws allowing students to self-carry and self-administer rescue inhalers at school. This means your child can keep their inhaler on them — in their backpack, pocket, or desk — and use it when needed without waiting for the school nurse. [Source: src7]

You'll typically need a form signed by your child's doctor and a parent. Ask the school office at the beginning of the year.

### PE and recess

PE teachers and recess supervisors need to know which students have asthma. This doesn't mean your child should be excused from PE — it means the adults supervising know to allow warm-up time, recognize symptoms, and respond if your child needs their inhaler. Modifications might include extended warm-up and cool-down periods. [Source: src7]

### Proper inhaler technique (still)

Even at school age, many children use inhalers incorrectly. A spacer should still be used with metered-dose inhalers to ensure medication reaches the lungs. Have your child's technique checked at every doctor visit. [Source: src7]

## Cough-variant asthma: the hidden version

Not all asthma involves wheezing. Some children present with a chronic dry cough — especially at night, during exercise, or after exposure to cold air — without ever wheezing. This is called cough-variant asthma, and it's frequently underdiagnosed because the classic wheeze is absent. [Source: src1]

**Think about cough-variant asthma if your child:**
- Has a persistent dry cough lasting more than 4 weeks
- Coughs during or after exercise
- Coughs at night, especially between 2–4 AM
- Coughs after exposure to cold air or allergens
- Has a family history of asthma or allergies

A trial of asthma treatment (bronchodilator with or without ICS) can help clarify the diagnosis. If the cough improves with treatment, that's strong evidence for asthma. [Source: src1]

## When asthma becomes an emergency

**🔴 Call 911 or go to the ER:**
- Rescue inhaler is not working after 2–3 doses over 20 minutes
- Your child cannot speak in full sentences due to breathlessness
- Severe retractions — visible skin pulling in at the ribs, throat, or above the collarbones
- Blue or gray lips, fingernails, or face
- Your child is confused, extremely drowsy, or unable to sit up
- Your child says "I can't breathe" and looks frightened

**🟡 Call your doctor (same day):**
- Needing rescue inhaler more than twice a week (outside of pre-exercise use) [Source: src2]
- Nighttime symptoms more than twice a month [Source: src2]
- Symptoms limiting activity or causing missed school [Source: src3]
- Any decline in peak flow readings (if your child uses a peak flow meter)
