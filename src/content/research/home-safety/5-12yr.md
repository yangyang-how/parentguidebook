## Research Brief

School-age children gain independence — biking to a friend's house, cooking in the kitchen, staying home alone for the first time. The safety challenge shifts from constant supervision to teaching self-protection. This article covers bike and helmet safety, sports safety, home-alone readiness, fire safety, internet safety basics, and kitchen safety for emerging independence.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Wheels and Helmets
- Key points: Helmets reduce head injury by 85%, under-10 not in streets, proper fit
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: No helmet, no ride. Non-negotiable.

### Section 2: Sports Safety and Concussions
- Key points: Equipment, warm-up, concussion protocol, age-appropriate training
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: If your child hits their head and seems "off" — pull them out. No game is worth a brain injury.

### Section 3: Home Alone — When Are They Ready?
- Key points: Generally 10-12, readiness signs, graduated approach
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The question isn't "what age?" It's "can THIS child handle it?"

### Section 4: Car, Bike, and Pedestrian Safety
- Key points: Booster until 4'9", back seat until 13, under-10 can't cross alone, street riding
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Your 8-year-old in a seatbelt without a booster is not safe — they need that booster until 4'9"

### Section 5: Fire, Kitchen, and Internet
- Key points: Fire drill practice, kitchen safety rules before cooking, internet safety basics
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5, Source 6, Source 9

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Helmet use is lower in some Chinese communities — normalize it as essential, not optional
- Internet safety concerns differ between US and Chinese internet environments
- Home-alone readiness standards vary by culture and by local law
- Sports safety culture differs — Chinese emphasis on academic over sports may mean less sports injury exposure but also less awareness
- Kitchen safety and cooking independence may be encouraged earlier or later depending on cultural expectations
