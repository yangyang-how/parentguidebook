## Research Brief

Ages 2-5 are the golden age of big questions. Children enter the legendary "why?" phase, develop their first concepts about death (often seeing it as reversible), become obsessed with fairness, create imaginary friends, engage in magical thinking, and ask their first questions about God and creation. This is when parents are most likely to be caught off guard by the depth of their child's philosophical curiosity. The evidence is clear: engage with the questions, don't shut them down.

- **Type:** cohort-study

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The "Why?" Tsunami
- Key points: 76 questions/hour, they evaluate your answers, sophistication increases, take them seriously
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your child's relentless "Why?" is not a test of your patience — it's a sign of a mind on fire

### Section 2: Death — "Will They Come Back?"
- Key points: Reversible at 3-4, irreversible by 5-6, honest age-appropriate answers, follow their lead
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: When your child asks about death, they're not ready for a theology lecture. They need to know they're safe.

### Section 3: Fairness — "That's Not FAIR!"
- Key points: Sense emerges by 3, initially egocentric, becoming more other-oriented, genuine moral development
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: Their outrage at unfairness is annoying AND developmentally important

### Section 4: Magical Thinking, Imaginary Friends, and the Supernatural
- Key points: Magical thinking is adaptive, imaginary friends are healthy (37-65%), God concepts develop, animism is normal
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 4, Source 7, Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: When your child talks to their imaginary friend or tells you the moon follows them home — let them

### Section 5: How to Engage With Big Questions
- Key points: Honest answers, follow the child's lead, ask questions back, don't shut down inquiry
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: "That's a great question — what do you think?" is almost always the right first response

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, death is often taboo for children (不吉利) — the article should gently challenge this avoidance
- Religious education varies enormously — the article must work for families with strong religious commitments and none
- Imaginary friends may be seen as worrisome in Chinese culture — normalize with evidence
- The "why?" phase is equally intense in Chinese (为什么) and can exhaust caregivers in any language
- Magical thinking and ancestor veneration intersect in Chinese culture — handle with respect
- "Who made the world?" has different answers in different families — present as a universal question, not an opportunity for any particular answer
