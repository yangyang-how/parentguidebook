## Research Brief

Routines are the invisible architecture of family life — they provide predictability, build executive function, support self-regulation, and create the rhythms that make daily life manageable. This overview covers why routines matter (the science of predictability and security), the difference between routines and rituals, flexibility within structure, and cultural variation in how families organize daily life.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Why Routines Matter (The Science)
- Key points: Predictability builds EF, self-regulation, and security; reduces cognitive load
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: A routine isn't a rigid schedule — it's a rhythm that tells your child the world is predictable and safe

### Section 2: Routines vs Rituals
- Key points: Routines = what we do; Rituals = who we are; both protective
- Supported by: Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: "Bath, book, bed" is a routine. Adding the same goodnight song every night makes it a ritual.

### Section 3: The Power of Family Meals
- Key points: ≥3/week, conversation quality, no screens, one of most protective factors
- Supported by: Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: If you could only do one family routine, make it dinner together

### Section 4: Bedtime as Cornerstone
- Key points: Predicts language and school readiness, AAP priority, specific components
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The bedtime routine is the most evidence-backed parenting habit you can build

### Section 5: Flexibility Within Structure
- Key points: Consistent ≠ rigid, choices within structure, cultural variation
- Supported by: Source 9, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Good routines bend without breaking

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese family routines are heavily shaped by academic schedules — homework routines may dominate evening structure
- Multi-generational Chinese households may have more complex routine negotiation
- Family meals in Chinese culture are already culturally valued (团圆饭) — leverage this existing strength
- Flexibility within routine may need to be explicitly taught in cultures that favor more rigid structure
- Disruption of routines during immigration, divorce, or major transitions is a specific risk
