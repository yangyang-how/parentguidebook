## Research Brief

Most newborns have no visible teeth, but oral care starts from birth. This article covers what parents need to know about their newborn's oral health in the first month: cleaning gums, the rare occurrence of natal teeth, bacterial transmission from caregivers, and when the first teeth will eventually appear.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Your Newborn's Mouth — What's There (and What's Not)
- Key points: No teeth yet (usually), gums are healthy and functional, jaw is growing
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Even without teeth, the mouth needs attention

### Section 2: Cleaning Baby's Gums
- Key points: Wipe gums with damp cloth after feedings, establishes routine early
- Supported by: Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Simple, takes 30 seconds, builds a habit for both of you

### Section 3: Your Oral Health Matters for Your Baby
- Key points: Bacteria transmission, pregnancy dental care, avoid sharing utensils
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: Taking care of your own teeth is one of the best things you can do for your baby's future dental health

### Section 4: Natal Teeth — When Babies Are Born With Teeth
- Key points: Rare (1 in 2,000-3,500), usually normal teeth erupting early, treatment usually unnecessary
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 4, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: Surprising but usually harmless — when to call the doctor

### Section 5: Pacifiers in the First Month
- Key points: Safe and may reduce SIDS risk, never dip in sugar/honey
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: One less thing to worry about — pacifiers are fine

### Section 6: When to Expect the First Tooth
- Key points: Around 6 months on average, wide normal variation
- Supported by: Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Every baby is different — no need to worry if it's early or late

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Natal teeth are rare enough that most parents won't encounter them, but scary enough that finding one warrants reassurance in the article
- The "don't share utensils" advice about bacterial transmission is evidence-based but can feel judgmental — frame it as information, not blame
- Cultural note: In some Chinese traditions, natal teeth are considered auspicious or inauspicious depending on the region — the article should be culturally neutral
