# Sleep Domain Research — All Ages

## 1. Newborn Sleep (0–3 months)

### Total hours
- Newborns sleep 14–17 hours per 24-hour period (National Sleep Foundation)
- Sleep is distributed roughly evenly: ~8–9 hours daytime, ~8 hours nighttime
- Sleep cycles are short: 40–60 minutes (vs 90 min adult cycles)
- ~50% of newborn sleep is REM ("active sleep")

### No circadian rhythm
- Newborns are born without a developed circadian rhythm
- Sleep is distributed more evenly across 24 hours with little day/night distinction
- Many newborns have days and nights confused
- At 4–6 weeks, circadian rhythm begins to develop; responds to light/dark
- By 8–9 weeks, melatonin and cortisol release start following circadian pattern

### Safe sleep ABCs
- **Alone** (own sleep surface, no bed-sharing)
- **Back** (supine position for every sleep)
- **Crib** (firm, flat surface — crib, bassinet, or portable play yard meeting CPSC standards)
- No loose blankets, pillows, stuffed toys, bumpers, or soft items
- No weighted objects (blankets/sleepers/swaddles)
- Room-sharing (same room, separate surface) for at least 6 months — reduces SIDS risk by up to 50%
- Source: AAP 2022 updated safe sleep recommendations

### Swaddling
- AAP: when done correctly, swaddling can help calm infants and promote sleep
- Must stop swaddling when baby shows signs of rolling (typically ~3–4 months, can be earlier)
- Risk: swaddled baby who rolls to stomach may not be able to return to back → suffocation risk
- Swaddling may decrease arousal, making it harder for baby to wake up
- Source: HealthyChildren.org (AAP)

## 2. Infant Sleep (3–12 months)

### AASM recommended hours
- 4–12 months: 12–16 hours per 24 hours (including naps)
- AAP endorses AASM recommendations

### 4-month sleep regression
- Not truly a "regression" — permanent change in sleep architecture
- Before: immature sleep alternating between "active" and "quiet" sleep
- After: adult-like sleep cycles with distinct stages (light NREM → deep NREM → REM)
- More frequent wake-ups during lighter sleep stages
- Significant individual variation — some babies have no detectable regression
- Source: Sleep Foundation, BabySleepScience

### Sleep consolidation
- Begins around 3–4 months
- Babies start sleeping longer stretches at night
- By 6 months, many (not all) babies can sleep 6–8 hour stretches
- Night feedings often still needed through 6–12 months depending on growth

### Nap transitions
- 0–3 months: 3–5 naps/day
- 4–6 months: typically 3 naps
- 6–9 months: transition to 2 naps
- 12–15 months: transition to 1 nap

### Sleep training evidence
- Multiple systematic reviews show sleep training improves infant sleep
- About 1 in 4 to 1 in 10 infants benefit compared to no training
- No adverse effects reported after 5 years in RCTs
- Graduated extinction (Ferber): effective, no long-term harm to cortisol, attachment, or behavior
- "Camping out" (gentle method): equally effective, less stressful for parents, better for high-separation-anxiety babies
- Unmodified extinction ("cry it out"): effective but more distressing for parents
- Key RCT (Gradisar et al. 2016): sleep training at 8 months showed no difference on behavioral, emotional, or attachment outcomes at age 6
- Behavioral approaches are recommended as first-line before any pharmacological intervention
- Sources: PMC5962992, Scientific American, NPR, Psychology Today

### When to stop swaddling
- When baby shows ANY signs of rolling (typically 3–4 months)
- Transition to sleep sack/wearable blanket

## 3. Toddler Sleep (1–3 years)

### AASM recommended hours
- 1–2 years: 11–14 hours per 24 hours (including naps)

### Nap dropping
- Most toddlers transition from 2 naps to 1 nap between 12–18 months
- Single nap usually 1–3 hours, typically after lunch
- Nap needs persist through age 3 for most children

### Night terrors vs nightmares
**Night terrors:**
- Occur during deep NREM sleep, usually 1–2 hours after falling asleep
- Child appears awake (screaming, thrashing) but is actually asleep
- No memory of the episode
- Child is unresponsive to comfort
- Peak prevalence: ages 2–12
- Management: do NOT try to wake the child; keep them safe; episode resolves in minutes

**Nightmares:**
- Occur during REM sleep, typically second half of night / early morning
- Child wakes up fully, can describe the dream
- Responds to comfort and reassurance
- Can remember the dream
- More common from age 3 onward

### Bedtime resistance
- Common developmental behavior in toddlers
- Often linked to increasing autonomy drive
- Consistent bedtime routine is most effective intervention

### Crib-to-bed transition
- Most experts recommend waiting until age 2.5–3 years
- Earlier transitions often lead to more bedtime resistance and night waking
- Safety rails recommended
- Main reason to transition early: climbing out of crib (safety concern)

## 4. Preschool Sleep (3–5 years)

### AASM recommended hours
- 3–5 years: 10–13 hours per 24 hours (including naps)

### Dropping the nap
- Most children drop their nap between ages 3–5
- Average age is around 3–4 years
- Signs of readiness: taking a long time to fall asleep at naptime, napping interferes with nighttime sleep

### Bedtime fears
- Very common at this age due to developing imagination
- Fears of the dark, monsters, being alone
- Management: validate fears, use nightlight, establish security routines
- Avoid dismissing fears ("there's nothing to be afraid of")

### Melatonin evidence
- AAP does NOT recommend routine use in healthy children
- Limited evidence base for typically developing children
- Behavioral approaches should be first-line treatment
- No studies on efficacy/safety in children under 2
- Product quality concern: 2017 study found >70% of supplements had incorrect dosage on label
- ~11,000 ER visits from accidental pediatric melatonin ingestions
- If used, should be under pediatrician guidance, lowest effective dose, short duration
- Sources: HealthyChildren.org (AAP), AASM health advisory, PMC10968776

## 5. School-Age Sleep (5–12 years)

### AASM recommended hours
- 6–12 years: 9–12 hours per 24 hours

### Screens and sleep onset delay
- 90% of studies found association between screen use and delayed bedtime / decreased total sleep
- Mechanisms: time displacement, psychological stimulation, blue light melatonin suppression
- Evening blue light can cause ~1.1-hour circadian phase delay
- However: blue light filters showed only minimal sleep improvements in studies
- Causality debate: unclear if screens cause poor sleep or poor sleepers use more screens
- Sources: PMC5839336, PMC10903530, Sleep Health Journal

### Sleep and academic performance
- Each hour less of nightly sleep: ~0.07 GPA decline on 4.0 scale (college study)
- Insufficient sleep linked to attention, behavior, and learning problems
- Also linked to obesity, psychological well-being issues, impaired cognitive functioning
- Source: AASM consensus statement

### Sleepwalking
- Prevalence: 2–14% of children
- Peak incidence: age 10–12 years
- 40–60 minute cycles in deep NREM sleep
- Strong genetic component: 22.5% prevalence with no parental history → 47.4% with one parent → 61.5% with both parents
- Usually self-limiting, resolves by adolescence
- Management: safety measures, scheduled awakening technique (wake 15 min before usual episode)
- Sources: JAMA Pediatrics 2015, Cleveland Clinic

## 6. Teen Sleep (12–18 years)

### AASM recommended hours
- 13–18 years: 8–10 hours per 24 hours

### Circadian shift / biological delay
- Puberty triggers delayed melatonin release → later natural bedtime
- Teens biologically driven to fall asleep later and wake later
- Slower accumulation of sleep drive compared to adults
- Internal clock interprets environmental time cues differently from adults
- This is biology, not laziness or bad habits
- Sources: PMC2820578, PMC3130594

### School start time research
- Early school times conflict with biological sleep phase
- Delaying start times 25–60 minutes → 25–77 more minutes of sleep per school night
- Benefits: reduced daytime sleepiness, depression, caffeine use, tardiness
- AAP has recommended middle/high schools start no earlier than 8:30 AM
- Sources: PMC4844764, PMC7177233

### Sleep deprivation prevalence
- Most US teens get insufficient sleep (under 8 hours on school nights)
- Insufficient teen sleep linked to: obesity, diabetes, depression, self-harm, suicidal thoughts, suicide attempts, accidents, injuries
- Source: AASM consensus statement

## 7. Safe Sleep Deep Dive

### SIDS/SUID statistics
- ~3,500–3,700 SUID deaths per year in the US
- ~2,500 attributed to SIDS specifically
- Significant decline in 1990s after "Back to Sleep" campaign
- Death rate has plateaued since ~2000
- Racial disparities persist: African American and AI/AN infants at higher risk
- Peak vulnerability: 1–6 months, especially 2–3 months
- Source: CDC, NICHD

### Triple Risk Model (Filiano & Kinney)
1. **Vulnerable infant** — intrinsic vulnerability (impaired arousal, cardiorespiratory, autonomic responses; serotonin deficits)
2. **Critical developmental period** — peak at 2–4 months when homeostatic controls are immature
3. **Exogenous stressor** — unsafe sleep environment (prone position, soft bedding, overheating, smoke exposure)
- SIDS occurs when all three factors intersect
- Source: PMC, StatPearls

### Risk factors
- Prone/side sleeping
- Soft bedding, loose items in sleep space
- Bed-sharing (especially with smoking, alcohol, drugs)
- Prenatal/postnatal smoke exposure (documented in 64% of SIDS infants)
- Overheating
- Prematurity and low birth weight
- Upper respiratory infection in prior 4 weeks (many SIDS cases)

### Protective factors
- Back sleeping
- Breastfeeding (reduces risk ~50% at all ages through infancy)
- Pacifier use (associated with reduced SIDS risk)
- Room-sharing without bed-sharing (reduces risk up to 50%)
- Fan use (one study showed reduced risk)
- Immunizations (up to date)

### Co-sleeping / bed-sharing debate
- AAP 2022: "unable to recommend bed sharing under any circumstances"
- Counterpoint: some researchers argue bedsharing without hazards (no smoking, no alcohol/drugs, no sofa) may not increase risk and may facilitate breastfeeding
- Blair et al. (NZ study): co-sleeping only significant risk when parents smoked
- Breastfeeding mothers naturally adopt protective "C-curl" position
- Bed-sharing facilitates more night-time breastfeeding
- Article approach: present AAP position clearly, acknowledge the debate exists, note specific high-risk factors
- Sources: AAP 2022, PMC9792691, Frontiers in Pediatrics

## 8. Sleep Environment

### Room temperature
- Recommended: 68–72°F (20–22°C)
- Overheating increases SIDS risk
- Dress baby in one layer more than adult would wear

### Darkness
- Dark room promotes melatonin production
- Use blackout curtains for naps and early bedtimes
- Dim nightlight acceptable for nighttime feeds/changes

### White noise
- Mixed evidence; low-intensity noise may be beneficial
- Maximum volume concern: some machines exceed 91 dB on max (exceeds occupational safety limits)
- Recommendation: keep volume under 50 dB, place away from baby's head
- Use consistent sound (white/pink/brown noise) not variable sounds
- Source: PubMed 38663282

### Room-sharing duration
- AAP: same room for at least 6 months, ideally 12 months
- Reduces SIDS risk by up to 50%
- Separate sleep surface (not same bed)

## 9. Common Sleep Disorders in Children

### Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)
- Affects 1–5% of children
- Peak: ages 2–8 (coincides with adenotonsillar hypertrophy)
- Primary cause in children: enlarged tonsils and adenoids
- Symptoms: snoring, mouth breathing, restless sleep, pauses in breathing
- First-line treatment: adenotonsillectomy
- Second-line: CPAP
- Sources: AAFP, Johns Hopkins

### Restless legs syndrome (RLS)
- Neurological condition: overwhelming urge to move limbs, unpleasant leg sensations
- Can be difficult to recognize in children (they may describe it as "itchy bones" or "wiggly legs")
- Association with ADHD
- Iron deficiency may contribute
- Source: AAFP

### Pediatric insomnia
- 20–30% of children suffer from insomnia
- Behavioral insomnia of childhood: sleep-onset association type and limit-setting type
- CBT-I adapted for children/families is first-line treatment

### Delayed sleep phase disorder in teens
- Prevalence: 7–16% of adolescents
- Biologically driven phase delay exacerbated by evening light exposure
- Treatment: CBT + bright light therapy (6 sessions) — moderate-to-large improvements
- Melatonin administration also effective
- Benefits maintained at 6-month follow-up
- Associated with: daytime sleepiness, anxiety, depression, school dropout risk
- Sources: PMC4142652, PMC3208844

## 10. Parent Sleep

### Sleep deprivation effects
- Nearly universal in first 3 months (newborns eat every 2–4 hours)
- Short-term: irritability, poor concentration, impaired decision-making
- Chronic: increased risk of depression, anxiety, high blood pressure
- Driving while sleep-deprived as dangerous as driving under the influence
- Less positive parenting with more fragmented sleep

### When it becomes dangerous
- Connection to postpartum depression and anxiety
- Sleep deprivation linked to suicidal ideation in women with postpartum depression
- Warning signs:
  - Cannot sleep even when baby is asleep
  - Persistent mood changes, anxiety, panic attacks
  - Thoughts of harming self or baby
  - Extreme fatigue interfering with basic daily tasks
  - Loss of interest in things usually enjoyed
  - Difficulty bonding with baby
  - Loss of appetite
  - Feeling incapable of being a good parent

### Resources
- National Maternal Mental Health Hotline (US): 1-833-852-6262 (24/7)
- Postpartum Support International: 1-800-944-4773

### Survival strategies
- Shift-based sleeping: partners alternate who responds to baby
- "Sleep when the baby sleeps" — cliché but evidence-supported for the first weeks
- Accept help from family/friends for daytime coverage
- If difficulty sleeping persists beyond a few weeks when baby is sleeping, consult a healthcare provider
