# Your Newborn's Bones & Movement — Soft Spots, Reflexes, and Vitamin D

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — most of what you'll notice is normal newborn anatomy doing exactly what it should.**

**The short version:** Your newborn's skeleton is softer, bendier, and more numerous than yours — about 270 bones compared to an adult's 206, most of them still cartilage rather than hard bone. Their skull has six soft spots (fontanelles) that allowed the head to squeeze through the birth canal, and their movements are driven by built-in primitive reflexes, not conscious control. The one action item: start vitamin D drops (400 IU daily) within the first few days of life.

**Normal:** Cone-shaped head after delivery, jerky limb movements, startle reflex, soft spots you can feel on the skull.
**Not normal:** One arm not moving like the other, absent startle reflex, bulging or deeply sunken soft spot, extreme floppiness or stiffness.

## Your newborn's skeleton — soft by design

Your baby was born with roughly 270 separate bones — far more than the 206 you carry around as an adult. [Source: src2] The difference is that many of those bones haven't fused yet, and a large proportion of the skeleton is still cartilage rather than solid bone. This is not a defect. It is engineering.

A rigid skeleton could never fit through the birth canal. Your baby's body solved this problem by keeping things flexible. The skull, in particular, is made up of five major bones (two frontal, two parietal, and one occipital) separated by connective tissue junctions called **cranial sutures**. These sutures allow the skull bones to shift and overlap during delivery. [Source: src2]

### Fontanelles: the soft spots

The places where sutures meet are the **fontanelles** — the soft spots you can feel on your baby's head. Newborns have six of them at birth. [Source: src1]

The two you'll notice most:

- **The anterior fontanelle** — the larger diamond-shaped spot on the top of the head. This one stays open until roughly 12 to 18 months of age.
- **The posterior fontanelle** — the smaller triangular spot near the back of the head. This typically closes by 6 to 8 weeks. [Source: src1]

Fontanelles close through a process called **intramembranous ossification** — the body gradually converts the connective tissue into bone. [Source: src1] In the meantime, the open fontanelles serve an important purpose: they give your baby's rapidly growing brain the room it needs to expand.

**It's fine to gently touch the soft spots.** They are covered by a tough membrane. Normal gentle handling — washing the head, stroking your baby's hair — will not hurt them.

### Skull molding after birth

If your baby was born vaginally, their head may have looked cone-shaped or slightly lopsided in the first hours. This is called **skull molding**, and it happened because the skull bones shifted to fit through the birth canal. [Source: src2] It is completely normal and typically resolves within a few days. The head will round out on its own.

## Primitive reflexes — your baby's built-in movement

Your newborn doesn't control their movements the way you do. Their brain's cortex — the part responsible for voluntary movement — is still immature. Instead, their movements are driven by **primitive reflexes**: involuntary motor responses that originate in the brainstem and are present from birth. [Source: src3]

These reflexes are not random. They are survival tools, and their presence at birth is a sign that your baby's nervous system is developing normally. Your pediatrician will check for them.

### The Moro (startle) reflex

**What triggers it:** A sudden noise, a feeling of falling, or a change in position.

**What it looks like:** Your baby's arms fly outward with fingers spread wide, then quickly pull back in toward the body — as if reaching out for something to hold on to. [Source: src4]

**How long it lasts:** The Moro reflex is present at birth and begins to fade around 12 weeks. It should be completely gone by 6 months. [Source: src4]

**Why it matters:** The Moro reflex should be **symmetric** — both arms should respond the same way. If one arm doesn't extend or seems limp, this could indicate a clavicle fracture or nerve injury from delivery and should be evaluated. [Source: src4]

### The palmar grasp reflex

**What triggers it:** Stroking or pressing the palm of your baby's hand.

**What it looks like:** Your baby's fingers close tightly around whatever touched the palm. The grip can be surprisingly strong. [Source: src6]

**How long it lasts:** Present at birth, disappears by about 5 to 6 months as voluntary grasping develops. [Source: src6]

### The rooting reflex

**What triggers it:** Stroking the cheek or corner of the mouth.

**What it looks like:** Your baby turns their head toward the touch with mouth open, searching for something to suck. [Source: src5]

**How long it lasts:** Present at birth in full-term infants and integrates by 4 to 6 months as the brain matures. [Source: src5]

This reflex exists to help your baby find the breast or bottle. It is one of the most important feeding reflexes, and its presence is a reassuring sign that the nervous system is functioning as expected.

## Vitamin D — starting in the first few days

Here is the one clear action item from this article: **all infants need 400 IU of vitamin D daily, beginning within the first few days of life.** [Source: src7]

This recommendation comes from the American Academy of Pediatrics and applies to all infants — breastfed, formula-fed, or a combination.

**Why?** Breast milk is excellent nutrition, but it does not contain enough vitamin D on its own. [Source: src8] Vitamin D is essential for bone development, and without supplementation, breastfed infants are at risk for deficiency. This is not a flaw in breast milk — it is simply the reality that humans evolved getting vitamin D from sun exposure, and we no longer rely on that.

**Does formula count?** Most infant formulas are fortified with vitamin D. If your baby drinks at least 32 ounces of formula per day, they may not need a separate supplement. But in the first month, most newborns are not yet drinking that volume, so your pediatrician may still recommend drops.

**How to give it:** Vitamin D drops for infants are widely available over the counter. A single drop delivers 400 IU. You can place it directly on the nipple before feeding or on a pacifier. [Source: src11]

Research confirms that 400 IU/day from early infancy effectively raises vitamin D levels to a healthy range by 4 months of age. [Source: src11]

## Birth-related bone injuries

The most common birth-related bone injury is a **clavicle (collarbone) fracture**, occurring in roughly 0.5% to 4% of all births. [Source: src10]

**Risk factors include:**
- Large baby
- Shoulder getting stuck during delivery (shoulder dystocia)
- Narrow birth canal
- Use of assisted delivery tools (forceps, vacuum) [Source: src9]

**The good news:** Newborn bones heal remarkably fast. Most clavicle fractures resolve on their own without any treatment, and surgery is almost never needed. [Source: src9] You may notice a small bump along the collarbone as the bone heals — this is normal callus formation and will smooth out over time.

**How it's detected:** Often the first sign is an **asymmetric Moro reflex** — your baby startles normally on one side but doesn't extend the arm on the injured side. Your baby may also seem uncomfortable when that arm is moved or when picked up under the arms. [Source: src4]

Neonatal clavicular fractures typically resolve spontaneously without long-term complications. [Source: src10]

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Call your doctor promptly or go to the emergency room:**
- **Absent reflexes** — your newborn does not exhibit a Moro (startle) reflex, grasp reflex, or rooting reflex. Absent primitive reflexes at birth are abnormal and need investigation. [Source: src3]
- **Asymmetric movement** — one arm or leg moves noticeably less than the other, or the Moro reflex is one-sided. This may indicate a fracture or nerve injury. [Source: src4]
- **Bulging fontanelle** — a soft spot that feels tense and is bulging outward when your baby is upright and calm. This can indicate increased pressure inside the skull and requires urgent evaluation. [Source: src1]
- **Deeply sunken fontanelle** — a soft spot that appears noticeably depressed. This can be a sign of dehydration. [Source: src1]
- **Extreme floppiness (hypotonia)** — your baby feels unusually limp, like a rag doll, with very little muscle tone. This can indicate a neuromuscular condition and needs evaluation. [Source: src3]
- **Extreme stiffness (hypertonia)** — your baby's limbs feel rigid and difficult to move. [Source: src3]
- **No spontaneous movement** — your baby does not move their limbs on their own at all.

**🟡 Mention at your next pediatric visit:**
- Mild head asymmetry that doesn't resolve within a week of birth
- A small bump along the collarbone (likely healing fracture — your doctor should confirm)
- You're unsure whether your baby's reflexes look normal

> **A note for new parents:** Newborns are strange-looking creatures. Their heads are oddly shaped, their movements are jerky and involuntary, and they have soft spots on their skulls that feel alarming to touch. All of this is normal. Their skeleton was designed to be flexible enough to arrive safely and strong enough to grow into a full-sized human being. The things that matter right now are simple: vitamin D drops, safe handling, and knowing the handful of warning signs above. Everything else sorts itself out.
