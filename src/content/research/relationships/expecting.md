## Research Brief

Pregnancy restructures every significant relationship — the couple relationship faces its biggest test, existing children must be prepared for a new sibling, grandparents negotiate new roles, and the parent-to-be begins forming a bond with an unborn child. This article covers the couple relationship during pregnancy, preparing siblings, establishing grandparent roles and boundaries, and nurturing the prenatal bond.

## Sources

- **Date:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.gottman.com/
- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The Couple Relationship — Before the Storm
- Key points: 67% decline, 33% who thrive, prenatal programs work, the division-of-labor conversation
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Invest in your relationship now — you're about to stress-test it

### Section 2: Preparing Existing Children
- Key points: Age-appropriate preparation, concrete for young children, honest expectations
- Supported by: Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: Your firstborn doesn't need to be "excited" about the baby. They need to know they're still loved.

### Section 3: Grandparents — Roles and Boundaries
- Key points: Involvement is positive when boundaried, proactive boundary-setting, #1 source of conflict
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The boundary conversation with grandparents is uncomfortable now. It's explosive later if you skip it.

### Section 4: The Bond Begins
- Key points: Prenatal attachment, both parents bond, different pathways, predicts postnatal relationship
- Supported by: Source 5, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: The relationship with your child starts during pregnancy — for both parents

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, grandparent involvement during the perinatal period is the norm, not the exception — boundary-setting needs cultural sensitivity
- 坐月子 traditions affect grandparent dynamics — the grandmother's role is deeply culturally embedded
- Chinese couples may not be accustomed to explicit communication about labor division or parenting values — frame as practical, not confrontational
- Intergenerational pattern reflection is particularly relevant in Chinese families where filial piety may make questioning parents' approach feel disrespectful
