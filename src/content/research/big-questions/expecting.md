## Research Brief

Pregnancy prompts some of the deepest existential reflection in adult life — questions about mortality, meaning, legacy, and what kind of world you're bringing a child into. This article covers the existential dimension of becoming a parent, values clarification before the child arrives, how parents' own meaning-making frameworks shape what they'll teach, and preparing for the questions your child will inevitably ask.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Pregnancy as Existential Wake-Up Call
- Key points: Identity reorganization, mortality salience, legacy thinking, meaning-making
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: If pregnancy is making you think about the meaning of life — that's exactly what it's supposed to do

### Section 2: What Do You Actually Believe?
- Key points: Values clarification, examining your own upbringing, unexamined defaults
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 4
- Parent-facing angle: Your child will absorb your values whether you choose them or not. Better to choose.

### Section 3: Talking Values With Your Partner
- Key points: Align on faith, discipline, education, values before baby arrives, shared meaning
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: The conversation about "what do we want to teach our child?" is harder before the baby — and easier than after

### Section 4: Preparing for the Questions
- Key points: Religious and secular frameworks, children will ask about death/God/fairness, your framework shapes your responses
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Your child will ask you about death, God, and fairness. What will you say?

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, existential/philosophical reflection may be less explicitly encouraged during pregnancy — 胎教 focuses more on education and environment
- Religious diversity between Chinese and Western audiences is significant — article must work for Buddhist, Christian, secular, and other frameworks
- Partner disagreements about religion and values can be a major source of conflict — present as a dialogue to have, not a problem to solve
- Some parents may find existential reflection during pregnancy anxiety-inducing rather than helpful — validate both responses
- Mortality salience should be presented as normal and generative, not morbid
