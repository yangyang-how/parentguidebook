# Your Teen's Bones — Peak Bone Mass, Sports Injuries, and Scoliosis

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — adolescence is the last major window to build bone density for life, and several serious threats can derail that process.**

**The short version:** Your teen is building a "bone bank" right now — one they'll draw from for the rest of their life. Roughly 90% of peak bone mass is locked in by age 18–20, with a remarkable 25% of adult bone laid down in just two years of peak pubertal growth. [Sources: src4, src2] Calcium needs are at their highest ever (1,300 mg/day), yet fewer than 15% of teenage girls actually meet that target. [Source: src3] Meanwhile, sports injuries — especially ACL tears and stress fractures — are common, eating disorders can devastate bone health within months, and scoliosis needs screening before curves progress too far.

**Key points:**
- 90% of peak bone mass is built by age 18–20 — this window doesn't reopen [Source: src4]
- Teens need 1,300 mg calcium/day, but most fall far short [Source: src3]
- Vitamin D deficiency affects 17–47% of adolescents [Source: src5]
- Female athletes have 2–9x higher ACL injury risk than males in the same sports [Source: src10]
- Up to 90% of patients with anorexia have low bone density [Source: src8]
- Scoliosis affects 1–3% of adolescents; bracing works 75% of the time when caught early [Sources: src11, src12]

## The last big window — building bone for life

Think of adolescence as the final deposit period for your teen's lifelong bone bank. After this, the account is mostly closed.

Peak bone mineral content velocity — the fastest rate of bone building — occurs around age 12.5 in girls and age 14 in boys. During the two years surrounding that peak, adolescents accumulate over 25% of their total adult bone mass. [Source: src2] By age 18–20, approximately 90% of peak bone mass is already in place. [Source: src4] Bone continues to build slowly into the late 20s, but the heavy lifting is done during puberty. [Source: src14]

**What this means practically:** The habits your teen builds now — what they eat, how they move, whether they get enough sunlight — have a disproportionate impact on their skeleton for the next 60+ years. A teen who reaches a high peak bone mass enters adulthood with a larger reserve to draw from as natural bone loss begins later in life. [Source: src4]

Half of a woman's total body calcium and up to two-thirds of a man's are deposited during puberty alone. [Source: src5] This is why nutritional deficiencies during adolescence are especially damaging — the body simply can't make up the difference later.

## Calcium and vitamin D — teenagers need the most

### Calcium: 1,300 mg per day

Adolescents ages 10–20 need 1,300 mg of calcium daily — more than at any other stage of life. [Sources: src3, src6] That's roughly the equivalent of four glasses of milk, or three cups of yogurt, or some combination of dairy, fortified foods, and calcium-rich vegetables.

**Most teens don't come close.** Fewer than 15% of adolescent girls in the United States consume the recommended amount. The average intake for teenage girls is just 876 mg/day — about two-thirds of what they need. [Source: src3]

**Good calcium sources:**
- Dairy (milk, yogurt, cheese) — the most bioavailable source
- Fortified plant milks and orange juice (check labels — absorption varies)
- Tofu made with calcium sulfate
- Leafy greens (kale, bok choy — spinach has calcium but poor absorption)
- Canned fish with bones (sardines, salmon)

> **For parents:** If your teen avoids dairy, they need a deliberate plan to get calcium from other sources. Plant milks vary widely in calcium content and bioavailability — not all are equivalent to cow's milk. Read labels and consider a supplement if dietary intake consistently falls short. Talk to your teen's doctor about what makes sense. [Source: src1]

### Vitamin D: 600 IU per day

Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption. The recommended intake for adolescents is 600 IU per day. [Source: src6] Cross-sectional studies have found vitamin D deficiency in 17% to 47% of adolescents — a surprisingly wide and high range. [Source: src5] Teens with darker skin and those living in northern latitudes or spending most of their time indoors are at higher risk.

### Weight-bearing exercise: the third pillar

Calcium and vitamin D get the headlines, but weight-bearing physical activity is equally important. The AAP identifies all three — calcium, vitamin D, and weight-bearing exercise — as the key factors for achieving optimal bone health during childhood and adolescence. [Source: src1]

Weight-bearing activities include running, jumping, dancing, basketball, soccer, gymnastics, and resistance training. Swimming and cycling, while excellent for cardiovascular fitness, don't load the skeleton in the same way and are less effective for bone building.

## Sports and the adolescent skeleton

### ACL injuries

Female adolescents participating in jumping and pivoting sports — basketball, soccer, volleyball, skiing — have a two to nine times greater chance of tearing their ACL compared to male athletes in the same sports. [Source: src10] The reasons are multifactorial: anatomical differences (wider pelvis, narrower femoral notch), hormonal influences, and neuromuscular patterns (landing with straighter knees, greater knee valgus).

**The good news:** ACL prevention programs work and are underutilized. Neuromuscular training warm-ups — programs like FIFA 11+ — have been shown to reduce ACL injury risk significantly. These involve balance exercises, plyometrics, and learning to land with proper knee alignment. [Source: src10]

### Stress fractures

Stress fractures — tiny cracks in bone from repetitive loading — are more common in adolescent athletes than many parents realize, and more common in female athletes. [Source: src10] They typically present as localized pain that worsens with activity and improves with rest. Common sites include the shin (tibia), foot (metatarsals), and lower back (pars interarticularis).

Risk factors include rapid increases in training volume, single-sport specialization, inadequate nutrition, and — in female athletes — menstrual irregularities (more on this below).

### Growth plate injuries

The growth plates (physes) at the ends of long bones are the weakest link in a teenager's musculoskeletal system. Until the plates close — typically by age 14–16 in girls and 16–18 in boys — they're more vulnerable to injury than the surrounding ligaments and tendons. A force that would cause a sprain in an adult may cause a growth plate fracture in a teen.

> **For parents:** Diversified sport participation — playing multiple sports rather than specializing in one before age 12 — reduces overuse injury risk. The AAP recommends against early single-sport specialization for exactly this reason.

## The female athlete triad and RED-S

The female athlete triad describes three interconnected conditions seen in competitive female athletes: disordered eating, menstrual irregularity, and low bone density. [Source: src7] The concept has been broadened to RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport), which recognizes that under-fueling affects male athletes too and impacts far more than just these three systems.

The core problem is energy availability — the athlete isn't eating enough to fuel both their training and their body's basic needs. Under-fueling can lead to stress fractures, delayed growth, poor muscle development, and prolonged recovery from injuries. [Source: src7]

**Warning signs to watch for:**
- Restrictive eating patterns or obsessive calorie counting
- Missed or irregular periods in female athletes
- Stress fractures or frequent injuries
- Declining performance despite heavy training
- Fatigue, mood changes, or social withdrawal
- Excessive exercise beyond what the sport requires

> **For parents:** Amenorrhea (missed periods) in a teenage athlete is **never** normal and always warrants medical evaluation. It's not a sign that she's "training hard enough" — it's a sign that her body is shutting down non-essential functions because it isn't getting enough fuel. [Source: src9]

## Eating disorders and bone health

This section is not just about athletes. Eating disorders occur across all demographics, all sports, and all genders.

### The damage is severe and fast

Up to 90% of patients with anorexia nervosa have low bone mineral density. [Source: src8] More than half of girls with anorexia show significant bone loss in less than one year. [Source: src8] The mechanism is straightforward: dietary restriction leads to low estrogen levels and increased bone breakdown, combined with reduced IGF-1 secretion and therefore reduced bone formation. It's a double hit — more bone is lost and less new bone is built. [Source: src9]

Patients with anorexia have approximately three times the fracture risk of their peers. [Source: src8]

### Why adolescent onset is especially devastating

Those who develop an eating disorder at a younger age experience more severe bone health consequences because they're unable to reach peak bone mass during the critical window. [Source: src8] An adult who develops anorexia loses bone they already built. A teenager who develops anorexia never builds it in the first place. The deficit may never be fully recovered.

### What recovery looks like

Addressing the eating disorder itself is essential for bone recovery. No amount of calcium supplementation or exercise can compensate for the hormonal disruption caused by severe caloric restriction. [Source: src9] Weight restoration, return of normal menstrual cycles, and adequate nutrition are the foundations of bone recovery — though full recovery may take years, and some bone density may never be regained.

> **For parents:** If your teen is losing weight rapidly, refusing meals, exercising compulsively, or showing signs of body image distortion, their bones are likely already affected. Early intervention matters — both for the eating disorder itself and for the bone loss that accompanies it. Talk to your teen's doctor promptly. [Source: src8]

## Scoliosis — screening and bracing

### What it is

Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) is a lateral curvature of the spine that develops during the growth spurt of puberty. It affects 1–3% of adolescents with a Cobb angle of at least 10 degrees. [Source: src11] While it occurs in both sexes, girls are 10 times more likely than boys to have curves that progress to 30 degrees or greater — the threshold where treatment becomes more likely. [Source: src11]

### Should you screen?

This is genuinely debated. The major orthopedic societies (SRS, POSNA, AAOS, AAP) recommend screening examinations — for girls at ages 10 and 12, and for boys once at age 13 or 14. [Source: src12] However, the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) concluded that evidence is insufficient to recommend universal screening in asymptomatic adolescents. [Source: src11]

**The practical middle ground:** Ask your teen's pediatrician to check their spine during routine well-child visits. A simple forward-bend test (Adam's test) takes seconds and can identify curves that warrant further evaluation.

### Bracing works when caught early

For moderate curves (25–40 degrees) in a still-growing adolescent, bracing is the primary treatment — and it works. A landmark study showed 75% of braced patients had successful outcomes (curve did not progress to surgical threshold), compared to just 42% of those who were only observed. [Source: src12]

Bracing is most effective when the teen still has significant growth remaining. This is why early detection matters: by the time a curve is obvious to the naked eye, it may have progressed beyond the window where bracing is most effective.

## When to see the doctor

**🔴 Go to the ER:**
- Sudden severe bone or joint pain after an injury, with inability to bear weight
- Obvious deformity of a limb after a fall or collision
- Signs of a serious fracture — swelling, bruising, inability to move the limb
- Bone pain that wakes your teen from sleep, especially if accompanied by fever

**🟡 Schedule an appointment:**
- Persistent pain during or after sports activities lasting more than 2 weeks
- Missed or irregular periods in a female athlete [Source: src7]
- Signs of disordered eating or rapid unexplained weight loss [Source: src8]
- Visible asymmetry in the shoulders, waist, or hips (possible scoliosis) [Source: src11]
- Frequent fractures — two or more in a year, or fractures from minimal trauma [Source: src13]
- Bone or joint pain that's not improving with rest
- A teen who has stopped exercising or dropped out of sports due to pain
- Declining height velocity or delayed puberty

> **A note on "growing pains" vs. concerning pain:** Bilateral (both sides) aching that occurs at night and isn't related to activity is usually benign growing pain. Unilateral (one side) pain that's activity-related, progressively worsening, or waking your teen from sleep is different — and needs evaluation. [Source: src13]
