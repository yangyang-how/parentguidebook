# Your Baby's Breathing at 1–6 Months — RSV, Bronchiolitis, and Breathing Emergencies

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — this age overlaps with peak SIDS risk and peak bronchiolitis season.**

**The short version:** Between 1 and 6 months, your baby's respiratory system is maturing but still vulnerable. Bronchiolitis — usually caused by RSV — is the most common lower respiratory infection in this age group and the leading cause of infant hospitalization during winter months. Most cases are mild and resolve with supportive care at home, but parents need to know when breathing difficulty crosses into an emergency.

**Key points:**
- Normal respiratory rate: 30–60 breaths per minute [Source: src7]
- Periodic breathing continues but should be decreasing [Source: src7]
- RSV/bronchiolitis peaks around day 3–5, then gradually improves over 7–10 days [Source: src3]
- New prevention options: maternal RSV vaccination and nirsevimab for infants [Source: src9]
- Peak SIDS risk is 2–4 months — safe sleep practices remain critical [Source: src8]

---

## Normal breathing at 1–6 months

Your baby's normal respiratory rate at this age is 30 to 60 breaths per minute. When counting, make sure your baby is calm — crying or feeding will temporarily increase the rate. Count for a full minute for the most accurate reading. [Sources: src4, src7]

Periodic breathing (short pauses of 5–10 seconds) may still occur but should be less frequent than in the newborn period. Breathing should generally sound easier and more regular than it did in the first few weeks.

**When breathing sounds normal but different:** Babies at this age may still sound snuffly or congested, especially if they have a mild cold. Because they breathe primarily through their nose, even small amounts of mucus can be noisy. Saline drops and gentle nasal suction before feeds usually help.

## Bronchiolitis and RSV: the big one

Bronchiolitis is the most common lower respiratory infection in babies under 1 year. It's caused by a viral infection — most often respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) — that inflames the small airways (bronchioles) in the lungs, causing swelling and mucus buildup. [Source: src1]

### What it looks like

Bronchiolitis typically starts like a regular cold: runny nose, mild cough, maybe a low fever. Over the next few days, it can progress to wheezing, difficulty feeding, and increased work of breathing. Symptoms usually peak around day 3 to 5 of the illness, then gradually improve. Most cases resolve within 7 to 10 days, though the cough may linger longer. [Sources: src2, src3]

**Who's at highest risk for severe disease:**
- Babies under 12 weeks old [Source: src2]
- Premature infants [Source: src2]
- Babies with underlying heart or lung conditions [Source: src2]

### What helps at home

Bronchiolitis treatment is **supportive only**. This is important to understand because it means there's no antibiotic, no antiviral, and no special medication that speeds recovery. What helps is keeping your baby comfortable and hydrated. [Source: src1]

**What to do:**
- **Nasal suctioning before feeds** — this is one of the most helpful things you can do. A baby who can't breathe through their nose can't feed effectively. Use saline drops and a bulb syringe or nasal aspirator. [Source: src1]
- **Frequent, smaller feeds** — your baby may tire more easily during feeds. Offer smaller amounts more often rather than waiting for full feeds. [Source: src3]
- **Keep baby upright after feeds** when possible — this can help with breathing comfort
- **Watch for dehydration** — fewer wet diapers, dry mouth, no tears when crying. Dehydration from poor feeding is often a bigger immediate concern than the respiratory symptoms themselves. [Source: src3]

### What doesn't help

Here's where parents are often surprised:

**Bronchodilators (like albuterol) are NOT recommended for bronchiolitis.** Unlike asthma, where the airways are constricting, bronchiolitis involves swelling and mucus in tiny airways. Bronchodilators don't work on this kind of obstruction. [Source: src1]

**Corticosteroids are NOT recommended for bronchiolitis.** Despite being useful for other respiratory conditions, steroids have not been shown to help with bronchiolitis. [Source: src1]

**Antibiotics don't help** — bronchiolitis is caused by a virus. [Source: src1]

> **For parents in the ER:** If your baby is admitted for bronchiolitis, treatment will focus on oxygen (if needed), IV fluids (if dehydrated), and nasal suctioning. Don't be alarmed if the doctor doesn't prescribe a medication — the evidence says supportive care is the right approach. [Source: src1]

## Preventing RSV

There are now two new tools to protect your baby against RSV:

**Maternal RSV vaccination:** The Abrysvo vaccine, given to pregnant people at 32–36 weeks gestation, passes protective antibodies to the baby before birth. This provides passive immunity during the most vulnerable early months. [Source: src9]

**Nirsevimab:** A monoclonal antibody (not a vaccine) approved for infants 8 months or younger born during or entering their first RSV season. It's given as a single injection and provides protection throughout the RSV season (typically December–March in North America, though timing varies globally). [Source: src9]

**Basic prevention still matters:**
- Frequent handwashing, especially before touching the baby
- Avoiding close contact with people who are sick
- Keeping your baby away from crowded indoor spaces during RSV season when possible
- Not allowing anyone to smoke around your baby

> **Ask your pediatrician** about nirsevimab at your baby's next appointment if RSV season is approaching. This is a new option (approved in 2023) and not all families know about it yet. [Source: src9]

## When wheezing happens

About 20% of infants will have an episode of wheezing before their first birthday. Wheezing in young babies is common, usually caused by a viral infection, and does not necessarily mean your baby has asthma. Most infants who wheeze outgrow it. [Source: src5]

**What wheezing sounds like:** A high-pitched whistling sound, usually heard when your baby breathes out. It's different from the snuffly, congested sounds of a stuffy nose. [Source: src5]

**When wheezing needs attention:**
- Combined with fast breathing, retractions, or poor feeding
- Your baby seems to be working hard to breathe
- Wheezing comes on suddenly (possible foreign body — even at this age, babies can aspirate small objects)
- Your baby is lethargic or unusually sleepy

Not every wheeze needs an ER visit, but wheezing in a baby under 6 months should always be evaluated by a doctor — at minimum by phone. [Source: src5]

## Recognizing breathing emergencies

Learn these signs. They're the difference between "monitor at home" and "go to the hospital now."

**🔴 Go to the emergency room or call 911:**
- **Cyanosis** — blue or gray discoloration of the lips, tongue, or face [Source: src6]
- **Severe retractions** — the skin pulls in noticeably between the ribs, above the collarbones, or below the ribcage with each breath [Source: src6]
- **Nasal flaring** — the nostrils widen noticeably with every breath [Source: src6]
- **Grunting** — a sound at the end of each breath, as if straining [Source: src6]
- **Breathing stops for more than 20 seconds** [Source: src6]
- **Your baby is extremely lethargic** — limp, difficult to wake, not responding normally. Lethargy combined with breathing difficulty is a medical emergency. [Source: src10]

**🟡 Call your doctor (same day):**
- Fast breathing (consistently above 60/min when calm) [Source: src7]
- Wheezing for the first time [Source: src5]
- Refusing feeds or taking less than half the usual amount [Source: src3]
- Fewer wet diapers than normal (possible dehydration) [Source: src3]
- Cough with fever lasting more than a couple of days [Source: src10]

## Safe sleep during peak SIDS risk

The period from 2 to 4 months is when SIDS risk peaks. All the safe sleep practices from the newborn period continue to apply — and this is not the time to relax them. [Source: src8]

- Back to sleep, every sleep
- Firm, flat surface with nothing else in the sleep space
- Room sharing (not bed sharing) for at least 6 months
- No sleep positioners, wedges, or inclined surfaces
- Pacifier at sleep time is protective

If your baby is congested with bronchiolitis, you may be tempted to prop them up to help them breathe. **Do not use pillows, rolled blankets, or inclined surfaces in the crib.** If your baby needs to sleep slightly elevated, talk to your doctor about safe options — but a flat, firm surface remains the standard. [Source: src8]
