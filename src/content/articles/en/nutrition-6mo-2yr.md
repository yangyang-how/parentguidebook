# Starting Solids and Beyond (6 Months–2 Years) — First Foods, Allergens, and the Transition to Table Food

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — the timing of solid food introduction and early allergen exposure have lasting effects on your child's health.**

**The short version:** Around 6 months, your baby is ready to start eating food — but milk (breast or formula) remains their primary nutrition through the first year. The biggest updates in infant nutrition science: introduce common allergens **early** (around 6 months) to reduce allergy risk, prioritize **iron-rich foods** from the start, and know that baby-led weaning and spoon-feeding both work. By 12 months, your baby transitions to whole cow's milk, weans from the bottle, and joins the family table.

**Key takeaways:**
- Start solids around 6 months based on developmental readiness signs [Source: src2]
- Introduce peanut, egg, and other allergens early — this **reduces** allergy risk [Source: src1]
- Iron-rich foods should be among the first foods you offer [Source: src7]
- Baby-led weaning and spoon-feeding are equally safe — most families use both [Source: src6]
- Switch to whole cow's milk at 12 months, limit to 16–24 oz/day, and wean from bottle by 12–18 months [Source: src8, src10]

## When to start solids — and how to know your baby is ready

Most babies are ready for solid foods at around 6 months, but the specific age matters less than what your baby can do. [Source: src2]

**Signs of readiness:**
- Can sit upright with support and hold their head steady
- Shows interest in food — watches you eat, reaches for your plate
- Opens mouth when food approaches
- Has lost the tongue-thrust reflex (no longer automatically pushes food out with their tongue)

[Source: src2]

Solids should not be introduced before 4 months. Before that, the digestive system is not mature enough to handle food, and the risk of choking is higher. [Source: src2]

A key point that many new parents miss: **starting solids does not mean replacing milk.** Breast milk or formula remains the primary source of nutrition through the first year. Solid food at 6 months is about learning, exploring, and gradually building up — not about replacing what milk already provides.

### Meal frequency

Meal frequency should increase gradually as your baby gets more comfortable with food:

- **6–8 months:** 2–3 meals per day
- **9–11 months:** 3–4 meals per day, plus 1–2 snacks

[Source: src3]

Start with small amounts — a few teaspoons at a time — and let your baby guide how much they eat.

## The allergen revolution — introduce early, not late

If you were raised hearing that babies should avoid peanuts, eggs, and other allergenic foods until age 1, 2, or even 3 — **that advice has been completely reversed.** The evidence now strongly supports early introduction of common allergens to **reduce** allergy risk. [Source: src1]

### What the science says

The LEAP trial was the study that changed everything. Infants at high risk for peanut allergy who regularly ate peanut-containing food starting between 4–11 months had dramatically lower rates of peanut allergy compared to those who avoided peanut. And this protection persisted — children who consumed peanut early continued to show tolerance through 12 years of age. [Source: src1]

Multiple additional studies (LEAP-On, EAT) have confirmed: early introduction of peanut and egg reduces allergy risk. [Source: src4] Delaying introduction of allergenic foods beyond 6–12 months may actually **increase** allergy risk rather than decrease it. [Source: src4]

### Current guidelines

- Introduce peanut and egg around 6 months of age (not before 4 months)
- Introduce other common allergens around the same time
- Early introduction is especially important for babies with eczema or a family history of food allergy
- Pre-screening (skin or blood tests) before introduction is **no longer recommended** for most babies

[Source: src1]

### How to introduce allergens safely

- Start when your baby is already tolerating some solid foods
- Offer a small amount (e.g., thin peanut butter mixed into puree, or well-cooked scrambled egg)
- Wait and watch for 10–15 minutes before offering more
- Introduce one new allergen at a time, with 3–5 days between new allergens [Source: src2]
- **Once introduced, keep offering the food regularly** — ongoing, regular exposure is what maintains tolerance. A one-time introduction is not enough. [Source: src4]

**Signs of an allergic reaction to watch for:**
- Hives or redness on the skin
- Swelling of lips, tongue, or face
- Vomiting
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing (call emergency services immediately)

If a reaction occurs, stop the food and contact your pediatrician. For breathing difficulty or swelling of the tongue/throat, call emergency services.

## Iron — the nutrient to prioritize

By 6 months, your baby's iron stores from birth begin to deplete, and breast milk alone no longer provides sufficient iron or zinc. [Source: src3] Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency in children, and it can affect brain development. [Source: src7]

This is why iron-rich foods should be among the very first foods you introduce.

**Good iron-rich first foods:**
- Iron-fortified infant cereal (rice, oat, or multi-grain)
- Pureed or finely minced meat (beef, chicken, turkey)
- Mashed beans or lentils
- Tofu (soft, mashed)
- Dark leafy greens (pureed)

[Source: src7]

**Tip:** Pair iron-rich foods with vitamin C sources — such as pureed tomatoes, bell peppers, strawberries, or citrus — to enhance iron absorption. [Source: src7]

If your baby is breastfed, your pediatrician may also recommend supplemental iron drops starting at 4 months to bridge the gap until iron-rich solids are well-established.

## Baby-led weaning, purees, or both

There is no single correct approach to introducing solids. Research shows both traditional spoon-feeding and baby-led weaning (BLW) are safe and effective.

### Baby-led weaning

Baby-led weaning skips purees entirely. Instead, you offer soft, appropriately sized finger foods and let the baby feed themselves from the start. Research shows BLW is associated with greater food enjoyment and less food fussiness compared to traditional spoon-feeding. [Source: src6]

**And the choking question?** Studies find **no significant difference in choking risk** between baby-led weaning and traditional spoon-feeding when appropriate foods are offered. [Source: src6]

### Traditional spoon-feeding

Start with smooth purees and gradually increase texture over weeks and months. This is the approach most parents are familiar with, and it works well.

### The combined approach

A combined approach — offering both finger foods and purees — is the most common real-world practice. [Source: src6] Most families don't follow a strict method; they adapt to what works for their baby.

### Texture progression matters

Whatever approach you choose, aim to introduce textured and lumpy foods by 9–10 months. Babies who remain on only smooth purees past 10 months may have more difficulty accepting textures later.

## Choking hazards — the foods to modify or avoid

Choking is a legitimate safety concern, but it's preventable with knowledge. The risk comes from specific food shapes and textures, not from the act of self-feeding.

**High-risk choking foods for babies and young children:**
- Whole grapes (cut lengthwise into quarters, not circles)
- Hot dogs (cut lengthwise, then into small pieces)
- Raw carrots (cook until soft or grate finely)
- Popcorn
- Whole nuts
- Chunks of cheese or meat (shred or cut small)
- Hard candy
- Large spoonfuls of nut butter (thin it and mix into other foods)

[Source: src5]

**Key safety rules:**
- Cut round foods into quarters, not circles [Source: src5]
- Always supervise mealtimes
- Baby should be sitting upright in a high chair, never eating while reclined, in a car seat, or while walking/crawling
- Never leave a baby alone with food

**Gagging is not choking.** When babies start solids, gagging is common and expected — it's a safety reflex that pushes food forward before it reaches the airway. Gagging is loud (coughing, sputtering); true choking is silent. Know the difference, and take an infant CPR class before starting solids.

## The transition away from breast and bottle (12–18 months)

As your baby approaches their first birthday, several feeding transitions happen in relatively quick succession. None of them need to happen overnight.

### Whole cow's milk at 12 months

At 12 months, you can introduce whole cow's milk as a drink. Not before — cow's milk before 12 months does not provide the right nutrient balance for infants and can be difficult to digest. [Source: src8]

**Whole milk, not low-fat.** Until age 2, children need the fat in whole milk for brain development. [Source: src8]

**But don't overdo it.** Limit whole milk to **16–24 ounces (2–3 cups) per day.** [Source: src8] Excessive milk consumption is one of the leading causes of iron deficiency in toddlers — it fills children up so they skip iron-rich foods, and milk itself can reduce iron absorption. [Source: src8]

If your baby resists the switch from formula or breast milk, try mixing small amounts of cow's milk with their familiar milk and gradually adjusting the ratio.

### Bottle weaning by 12–18 months

The AAP recommends weaning from the bottle by 12–18 months. [Source: src10] Prolonged bottle use is associated with dental cavities, especially when bottles are used at bedtime with milk or juice. [Source: src10]

Transition to an open cup or straw cup — not a sippy cup. Open and straw cups promote better oral motor development. [Source: src10]

### Juice limits

No juice before 12 months. After 12 months, limit to no more than **4 ounces per day.** [Source: src9] Whole fruit is always a better choice — it has the fiber that juice strips out. Excessive juice causes diarrhea, gas, bloating, and dental cavities. [Source: src9]

Water and milk should be the primary beverages.

## Honey warning

**Never give honey to a baby under 12 months.** Honey can contain spores of *Clostridium botulinum*, which can cause infant botulism — a serious and potentially life-threatening illness. This applies to all forms of honey, including honey in baked goods.

After 12 months, a child's digestive system is mature enough to handle these spores safely.

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Call your doctor promptly:**
- Signs of an allergic reaction after eating — hives, swelling, vomiting, difficulty breathing
- Your baby chokes on food (not gags — true choking is silent, and the baby cannot cough or cry)
- Weight loss or failure to gain weight
- Your baby refuses all solid foods past 8–9 months of age
- Projectile vomiting after eating (not occasional spit-up)

**🟡 Mention at your next pediatric visit:**
- Your baby gags frequently on textures (some gagging is normal; persistent gagging may need evaluation)
- You're unsure how to introduce allergens safely
- Constipation after starting solids (common and usually manageable with diet adjustments)
- Your baby seems to reject most foods and you're concerned about variety
- Questions about when to stop formula or breast milk

> **A note on first foods:** Starting solids is exciting and messy. Your baby will smear food on their face, drop it on the floor, spit it out, and possibly look at you like you've just offered them a personal insult. This is all normal. The goal at 6 months is exposure and exploration, not caloric intake. Milk is still doing the heavy lifting. Let your baby play with food, taste it, and figure out what this whole eating thing is about — on their own timeline.
