## Research Brief

The first six months of life are the baby's first great adaptation — from womb to world. Newborns must regulate temperature, breathing, feeding, and sleep-wake cycles in a radically new environment. Meanwhile, parents are adapting to an equally radical new reality. This article covers the infant's adaptation to extrauterine life, early routine establishment vs flexibility, temperament emergence, and how the feeding/sleeping balance shapes both infant and parent adaptability.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: The First Great Adaptation — Birth
- Key points: Massive physiological transition, skin-to-skin supports it, the journey from womb to world
- Supported by: Source 1
- Parent-facing angle: Your baby just did the hardest thing they'll ever do: be born. They're already adaptable.

### Section 2: Routines, Flexibility, and Finding a Rhythm
- Key points: Predictable routines support regulation, flexible > rigid, patterns emerge by 3-4 months
- Supported by: Source 3, Source 5
- Parent-facing angle: You're not failing if there's no schedule at 6 weeks. You're adapting — which is exactly right.

### Section 3: Meeting Your Baby's Temperament
- Key points: Temperament becomes visible, adaptability varies, goodness of fit approach
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: If your baby melts down at every new face or place, that's temperament, not a problem to fix

### Section 4: Your Own Adaptation
- Key points: Peak adjustment in first 3 months, tolerance for ambiguity, responsive caregiving
- Supported by: Source 6, Source 4, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: You're learning a new human's language. That takes time. Give yourself the same patience you give your baby.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese 坐月子 traditions provide structure during the first month — this can be supportive of adaptation or overly rigid
- Grandparent caregiving advice ("let them cry," "feed on schedule") may conflict with responsive caregiving evidence
- Cultural expectations for rapid "bounce back" post-birth are unrealistic — normalize the adaptation timeline
- Some infants' difficulty adapting may signal sensory processing differences — note when evaluation is warranted
