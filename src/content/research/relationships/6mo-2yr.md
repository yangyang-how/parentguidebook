## Research Brief

Between 6 months and 2 years, attachment patterns crystallize, separation anxiety tests the bond, siblings may be introduced, and co-parenting communication becomes either a strength or a flashpoint. This article covers attachment style emergence, separation anxiety and trust, sibling introduction, and effective co-parenting communication during the toddler chaos.

## Sources

### Source 1: Ainsworth — Strange Situation and Attachment Styles
- **Publication:** Developmental psychology / contemporary reviews
- **Date:** 2023
- **URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4085672/
- **Type:** textbook

**Key passages:**
> "Ainsworth's Strange Situation identified three primary attachment styles: secure (60%), avoidant (20%), and anxious-ambivalent (15%). Disorganized attachment (5-10%) was added later."

> "Secure attachment: child protests separation but is comforted by reunion. Avoidant: minimal distress, ignores caregiver at reunion. Anxious: extreme distress, not easily comforted."

> "Attachment style at 12-18 months predicts relationship patterns into adulthood, though it can be modified by later experience."

**Claims supported:**
- Four attachment styles with known prevalence
- Each style has specific behavioral signatures
- Predictive but modifiable

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Attachment Styles Crystallize
- Key points: Four styles, behavioral signatures, predictive but modifiable, internal working models
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: Your child is building their template for all future relationships right now

### Section 2: Separation Anxiety = Healthy Attachment
- Key points: Peaks 10-18 months, sign of bond, goodbye rituals, never sneak away
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: Your toddler sobbing at drop-off is proof they love you. That's the good news and the hard news.

### Section 3: New Siblings
- Key points: Intense toddler reactions, one-on-one time is critical, involvement > competition
- Supported by: Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: Your toddler isn't being "bad" about the new baby — they're terrified of being replaced

### Section 4: Co-Parenting and Extended Family
- Key points: Communication, no criticizing partner's caregiving, multiple attachments, grandparent dynamics
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5, Source 7
- Parent-facing angle: How you talk about and to your co-parent in front of your child shapes their world

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Chinese grandparents as primary weekday caregivers creates complex attachment dynamics — the child may form primary attachment to grandparent
- Criticizing in-laws' caregiving is a major source of Chinese family conflict — frame co-parenting broadly
- Multiple caregiver attachments are common in Chinese families and the evidence supports this arrangement when quality is high
- Separation anxiety may be handled differently in Chinese culture (less tolerance for crying) — present evidence for gradual approach
