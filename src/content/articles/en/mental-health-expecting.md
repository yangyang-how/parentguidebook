# Mental Health During Pregnancy — What Expectant Parents Need to Know

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — if you're struggling emotionally during pregnancy, talk to your provider. Treatment is safe and effective.**

**The short version:** Prenatal depression and anxiety affect up to 1 in 5 pregnant women — making them among the most common complications of pregnancy. Yet most cases go unrecognized and untreated. Screening is quick, treatment works, and getting help is one of the most important things you can do for both yourself and your baby.

**Key facts:**
- **10–20% of pregnant women** experience depression, and prenatal anxiety is at least as common [Source: src3, src5]
- Untreated prenatal mental illness is linked to **preterm birth, low birth weight, and effects on fetal brain development** [Source: src3, src5]
- ACOG recommends **universal screening** at the first prenatal visit and again later in pregnancy [Source: src1]
- **Psychotherapy is the recommended first-line treatment** — and the decision about medication is individualized, not a blanket "no" [Source: src4]
- About **10% of expectant fathers** also experience depression during the perinatal period [Source: src6]

**If you need help now:**
- **National Maternal Mental Health Hotline:** 1-833-943-5746 (24/7, free, confidential)
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text 988
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text HOME to 741741

## Prenatal depression and anxiety are common

There is a persistent cultural assumption that pregnancy should be a time of joy. For many people, it is — at least some of the time. But for a significant number of expectant parents, pregnancy brings depression, anxiety, or both, and the gap between expectation and experience can make it harder to recognize what's happening or to ask for help.

The numbers are clear: approximately 10–20% of pregnant women experience clinically significant depression. [Source: src3] Globally, about 10% of pregnant women experience a mental health condition, rising to 15.6% in lower-income settings. [Source: src8] Prenatal anxiety is at least as common as prenatal depression — and the two frequently co-occur. [Source: src5]

These are not small numbers. In a prenatal class of twenty, two to four people may be silently struggling.

### What prenatal depression looks like

Prenatal depression can be easy to miss because some of its symptoms overlap with normal pregnancy experiences — fatigue, sleep changes, appetite shifts, difficulty concentrating. But there are signals that go beyond what pregnancy itself explains:

- Persistent sadness, emptiness, or emotional numbness that lasts most of the day, more days than not
- Loss of interest in things you normally care about — including the pregnancy itself
- Feelings of worthlessness, excessive guilt, or a sense that you will be a terrible parent
- Difficulty bonding with the pregnancy or feeling emotionally disconnected from the baby
- Withdrawal from your partner, friends, or family
- Difficulty functioning at work or at home
- Thoughts of self-harm, or a feeling that others would be better off without you

If that last point describes you, please reach out today — to your partner, a friend, your provider, or one of the crisis lines listed in this article. You are not a burden. You are a person who needs and deserves support.

### What prenatal anxiety looks like

Prenatal anxiety often hides behind the label of "normal worry." Every expectant parent worries. But clinical anxiety is different in both intensity and impact:

- Persistent, overwhelming worry that something is wrong with the baby, even when every test is normal
- Racing thoughts you cannot slow down
- Physical symptoms: heart racing, nausea beyond morning sickness, muscle tension, difficulty breathing
- Sleep disruption that goes beyond pregnancy-related discomfort
- Inability to relax or enjoy anything about the pregnancy
- A constant feeling that something terrible is about to happen

Elevated maternal cortisol — the stress hormone — associated with chronic anxiety can affect fetal brain development, particularly the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. Children of mothers with untreated prenatal anxiety show higher rates of emotional and behavioral difficulties. [Source: src5]

This is not meant to add to your worry. It is meant to validate that prenatal anxiety is real, it has biological consequences, and it is treatable. Getting treatment is not selfish — it is one of the most important things you can do for your baby.

## Why screening matters

In 2023, ACOG issued a landmark clinical practice guideline recommending that all perinatal women be screened for mental health conditions at the initial prenatal visit, later in pregnancy, and at postpartum visits, using a standardized, validated instrument. [Source: src1]

This matters because casual "how are you feeling?" conversations are not screening. They miss most cases. Validated tools catch what conversation does not.

### The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)

Despite its name, the EPDS has been validated for use during pregnancy, not just after birth. It is a 10-item questionnaire that takes about five minutes to complete. A score of 10 or greater suggests that depression may be present and further evaluation is recommended. [Source: src7]

The EPDS is freely available online. If your provider has not screened you, you can ask them to — or complete it yourself and bring the results to your next appointment.

### One critical screening step

ACOG also recommends screening for bipolar disorder before starting an antidepressant, because 1 in 5 women who screen positive for depression may have undiagnosed bipolar disorder. [Source: src1] This matters because antidepressants alone can trigger manic episodes in someone with bipolar disorder. The screening takes minutes and can prevent a dangerous treatment error.

### If your provider doesn't ask

Some providers are not yet following the 2023 screening guidelines. Barriers include time constraints, lack of training, and uncertainty about what to do with a positive screen. [Source: src3] If your provider has not asked about your emotional health, it is not because it doesn't matter. You can raise it yourself — "I'd like to be screened for prenatal depression and anxiety" is a complete sentence that any provider should take seriously.

## How untreated mental illness affects your baby

This section requires directness. Untreated prenatal depression and anxiety are not just painful for you — they carry measurable risks for your baby.

Untreated prenatal depression is associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, and impaired infant neurodevelopment. [Source: src3] Chronic prenatal anxiety elevates cortisol levels, and sustained exposure to elevated maternal cortisol can affect the developing fetal brain — particularly areas involved in emotional regulation and memory. [Source: src5]

Children of parents with untreated prenatal anxiety show higher rates of emotional and behavioral difficulties later in childhood. [Source: src5]

**The point of this information is not to add guilt.** Guilt is the enemy of action. The point is this: the risk of leaving a mental health condition untreated during pregnancy often outweighs the risks of treatment. [Source: src4] Getting help protects you and your baby. It is not a luxury, an indulgence, or a sign of weakness. It is medical care.

## Treatment during pregnancy is safe and effective

There is a widespread fear that nothing can be done about mental health during pregnancy — that you must simply endure until the baby arrives. This is not true.

### Psychotherapy

Psychotherapy is the recommended first-line treatment for prenatal depression and anxiety. [Source: src4] Two approaches have particularly strong evidence:

- **Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT):** Helps you identify and change patterns of negative thinking. Effective for both depression and anxiety.
- **Interpersonal therapy (IPT):** Focuses on relationships and role transitions — particularly relevant when your identity, relationships, and daily life are all changing at once.

The USPSTF specifically recommends that clinicians refer pregnant individuals who are at increased risk for depression to counseling interventions, noting that CBT and IPT are effective not just as treatment but as prevention. [Source: src2]

### Medication

The decision about psychiatric medication during pregnancy is individualized — it is not a blanket "no." [Source: src4]

ACOG's 2023 guideline states plainly: "Untreated mental health conditions during pregnancy pose risks to both maternal and fetal health that often outweigh the potential risks of treatment." [Source: src4]

For many women, continuing or starting an SSRI during pregnancy is the right medical decision. Sertraline and fluoxetine have the most extensive safety data during pregnancy. The risks of medication must be weighed against the well-documented risks of untreated illness — not against a fantasy of zero risk.

If you are currently taking psychiatric medication and become pregnant, do not stop your medication without talking to your prescriber. Abruptly discontinuing medication can trigger a relapse that is worse than the original condition.

### What you can do while pursuing treatment

Treatment by a professional is the foundation. While you are getting connected to care, these evidence-based strategies can also help:

- **Physical activity** — even a daily 20-minute walk has documented antidepressant and anti-anxiety effects
- **Sleep protection** — this becomes harder as pregnancy progresses, but prioritizing rest when possible matters
- **Social connection** — isolation feeds depression and anxiety. Reach out, even when it feels hard.
- **Reducing unnecessary stressors** — this is the time to lower your standards about non-essential tasks

## Partners need support too

Approximately 10% of expectant fathers experience depression during the perinatal period. [Source: src6] Paternal depression during pregnancy is associated with increased relationship conflict, reduced relationship satisfaction, and poorer infant outcomes. [Source: src6]

Yet partner mental health screening is almost never conducted. Most prenatal visits are focused entirely on the pregnant parent's physical health, and even emotional screening — when it happens — rarely includes the partner in the room.

If you are the non-pregnant partner and you recognize yourself in the symptoms described in this article — persistent sadness, irritability, emotional withdrawal, anxiety that goes beyond normal expectant-parent worry, difficulty sleeping, loss of interest in things you used to enjoy — you deserve help just as much as anyone else. The EPDS can be used for partners, and your primary care provider can evaluate you.

Your mental health affects the family system. When one parent is struggling, the other is more likely to struggle too. Getting help for yourself is not selfish — it is something you do for your partner and your baby.

## What to watch for

### 🔴 Contact your provider promptly

- Thoughts of harming yourself
- Feeling that your family would be better off without you
- Inability to eat, sleep, or function in daily life
- Panic attacks that are becoming more frequent or severe
- Hearing voices or feeling disconnected from reality
- Symptoms that are getting worse despite your best efforts

### 🟡 Bring this up at your next appointment

- Sadness or anxiety that lasts most of the day, more days than not, for two or more weeks
- Difficulty feeling connected to the pregnancy
- Withdrawal from your partner, friends, or activities
- Sleep problems that go beyond normal pregnancy discomfort
- Feelings of worthlessness, guilt, or dread about becoming a parent
- A partner who seems emotionally withdrawn, irritable, or unlike themselves

## Getting help

**If your provider hasn't screened you:** Ask for it. "I'd like to be screened for prenatal depression and anxiety" is all you need to say.

**If you've been screened and scored high:** A positive screen is not a diagnosis — it is a signal that further evaluation is needed. This typically means a conversation with your provider or a referral to a mental health professional.

**If cost or access is a barrier:** The National Maternal Mental Health Hotline (1-833-943-5746) provides free, confidential support 24/7 and can help you find local resources. [Source: src10] Many community health centers offer sliding-scale mental health care. Telehealth has expanded access significantly — you may be able to see a therapist from home.

**If you are in crisis:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text **988** (24/7)
- **National Maternal Mental Health Hotline:** **1-833-943-5746** (24/7, free)
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text **HOME** to **741741**
