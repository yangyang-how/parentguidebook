## Research Brief

Toddlers and preschoolers are notoriously picky eaters — and that's developmentally normal. This article covers managing picky eating without power struggles, appropriate portion sizes, building a balanced diet from the five food groups, limiting added sugar and juice, mealtime behavior strategies, and growth monitoring. The key message: parents provide the what, when, and where; children decide the whether and how much.

## Suggested Outline

### Section 1: Picky Eating Is Normal (Really)
- Key points: 25-50% of toddlers are picky, intake varies 30% day-to-day, 15 exposures to accept a food
- Supported by: Source 1, Source 10
- Parent-facing angle: Your child refusing broccoli 10 times isn't stubbornness — it's biology. Keep offering.

### Section 2: The Division of Responsibility
- Key points: Parents choose what/when/where, child chooses whether/how much, pressure backfires
- Supported by: Source 2, Source 3
- Parent-facing angle: The most effective feeding strategy is also the most counterintuitive: stop trying to make them eat

### Section 3: What and How Much to Offer
- Key points: ~1,000-1,600 cal/day, all five food groups, portion sizes, low-fat milk at age 2
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 8
- Parent-facing angle: A toddler portion is about 1/4 of an adult portion. That tiny amount is probably enough.

### Section 4: Sugar, Juice, and the Foods to Limit
- Key points: <10% calories from added sugar, juice limits, sugary drinks, processed snacks
- Supported by: Source 4, Source 5, Source 6
- Parent-facing angle: You don't need to ban sugar — you need to keep it in its place

### Section 5: Family Meals and Mealtime Strategies
- Key points: Regular schedule, eat together, no screens, no short-order cooking, model eating variety
- Supported by: Source 7, Source 2
- Parent-facing angle: The dinner table is where food habits are formed — make it low-pressure and consistent

### Section 6: When to Worry About Growth
- Key points: Growth trajectory, crossing percentiles, when evaluation is needed
- Supported by: Source 9
- Parent-facing angle: Track the pattern, not the number. One low percentile means nothing — a dramatic change in trajectory does.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- In Chinese culture, grandparents often chase-feed children or use screens to distract during meals — these practices undermine self-regulation. The article should address this tactfully.
- Rice and noodles dominate Chinese toddler diets, which are low in iron and fiber. Suggest iron-rich additions rather than replacing these cultural staples.
- "Clean your plate" (不要浪费) is deeply embedded in Chinese culture due to historical food scarcity. This needs to be addressed with cultural sensitivity.
- The 2020-2025 Dietary Guidelines apply to ages 2+; children under 2 have different needs (higher fat, no sugar restriction).
- Toddler milk formulas (marketed heavily in China) are unnecessary and often high in sugar — this is a marketing issue, not a nutrition need.
