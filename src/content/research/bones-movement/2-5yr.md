## Research Brief

Between ages 2 and 5, children transition from toddling to running, jumping, climbing, and hopping — a period of explosive gross motor refinement. Physiologic bowlegs resolve and may shift to mild knock-knees (peaking around age 3-4). Fractures become more common as mobility increases, with greenstick and buckle fractures being typical pediatric patterns. Calcium needs rise to 700 mg/day (ages 1-3) and 1,000 mg/day (ages 4-8), with vitamin D at 600 IU/day. Diet remains the preferred source over supplements per AAP guidance.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Fractures in non-mobile or pre-walking children are concerning for non-accidental injury — but in active preschoolers, fractures from falls are expected
- "Toddler fracture" (spiral tibial fracture) is a specific entity caused by low-energy twisting — sounds alarming but is benign
- Knock-knees that are asymmetric, painful, or worsening after age 4 may indicate pathology (Blount's, rickets, metabolic bone disease)
- Picky eating is very common at ages 2-5 and can affect calcium intake — practical food suggestions are valuable
- Growth plate (physis) injuries in this age group can affect future bone growth — if suspected, imaging and follow-up are important
- 200,000+ playground injuries per year — safety framing should encourage active play while acknowledging real risks
