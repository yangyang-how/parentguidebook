## Research Brief

Months 1-6 is the pre-eruption and early eruption period. Many babies get their first tooth during this window. This article covers teething signs and relief, when to start brushing, the first dental visit timeline, and feeding practices that protect future dental health.

## Sources

### Source 1: Symptoms Associated with Infant Teething: A Prospective Study
- **Publication:** Pediatrics (AAP)
- **Date:** 2000
- **URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10742315/
- **Type:** cohort-study

**Key passages:**
> "Increased biting, drooling, gum-rubbing, sucking, irritability, wakefulness, ear-rubbing, facial rash, decreased appetite for solid foods, and mild temperature elevation were statistically associated with teething."

> "Symptoms were only significantly more frequent in the 4 days before tooth emergence, the day of emergence, and 3 days after it — an 8-day window."

> "No life-threatening symptoms were associated with teething. Fever above 38.9°C was not associated with teething."

**Claims supported:**
- Teething causes real but mild symptoms over an ~8-day window
- High fever is NOT a teething symptom — seek medical attention
- Drooling, irritability, and gum-rubbing are the most common signs

### Source 2: AAPD — Infant Oral Health Care Guidelines
- **Publication:** American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry
- **Date:** 2023
- **URL:** https://www.aapd.org/assets/1/7/g_infantoralhealthcare.pdf
- **Type:** guideline

**Key passages:**
> "Oral hygiene measures should be implemented no later than the time of eruption of the first primary tooth."

> "Cleansing the infant's teeth as soon as they erupt with a soft toothbrush will help reduce bacterial colonization."

**Claims supported:**
- Start brushing with a soft infant toothbrush as soon as the first tooth appears
- Before teeth erupt, continue wiping gums with damp cloth

### Source 6: Breast and Bottle Feeding as Risk Factors for Dental Caries: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
- **Publication:** PLOS ONE
- **Date:** 2015
- **URL:** https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4651315/
- **Type:** systematic-review

**Key passages:**
> "Breastfeeding for up to 12 months does not involve any increased risk of tooth decay; in fact, it can be a protective factor compared to formula."

> "Bottle feeding, particularly with sweetened beverages or at bedtime, increases caries risk."

**Claims supported:**
- Breastfeeding up to 12 months is protective for dental health
- Don't put baby to bed with a bottle of formula or juice

## Red Flags & Nuances

- Teething does NOT cause high fever (>38.9°C/102°F) — this misconception delays medical attention for real illness
- Benzocaine teething gels are still widely sold despite FDA warnings — be explicit about the risk
- Amber teething necklaces are a choking and strangulation hazard with no proven benefit — worth mentioning without being preachy
- Cultural note: In Chinese tradition, some grandparents may suggest rubbing alcohol on gums for teething pain — this is dangerous and should be explicitly discouraged
