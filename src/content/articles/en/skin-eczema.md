# Eczema in Children — A Complete Guide to Atopic Dermatitis

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — eczema is the most common chronic skin condition in children, and effective management makes a real difference in your child's comfort and quality of life.**

**The short version:** Eczema (atopic dermatitis) affects more than 20% of children in industrialized countries. [Source: src1] About 45% of cases begin before 6 months of age, and 89% start before age 5. [Source: src1] [Source: src2] It is a chronic condition caused by a defective skin barrier and an overactive immune response — not by poor hygiene, bad parenting, or anything you did wrong. The foundation of management is daily moisturization combined with appropriate anti-inflammatory treatment during flares. With consistent care, most children's eczema can be well controlled, and many children see significant improvement or resolution by adolescence.

**Key takeaway:** Eczema responds to proactive, consistent management. Undertreating it — hoping flares will resolve on their own — leads to worse outcomes, more infections, more sleep loss, and potentially more scarring.

---

## What is eczema?

Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is a chronic inflammatory skin condition characterized by dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It is not contagious. It is not an infection. It is the result of two things working together: a defective skin barrier that loses moisture and lets irritants in, and an immune system that overreacts to those irritants. [Source: src3]

### The skin barrier problem

In healthy skin, the outermost layer (stratum corneum) acts like a brick wall — skin cells are the bricks, and lipids (fats) are the mortar holding them together. In eczema, this wall has gaps. Moisture escapes. Irritants, allergens, and microbes get in. The skin becomes dry, inflamed, and itchy.

In 20 to 30% of children with eczema, this barrier defect is partly due to mutations in a gene called **filaggrin**, which plays a key role in skin barrier function. [Source: src3] Having a filaggrin mutation does not guarantee eczema, but it significantly increases the risk.

### Who gets eczema?

Eczema runs in families. If one parent has eczema, asthma, or hay fever (the "atopic triad"), a child's risk is higher. If both parents are atopic, the risk increases further.

**The numbers:**
- More than 20% of children in industrialized countries are affected [Source: src1]
- 45% of cases begin before 6 months of age [Source: src1]
- 89% of cases begin before age 5 [Source: src2]
- About 60% of children with eczema will see significant improvement by adolescence, though some carry it into adulthood

## How eczema looks at different ages

Eczema does not look the same at every age. Knowing where to expect it helps you recognize it — and helps you distinguish it from other conditions.

### Infants (0–6 months)
- **Location:** Face (cheeks, forehead, chin), scalp, outer surfaces of arms and legs
- **Appearance:** Red, dry, rough patches that may weep, crust, or develop small bumps
- **Key distinction:** The diaper area is usually spared — if the rash is concentrated in the diaper zone, it is more likely diaper dermatitis [Source: src4]

### Older infants and toddlers (6 months–2 years)
- **Location:** Face and outer limbs continue; may begin shifting toward flexural areas
- **Appearance:** Patches may become thicker and more defined; scratching marks become visible as babies develop the coordination to scratch

### Preschool and school-age (2–12 years)
- **Location:** Shifts to **flexural areas** — creases of the elbows, backs of the knees, wrists, ankles, and neck [Source: src2]
- **Appearance:** Skin may become thickened and leathery from chronic scratching (lichenification)

### Adolescence (12–18 years)
- **Location:** Flexural areas, hands, face, eyelids
- **Appearance:** Chronic, thickened patches; may be aggravated by stress, hormonal changes, and environmental factors

### Eczema on darker skin tones

On darker skin, eczema may appear as gray, purple, or dark brown patches rather than the classic red. After flares resolve, areas of **post-inflammatory hypopigmentation** (lighter patches) or **hyperpigmentation** (darker patches) may be visible. These pigment changes are temporary but can take weeks to months to fade. Eczema on darker skin is frequently underdiagnosed because clinicians may not recognize the non-red presentation.

## The foundation: daily moisturization

This is the single most important thing you can do for your child's eczema, and it never stops — not during flares, not between flares, not when the skin looks clear.

### Why moisturizing matters

Every treatment guideline — AAP, NICE, AAD — agrees: emollients should be the basis of eczema management for all patients, at all severity levels. [Source: src5] [Source: src6] Moisturizers work by filling gaps in the defective skin barrier, trapping water in the skin, and reducing the dryness and irritation that trigger flares.

### How to moisturize effectively

**The "soak and seal" technique:**
1. Bathe your child in lukewarm water (not hot) for 5 to 10 minutes
2. Use a gentle, fragrance-free cleanser — or just water
3. Pat the skin gently until slightly damp (not dripping, not dry)
4. Within 3 minutes, apply a thick, fragrance-free moisturizer over the entire body [Source: src7]

**Frequency:** At least twice daily, and always after bathing. [Source: src7]

### Choosing the right moisturizer

- **Ointments** (like petroleum jelly) have the highest oil content and trap the most moisture. They can feel greasy but are the most effective barrier.
- **Creams** are the middle ground — less greasy than ointments, more effective than lotions. These are often the most practical choice for daily use.
- **Lotions** have the highest water content and the least staying power. They evaporate quickly and are generally not recommended for eczema management.

**Non-negotiables:** Fragrance-free. Dye-free. Alcohol-free. Fewer ingredients is generally better.

## Treating flares: topical corticosteroids

When eczema flares — red, itchy, inflamed skin — moisturizer alone is not enough. Topical corticosteroids are the first-line treatment for eczema flares and have been used safely for decades. [Source: src3]

### How to use them

Apply a thin layer of the prescribed steroid to inflamed areas only (not to unaffected skin). Continue until the flare is under control — your doctor will advise on duration. Then return to moisturizer-only care.

### Potency levels

Topical steroids come in a range of potencies: [Source: src6]

- **Mild** (e.g., hydrocortisone 1%) — appropriate for the face, neck, skin folds, and mild eczema on the body
- **Moderate** (e.g., triamcinolone 0.1%) — for moderate eczema on the body
- **Potent** (e.g., betamethasone 0.05%) — for severe eczema, under specialist supervision only

The right strength depends on the severity of the flare and the location on the body. Thinner skin (face, eyelids, genitals) requires milder steroids; thicker skin (palms, soles) can tolerate stronger ones.

### Addressing steroid phobia

Many parents are afraid to use topical steroids on their child's skin. This fear is understandable — and it is one of the biggest barriers to effective eczema treatment. [Source: src3]

**The reality:**
- Low-to-medium-potency topical steroids used appropriately for eczema flares are safe
- The concern about skin thinning is valid for high-potency steroids used continuously for long periods — this is not what appropriate eczema treatment looks like
- Undertreating eczema carries its own risks: skin infections, sleep disruption, chronic scratching that leads to scarring, and quality-of-life impairment
- Talk to your doctor about the specific potency and duration appropriate for your child

### Proactive therapy — preventing the next flare

Once a flare is controlled, you can help prevent it from coming back. Applying topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors to previously affected areas **twice per week** — even when the skin looks clear — significantly reduces flare frequency. This approach, called **proactive therapy**, is recommended in the AAP's 2025 clinical report. [Source: src3]

## Beyond steroids: other treatment options

### Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus)

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory creams that are particularly useful for sensitive areas (face, eyelids, skin folds) where long-term steroid use is not ideal. They can also be used for proactive maintenance therapy. [Source: src3]

### Crisaborole

A non-steroidal topical phosphodiesterase-4 (PDE4) inhibitor approved for children 3 months and older. It works differently from both steroids and calcineurin inhibitors and can be an option for mild-to-moderate eczema. [Source: src3]

### Tapinarof

A newer non-steroidal topical treatment approved for children aged 2 years and older. [Source: src8]

### Dupilumab

A biologic medication (injection) that blocks specific immune pathways driving eczema inflammation. Approved for children as young as 6 months with moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis that is not adequately controlled by topical therapies. [Source: src3] Dupilumab has been transformative for many children with severe eczema.

### Topical ruxolitinib

A JAK inhibitor cream approved for patients 12 years and older. [Source: src3]

## The eczema–food allergy connection

About 30% of children with moderate-to-severe eczema also have food allergies. The relationship is complex and bidirectional. [Source: src9]

### What we know

Infants with eczema are significantly more likely to develop food allergies:
- One in five infants with eczema develop allergy to peanut, egg white, or sesame, compared to one in twenty-five infants without eczema [Source: src9]
- Infants with eczema are approximately 11 times more likely to develop peanut allergy [Source: src9]

The leading theory is **transcutaneous sensitization** — food proteins from the environment penetrate the disrupted skin barrier, and the immune system reacts to them as threats. This is why aggressive eczema management may matter beyond just the skin: by repairing the skin barrier, you may reduce the window for allergic sensitization. [Source: src10]

### What this means for your child

- **Early allergen introduction** (around 6 months) is now recommended, particularly for infants with eczema [Source: src10]
- **Do not eliminate foods** from your child's diet based on eczema alone without proper allergy testing — unnecessary dietary restrictions can cause nutritional problems and may paradoxically increase allergy risk [Source: src9]
- If you suspect a specific food trigger, work with your pediatrician or allergist for appropriate testing

## Infected eczema

Broken, inflamed skin is an open invitation for bacteria and viruses. Children with eczema are prone to skin infections, and recognizing them early matters.

### Bacterial infection (impetiginized eczema)

**Signs:** Eczema that suddenly worsens, with increased redness, weeping, golden-yellow crusting, swelling, or pain. Your child may develop a fever.

**What to do:** See your pediatrician. Infected eczema typically requires topical or oral antibiotics in addition to ongoing eczema treatment.

### Eczema herpeticum — a medical emergency

**What it is:** Herpes simplex virus infection of eczematous skin. This is a rare but serious complication.

**Signs:** Sudden eruption of clusters of small, painful, punched-out blisters on or near eczematous skin. Fever. The child looks and acts unwell.

**What to do:** **Seek emergency medical care immediately.** Eczema herpeticum requires antiviral treatment (acyclovir) and can become dangerous if untreated.

## Living with eczema — practical advice

### Sleep

Eczema itching often worsens at night. Strategies that help:
- Moisturize generously at bedtime
- Keep the bedroom cool (heat worsens itching)
- Dress your child in soft, breathable cotton
- Keep nails trimmed short to minimize scratch damage
- Wet wrap therapy (applying damp wraps over moisturized skin at night) can help during severe flares — ask your doctor for instructions

### Clothing and fabric

- Cotton and silk are generally well tolerated
- Avoid direct skin contact with wool — it is a common irritant
- Wash new clothing before wearing to remove chemical residues
- Use fragrance-free, dye-free laundry detergent
- Consider an extra rinse cycle

### School and childcare

- Send moisturizer with your child for reapplication
- Inform caregivers about your child's eczema routine and any triggers
- If eczema is affecting your child's participation in activities, effective treatment — not activity avoidance — is the answer

### When skin affects confidence

Visible eczema can affect self-esteem at any age, but especially in school-age and teenage years. Take your child's concerns seriously. Effective treatment is the most concrete thing you can do to help. If eczema is causing emotional distress, withdrawal from activities, or signs of anxiety or depression, discuss this with your pediatrician.

## Can you prevent eczema?

The honest answer: probably not, if your child is genetically predisposed.

Early studies suggested that daily emollient use from birth might reduce eczema incidence by up to 50%. [Source: src11] However, larger trials and a 2022 Cochrane review found no consistent evidence that emollient use prevents eczema, and one large trial (BEEP) found a possible increase in skin infections in the emollient group. [Source: src12] [Source: src13]

**The bottom line:** Moisturizing your baby's skin is good for skin health and comfort. It should not be relied on as eczema prevention. If your child develops eczema, it is not because you didn't moisturize enough.

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Seek medical attention promptly:**
- **Signs of eczema herpeticum** — sudden cluster of painful blisters, fever, unwell child
- **Signs of bacterial infection** — rapidly increasing redness, swelling, warmth, pus, or fever
- **Eczema not responding** to appropriate treatment after 2 to 4 weeks
- **Severe sleep disruption** from itching

**🟡 See your pediatrician soon:**
- You are using topical steroids regularly and want guidance on long-term management
- Eczema is spreading to new areas
- You suspect a food or environmental trigger
- Your child's eczema is affecting quality of life, school, or social activities
- You want to discuss newer treatment options (biologics, non-steroidal topicals)
