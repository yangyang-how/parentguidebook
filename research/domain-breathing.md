# Breathing & ENT Domain Research — All Ages

Compiled: 2026-03-25

## 1. Newborn Breathing (0–3 months)

### Normal respiratory rate
- Normal newborn respiratory rate: 30–60 breaths per minute
- Median respiratory rate ~46 breaths/min at 2 hours of life, then 42–44 breaths/min
- 95th percentile: 65 breaths/min at 2 hours, 58–60 thereafter
- Source: AAP Pediatrics, "Respiratory Rate During the First 24 Hours of Life in Healthy Term Infants"

### Periodic breathing
- Irregular/periodic breathing normal in first few weeks
- Brief pauses in breathing up to 10 seconds are normal (not pathological apnea)
- Pathological apnea: pauses >20 seconds OR shorter pauses with cyanosis/bradycardia
- Source: HealthyChildren.org (AAP)

### Obligate nasal breathing
- Infants up to ~3 months are "obligate nasal breathers" — primarily breathe through nose
- Narrow nasal passages mean even mild congestion causes noticeable difficulty
- Nasal saline drops + bulb suction are first-line for congestion
- Sneezing is normal — it's how newborns clear nasal passages (not necessarily a cold)
- Source: PMC5710987, StatPearls

### RSV prevention (2024–2025 guidelines)
- RSV is leading cause of bronchiolitis and pneumonia in infants
- Nirsevimab (Beyfortus): long-acting monoclonal antibody, single dose for first RSV season
  - Infants <5 kg: 50 mg; ≥5 kg: 100 mg
  - Recommended for infants <8 months entering first RSV season if mother didn't receive RSV vaccine
- Clesrovimab: second monoclonal antibody approved 2025
- Abrysvo (Pfizer): maternal RSV vaccine given at 32–36 weeks gestation
  - Most infants need either maternal vaccine OR infant antibody, not both
- Palivizumab (Synagis) no longer routinely recommended; discontinued in US after Dec 31, 2025
- RSV season: typically October–March in continental US
- Source: CDC RSV Immunization Guidance; AAP Pediatrics 2025 policy statement; AAP RSV FAQ

### Bronchiolitis
- Most common in infants <12 months; peak 2–6 months
- Usually caused by RSV (also rhinovirus, human metapneumovirus)
- Symptoms: runny nose → cough → wheezing → possible feeding difficulty
- Treatment is supportive only: nasal suctioning, hydration, oxygen if needed
- No benefit from albuterol, steroids, or antibiotics for bronchiolitis
- Risk factors for severe disease: age <12 weeks, prematurity, cardiopulmonary disease, immunodeficiency
- Hospitalization: inability to maintain hydration, persistent oxygen need, apnea
- Source: AAP Clinical Practice Guideline (Pediatrics 2014); UCSF Consensus Guidelines

## 2. Infant/Toddler Respiratory (3 months–3 years)

### Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis)
- Viral infection causing upper airway inflammation/swelling
- Classic: barky "seal-like" cough, hoarse voice, inspiratory stridor
- Peak age: 6 months to 3 years (can occur up to age 6)
- Usually parainfluenza virus; symptoms last 3–7 days, worst at night
- Treatment:
  - Mild: cool mist, comfort, single dose oral dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg
  - Moderate-severe: dexamethasone + nebulized epinephrine in ED
  - Dexamethasone improvement begins within 2–3 hours, persists 24–48 hours
  - Steroids recommended for ALL severities (even mild)
- ER signs: stridor at rest, significant retractions, cyanosis, drooling, unable to swallow
- Source: PMC5804741; CPS guidelines; StatPearls; NEJM dexamethasone RCT

### Wheezing in young children
- Very common: ~30% of children wheeze before age 3
- Most viral-induced wheezing (triggered by colds) — NOT necessarily asthma
- "Episodic viral wheeze" vs "multiple-trigger wheeze" distinction
- Only ~30% of wheezing preschoolers will have asthma by school age
- Modified Asthma Predictive Index helps identify which wheezers likely develop asthma
- Source: GINA 2024/2025; StatPearls Pediatric Asthma

### Pneumonia in children
- Viral etiology in 60–90% of pediatric CAP in developed countries
- RSV most common viral pathogen in children <2 years (up to 40%)
- Difficult to distinguish bacterial vs viral clinically
- Signs: fever, cough, tachypnea (most reliable sign), retractions, decreased breath sounds
- Treatment: amoxicillin first-line for presumed bacterial; 5-day course for most
- Azithromycin for suspected atypical (Mycoplasma); oseltamivir for influenza
- Source: PMC10839192; IDSA/PIDS 2011 guidelines; StatPearls

## 3. Adenoids and Tonsils

### Adenoid hypertrophy
- Adenoids: lymphoid tissue in nasopharynx; grow from birth, peak size 3–7 years, shrink by puberty
- Hypertrophy is extremely common — most common cause of nasal obstruction in children
- Symptoms:
  - Chronic mouth breathing (open-mouth posture, especially during sleep)
  - Snoring — often loud, nightly
  - Obstructive sleep apnea (pauses, gasping, restless sleep)
  - Hyponasal "stuffy" voice
  - Recurrent or chronic ear infections (adenoids block Eustachian tube drainage)
  - Chronic nasal congestion/rhinorrhea
  - "Adenoid facies": elongated face, open mouth posture (from chronic mouth breathing)
  - Halitosis (bad breath)
- Connection to ear infections: enlarged adenoids physically block Eustachian tubes → fluid trapping → recurrent otitis media and otitis media with effusion
- Connection to sleep: OSA in children 2–4% prevalence; adenotonsillar hypertrophy is the #1 cause
- Source: Merck Manual; NCBI Bookshelf (NBK536881); Boston Children's Hospital; PMC7100808

### When surgery is recommended
- Adenoidectomy indications:
  - Obstructive sleep apnea from adenoid hypertrophy
  - Recurrent acute otitis media (3+ in 6 months or 4+ in 12 months) with adenoid hypertrophy
  - Chronic otitis media with effusion (fluid >3 months with hearing loss)
  - Chronic sinusitis not responding to medical therapy
  - Nasal obstruction causing significant quality-of-life impact
- Often combined with tonsillectomy (adenotonsillectomy, "T&A") when tonsils also enlarged
- Source: AAO-HNS guidelines; PMC7100808

### Adenoidectomy outcomes
- Improves sleep apnea symptoms in ~85% of children
- Does NOT weaken immune system
- Safe procedure: adverse events rare, hospitalization uncommon
- Mean operative time ~15 minutes (endoscopic radiofrequency approach)
- Primary bleeding rate: 0.3%
- No deaths related to adenoidectomy in large population studies
- Children with severe OSA may need follow-up sleep study to confirm resolution
- Source: PMC7100808; Nature Scientific Reports 2025; PMC9543311

## 4. Ear Infections (Otitis Media)

### Prevalence
- AOM affects ~80% of children by age 5 (most common reason children prescribed antibiotics)
- Highest incidence: 6–24 months
- 75% of children have at least one episode by age 3
- Risk factors: daycare attendance, bottle-feeding while lying down, pacifier use, secondhand smoke, family history
- Source: AAP Pediatrics 2013; StatPearls

### Acute otitis media (AOM) vs otitis media with effusion (OME)
- AOM: bacterial infection with acute symptoms (ear pain, fever, bulging eardrum)
- OME: fluid behind eardrum WITHOUT active infection; often follows AOM
- OME can persist for weeks-months; may cause temporary hearing loss
- Source: AAP guidelines

### Antibiotic guidelines (AAP)
- Watch-and-wait option for:
  - Children ≥24 months with mild symptoms, unilateral AOM
  - Children 6–23 months with mild symptoms, unilateral AOM (without otorrhea)
  - Criteria: otalgia <48 hours, fever <39°C (102.2°F), pain controlled with analgesics
- Immediate antibiotics for:
  - All children <6 months
  - Severe symptoms (moderate-severe otalgia, otalgia ≥48 hours, fever ≥39°C)
  - Bilateral AOM in children 6–23 months
  - AOM with otorrhea (ruptured eardrum)
- First-line antibiotic: amoxicillin (80–90 mg/kg/day)
- ~70% resolve on their own within 2–3 days; ~80% within a week
- Source: AAP Pediatrics 2013; PMC10231305

### Ear tubes (tympanostomy tubes)
- Recommended for recurrent AOM (3+ in 6 months or 4+ in 12 months with 1 in past 6 months)
- Recommended for chronic OME with effusion >3 months, especially with hearing loss
- Small tubes placed through eardrum; allow fluid drainage and ventilation
- Usually fall out on their own in 6–18 months
- Very common procedure — ~667,000/year in US
- Source: AAO-HNSF guidelines; Children's Minnesota Otitis Media Guideline

## 5. Asthma

### Prevalence
- ~8% of US children (~6 million) currently have asthma
- Most prevalent chronic respiratory condition in children worldwide (~300 million cases)
- Higher prevalence in boys before puberty; equalizes or reverses after puberty
- Higher in Black, Hispanic, and low-income children (health disparity)
- Source: CDC; GINA 2024; StatPearls

### Diagnosis challenges in young children
- Cannot reliably do spirometry until age ~5–6
- Diagnosis in children <5 is clinical: pattern of symptoms + risk factors + treatment response
- Modified Asthma Predictive Index: recurrent wheeze + (1 major: parent asthma, eczema, allergic sensitization) or (2 minor: allergic rhinitis, wheeze without colds, eosinophilia ≥4%)
- Many young wheezers DON'T develop asthma — only ~30% do
- Source: GINA 2024/2025; StatPearls

### Triggers
- Viral infections (most common trigger in young children)
- Allergens: dust mites, pet dander, mold, pollen, cockroach
- Environmental: cold air, exercise, tobacco smoke, air pollution
- Irritants: strong odors, cleaning products
- Environmental triggers account for up to 80% of exacerbations in urban children
- Source: GINA 2024; NAEPP guidelines

### Treatment (step-wise approach)
- Step 1: as-needed SABA (albuterol) for intermittent symptoms
- Step 2: low-dose inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) daily — budesonide, fluticasone
- Step 3: medium-dose ICS or low-dose ICS + LABA
- Step 4: medium-dose ICS + LABA; consider specialist referral
- Children ≤5: SABA via pMDI with spacer + facemask
- Asthma action plans: green (doing well), yellow (caution), red (emergency) zones
- Source: GINA 2025; NAEPP Expert Panel Report 4

### Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction
- Common in children with asthma (~90% of asthmatics)
- Can also occur in children without asthma diagnosis
- Prevention: 2 puffs SABA 15–30 minutes before exercise
- Warm-up period helps; cold dry air worsens it
- Source: ACAAI; GINA

## 6. Allergies

### Allergic rhinitis prevalence
- Prevalence: >5% at age 3, 8.5% at 6–7 years, 14.6% at 13–14 years
- US estimates: 10–20% of children
- Often coexists with asthma (allergic march: eczema → food allergy → allergic rhinitis → asthma)
- Source: ISAAC study; Frontiers in Immunology 2024; Medscape

### Symptoms
- Nasal congestion, sneezing, runny nose (clear discharge), itchy nose/eyes/throat
- "Allergic salute" (upward rubbing of nose), "allergic shiners" (dark circles under eyes)
- Mouth breathing from chronic congestion
- Source: ACAAI; AAP

### Allergy testing
- Skin prick test: sensitivity 68–100%, specificity 70–91%
- Serum-specific IgE: can be done at any age
- Testing recommended when symptoms significantly affect quality of life or when immunotherapy is being considered
- No strict minimum age — both skin prick and serum IgE valid at any age
- Source: Frontiers in Immunology 2024; EUFOREA algorithm

### Immunotherapy
- Only causal/disease-modifying treatment for allergic respiratory disease
- Subcutaneous (SCIT) or sublingual (SLIT)
- Duration: typically 3–5 years
- Real-world data: reduces allergic rhinitis medication use, asthma exacerbations, new OCS prescriptions over 9 years
- May prevent progression of allergic march (rhinitis → asthma)
- Generally started age 5+ (though can be considered earlier)
- Source: PMC12666749; Frontiers in Pediatrics 2023

## 7. Common Colds

### Frequency by age
- Infants and preschoolers: 6–8 colds per year (up to 10–12 if in daycare)
- School-age: 5–6 per year
- Adolescents: ~4 per year (adult level)
- Most colds: rhinovirus (~50%), also coronavirus, adenovirus, parainfluenza
- Source: PMC2722603; AAFP 2012; HealthyChildren.org

### Why antibiotics don't help
- Colds are caused by viruses; antibiotics treat bacteria only
- Antibiotics do NOT prevent bacterial complications (ear infections, sinusitis)
- Unnecessary antibiotics cause: diarrhea, allergic reactions, antibiotic resistance
- Source: HealthyChildren.org (AAP); AAFP

### What helps
- Nasal saline irrigation (6x/day speeds resolution; reduces need for antipyretics and decongestants)
- Honey (age ≥1 year) for cough — evidence-supported
- Acetaminophen/ibuprofen for fever and discomfort
- Humidifier in bedroom
- Fluids, rest
- No evidence for: OTC cough/cold medicines in children <6 (AAP recommends against)
- Source: AAFP 2019; PMC2722603; HealthyChildren.org

### When it's not just a cold
- Fever lasting >3 days or returning after improvement
- Ear pain or ear discharge
- Sinus pain/pressure lasting >10 days
- Rapid breathing, difficulty breathing
- Wheezing
- High fever (>104°F/40°C) at any point
- Source: HealthyChildren.org; AAFP

## 8. Sleep-Disordered Breathing

### Prevalence
- Habitual snoring: 7–12% of children
- Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA): 2–4% of children; peak age 2–6 years
- Primary cause in children: adenotonsillar hypertrophy
- Source: AASM; PMC8470037

### Connection to behavior/ADHD
- SDB prevalence in children with ADHD: 25–57%
- 77.78% of children with ADHD show significant snoring
- 50% of ADHD children diagnosed with OSA on sleep study
- SDB can cause/worsen: inattention, hyperactivity, behavioral problems, learning difficulties
- Some children diagnosed with ADHD actually have untreated sleep apnea
- Treatment of OSA (adenotonsillectomy) improves ADHD symptoms in multiple studies
- AAP: screen children with hyperactive/inattentive behavior for sleep-disordered breathing
- Source: Frontiers in Sleep 2024; PMC5753659; PMC8470037; AASM

### Diagnosis
- Gold standard: overnight polysomnography (sleep study)
- Sleep study measures: airflow, oxygen levels, brain waves, muscle activity, heart rate
- Indicated when: habitual snoring + any of: witnessed apneas, gasping, restless sleep, daytime sleepiness, behavioral problems, poor growth
- Source: AASM; AAP

### Treatment
- First-line: adenotonsillectomy (cures OSA in ~85% of otherwise healthy children)
- If surgery not appropriate or incomplete resolution: CPAP
- Weight loss if obesity is contributing factor
- Nasal corticosteroids + montelukast for mild residual OSA
- Source: AAP; AASM

## 9. Choking and Foreign Body Aspiration

### Risk
- Most common in children under 3 years (81% of cases)
- Fourth leading cause of accidental death in children; third in infants <1 year
- Peak risk: 1–3 years (developing molars, tendency to put objects in mouth, immature swallowing coordination)
- Source: PMC7369691; StatPearls; PMC11090237

### Common dangerous objects
- Food: peanuts/nuts (most common), hot dogs (most fatal food item — 17% of food aspiration deaths), grapes, popcorn, raw carrots, hard candy, seeds
- Non-food: latex balloons (most fatal non-food item — 29% of aspiration deaths), small toy parts, coins, button batteries, pen caps
- Source: PMC7369691; EMRA

### Signs
- Sudden onset coughing/choking while eating or playing with small objects
- Choking episode present in 75–90% of cases
- Persistent cough (69%), difficulty breathing (69%)
- Wheeze or decreased breath sounds on one side
- If complete obstruction: inability to cough, cry, or speak; cyanosis
- Source: StatPearls; EB Medicine

### Emergency response
- Infants (<1 year): 5 back blows + 5 chest compressions (NOT abdominal thrusts)
- Children (>1 year): abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver)
- Call 911 if: object not dislodged, child becomes unresponsive, child turning blue
- AAP recommends CPR/choking first aid training for all parents and caregivers
- Source: AHA/AAP guidelines; StatPearls

### Prevention
- Cut food into small pieces; no round/hard foods before age 4 (grapes halved lengthwise, hot dogs quartered)
- Supervise meals — no eating while running/playing
- Keep small objects out of reach; check toy age ratings
- No latex balloons for young children
- Source: AAP; HealthyChildren.org

## 10. When to Go to the ER — Respiratory Emergencies

### Signs of respiratory distress (call 911 or go to ER)
- **Retractions:** skin pulling in at neck, between ribs, or below ribcage with each breath
- **Nasal flaring:** nostrils widening with each breath
- **Grunting:** sound with each exhale (body trying to keep airways open)
- **Tachypnea:** breathing much faster than normal for age
- **Cyanosis:** blue/gray color around lips, inside lips, fingernails
- **Stridor:** high-pitched sound when breathing in (suggests upper airway narrowing)
- **Altered consciousness:** unusually sleepy, difficult to wake, confused
- **Inability to speak or cry** (in older children — suggests severe obstruction)
- **Tripod positioning:** leaning forward, hands on knees (sign of severe distress)
- Source: CHOP; Nationwide Children's Hospital; Stanford Children's Health

### Specific emergencies
- **Croup emergency:** stridor at rest, drooling/unable to swallow, severe retractions, cyanosis
- **Asthma emergency:** reliever inhaler not helping, can't complete sentences, lips blue, rapid breathing at rest
- **Epiglottitis:** rare but life-threatening; high fever, drooling, muffled voice, "sniffing position" — call 911, do NOT examine throat
- **Anaphylaxis:** sudden wheeze/stridor + hives/swelling + any GI symptoms — give epinephrine (EpiPen), call 911
- **Foreign body:** complete obstruction, unable to cough/cry/speak — begin choking rescue maneuvers, call 911
- Source: HealthyChildren.org; CHOP; AAP

### Normal respiratory rates by age (for reference)
- Newborn: 30–60 breaths/min
- Infant (1–12 months): 30–60 breaths/min
- Toddler (1–3 years): 24–40 breaths/min
- Preschool (4–5 years): 22–34 breaths/min
- School-age (6–12 years): 18–30 breaths/min
- Adolescent (13–18 years): 12–20 breaths/min
- Source: WHO; Stanford Children's Health
