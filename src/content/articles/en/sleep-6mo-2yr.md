# Sleep from 6 Months to 2 Years — Night Waking, Nap Transitions, and New Battles

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟢 **Urgency: Good to know — most sleep challenges at this age are behavioral and solvable.**

**The short version:** Between 6 months and 2 years, sleep patterns consolidate, but new challenges emerge. Night waking peaks around 6 to 12 months — often driven by separation anxiety and sleep associations rather than hunger. Naps transition from two to one. The bedtime routine becomes your single most powerful tool. And toward the end of this period, the question of when to move from crib to bed starts looming. Most sleep problems at this age are behavioral, which means they respond well to consistent strategies.

**Normal:** Brief night wakings. Increased bedtime resistance around 12 to 18 months. Fussiness during nap transitions. Occasional night terrors starting around age 2.
**Not normal:** Loud snoring most nights. Breathing pauses during sleep. Persistent inability to fall asleep despite consistent routine.

## How much sleep does your baby or toddler need?

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine recommends:

- **6 to 12 months:** 12 to 16 hours per 24 hours, including naps [Source: src2]
- **1 to 2 years:** 11 to 14 hours per 24 hours, including naps [Source: src2]

These numbers include all daytime naps. A 9-month-old sleeping 11 hours at night with two 1.5-hour naps is getting 14 hours total — well within the recommended range.

Sleeping the recommended number of hours is associated with improved attention, behavior, learning, memory, emotional regulation, and mental and physical health. [Source: src2] Insufficient sleep in early childhood is associated with poorer executive function development — including impulse control and working memory — and may have cascading effects on cognitive development. [Source: src9]

### Nap transitions

Naps typically follow this trajectory during this period:

- **6 to 9 months:** Two naps per day (morning and afternoon)
- **12 to 18 months:** Transition from two naps to one [Source: src5]
- **18 months to 2 years:** One nap per day, usually after lunch, lasting 1 to 3 hours

**Signs your toddler is ready to drop to one nap:**
- Consistently taking a long time to fall asleep for the morning nap
- Taking the morning nap but then refusing the afternoon nap
- Napping well during the day but struggling to fall asleep at bedtime

The transition from two naps to one is often messy. There may be a few weeks where one nap isn't enough but two naps are too many. This is temporary. During the transition, you may need to move bedtime earlier temporarily to prevent overtiredness.

## Night waking — why it peaks now

Parent-reported night waking peaks between 6 and 12 months. About 30% of infants and toddlers experience night waking that parents consider a problem. [Source: src3] If your baby is waking frequently at night during this period, you are in a very large group of parents dealing with the same thing.

### Why it happens

The most common cause of night waking at this age is **sleep-onset associations.** Your baby has learned to associate falling asleep with a specific condition — nursing, rocking, being held, or a caregiver's presence — and when they naturally wake between sleep cycles during the night (which all humans do), they cannot return to sleep without that same condition being recreated. [Source: src4]

This is behavioral insomnia of childhood, sleep-onset association type — and it is the most common sleep problem in infants and toddlers. [Source: src4] It is not a medical condition. It means your baby has learned one way to fall asleep and hasn't yet learned another.

Other contributors to night waking at this age:
- **Separation anxiety** — peaks around 8 to 10 months and can make bedtime and night waking harder
- **Teething** — can cause temporary discomfort that disrupts sleep
- **Developmental leaps** — learning to crawl, stand, or walk can temporarily increase night waking as your baby's brain processes new skills
- **Illness** — ear infections, colds, and other illnesses are common sleep disruptors

### What works

**A consistent bedtime routine is the single most effective sleep intervention at any age.** A predictable sequence of 3 to 4 steps — bath, pajamas, book, lights out — done at the same time and in the same order every night, provides the cues your baby needs to prepare for sleep. [Source: src5]

The optimal bedtime is when your child shows signs of sleepiness — rubbing eyes, yawning, getting fussy — not when your schedule dictates. Timing bedtime to your child's natural sleepiness window reduces bedtime resistance. [Source: src5]

**Evidence-based strategies for persistent night waking:**
- **Graduated extinction:** Put your baby down awake, leave the room, and return at gradually increasing intervals to offer brief comfort (without picking up). [Source: src4]
- **Bedtime fading:** Temporarily shift bedtime to when your baby naturally falls asleep, then gradually move it earlier.
- **Gradual withdrawal (camping out):** Sit near the crib until your baby falls asleep, then move your chair a little farther away each night over 1 to 2 weeks.

All of these approaches are evidence-based, and the research does not show one to be clearly superior. [Source: src4] Choose the one that feels most manageable for your family.

## Safe sleep after 6 months

The safe sleep rules evolve slightly after 6 months, but the fundamentals remain:

- **Once your baby can roll both ways** — from back to stomach and from stomach to back — you can allow them to sleep in the position they assume on their own. You should still place them on their back initially. [Source: src1]
- **Room-sharing** without bed-sharing remains recommended through at least the first 6 months, and ideally for the first year. [Source: src1]
- **No loose bedding** in the crib — this continues until your child transitions to a toddler bed. [Source: src7]
- **SIDS risk drops significantly after 6 months** but does not reach negligible levels until after the first birthday. [Source: src1]

The crib should remain a firm, flat mattress with a fitted sheet and nothing else — no blankets, pillows, or stuffed animals — throughout this period.

## The crib-to-bed transition

Most sleep experts recommend waiting until around age 2 for the crib-to-bed transition — unless your child is climbing out of the crib, which is a safety issue that forces the move earlier. [Source: src6]

**Why waiting is usually better:**
- Younger toddlers are not developmentally ready for the freedom of an open bed
- Early transitions often lead to more night waking and bedtime resistance
- The crib is a contained, safe sleep environment

**When to transition:**
- Your child can get a leg up to the top of the crib rail (climbing risk) [Source: src6]
- Your child is around 2 to 3 years old and developmentally ready

**Making the transition:**
- Toddler-proof the entire room — your child can now get out of bed and wander [Source: src6]
- Use safety rails on the bed
- Keep the bedtime routine exactly the same
- Be prepared for a few weeks of adjustment

## Night terrors — they can start now

Night terrors typically begin between ages 2 and 6, peaking around age 3.5. About one-third of children experience them. [Source: src8] They can start appearing toward the end of this age range.

Night terrors look alarming: your child may scream, thrash, appear terrified, and be inconsolable — but they are not fully awake and will have no memory of the episode. [Source: src8]

**What to do during a night terror:**
- Do not try to wake your child — this usually makes it worse
- Stay nearby and keep them safe from injury
- The episode will pass in a few minutes
- Your child will fall back into quiet sleep afterward

Night terrors are different from nightmares. Nightmares happen during REM sleep in the second half of the night, the child wakes up fully and remembers the dream, and they benefit from comfort and reassurance. Night terrors happen during deep non-REM sleep in the first few hours after falling asleep, the child is not truly awake, and they don't remember it.

## When sleep problems need professional help

Most sleep issues at this age are behavioral and respond well to consistent strategies. But some situations warrant professional evaluation.

Early sleep problems can persist — children with sleep difficulties in the first year are more likely to have continued problems. [Source: src3] Addressing sleep issues sooner rather than later can prevent them from becoming entrenched.

**Talk to your pediatrician if:**
- Your baby snores loudly most nights, especially if you hear pauses or gasping
- Night waking persists despite several weeks of consistent behavioral strategies
- Your child seems excessively sleepy during the day
- Sleep problems are significantly affecting your child's daytime behavior or development
- Sleep problems are significantly affecting your own mental or physical health

---

## When to call the doctor

**🔴 Call your doctor right away:**
- Breathing pauses, gasping, or choking during sleep
- Blue or gray skin color during or after sleep
- Your child is extremely difficult to wake
- Excessive limpness or stiffness during sleep

**🟡 Mention at your next visit:**
- Regular loud snoring
- Night waking that has not improved despite consistent bedtime routine and strategies
- Your child seems to need significantly more or less sleep than recommended
- Frequent night terrors (more than once per week)
- You are struggling with sleep deprivation or mood changes

> **A note for this stage:** This is the age where sleep problems go from "this is just what babies do" to "we should probably do something about this." The good news is that most of what's happening is behavioral — meaning it responds to changes you can make. The consistent bedtime routine is not glamorous advice, but it is the most evidence-based thing you can do. And if consistent effort isn't working after several weeks, that's useful information for your pediatrician, not a sign that you're failing.
