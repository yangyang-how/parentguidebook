## Research Brief

Adolescence (12–18 years) marks the transition to Piaget's formal operational stage, characterized by abstract thinking, hypothetical-deductive reasoning, and metacognitive sophistication. Neuroimaging research reveals that the prefrontal cortex — critical for planning, decision-making, and impulse control — does not fully mature until the mid-20s. The mismatch between a rapidly developing limbic system and a still-maturing prefrontal cortex explains many hallmark adolescent behaviors, including risk-taking and emotional intensity.

## Red Flags & Nuances

- The prefrontal cortex is not fully mature until the mid-20s — do not expect adult-level judgment from teenagers
- "Adolescent egocentrism" (imaginary audience, personal fable) is normal and not narcissism
- Not all teens develop formal operational thinking — educational quality and opportunity matter
- Risk-taking is partly developmental, not purely behavioral — brain architecture explains it
- Sleep deprivation significantly impairs adolescent cognitive function; school start times matter
