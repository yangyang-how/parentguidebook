# Mental Health at Ages 12–18 — Depression, Self-Harm, and How to Reach Your Teen

**⏱️ Quick Summary (30 seconds)**

🟡 **Urgency: Important — suicide is the second leading cause of death for ages 10–24. This is a public health emergency, and parents are on the front line.**

**The short version:** Adolescence is when mental health conditions reach their peak incidence. Depression and anxiety rates have surged, self-harm affects 1 in 5 teens, and half of affected adolescents receive no treatment at all. Social media, academic pressure, and the developmental challenges of adolescence create a perfect storm. But treatment works — and the conversations you have with your teenager about their mental health may be the most important conversations of their life.

- **Suicide** is the 2nd leading cause of death for ages 10–24. In 2021, **22% of high school students** seriously considered attempting suicide. [Source: src2]

## The numbers are alarming — and they are real

This is not alarmism. These are facts from the CDC, and they deserve to be stated plainly.

Suicide is the second leading cause of death for Americans aged 10–24. [Source: src2]

In 2021, 22% of high school students seriously considered attempting suicide. Ten percent made an attempt. [Source: src2]

Rates are disproportionately high among LGBTQ+ youth, American Indian/Alaska Native youth, and Black youth. [Source: src2]

This is not about "kids these days" being weak or dramatic. This is a public health emergency that has been building for years — accelerated by the pandemic, amplified by social media, and compounded by a mental health care system that cannot meet the demand.

The parents reading this article are not powerless. You are, in fact, the most important resource your teenager has. Knowing what to look for, knowing what to say, and knowing when to act can change the trajectory of a young person's life.

## Recognizing depression and anxiety in teens

Teen depression does not always look like sadness. It often presents as: [Source: src1, src9]

- **Irritability and anger** — constant arguments, explosive reactions to small things, hostility
- **Social withdrawal** — pulling away from friends, quitting activities, spending all time alone in their room
- **Academic decline** — not because they cannot do the work, but because they do not see the point
- **Changes in sleep** — sleeping excessively or barely sleeping at all
- **Changes in appetite** — eating far more or far less than usual
- **Loss of interest** — nothing is fun anymore, nothing matters
- **Fatigue** — persistent exhaustion that is not explained by their schedule
- **Statements of hopelessness** — "What's the point?" "I don't care about anything." "Nothing will ever get better."

A "moody teenager" who has stopped seeing friends, lost interest in everything, sleeps all day, and says nothing matters is not just being a teenager. That pattern is depression until proven otherwise.

### Anxiety in adolescents

Anxiety often coexists with depression and may present as:

- Excessive worry about grades, social acceptance, the future, or catastrophic scenarios
- Avoidance of social situations, school, or new experiences
- Physical symptoms: racing heart, chest tightness, difficulty breathing, nausea
- Panic attacks
- Perfectionism that causes paralysis — unable to start or complete tasks
- Reassurance-seeking that never seems to satisfy the worry

The USPSTF recommends universal depression screening for all adolescents aged 12–18, and universal anxiety screening for ages 8–18. [Source: src1, src2] If your teen has not been screened at a well-visit, ask their provider.

## Suicide — the conversation you must have

This section exists because it can save lives.

### Asking about suicide does not plant the idea

This is one of the most important facts in adolescent mental health, and it directly contradicts what many parents believe: **asking your teenager about suicidal thoughts does not increase suicidal ideation.** Research consistently shows that asking reduces, rather than increases, distress — because it gives permission to talk about something that may feel unspeakable. [Source: src4]

The AAP supports screening for suicide risk in all adolescents at well-visits and mental health encounters, using validated tools like the Ask Suicide-Screening Questions (ASQ) toolkit. [Source: src4]

### Warning signs

- Talking about wanting to die or wanting to kill themselves
- Looking for ways to end their life (searching online, accessing means)
- Talking about feeling hopeless or having no reason to live
- Talking about feeling trapped or being a burden to others
- Increasing use of alcohol or drugs
- Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities
- Sleeping too much or too little
- Giving away possessions
- Acting agitated, reckless, or engaging in risky activities
- Dramatic mood changes
- Saying goodbye in ways that feel final

### What to do

**Ask directly.** "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" or "Are you thinking about suicide?" Direct language is better than euphemisms.

**Listen without judgment.** Do not minimize ("You have so much to live for"), redirect ("Think about how this would affect me"), or problem-solve prematurely. Listen.

**Do not leave them alone** if you believe they are in immediate danger.

**Remove means.** Lock up or remove firearms, medications, and other means of self-harm. Means restriction is one of the most evidence-based suicide prevention strategies.

**Get professional help immediately.** Call 988, go to the emergency department, or contact their provider.

**988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text **988** — 24/7, available in 240+ languages.

## Self-harm — understanding, not judging

Nonsuicidal self-injury (NSSI) — cutting, burning, scratching, hitting oneself — affects approximately 15–20% of adolescents at some point. [Source: src5]

### Why teens self-harm

Self-harm is most commonly used as an emotion regulation strategy — a way to manage overwhelming feelings that the teenager does not have other tools to process. It is not attention-seeking. It is not manipulation. It is a young person in pain, using the only coping mechanism they have found that provides temporary relief from intolerable emotions. [Source: src5]

### It is a risk factor for suicide

While NSSI is not the same as a suicide attempt, it is a significant risk factor for later suicide attempts and should always be taken seriously. [Source: src5] The line between self-harm and suicidal behavior can shift, particularly when a teenager's emotional state worsens.

### If you discover your teen is self-harming

Your first response matters enormously.

**Do:** Express concern and love. "I can see you're in a lot of pain. I love you and I want to help." Stay calm, even if you are frightened.

**Do not:** React with anger, horror, or punishment. Do not say "Why would you do this to yourself?" or "Stop it right now." Do not threaten to take away their privacy unless safety requires it.

**Do:** Get them connected with a mental health professional as soon as possible. Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) for adolescents is the most evidence-based treatment for NSSI, and it teaches the emotional regulation skills that self-harm was substituting for. [Source: src5]

## Social media and the teen brain

The U.S. Surgeon General issued a 2023 advisory stating that adolescents who spend more than 3 hours per day on social media face double the risk of poor mental health outcomes, including depression and anxiety symptoms. [Source: src3]

### What the research actually shows

The relationship between social media and teen mental health is real but nuanced. Not all social media use is equally harmful: [Source: src3, src10]

**Social comparison** is the primary mechanism linking social media to depression and body dissatisfaction. Platforms designed around curated images of other people's lives create a relentless comparison engine. [Source: src10]

**Passive consumption** (scrolling, viewing) is associated with worse outcomes than active use (creating, interacting with friends). [Source: src10] The teenager who spends two hours creating content and messaging friends is in a different situation than the one who spends two hours silently scrolling through other people's highlight reels.

**Sleep displacement** mediates much of the association between social media and poor mental health. [Source: src10] Nighttime phone use disrupts sleep, and disrupted sleep worsens anxiety and depression. This is one of the most actionable findings: keeping phones out of bedrooms at night addresses a significant portion of the problem.

### What this means for parents

Social media is not inherently destructive, and the solution is not a binary "ban or allow." The evidence supports:

- **Delaying social media access** as long as reasonably possible
- **Limiting total daily screen time**, with particular attention to passive scrolling
- **Keeping devices out of bedrooms at night** to protect sleep
- **Talking about what they see online** — making social media a topic of open conversation, not a secret activity
- **Modeling healthy technology use yourself**

The Surgeon General's advisory explicitly noted that social media platforms are designed to maximize engagement through features that exploit adolescent developmental vulnerabilities. [Source: src3] Your teenager is not weak for being affected. They are the target of billion-dollar engagement engineering.

## Substance use as self-medication

Approximately 15% of adolescents aged 12–17 used illicit substances in the past year. [Source: src6] And here is the number that makes substance use a mental health issue, not just a behavioral one: about 60% of adolescents with substance use disorders also have a mental health condition. [Source: src6]

Substance use in teenagers often begins as self-medication for untreated depression, anxiety, or trauma. [Source: src6] The teenager who starts drinking is frequently the teenager who is trying to numb feelings they do not know how to manage. The teenager who uses marijuana daily may be self-treating anxiety they have never been evaluated for.

This does not mean substance use is acceptable or harmless. It means that when a teenager starts using substances, the right question is not just "What are they taking?" but "What are they trying to not feel?"

Treatment for co-occurring substance use and mental health conditions must address both. Treating the substance use without addressing the underlying mental health condition leads to relapse. Treating the mental health condition while ignoring the substance use is equally incomplete.

## Access to care — the systemic failure

Only about 50% of adolescents with mental health conditions receive any treatment. [Source: src8]

This is not because the other 50% do not need it or do not want it. The barriers are systemic:

- **Cost.** Even with insurance, copays for therapy can be prohibitive. Uninsured families face even higher barriers.
- **Provider shortage.** There are not enough child and adolescent psychiatrists, particularly in rural and underserved areas.
- **Stigma.** Despite progress, mental health stigma continues to prevent families from seeking help — particularly in communities where mental health is seen as a personal failing rather than a medical condition.
- **Cultural barriers.** In many communities, seeking mental health treatment carries enormous stigma. Families may prefer to handle difficulties privately rather than involve outside professionals.
- **The teenager themselves.** Adolescents may resist treatment, deny problems, or refuse to engage. Working with a reluctant teenager requires patience, collaboration, and sometimes creative approaches to engagement.

### What you can do about systemic barriers

- **School-based mental health services.** Many schools now have counselors, social workers, or school-based therapy programs. These remove transportation and cost barriers and meet teenagers where they already are.
- **Telehealth.** Online therapy has expanded access dramatically. Your teen may be more comfortable talking to a therapist through a screen than sitting in an office.
- **Community mental health centers.** These often offer sliding-scale fees and can provide services regardless of insurance status.
- **Start with the pediatrician.** Primary care providers can screen, initiate treatment for mild-moderate cases, and refer to specialists.

## Treatment that works

### For mild to moderate depression

Psychotherapy — specifically CBT (cognitive behavioral therapy) or IPT (interpersonal therapy) — is the recommended first-line treatment for mild to moderate adolescent depression. [Source: src9]

### For moderate to severe depression

Combination therapy — medication plus psychotherapy — is recommended. Fluoxetine (Prozac) has the strongest evidence base for adolescent depression. [Source: src9]

**An important note about medication:** All adolescents started on antidepressants should be monitored closely for increased suicidality, particularly in the first few weeks of treatment. [Source: src9] This is not a reason to avoid medication when it is needed — it is a reason to ensure close follow-up. The black box warning on antidepressants has, paradoxically, caused some providers and families to avoid medication that could be life-saving, leading to an increase in untreated depression and completed suicides. The evidence is clear: for moderate to severe teen depression, the benefits of appropriate medication outweigh the risks.

### For self-harm

Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) for adolescents is the most evidence-based treatment. [Source: src5]

### For the family

Family involvement in treatment is essential. The teenager exists within a family system, and treatment that does not engage that system is less effective. This does not mean the family caused the problem — it means the family is part of the solution.

## What to watch for

### 🔴 Act now

- Any talk of suicide, wanting to die, or being a burden
- Evidence of self-harm (cuts, burns, scratches — often on forearms, thighs, or abdomen)
- Giving away possessions or saying goodbye
- Sudden calmness after a period of deep depression (this can signal a decision has been made)
- Access to means (firearms, stockpiled medications)
- A suicide attempt — this is always a 911 situation

**Call 988, go to the emergency department, or call 911.**

### 🟡 Talk to their provider soon

- Persistent sadness, irritability, or hopelessness lasting more than two weeks
- Social withdrawal — pulling away from friends and activities
- Academic decline that is not explained by other factors
- Sleep disruption (too much or too little) that persists
- Changes in appetite or weight
- Loss of interest in everything
- Increased substance use
- Excessive social media use combined with mood changes
- Expressions of worthlessness or "not caring" about anything

Keep these numbers saved in your phone. Share them with your teenager.

- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Call or text **988** — 24/7, 240+ languages
- **Crisis Text Line:** Text **HOME** to **741741**
